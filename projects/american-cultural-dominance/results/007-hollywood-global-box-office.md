# Hollywood Global Box Office Share

## Summary
Hollywood's share of global box office revenue tells one of the sharpest stories of American cultural dominance. Before WW1, France controlled ~85% of global film screens; by the 1920s, Hollywood had seized majority global market share and never relinquished it. Today, US-produced or co-produced films command roughly 60–70% of worldwide box office revenue outside of countries with deliberate protectionist quotas (China, India, South Korea). The key inflection point was WW1, which destroyed European production capacity while Hollywood scaled up — a structural advantage that compounded for a century.

## Penetration Score Definition
**Denominator:** US-produced films' share of global box office revenue (or, where revenue data is unavailable, share of films exhibited on screens worldwide).
**0% anchor:** No American-produced films are shown anywhere outside the US; every national cinema is entirely domestic.
**100% anchor:** Every film shown in every cinema worldwide is a US production; no domestic film industry exists in any other country.

## Methodology
The penetration score represents Hollywood's estimated share of global box office revenue (or screen share for pre-revenue-tracking eras). This combines:

1. **Pre-1920:** Screen/exhibition share data from film history scholarship (e.g., Pathé's documented 50% global share, French films' 85% of screens in 1913).
2. **1920s-1960s:** Estimated from regional data points — if Hollywood held 94% in Australia, 85% in Brazil, 70% in France, ~30% in Germany (with protectionist policies), and was excluded from Soviet/Axis markets, global share is triangulated.
3. **1970s-2000s:** Industry sources, MPAA data, regional box office databases.
4. **2010s-present:** More precise data from Comscore, national box office databases, MPA annual reports. North America's 27-29% of global BO plus Hollywood's international earnings.

The metric measures US-produced or US-studio-produced films. Co-productions (e.g., UK-US Marvel films) are counted as US given the studio's nationality. This is consistent throughout.

**Key methodological challenge:** "Global box office" as a concept barely existed before the 1990s. Earlier estimates are based on exhibition share (what % of screens showed American films) rather than revenue share. These proxies are directionally consistent but less precise.

## Notes
- **The WW1 inflection is dramatic.** France went from controlling 85% of global screens (1913) to showing 70% Hollywood films within a decade. This is arguably the single most important event in American cultural export history — the war didn't just help Hollywood, it destroyed the competition.
- **Three notable holdouts:** India, South Korea, and China have maintained strong domestic film industries. Common factors: large domestic populations, strong linguistic/cultural distinctiveness, and (in China/Korea's case) deliberate government protection via screen quotas and import limits.
- **Japan is a partial holdout:** Domestic films now hold ~75% of box office, but Japan was heavily penetrated by Hollywood in the post-WW2 occupation era. The recovery took decades.
- **Australia and Latin America show near-total dominance.** English-speaking markets and markets without strong protectionist policies show Hollywood shares of 85-97%.
- **The 2010s-2020s decline is real but nuanced.** Hollywood's absolute revenue continued growing, but the global pie grew faster, driven by China's box office explosion. Hollywood's share dropped not because fewer people watched American films, but because China's domestic market grew enormously with heavy local content quotas (only ~34 foreign film imports/year).
- **The "Big Five" distribution advantage persists:** Disney, Warner, Universal, Sony, and Paramount control global distribution networks that no other national industry can match. Even where domestic films are popular, American studios often control the distribution of those films.
- **Co-production blurring:** Many "British" or "Australian" box office hits (Harry Potter, Mad Max: Fury Road) are financed and distributed by US studios. The true penetration of American capital and creative control is higher than raw national-origin statistics suggest.
