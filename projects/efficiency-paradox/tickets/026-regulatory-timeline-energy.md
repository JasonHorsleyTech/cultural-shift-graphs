# Regulatory Timeline: Energy Efficiency Standards (Vehicles, Lighting, Appliances)

## What to Research

A timeline of major US federal energy efficiency regulations affecting the domains in this study. For each regulation: when enacted, when effective, what it mandated, and estimated energy savings impact.

This provides the **regulatory context annotation** for the graph — which domains had strong regulatory intervention vs. which were market-driven.

## Where to Look

1. **Primary:** DOE Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy (EERE) — maintains a list of all appliance and equipment standards with effective dates and savings estimates.
2. **Secondary:** Congressional Research Service (CRS) reports on CAFE standards, EISA 2007, EPAct 1992.
3. **Tertiary:** ACEEE (American Council for an Energy-Efficient Economy) — tracks and summarizes all efficiency standards.
4. **For CAFE:** NHTSA rulemaking documents and EPA joint rulemakings.
5. **For lighting:** DOE EISA 2007 implementation documents.

## Scope

Cover these regulatory categories:
1. **Vehicle fuel economy (CAFE):** Original 1975 act, standards progression, 2012 Obama rule, 2020 SAFE rule, 2022 Biden reinstatement.
2. **Lighting (EISA 2007):** Phase-out of incandescent bulbs (2012-2014 effective), 2017 backstop provision, 2022 Biden enforcement.
3. **Refrigerators:** 1987 NAECA (initial), 1990/1993/2001/2014 tightened standards.
4. **HVAC (cooling):** SEER minimum standards — 1992 (SEER 10), 2006 (SEER 13), 2015 regional (SEER 14), 2023 (SEER2 15).
5. **HVAC (heating):** AFUE minimums — 1992 (78%), 2015 (80%), plus regional condensing requirements.
6. **Clothes washers/dryers:** DOE standards — 1994, 2004, 2007, 2015 water factor and energy factor.
7. **Freight trucks:** EPA/NHTSA Phase 1 (2014-2018) and Phase 2 (2021-2027) GHG/fuel efficiency standards for medium and heavy-duty vehicles.

|--------|-----------|----------------|-------|

## Data Gaps

- Savings estimates from DOE rulemakings are prospective (what they expected). Actual savings may differ. Use DOE's own estimates — they're consistent and citable.
- State-level regulations (especially California) often preceded federal standards. Note this for lighting and appliances but don't try to catalog all 50 states.

## What "Good Data" Looks Like

- A clear chronological table showing when each major regulation took effect.
- Enough detail to annotate the graph: "this efficiency gain happened in a regulated domain" vs. "this happened in an unregulated domain."
- The punchline for the graph: domains with strong regulation (water, refrigeration, lighting) tend to show higher EDC (more savings captured) because regulation prevents the behavioral rebound from eating the gains.
