# Dust Bowl Farmers

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Great Plains dryland farmers (wheat-dominant)
- **Country/Region:** United States, Southern Great Plains (Oklahoma, Kansas, Texas panhandle, Colorado, New Mexico)
- **Peak Year:** 1930 (last census before drought devastation; farm expansion peaked in late 1920s)
- **Peak Workforce:** ~1,200,000 farm operators and laborers in the core Dust Bowl states (estimated from Census of Agriculture 1930 — Oklahoma alone had ~200,000 farms, Kansas ~166,000, Texas panhandle and western portions ~150,000+, Colorado ~60,000+, New Mexico ~30,000+; average ~2 workers per farm)
- **Primary Mechanism:** discrete_shock
- **Cause Tags:** environmental, resource
- **Killing Event:** Prolonged drought (1930–1940) combined with decades of destructive agricultural practices (deep plowing, sod-busting) stripped topsoil across the Southern Plains, causing massive dust storms that made farming impossible and drove ~2.5 million people from the region — compounded catastrophically by the simultaneous Great Depression price collapse.

**Important caveat:** The 1935 national farm count paradoxically *increased* because the Depression pushed urban unemployed back onto subsistence plots. This masks the devastation in the Dust Bowl core, where farms were being abandoned. The 20 hardest-hit Dust Bowl counties went from 120,859 population in 1930 to 97,606 in 1940 — a 19.2% decline. High-erosion counties lost 8.1% of population relative to low-erosion counties (Hornbeck 2012). The real displacement is visible at the county level, not the national farm count.

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1931 (drought onset; first major crop failures)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~5 (by 1935, core Dust Bowl county farm population had declined ~25% from 1930 levels)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** N/A at the county level within the 1930s decade; the 20 core counties lost ~19% by 1940, but some individual counties lost 40–50% (e.g., Baca County CO lost 41.3% of population 1930–1940)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** N/A — the profession (Great Plains farming) was not destroyed, it was restructured. The region is farmed again today.
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** N/A
- **Decline Shape:** cliff (1931–1936 was catastrophic; partial recovery after 1937–1938 rains and conservation programs)
- **Current Status:** contracted (10-50%) — Great Plains farming continues but with far fewer, far larger farms. The 2022 Census of Agriculture shows continued consolidation. Many former Dust Bowl counties never recovered their 1930 population levels.

**Key finding:** The NBER (Long & Siu, 2016) found that 62.9% of Dust Bowl migrants were still residing in one of the Dust Bowl states in 1940. The California "Okie" migration, while iconic, was not the majority experience. Most displaced farmers stayed regional.

## Surplus Capture

Who benefited from the efficiency gains or cost savings? Allocate roughly:

This event is unusual — it's a *loss* event, not an *efficiency* event. There were no efficiency gains; environmental destruction destroyed surplus for everyone. The "surplus" question applies to the post-Dust Bowl restructuring:

- **Capital owners:** 40% — Large landholders who survived (lower debt, deeper wells, more reserves) bought out failed farms at foreclosure prices. Post-WWII, the shift to irrigated industrial agriculture (Ogallala Aquifer) concentrated land ownership.
- **Consumers:** 25% — Long-term, conservation practices and restructured farming produced more stable food supply. Short-term consumers faced higher prices from crop failures.
- **Adjacent workers:** 10% — California growers benefited from a huge labor surplus that depressed migrant wages. Oil industry in Oklahoma absorbed some displaced workers.
- **Government/public:** 25% — Massive expansion of federal agricultural programs (AAA, SCS, FSA). Created permanent federal role in agriculture. Soil Conservation Service (now NRCS) remains active today. Public benefited from prevented future erosion.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
**Hugh Hammond Bennett** — A USDA soil scientist who had been warning about erosion since the 1920s. When the Dust Bowl hit, he was perfectly positioned. On April 19, 1935 — five days after Black Sunday — he testified before Congress while a dust cloud from the Plains darkened the Washington D.C. sky outside the windows. Congress established the Soil Conservation Service that same year, with Bennett as its first chief. He became known as "the father of soil conservation."

**Early migrant families (1931–1933)** — Families who left the Plains in the first years of drought, before the worst storms and before land values collapsed completely, were able to sell their farms for some value and arrive in California ahead of the massive 1935–1938 migration wave. Early arrivals faced less competition for work and less anti-Okie discrimination.

### False Movers
**Suitcase farmers of the 1920s** — Speculators who bought Great Plains land during the WWI wheat boom, often farming it from a distance ("suitcase farming"). They plowed up native grassland for wheat, leveraging heavily to buy more acreage. When both prices and rain failed simultaneously, they were wiped out — their debt far exceeded the collapsed value of their land. In 1930, farm mortgage debt was 190% of net farm personal income nationally; in the Plains it was worse.

**Farmers who doubled down on wheat acreage** — Some responded to falling per-bushel prices by plowing more acreage to maintain total revenue. This accelerated topsoil destruction: Kansas seeded 13.5 million acres of wheat in 1935, but harvested only 6.9 million — the rest was blown out or buried by drifting soil.

### Deniers
**"Rain follows the plow"** — The pseudoscientific belief, promoted by land speculator Charles Dana Wilber in his 1881 book *The Great Valleys and Prairies of Nebraska and the Northwest*, that farming itself would increase rainfall in arid regions. The theory held that plowing exposed soil moisture to the sky, that planted trees increased precipitation, and that human activity created rain-bearing clouds. It was used to justify settlement of semi-arid land clearly unsuitable for dryland farming. As Wilber wrote: "God speed the plow... By this wonderful provision, which is only man's duty to obey, the clouds are dispensing copious rains."

**Promotional railroad and land company materials** encouraged settlement throughout the 1870s–1910s, advertising the Great Plains as fertile farmland rather than the semi-arid grassland it was.

### Survival Pattern
Survivors had lower debt, diversified crops (not all-wheat monoculture), willingness to adopt conservation practices (contour plowing, crop rotation, shelterbelts), and either the capital reserves to endure multiple failed harvests or the willingness to migrate early before options narrowed.

## Unique Texture

1. **Black Sunday turned day to night across 300 miles.** On April 14, 1935, a cold front collided with warm air, kicking up a dust cloud 10,000 feet high, 800 miles long, and 300–500 miles wide. It traveled at 60 mph, displacing an estimated 300,000 tons of topsoil. Associated Press reporter Robert Geiger, caught in Boise City, Oklahoma, coined the term "Dust Bowl" the next day. (National Weather Service; Smithsonian)

2. **Dust pneumonia killed an estimated 7,000 people.** The prairie dust was finer than a period at the end of a sentence, with high silica content causing a form of silicosis. Children and elderly were most vulnerable. There are no official death statistics — the Red Cross reported 17 dust pneumonia deaths and 3 dust suffocation deaths in Kansas alone as of April 24, 1935. The total toll was likely much higher. (Harvard Medicine; Wikipedia — Dust pneumonia)

3. **The Ogallala Aquifer is a slow-motion replay.** After the Dust Bowl, the Southern Plains were re-farmed using center-pivot irrigation from the Ogallala Aquifer starting in the 1950s. Today the aquifer is being depleted at an annual volume equivalent to 18 Colorado Rivers. Losses between 2001 and 2011 equaled a third of all cumulative depletion during the entire 20th century. Once depleted, it will take over 6,000 years to replenish naturally. (NOAA; Scientific American)

4. **Steinbeck's research was real journalism, not fiction.** *The Grapes of Wrath* (1939) grew out of a seven-article series Steinbeck wrote for the San Francisco News in 1936. He toured migrant camps, read FSA camp manager Tom Collins' 30-page weekly reports, and used photographer Dorothea Lange's images. Collins became the model for the character Jim Rawley. Sanora Babb, an FSA field worker, had written her own novel about the migrants (*Whose Names Are Unknown*) — but her publisher shelved it when Steinbeck's book came out first. (National Archives; Library of Congress)

5. **Between 1930 and 1935, nearly 750,000 farms nationwide disappeared through foreclosure or bankruptcy.** During 1933 alone, over 200,000 farms underwent foreclosure. Farm mortgage debt was 190% of net farm personal income in 1930 — meaning farmers owed nearly twice what they earned. (Encyclopedia.com; USDA ERS)

## Known Data Gaps

- **County-level farm workforce data (not just population):** Census of Agriculture gives farm counts by county, but the number of farm *workers* per farm is estimated, not counted at the county level for this era. The "~1.2 million" peak workforce is a rough estimate.
- **Dust Bowl vs. Depression separation:** Impossible to cleanly disentangle environmental displacement from economic displacement. They were simultaneous and mutually reinforcing. All income data reflects both shocks.
- **Individual tracking of displaced farmers:** No panel data exists. We know where people ended up (1940 Census state of birth vs. residence) but not the occupation-to-occupation transition paths.
- **Dust pneumonia death toll:** No official statistics exist. The 7,000 figure is widely cited but unverifiable. Hospital and death records from rural Plains counties in the 1930s are incomplete.
- **Income data for Great Plains specifically vs. US farm average:** State-level per-farm income breakdowns exist in census archives but were not digitized in accessible form. The income timeline uses national farm averages, which understate the Plains collapse.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium
- **Workforce numbers:** medium — Census of Agriculture provides farm counts every 5 years, but farm workforce estimates require multipliers that introduce uncertainty. County-level population data from Hornbeck (2012) is solid.
- **Income data:** low-medium — National farm income averages are well-documented (USDA/BEA), but Great Plains-specific per-farm income requires piecing together wheat prices, crop yields, and federal payments. The overlap with the Depression makes attribution uncertain.
- **Where-they-went:** medium — The NBER migration study (Long & Siu 2016) using linked census records is excellent for state-level flows. The California migration is well-documented by Gregory (1989). Intra-state rural-to-urban moves are inferred, not tracked.
