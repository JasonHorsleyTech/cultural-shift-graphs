# 001: US Postal Mail — Results

## Summary

US postal mail is the oldest communication system in this dataset and arguably the most thoroughly documented, thanks to USPS annual reports going back to the 19th century. Total mail volume peaked at 213.1 billion pieces in FY 2006, then declined precipitously as digital communication displaced first-class personal and business correspondence. By 2025, volume had roughly halved to 108.7 billion pieces.

The "spam" story of postal mail is the rise of third-class / Standard Mail / Marketing Mail (the category has been renamed several times). Bulk advertising mail was negligible before 1900, grew modestly through the mid-20th century, then exploded in the 1970s–2000s as the direct marketing industry matured and USPS offered deeply discounted bulk rates. By 1981, third-class mail represented over 30% of total volume. Standard Mail volume first exceeded First-Class Mail volume around 2005 (roughly 101 billion vs. 98 billion pieces). Marketing Mail exceeded 50% of total mail volume around 2012–2014, where it has remained since. In 2025, Marketing Mail constitutes approximately 51% of total volume — and since first-class personal correspondence is now a tiny fraction of First-Class Mail (most of which is bills, statements, and business transactional mail), the real "spam" share of what lands in a residential mailbox is likely 70–80%.

Regulatory responses to junk mail have been remarkably weak compared to other communication channels. There is no federal "Do Not Mail" registry analogous to the Do Not Call list. The Deceptive Mail Prevention and Enforcement Act (1999) targeted sweepstakes fraud, not bulk advertising generally. The primary defense — the DMAchoice opt-out service — is voluntary and only covers addressed mail from participating companies. USPS has little incentive to curb junk mail: Marketing Mail generates roughly $15–16 billion in annual revenue and effectively subsidizes the declining first-class mail system.

## Key Data

**System profile:** Launched 1775 | Spam crossover ~2012 | Still alive | 237 years to spam saturation

### By the Numbers

- **213.1 billion** pieces at peak volume (2006) — now halved to 108.7B
- **237 years** from launch to spam majority — the slowest crossover of any communication system
- **$15.8 billion** in annual Marketing Mail revenue (2025) — junk mail is USPS's financial lifeline
- **70–80%** of what lands in a residential mailbox is likely advertising
- **0.1%** of Americans consider postal mail their primary communication method (2025, down from 95% in 1850)
- **$0.78 → $0.80** cost per stamp (2026 dollars) across 125 years — remarkably flat in real terms

### Regulations

| Year | Name | What it did |
|------|------|-------------|
| 1967 | Pandering Advertisement Statute (39 USC 3008) | Lets individuals request specific mailers stop sending material they consider pandering. One mailer at a time, recipient-initiated. |
| 1991 | Telephone Consumer Protection Act (TCPA) | Primarily targeted phone/fax spam, but set precedent for unsolicited commercial communication restrictions. |
| 1999 | Deceptive Mail Prevention and Enforcement Act | Required sweepstakes disclosures and opt-outs. Targeted fraud, not bulk advertising generally. |
| 2006–2010 | Do Not Mail legislative attempts | Multiple bills proposed a national Do Not Mail registry. All failed — USPS and the direct mail industry killed every one. |

### Defenses

| Year | Defense | Description |
|------|---------|-------------|
| 1967 | Form 1500 opt-out | Request specific mailers stop sending sexually oriented advertising. Expanded informally to other unwanted mail. |
| 1971 | Mail Preference Service | DMA launched a mail-based opt-out for consumers. Predecessor to DMAchoice. |
| 2012 | DMAchoice online opt-out | Online service to opt out of direct mail from participating companies. Voluntary — doesn't cover all mailers or saturation mail. |
| 2017 | USPS Informed Delivery | Digital preview of incoming mail via email/app. Not a filter, but functionally an inbox for physical mail. |

### Timeline Highlights

- **1850** — 18.4 million pieces/year, 1% spam. Mail is almost entirely personal correspondence. Postage costs ~$1.19 in today's dollars. Rural areas have limited postal access.
- **1900** — 7.1 billion pieces. Rural Free Delivery (1896) and penny postcards (1898) blow the doors open. Sears and Montgomery Ward catalogs drive early bulk mail growth. Fewer than 1.4 million phones exist in the US.
- **1981** — Third-class mail hits 30% of total volume for the first time, growing 18% in a single year (1984). USPS's discounted bulk rates are an accelerant.
- **2005–2006** — The crossover: Standard Mail (102.5B) exceeds First-Class (97.6B) for the first time. Peak total volume: 213.1 billion pieces. But personal letters are already a tiny fraction of First-Class — most is bills, statements, and business mail.
- **2012–2014** — Marketing Mail crosses 50% of total volume. The system is now majority junk.
- **2025** — Volume halved since peak. Marketing Mail (57.5B) is 51% of total. USPS operating expenses: $89.8B. The stamp price may hit $0.82. The system is in managed decline, kept alive by the very spam that degraded it.
