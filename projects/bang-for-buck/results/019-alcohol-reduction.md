# Result: Alcohol Reduction (Brief Interventions)

## Summary

Brief alcohol interventions (5–15-minute screening + motivational counseling during primary care visits) are among the most cost-effective preventive health services known. The Cochrane review (Kaner et al. 2018, 69 trials, 33,642 participants) found a mean reduction of 20 g/week (~2.5 US standard drinks) in hazardous drinkers at 12 months. Solberg et al. (2008) ranked alcohol SBI as one of the highest-value preventive services among 25 evaluated, with a cost-effectiveness ratio of $1,755/QALY from the health-system perspective. For Bob — a 25th-percentile American who, if he drinks heavily, does so more frequently and intensely than higher-income peers — the intervention is essentially free when delivered during an existing visit and produces modest but real QALY gains. The main challenge is that Bob may not have regular primary care access.

## QALY Evidence
- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.03
- **Range:** 0.01 – 0.07 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** Solberg et al. (2008), "Primary Care Intervention to Reduce Alcohol Misuse: Ranking Its Health Impact and Cost Effectiveness," *American Journal of Preventive Medicine*; Tariq et al. (2009), Dutch cost-effectiveness model in *PLOS ONE*
- **Study type:** Meta-analyses and cost-effectiveness modeling (Solberg synthesized RCTs 1992–2004; Tariq built a Markov model over 100-year horizon; Cochrane 2018 pooled 69 RCTs)
- **Population studied:** General adult population in primary care settings (US, Netherlands, UK). Hazardous/harmful drinkers identified via AUDIT or CAGE screening tools.
- **Population match to Bob:** Fair — Most studies use general-population primary care attendees. Lower-income heavy drinkers are underrepresented. However, CDC BRFSS data shows that while binge drinking prevalence is actually lower among those with income <$25K, those who do drink heavily have higher frequency (5.0 episodes/month) and intensity (8.5 drinks/occasion) than wealthier peers, suggesting a higher ceiling for benefit among low-income hazardous drinkers.
- **Key caveats:**
  - The 0.03 mid-estimate assumes Bob is a hazardous drinker who responds to the intervention and sustains a meaningful reduction. Population-level per-person-screened estimates are much lower (0.009 QALYs lifetime from Dutch model, ~0.044 per birth-cohort member from Solberg) because most people screened are not hazardous drinkers.
  - QALY gains accumulate over decades via reduced liver disease, cardiovascular events, cancers, and injuries — not immediate quality-of-life improvement.
  - Brief intervention effects may attenuate after 12 months without reinforcement. Cochrane 2018 found strong effects at 12 months but less data at longer follow-up.
  - The Cochrane mean reduction of 20 g/week is modest — equivalent to about 2.5 standard US drinks. For heavy drinkers consuming 20+ drinks/week, this may not move them below the "hazardous" threshold.
  - "No safe level" framing from recent WHO guidance vs. prior "J-curve" (moderate drinking as protective) debate — current evidence leans toward any reduction being beneficial for heavy drinkers.

## Cost Analysis (for Bob at ~$30k household income)
- **Annual direct cost:** $30
- **One-time startup cost:** $0
- **Startup cost amortized over 3 years:** $0/year
- **Total annual cost (best estimate):** $30
- **Cost range:** $0 – $150 (low – high)
- **With insurance (if applicable):** $0 (SBIRT is covered under ACA preventive services with no copay)
- **Without insurance:** $100–$250 per visit, but incremental cost of adding screening + brief counseling to an existing visit is only $25–50
- **Cost notes:** The intervention itself is extraordinarily cheap. AUDIT screening takes 2 minutes (near-zero marginal cost). Brief counseling adds 5–15 minutes to a visit. Project TrEAT (Fleming et al. 2000/2002) estimated total intervention cost at $205/patient including screening ($88), training ($23), intervention delivery ($55), and patient time/travel ($39) — but the incremental medical cost was only ~$55. WSIPP (2023 dollars) estimated program cost at $334/participant over the intervention lifecycle. The low-end $0 reflects that SBIRT is a covered ACA preventive benefit. The high-end $150 reflects Bob needing a dedicated visit he wouldn't otherwise have. The mid-estimate of $30 assumes Bob has at least one annual primary care visit where screening is added.

## Time Commitment
- **Hours per week:** 0.1
- **Category:** Low (<1 hr/wk)
- **Notes:** The brief intervention itself is 5–15 minutes, delivered 1–4 times per year. The behavior change (drinking less) does not require additional time — it actually frees time. Total time investment is approximately 30–60 minutes per year in medical appointments.

## Access and Barriers
- **Activation energy:** Medium
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - **Primary care access:** Bob may lack a regular doctor or insurance, making even "free" screening inaccessible. 25th-percentile adults have lower rates of annual checkups.
  - **Stigma:** Alcohol screening can feel judgmental, especially if Bob doesn't self-identify as having a "drinking problem." AUDIT framing helps vs. direct confrontation.
  - **Cultural norms:** Drinking may be deeply embedded in Bob's social circle. Reducing intake means swimming against social currents.
  - **Self-identification:** Brief interventions target "hazardous" drinkers, not alcohol-dependent individuals. Bob may not recognize himself as the target audience. About 70% of excessive drinkers are not alcohol-dependent.
  - **Motivation:** Brief interventions use motivational interviewing, which requires some receptiveness. Effectiveness is lower when forced or perfunctory.

## Side Effects and Risks
- No medical side effects from the intervention itself (it's a conversation)
- Social discomfort from reduced drinking in social contexts
- For alcohol-dependent individuals (not the target of brief intervention), abrupt cessation can cause withdrawal symptoms — but brief intervention targets hazardous drinkers, not dependent drinkers
- Small risk of psychological distress from confronting drinking patterns
- Possible anxiety about being "labeled" after screening

## Adherence
- **Real-world adherence rate:** 35%
- **Source:** Kaner et al. (2018) Cochrane Review; Fleming et al. (2002) Project TrEAT 48-month follow-up
- **Notes:** About 50–60% of those receiving brief intervention show reduced consumption at 12 months (Cochrane 2018). Effects attenuate over longer periods — Project TrEAT showed sustained but diminishing benefits at 48 months. The 35% figure reflects sustained meaningful reduction at 2+ years. Non-adherence is driven by return to social drinking contexts, lack of follow-up reinforcement, and the intervention's modest behavioral "dose." Booster sessions improve persistence. Real-world implementation studies show that only 30–50% of primary care providers consistently deliver SBIRT even when trained — the barrier is often on the provider side, not just the patient side.

## Evidence Quality
- **Tier:** 1
- **Tier 1** = Multiple RCTs or high-quality meta-analyses, Bob-adjacent populations
- **Key studies:**
  - Kaner et al. (2018). "Effectiveness of brief alcohol interventions in primary care populations." *Cochrane Database of Systematic Reviews*. 69 RCTs, 33,642 participants. Gold-standard systematic review.
  - Solberg et al. (2008). "Primary care intervention to reduce alcohol misuse ranking its health impact and cost effectiveness." *American Journal of Preventive Medicine*. Ranked alcohol SBI among the most cost-effective of 25 preventive services. $1,755/QALY (health system), cost-saving from societal perspective.
  - Tariq et al. (2009). "Cost-effectiveness of an opportunistic screening programme and brief intervention for excessive alcohol use in primary care." *PLOS ONE*. Dutch Markov model: €5,400/QALY, 56,000 QALYs gained at population level.
  - Purshouse et al. (2013). "Modelling the cost-effectiveness of alcohol screening and brief interventions in primary care in England." *Alcohol and Alcoholism*. £1,175/QALY for GP-delivered SBI.
  - Fleming et al. (2000, 2002). Project TrEAT. US primary care RCT showing 48-month sustained benefits, benefit-cost ratio of 4.3:1 (medical) and 39:1 (societal).
- **Limitations:**
  - Most CEA studies report population-level QALYs, not per-individual-responder QALYs, making translation to "Bob's personal benefit" require extrapolation.
  - QALY weights for alcohol consumption levels are poorly defined — studies model through disease outcomes (liver disease, cardiovascular events, injuries) rather than direct utility measurement.
  - The Cochrane review notes that brief interventions have "little impact on frequency of binges per week, drinking days per week, or drinking intensity" — the effect is primarily on total weekly consumption. This means the most dangerous drinking patterns (binge episodes) may persist even as average consumption drops.
  - Low-income populations are underrepresented in the RCT literature. Effect sizes may differ for Bob's socioeconomic context.
