# US Steel Workers

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Iron and steel mill production workers (blast furnace operators, rollers, heaters, furnace tenders, and related occupations in integrated steel mills)
- **Country/Region:** United States, concentrated in the Rust Belt (Pittsburgh PA, Youngstown OH, Gary IN, Bethlehem PA, Birmingham AL, Cleveland OH)
- **Peak Year:** 1953
- **Peak Workforce:** 650,000 (American Iron and Steel Institute)
- **Primary Mechanism:** gradual_erosion
- **Cause Tags:** globalization, tech_tool, consolidation
- **Killing Event:** A multi-decade convergence of rising import competition (Japan, Europe, then developing nations), the mini-mill revolution (electric arc furnaces replacing blast furnaces), and massive productivity gains that allowed the same steel output with a fraction of the workforce.

**Note on varying definitions:** The 2019/2025 figure of ~83,000 represents the narrower NAICS 3311 (iron and steel mills proper). The 2021 figure of 143,000 includes NAICS 3312 (steel product manufacturing from purchased steel). For the decline metrics below, the narrower definition (~83,000 current) against the historical peak of 650,000 is used, as this best captures the displacement of the original integrated-mill workforce.

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1953 (sustained decline from peak, accelerating sharply after 1979)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~16 years (1953 → ~1969, workforce ~490,000)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** ~27 years (1953 → ~1980, workforce ~325,000)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** ~40 years (1953 → ~1993, workforce ~163,000)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** ~66 years (1953 → ~2019, workforce ~83,000 in narrow definition)
- **Decline Shape:** stepped — long slow erosion 1953–1979, then a cliff during 1979–1984 (lost 50% in 5 years), followed by stabilization and continued slow erosion through 2020s.
- **Current Status:** remnant (<10% peak) — under narrow NAICS 3311 definition; contracted (10-50%) under broader industry definition including steel product manufacturing.

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: estimated

Pre-1970 wage data is sourced from BLS Wage Chronology bulletins for US Steel Corporation and Bethlehem Steel; post-1970 data from BLS CES average hourly earnings and academic studies. Exact median figures are estimated from contract wage data and BLS industry averages. All inflation adjustments use CPI-U.

- **Income at Peak:** ~$7,540/year ($145/week) in 1965, equivalent to ~$78,000 in 2024 dollars (BLS Wage Chronology / History Facts)
- **Income at Trough:** ~$35,000–$40,000/year for displaced workers who found new employment in service sector in 1980s–1990s, representing roughly 50% real income loss (academic studies of displaced steelworkers)
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** ~85% for those who remained in steel; ~50% for displaced workers who transitioned to service sector
- **Inflation Adjustment Method:** CPI-U

**Key observation:** Nominal wages rose steadily, but real wages peaked around 1973–1980 when total compensation (including generous union benefits — pensions, healthcare, vacation) was at its highest. The 1983 USWA concession eliminated $1.25/hour in wages plus COLA payments, vacation time, and Sunday premium pay — worth ~$3 billion to steelmakers. Today's steelworkers earn less in real terms and receive far fewer benefits.

## Surplus Capture

- **Capital owners:** 40% — Steel company shareholders initially suffered through bankruptcies, but surviving/restructured firms (Nucor, restructured US Steel) became highly profitable. Nucor's market cap grew enormously. Mini-mill operators captured massive gains from 10x productivity improvement with non-union labor.
- **Consumers:** 35% — Steel prices declined substantially in real terms. Import competition and mini-mill efficiency lowered costs for auto manufacturers, construction, appliance makers, and ultimately end consumers. 
- **Adjacent workers:** 10% — Mini-mill workers (Nucor's pay-for-performance system made teammates among the highest-paid in the industry, though far fewer in number). Some gains for downstream industries that benefited from cheaper steel.
- **Government/public:** 15% — Massive costs: PBGC took on billions in unfunded pension liabilities (Bethlehem Steel alone: $3.7 billion). Trade Adjustment Assistance, unemployment insurance, disability payments, and community redevelopment costs partially offset by tax revenue from more efficient successor companies.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
- **Ken Iverson / Nucor Corporation:** Joined Nuclear Corporation in 1962, became president in 1965. Built the first electric arc furnace mini-mill in Darlington, South Carolina in 1969 when skeptics said EAFs couldn't produce quality steel. Introduced radical pay-for-performance (weekly production bonuses) instead of union contracts. Today Nucor is the largest US steel producer with 24 EAF mills producing 29 million tons annually. Iverson saw that the integrated-mill model was dead and built the replacement.
- **Pittsburgh's institutional pivot:** Carnegie Mellon University and University of Pittsburgh invested heavily in computing, robotics, and biotechnology from the 1980s onward. Mayor Caliguiri envisioned the city as "a center for health care, a city of High Technology, a city of Robotics." The region transformed into an eds-and-meds economy — though this took decades and primarily benefited the next generation, not displaced steelworkers.

### False Movers
- **Voluntary Restraint Agreements (VRAs) and import quotas:** Throughout the 1970s–1990s, the US government imposed various trade protections. These slowed import penetration temporarily but gave integrated mills a false sense of security, delaying modernization. When protections expired, the industry was still uncompetitive. Workers who relied on protectionism to save their jobs found the reprieve was temporary.
- **Employee Stock Ownership Plans (ESOPs):** USWA President Lynn Williams led efforts to swap wage concessions for ownership stakes and board seats. Several worker-owned mills (Weirton Steel, others) were established. Most eventually failed anyway — worker ownership couldn't overcome the fundamental cost disadvantage of integrated mills vs. mini-mills.

### Deniers
- **David Roderick (US Steel CEO, 1979–1989):** Blamed foreign competition almost exclusively, calling foreign producers "mercantilists of the 18th Century" and campaigning relentlessly for import protection. Predicted imports would reach 40% of US consumption. While imports were a real factor, the larger threat — mini-mills and productivity technology — was domestic. Under Roderick, US Steel closed 7 of 12 mills and cut its workforce from 75,000 to 20,000, but the company's strategy focused on political lobbying rather than technological transformation.
- **Youngstown community response (1977–1978):** After Black Monday, community groups, churches, and unions organized to buy and reopen the Campbell Works. The plan required $100 million in federal loan guarantees. President Carter denied the request. Even had it been approved, the economics of the integrated mill model were failing everywhere — the effort, while understandable, was an attempt to save an obsolete production model.

### Survival Pattern
Workers who survived were geographically mobile (willing to leave steel towns), younger (able to retrain or enter college), or made the jump to mini-mill employment in different locations — essentially, they left behind both the place and the production model.

## Unique Texture

1. **The productivity paradox:** US steel shipments in 2005 were roughly equal to early 1960s levels, yet the industry employed 75% fewer workers. Man-hours per ton of finished steel fell from 10.1 in 1980 to 1.5 by 2017, with some mini-mills requiring just 0.5 hours. The steel didn't disappear — the steelworkers did. (AEI / AISI data)

2. **Bethlehem Steel's arc:** The company that built the Golden Gate Bridge, Empire State Building, Hoover Dam, George Washington Bridge, and Rockefeller Center — that employed 300,000 workers at peak — filed for bankruptcy in 2001 with 11,000 workers remaining. The PBGC took over its pension plan, the largest ever assumed: $7.8 billion in liabilities backed by only $3.5 billion in assets (45% funded). 95,000 retirees and workers lost or saw reduced benefits. (PBGC / Bethlehem Steel bankruptcy filings)

3. **Youngstown's half-life:** On September 19, 1977 — "Black Monday" — Youngstown Sheet and Tube announced closure of the Campbell Works, displacing 5,000 workers. Within five years, 50,000 jobs disappeared from the Mahoning Valley. Youngstown's population fell from over 150,000 at its peak to below 65,000 today. The event is credited with naming the "Rust Belt." (Belt Magazine oral history)

4. **The deaths-of-despair connection:** Studies found manufacturing job loss predicted up to 92,000 male and 44,000 female overdose deaths over a nearly two-decade period. Rust Belt workers lost 51.1% of pre-displacement wages on average after a plant closing — compared to 35.5% for displaced workers nationally. Between 1999 and 2017, fatal drug overdoses increased 386.5% among midlife adults in affected communities. (PMC / Brookings)

5. **The mini-mill inversion:** In 1981, mini-mills produced ~15% of US steel. By 2002, electric arc furnace mills produced over half. By 2014, there were 113 mini-mills versus only 11 integrated mills operating. Nucor, founded as a nuclear instrument company, became America's largest steel producer with pay-for-performance bonuses that made its non-union workers among the highest-paid in the industry. The old industry was replaced by a fundamentally different one producing the same product. (Wikipedia / NPR)

## Known Data Gaps

- **Year-by-year employment before 1974:** AISI reported ~700,000 through the 1950s and 650,000 peak in 1953, but granular annual figures between 1953 and 1974 are not available from the sources accessed. Interpolation was used for some data points.
- **Precise median income by year:** BLS Wage Chronology bulletins report contract wage rates (base + COLA + overtime), not median earnings distributions. The income table uses estimated figures from contract data, not individual-level survey data.
- **Individual-level "where they went" tracking:** No panel data tracks individual displaced steelworkers over time. Estimates are based on regional labor market analysis, census population changes, and academic studies of Rust Belt deindustrialization rather than worker-level data.
- **Narrow vs. broad industry definition:** Employment figures vary significantly depending on whether the count includes only primary iron and steel mills (NAICS 3311, ~83,000 today) or the broader steel product manufacturing sector (NAICS 3311+3312, ~143,000). Historical SIC 331 figures are closest to the broader definition. This makes precise decline ratios somewhat definition-dependent.
- **Mini-mill worker demographics:** Whether displaced integrated-mill workers actually transitioned to mini-mill employment is inferred from geographic and demographic analysis rather than tracked directly. Mini-mills were typically in different locations (South, rural) with different labor cultures (non-union, pay-for-performance).

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium-high
- **Workforce numbers:** medium-high — AISI and BLS provide reliable anchor points at 1953, 1974, 1980, 1984, 1990, and 2015+, though mid-period values are interpolated and definition changes between SIC/NAICS create comparability issues.
- **Income data:** medium — Contract wage data from BLS Wage Chronologies is reliable for union workers, but median earnings across all steelworkers (including non-union, mini-mill, varying occupations) are estimated. The total compensation story (benefits erosion) is well-documented qualitatively but hard to quantify precisely.
- **Where-they-went:** medium — No individual tracking. Inferred from regional population loss (well-documented), labor market studies (academic quality), and displaced-worker surveys. The broad pattern is clear (many never recovered, massive wage loss for those who did); the specific percentages are estimates.
