# Television — Content Access Cost

## Summary

The cost of accessing television content underwent a radical transformation from 1955 to 2025 — from entirely free over-the-air broadcast to a layered subscription model costing the median household $1,200–$1,800/year. The story has three acts: the free era (1955–~1978), the cable era (~1978–~2012), and the streaming era (~2012–present). Each transition added a new cost layer without fully removing the previous one. The critical insight for the graph: while the TV set itself became cheaper and better (a "progress wins" story), the total cost of watching television exploded. A 1955 household paid $0/year for content; a 2025 household pays $1,440–$1,680/year for internet + streaming, or $1,200–$1,800/year for cable. The TV hardware savings are dwarfed by the content access cost.

## Methodology

### Defining "content access cost"

Content access cost means the **recurring subscription and service fees required to watch TV programming beyond free over-the-air broadcast.** This includes:

- **Cable/satellite TV subscription** (the dominant model from ~1985–2015)
- **Streaming service subscriptions** (Netflix, Hulu, Disney+, HBO Max, etc. — dominant from ~2018 onward)
- **Internet service** is deliberately excluded from the primary content access line but noted as a caveat, because internet is shared infrastructure used for many purposes beyond TV. However, internet is now functionally required for most TV viewing (smart TVs, streaming), so the total cost of viewing annotation includes it implicitly in the 2020–2025 streaming cost figures (streaming services are unusable without internet).

### Tracking the "median household"

Rather than tracking the cheapest possible option (free OTA antenna, which is still $0/year in 2025), this tracks what the **median American household actually spent** on television content access in each era. Key transitions:

- **1955–1978:** Median = free OTA. Cable existed but was rare (<15% penetration).
- **1980–1985:** Transition period. Cable growing from 23% to 46%. Median is ambiguous — somewhere between $0 and the cable price. Reported as a range.
- **1990–2015:** Median = cable subscriber. Cable peaked at 68% penetration in the early 2000s and remained the majority model through ~2015.
- **2020–2025:** Median = internet + streaming. Cord-cutting crossed the 50% threshold around 2023. The median household now pays for internet + multiple streaming services rather than cable.

### Data source approach

- **1980–2010 cable prices:** FCC Cable Industry Prices Reports (mandated by the 1992 Cable Act), which surveyed actual cable system rates annually. These are the gold standard for cable pricing data. The FCC reported "expanded basic" rates — the tier most subscribers actually purchased.
- **2015–2025 cable prices:** FCC reports supplemented by industry surveys (CordCutting.com, J.D. Power, Parks Associates) as the FCC's cable-specific reporting became less frequent.
- **Streaming costs (2007–2025):** Service-by-service pricing history (well-documented) combined with household survey data on number of subscriptions and total spending (Deloitte, Parks Associates, FinanceBuzz, Reviews.org).
- **Pre-1989 cable prices:** Limited FCC data. The $7.69 figure for 1980 is from historical references citing FCC/NCTA data. The 1985 figure is interpolated.

### The "free TV still exists" caveat

It's important to note that **free over-the-air television still exists in 2025** and actually delivers more channels (30–100 in metro areas) than in 1955 (3–7). A viewer who buys a $20 antenna can watch free HD broadcast television with zero ongoing cost. The content access cost documented here reflects what the median household *chooses* to pay, not the minimum required. The minimum has always been $0.

## Known gaps

- **1975–1988 cable pricing:** Pre-FCC-mandate data is sparse. The $7.69 (1980) figure comes from secondary historical references. The $11.00 (1985) figure is interpolated and could be off by ±$2.
- **1995–2005 internet costs as TV input:** Internet wasn't needed for TV in this era, but its growing role in entertainment (pre-YouTube, pre-Netflix streaming) is hard to disentangle.
- **2015–2020 total household spending:** The split between cable households and cord-cutting households makes a single "median" number somewhat artificial. The data shows both paths.
- **Internet cost attribution:** The 2020–2025 content access figures implicitly include internet costs in the streaming model (since streaming is impossible without internet), but don't separately break out what fraction of the internet bill is "for TV." The full internet bill is embedded in the streaming household's monthly total.
