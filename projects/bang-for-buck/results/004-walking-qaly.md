# Result: Walking (30 min/day) — QALY Evidence

## Summary

Walking 150 minutes per week (30 min/day, 5 days/week) has robust evidence for all-cause mortality reduction and quality-of-life improvement. A walking-specific meta-analysis of 280,000 participants found an 11% reduction in all-cause mortality at this dose. Pooled cohort analyses of 650,000+ individuals found 3.4 years of life gained at equivalent activity levels. Direct QALY measurement via SF-6D utility instruments found a 0.112 QALY/year difference between those meeting activity guidelines and inactive individuals. The evidence is overwhelmingly observational (no RCTs can ethically randomize people to decades of inactivity), but the consistency, dose-response gradient, biological plausibility, and sheer sample sizes make this among the strongest evidence bases for any lifestyle intervention.

## QALY Evidence

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.08
- **Range:** 0.04 – 0.15 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** Synthesis of three evidence streams (see below)
- **Study type:** Meta-analyses and large pooled cohort analyses (observational)
- **Population studied:** General adult populations, predominantly middle-aged and older adults
- **Population match to Bob:** Fair — evidence is from general populations, not specifically low-income or high-BMI individuals. However, subgroup analyses suggest benefits are at least as large (and possibly larger) for sedentary and obese individuals at baseline.
- **Key caveats:**
  - No RCTs for walking on all-cause mortality — all evidence is observational
  - Residual confounding possible (healthier people self-select into walking)
  - Walking studies rarely isolate walking from other physical activity
  - Bob's worse baseline health could mean larger marginal gains, but this is extrapolated, not directly measured

### Evidence Stream 1: Walking-Specific Mortality Meta-Analysis

**Kelly et al. (2014)** — Systematic review of walking/cycling and all-cause mortality.
- 14 studies, 18 results, **280,000 participants**, 2.6 million person-years
- At 11.25 MET-hours/week (≈150 min/week moderate walking): **11% reduction in all-cause mortality** (95% CI: 4–17%)
- Relative risk: 0.89 (95% CI: 0.83–0.96)
- Dose-response was curvilinear — steepest benefit at lowest activity levels (13.75 × 10⁻³ risk reduction per MET-h/wk in the 1–120 min/wk range vs 2.07 × 10⁻³ above 120 min/wk)
- Annualized QALY from mortality component: ~0.04–0.05 QALY/year (converting 1.5–2 life years gained over remaining life expectancy)

### Evidence Stream 2: Pooled Cohort Life Expectancy Analysis

**Moore et al. (2012)** — Leisure-time physical activity and mortality, pooled analysis of 6 cohorts.
- **661,137 individuals**, 116,686 deaths, median 14.2-year follow-up
- At 7.5–14.9 MET-h/wk (≈150–299 min brisk walking): HR = **0.68** (95% CI: 0.66–0.69), **3.4 years of life gained** (95% CI: 3.2–3.6)
- By BMI: Normal weight +4.7 years, overweight +3.9, obese class I +3.4, obese class II+ +2.7 years
- Being active + obese (BMI 35+) vs inactive + obese: +2.7 years — still substantial
- Being active + normal weight vs inactive + obese: +7.2 years (95% CI: 6.5–7.9)
- Note: includes all moderate-vigorous activity, not walking-specific. Walking at 150 min/wk falls at ~8–12 MET-h/wk (depending on pace), placing it squarely in this benefit range.

### Evidence Stream 3: Direct QALY Measurement

**Dunlop et al. (2014)** — Meeting physical activity guidelines and QALYs, Osteoarthritis Initiative.
- 1,794 participants, accelerometer-measured activity, SF-6D utility scores at baseline and 2 years
- Meeting guidelines (≥150 min/wk MVPA) vs inactive: **0.112 QALY/year** (95% CI: 0.067–0.157) after full adjustment for SES and health factors
- Insufficiently active vs inactive: **0.058 QALY/year** (95% CI: 0.028–0.088)
- Both exceeded the minimally important difference for SF-6D (0.033), representing 10–20 additional days of perfect health per year
- Limitation: observational cross-sectional association, not causal intervention effect. Residual confounding likely inflates the estimate.

### Synthesis and Best Estimate Rationale

The best estimate of **0.08 QALY/year** reflects:
- Mortality component: ~0.05 QALY/year, derived from walking-specific 11% mortality reduction (Kelly et al.) and 3.4-year life expectancy gain (Moore et al.)
- Quality-of-life component: ~0.03 QALY/year for improvements in mobility, mood, pain, and energy while alive
- The direct measurement of 0.112 QALY/year (Dunlop et al.) supports the upper range but includes likely residual confounding
- Conservative adjustment applied: best estimate sits between the mortality-only floor (~0.04) and the direct measurement ceiling (~0.12)
- For Bob specifically (sedentary, BMI ~30+, mild depression), marginal benefit may exceed general-population estimates due to worse baseline, but this is inferred rather than directly measured

### Effect Modification by Baseline Health

Multiple studies confirm that the dose-response curve is steepest at the lowest activity levels — the least active people gain the most from initial increases in activity. Specifically:
- Health gains from a given physical activity increase are greatest among the most sedentary (PMC3808763)
- Obese individuals still gain 2.7 years of life expectancy from becoming active (Moore et al.)
- Exercise referral schemes show better cost-effectiveness in sedentary obese (£14,618/QALY) and depressed (£8,414/QALY) vs healthy sedentary (£20,876/QALY) populations, implying larger QALY gains in higher-risk groups

### Depression-Specific Benefits (Secondary)

- 150 min/wk brisk walking associated with 25% lower risk of depression (Mammen & Faulkner, 2013)
- At half that dose (75 min/wk), risk is 18% lower
- Walking-specific meta-analysis for depression found significant symptom improvement (Robertson et al., 2012)
- These benefits are partially captured in the all-cause QALY estimate; they are listed here for comparison with SSRIs/CBT in later tickets but should NOT be added to the QALY total

## Side Effects and Risks

- Musculoskeletal injury: very rare. Walking has the lowest injury rate of common exercise modalities. No injuries were reported in workplace walking intervention studies.
- Falls: low risk for adults under 65; moderate risk for older adults on uneven terrain
- Pedestrian-vehicle collisions: real but rare; mitigated by sidewalks, daylight walking, reflective clothing
- Overuse injuries (shin splints): uncommon, mostly in beginners who escalate too quickly
- Net risk assessment: negligible. Walking is the safest form of exercise studied.

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate:** 0.50
- **Source:** Composite estimate from multiple studies. Supervised walking programs: 62–74% at 12 months (SWEAT 2 trial). General exercise adherence: ~50% dropout in first 3–6 months (Dishman 1988, updated meta-analyses). Unsupervised self-initiated walking: likely 40–50% sustained at 12 months.
- **Notes:** Walking has higher adherence than most exercise forms because it requires no equipment, skill, or travel. Adherence improves with social support (walking partner/group), pedometer use, and flexible scheduling. Adherence drops with weather extremes, injury, and lack of safe walking routes. For Bob, shift work and safety concerns may reduce adherence below general-population rates.

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 1
- **Tier 1** = Multiple RCTs or high-quality meta-analyses, Bob-adjacent populations
- **Key studies:**
  - Kelly P, Kahlmeier S, et al. (2014). Systematic review and meta-analysis of reduction in all-cause mortality from walking and cycling. *Int J Behav Nutr Phys Act*, 11:132. [PMC4262114]
  - Moore SC, Patel AV, et al. (2012). Leisure time physical activity of moderate to vigorous intensity and mortality. *PLoS Med*, 9(11):e1001335. [PMC3491006]
  - Dunlop DD, Song J, et al. (2014). Relationship of meeting physical activity guidelines with quality-adjusted life years. *Arch Phys Med Rehabil*. [PMC4258164]
  - Arem H, Moore SC, et al. (2015). Leisure time physical activity and mortality. *JAMA Intern Med*, 175(6):959-967.
- **Limitations:**
  - All evidence is observational — no RCTs for walking on all-cause mortality exist
  - Cannot fully exclude healthy-user bias even after adjustment
  - Walking-specific data (Kelly et al.) shows smaller effect (11% mortality reduction) than general physical activity studies (32%), possibly due to different populations or the inability to isolate walking from other activities
  - QALY measurement (Dunlop et al.) from a single cohort with OA risk, using SF-6D which may underestimate mobility benefits
  - Most studies are from general populations, not specifically low-income or high-BMI — population match is "Fair" not "Good"
