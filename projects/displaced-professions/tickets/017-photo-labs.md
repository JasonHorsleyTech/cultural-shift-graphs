# Ticket 017: Photo Lab / Film Development Workers

## Event
The collapse of the photofinishing industry as digital photography eliminated the need for film processing, ~2000–2015. Related to but distinct from Kodak (ticket 002) — this is the retail/processing side, not the manufacturing side. Thousands of Fotomat kiosks, one-hour photo labs, and drugstore photo counters disappeared.

## Mechanism
**Tipping point** (tech_platform)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track US photofinishing employment from ~1995 to ~2020:

- NAICS 812921 (Photofinishing Laboratories) or SIC 7384
- Also track retail photo counter workers (drugstores, supermarkets)
- Peak: estimate total employment in film development/printing/processing
- Key dates: first consumer digital cameras (late 1990s), digital camera sales surpass film (2003), smartphone cameras (iPhone 2007), Instagram (2010)
- Fotomat peak: ~4,000 kiosks (1980s) — already declining before digital
- One-hour photo labs (Ritz Camera, Wolf Camera): track chain closures

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS QCEW data, NAICS 812921 (Photofinishing Laboratories)
2. BLS OES data, SOC 51-9151 (Photographic Process Workers and Processing Machine Operators)
3. Photo Marketing Association International (PMAI) industry reports
4. Census Bureau County Business Patterns for NAICS 812921

### Income Data
- BLS wage data for SOC 51-9151
- These were generally low-to-moderate wage jobs
- Track from 1998–2020

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS OES median annual wage for SOC 51-9151
2. CPI-U for inflation adjustment

### Where They Went
- General retail (drugstore staff shifted roles within the store)
- Other customer service
- Some into digital photography services (digital printing, large format)
- Photography as a profession (some lab workers were photography enthusiasts)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS retail employment data
2. CPS data for service-sector transitions

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Labs that pivoted to digital printing / photo books / large-format printing. Costco, Walgreens, and CVS that transitioned from film processing to digital kiosks (a partial save).
- **False movers:** Investment in Advanced Photo System (APS) film in the mid-1990s — marketed as film's answer to digital convenience.
- **Deniers:** "Digital will never match film quality." Professional photographers who stuck with film (some were right for artistic purposes, wrong for economic ones).
- **Survival pattern:** Pivot to digital output (people still want printed photos — they just don't shoot film).

### Unique Texture
- The Fotomat drive-through kiosk — an icon of suburban America, now completely gone
- The transition happened faster than Kodak's decline because retail is more exposed to consumer behavior shifts
- The "photo book" industry emerged from the ashes — self-published photo books replaced traditional albums
- Film photography's hipster revival (Lomography, etc.) — a tiny niche that proves the profession is dead but the medium isn't
- The smartphone as the final nail — not just digital cameras but cameras in everyone's pocket

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Annual BLS employment data for photofinishing from 1998–2020
- Establishment counts (number of labs/shops) over time
- Wage data for photo processing workers
- Digital camera and smartphone adoption timeline as context
- At least one chain-level case study (Ritz Camera, Fotomat, or similar)
