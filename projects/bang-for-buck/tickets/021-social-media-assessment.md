# Ticket 021: Social Media / Screen Time Reduction — Evidence Assessment

## Objective

Determine whether there is sufficient evidence to include "reducing social media or screen time" as a data point on the graph. This is likely the weakest of the borderline interventions.

## What to research

1. **Does any published study report QALYs or cost-effectiveness for screen time reduction?**
   - This is almost certainly going to be "no" — but check anyway
   - Search CEA Registry, PubMed, NICE

2. **What evidence DOES exist?**
   - RCTs on social media reduction (e.g., Hunt et al. 2018 — reducing social media to 30 min/day)
   - Outcomes measured: loneliness, depression, anxiety, well-being (but NOT QALYs)
   - Effect sizes: are they large enough to matter?

3. **Could we convert existing evidence to QALYs?**
   - If depression/anxiety score improvements are reported, could we map these to QALY gains?
   - This would be a stretch — flag the methodology if attempted

4. **Cost:**
   - Technically $0 (or negative — saves data plan costs?)
   - But the "cost" is giving up something people enjoy — utility loss
   - This is conceptually similar to the walking time-cost problem

## Decision criteria

**Include** if: there's at least one study with quantified mental health outcomes that can be reasonably mapped to QALYs, even as Tier 3 evidence.

**Exclude** if: evidence is too thin, too confounded, or only correlational. (Likely outcome.)

## Where to look

1. **Tufts CEA Registry** — "social media" or "screen time" (likely nothing)
2. **PubMed** — "social media reduction RCT" or "screen time mental health"
3. **Recent reviews** — Hancock et al. (2023), Allcott et al. (2020) on social media and well-being

## Output format

If **include:** full intervention result format.
If **exclude:** evidence assessment format with verdict and rationale.
