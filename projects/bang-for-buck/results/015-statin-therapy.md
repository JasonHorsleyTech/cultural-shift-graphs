# Result: Statin Therapy (Generic, Primary Prevention)

## Summary

Generic statin therapy (primarily atorvastatin 20mg) for primary cardiovascular prevention is one of the most cost-effective medical interventions in existence. For Bob — sedentary, elevated BMI, likely borderline lipids — statins sit in the "boring but effective" sweet spot: extremely cheap ($4/month at Walmart), minimal time commitment (swallow a pill), and backed by the most robust evidence base of any pharmaceutical. The CTT Collaboration's meta-analysis of 175,000+ participants across 27 trials shows a ~21% relative reduction in major vascular events per 1 mmol/L LDL reduction. The Cochrane systematic review finds all-cause mortality reduced by 14% (OR 0.86) in primary prevention. Side effects are far more modest than popular perception suggests — a large meta-analysis found no significant difference in muscle symptoms between statin and placebo groups, suggesting the majority of reported myalgia is nocebo effect. The main real side effect concern is a small increase in new-onset diabetes risk (~10% relative increase for moderate-intensity statins), but the cardiovascular benefit outweighs this by roughly 50-fold in absolute terms.

## QALY Evidence

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.02
- **Range:** 0.01 – 0.04 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** Pandya et al. (JAMA 2015), CTT Collaboration meta-analyses (Lancet 2012), Cochrane Systematic Review (2013, updated 2019)
- **Study type:** Multiple meta-analyses + cost-effectiveness microsimulation models
- **Population studied:** Adults aged 40-75 without prior CVD events, varying 10-year ASCVD risk thresholds (3%-30%)
- **Population match to Bob:** Good — Bob's elevated BMI, sedentary lifestyle, and socioeconomic stress markers place him at moderate-to-high cardiovascular risk (likely 10-15% 10-year ASCVD), which is squarely within the studied population. His risk profile means larger absolute benefits than the average primary prevention population.
- **Key caveats:**
  - Published CEA models report lifetime discounted QALYs (e.g., Pandya et al.: 0.051 lifetime QALYs at ≥7.5% ASCVD threshold). Converting to annual estimates requires assumptions about treatment duration and discount rates.
  - The Cochrane review reports that of 1,000 people treated for 5 years, 18 would avoid a major CVD event, and the NNT for all-cause mortality is 96 over 5 years.
  - Bob's higher baseline cardiovascular risk means absolute benefits are likely 50-100% larger than the average trial participant. The per-year estimate of 0.02 reflects this upward adjustment.
  - The 0.02 midpoint estimate already nets out the small QALY losses from pill-taking disutility (~0.002/year per the Scottish CEA model) and new-onset diabetes risk.

### QALY derivation detail

From CTT/Cochrane data for moderate-risk primary prevention:
- Fatal CVD events prevented: ~4.5 per 1,000 per 5 years → ~0.9/1000/year × ~9 QALYs/life-year saved = 0.008 QALYs/person/year
- Non-fatal CVD events prevented: ~13.5 per 1,000 per 5 years → ~2.7/1000/year × ~1.5 QALYs/event = 0.004 QALYs/person/year
- Additional all-cause mortality reduction beyond CVD: ~0.002-0.003 QALYs/person/year
- Subtotal for average trial participant: ~0.013 QALYs/year
- Adjusted upward for Bob's higher baseline risk: ~0.018-0.022 QALYs/year
- Net of side-effect disutility (pill-taking, diabetes risk): ~0.015-0.020 QALYs/year → midpoint 0.02

## Cost Analysis (for Bob at ~$30k household income)

- **Annual direct cost (medication):** $48 (atorvastatin 20mg via Walmart $4/month program)
- **One-time startup cost:** $0 (no equipment; first doctor visit and lipid panel covered under ongoing costs)
- **Startup cost amortized over 3 years:** $0/year
- **Total annual cost (best estimate):** $150
- **Cost range:** $100 – $250 (low – high)
- **With insurance:** $60–$120 (many plans cover generics with $0-$10 copay; preventive visits/labs may be fully covered under ACA)
- **Without insurance:** $150–$400 (depending on provider choice)
- **Cost notes:**
  - **Medication:** Generic atorvastatin 20mg at Walmart is $4/month ($48/year) or $10/90 days ($40/year). GoodRx coupons bring it to $2.64-$6 for 30 tablets at other pharmacies. Full retail without discounts is ~$97/month, but nobody needs to pay that.
  - **Provider visits:** At an FQHC with sliding scale fees (Bob at ~$30k income is around 200% FPL), primary care visits cost $20-50 per visit. Bob needs 1-2 visits/year (initial prescription + annual follow-up). Estimated $35-80/year.
  - **Lab work:** Lipid panel at a direct-to-consumer lab (Quest/Labcorp online ordering) costs $25-50. At FQHC, often included or discounted. One baseline + annual monitoring. Estimated $25-50/year.
  - **Primary cost estimate uses FQHC pricing:** $48 (medication) + $50 (visits) + $50 (labs) = $148, rounded to $150.

## Access and Barriers

- **Activation energy:** Medium
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - Requires a doctor visit and blood work to initiate — not something you can just start doing like walking
  - Need to find and access an FQHC or affordable provider if uninsured
  - Must remember to take a daily pill indefinitely (habit formation)
  - Follow-up visits for monitoring (though infrequent)
  - Cultural resistance to "being on medication" — many people resist the idea of taking a pill every day for life, especially for prevention of something they haven't experienced

## Side Effects and Risks

- **Muscle symptoms (myalgia):** Reported in 5-25% of users in observational studies, but RCT evidence shows the true pharmacological rate is only 1-2%. A Lancet meta-analysis of 29 double-blind RCTs (83,880 participants) found no significant difference in muscle symptoms between statin and placebo groups (RR 1.03, 95% CI 0.97-1.09). The nocebo/drucebo effect accounts for 38-78% of reported symptoms.
- **New-onset diabetes:** 10% relative increase with moderate-intensity statins (absolute: ~0.1%/year excess risk). Higher with high-intensity statins (36% relative increase). Most new diagnoses occur in people already near the diabetes diagnostic threshold. The CTT data shows cardiovascular benefit outweighs diabetes risk by ~50:1 in absolute terms.
- **Liver enzyme elevation:** RR 1.16 (not significant, 95% CI 0.87-1.54). Clinically meaningful liver injury is extremely rare.
- **Rhabdomyolysis:** Extremely rare — 3 cases per 19,410 participants in Cochrane review. Risk similar to placebo (RR 1.00).
- **No cancer risk:** RR 1.01 (95% CI 0.93-1.10) across 11 trials.

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate:** 48%
- **Source:** Cochrane review (77% in-trial compliance) combined with PLoS ONE observational studies showing only 47.9% adherent at 1 year for primary prevention, and USPSTF noting only 37% adherence with guideline-recommended statin therapy.
- **Notes:** In-trial compliance is ~77% (vs 70% for placebo), but real-world primary prevention adherence is dramatically lower at ~40-50%. Key reasons for discontinuation: (1) muscle symptoms (often nocebo-driven), (2) perception of not needing medication when feeling healthy, (3) cost concerns, (4) negative media coverage about statins, (5) primary prevention patients are 36% less likely to be adherent than secondary prevention patients (those who've already had a heart attack). Statin adherence is notably lower than other chronic medications.

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 1
- **Tier 1** = Multiple RCTs or high-quality meta-analyses, Bob-adjacent populations
- **Key studies:**
  - CTT Collaboration (2012): Individual participant data meta-analysis of 27 RCTs, 175,000+ participants. Per 1 mmol/L LDL reduction: 21% RR reduction in major vascular events, 10% RR reduction in all-cause mortality. Lancet.
  - Cochrane Systematic Review (Taylor et al., 2013): 18 RCTs, 56,934 participants. All-cause mortality OR 0.86 (95% CI 0.79-0.94). Combined CVD events RR 0.75 (95% CI 0.70-0.81).
  - Pandya et al. (JAMA 2015): Microsimulation CEA model. At ≥7.5% ASCVD threshold, ICER $37,000/QALY with branded statins, substantially lower with generics.
  - McConnachie et al. (Circulation 2022): Scottish CEA model. ASSIGN 20 strategy ICER £3,850/QALY. Extremely cost-effective.
- **Limitations:**
  - Most trial populations are predominantly white European and somewhat older (mean age 57). Bob's demographics may differ.
  - QALY estimates from CEA models involve modeling assumptions about treatment duration, discount rates, and quality-of-life weights.
  - Lower-income populations may have different risk profiles and comorbidities that modify the absolute benefit magnitude (likely in favor of larger benefit, but uncertain).
  - Interaction effects with other interventions (e.g., exercise, diet) are not modeled.
