# Sexual Liberation Through the Ages

Origin: Jason grew up in Baptist/Lutheran churches in 90s Florida being told his generation was the most sexually depraved ever. His instinct: every generation gets told that, and we might actually be historically *weird* — not for having porn (Romans had it on their walls) but for having maximum sexual access with maximum sexual anxiety.

## The core paradox

We have more access to sexual content than any civilization in history, yet we may also be more anxious, awkward, and rule-bound about actual sex than most of them. High access + high anxiety might make us an outlier.

## Potential graphs (multiple may be worth making)

### 1. Sexual access vs. sexual anxiety scatter plot
Plot 15-20 civilizations on two axes. X = sexual access/visibility in daily life. Y = sexual anxiety/repression. Hypothesis: most cultures cluster along a diagonal (more access = more comfort). Modern West is an outlier in the high-access, high-anxiety quadrant.

### 2. Sexual vocabulary ratio across languages over time
Track the ratio of technical/instructional sex terms (treating sex as skill: "cunnilingus") vs. derogatory/anxiety terms (weaponizing sex: slurs, insults). Plot as lines per language (English, French, Chinese, etc.) over time. Linguistic data exists — frequency of terms in published works, evolution of vocabulary. A culture with rich instructional vocabulary was probably more sexually literate. A culture where most sex words are insults was probably more repressed.

### 3. "Every generation thinks the kids are the worst"
Timeline of documented moral panics about sexual depravity. Sermons, editorials, obscenity trials, book bannings, congressional hearings, censorship actions. Y-axis = intensity of institutional response (laws passed, prosecutions). Would show the same panic recycled every 20-30 years, each claiming to be unprecedented. Directly provable through primary sources.

### 4. Sexual literacy composite index
Score civilizations on a rubric of ~50 specific, evidence-answerable questions. Focus on the AVERAGE person (peasant farmer, not elite). Metrics that survived workshopping:
- Contraception knowledge available to commoners
- Cultural acknowledgment of female pleasure (did their medical texts mention it? did they have a word for female orgasm?)
- Sex education infrastructure (formal instruction, manuals for commoners, oral tradition)
- Sexual vocabulary richness and type
- Consequence severity for sexual exploration (death vs. shame vs. nothing)

Metrics that were REJECTED as biased toward modern Western assumptions:
- Privacy conditions (Polynesians didn't need privacy and were fine)
- Partner selection freedom (arranged marriages sometimes have higher satisfaction)
- Birth intervals (only tracks PIV; ignores oral, manual, etc.)
- Female autonomy broadly (too blunt — a woman with no property rights in a sexually-literate culture might fare better than a legally-equal woman in a sexually-illiterate one)

### 5. The average person's sexual fulfillment (the God Database graph)
If you had perfect data: how many times did every human get "good sex"? Rate for females and males charted over time. Obviously impossible directly, but the composite index above is the closest proxy.

## Key insight from workshopping

The question for any historical culture isn't "were they sexually open?" (too vague, too biased by elite records). It's: **did the average person know how to have good sex, feel comfortable enough to learn and discuss it, and have the cultural infrastructure to support that?**

Most history is about elites. Foot binding was an elite practice — the peasant farmer wasn't breaking his daughters' feet when he needed them collecting eggs. Roman orgies were for the rich — the farmer outside Pompeii was tired from farming. Any research on this MUST focus on common people, not aristocrats.

## The presentation problem

Raw scores feel made-up. Solution: define a transparent rubric of 50 specific questions that a historian could answer with evidence (not "was the average wife happy?" but "did legal codes address wife-beating? to permit or restrict it?"). Apply the same rubric to every culture. Viewer can disagree with any answer but can see the methodology.

## Napkin sketch of 10 cultures (from conversation)

Rough vibes, not researched — just what's baked into LLM weights:

- **Ancient Rome:** High access for men, brutal status rules. Sex on the walls but rigid hierarchy about who does what to whom.
- **Heian Japan:** Aristocracy was staggeringly sexually literate. Wrote poetry about it. Peasants less documented.
- **Victorian England:** Our closest ancestor in the repression-plus-access paradox. Industrial-scale hypocrisy.
- **Ancient Greece:** Group sex on dinnerware. Women locked in the house. "Open" meant open for men.
- **Pre-contact Polynesia:** Sex taught like cooking. Formal instruction for adolescents. Missionaries were horrified.
- **Qing Dynasty China:** Publicly prudish, privately elaborate. Pillow books given to brides. At least they had an instruction manual.
- **Aztec Empire:** Death penalty for infidelity. But within marriage, sex was celebrated. Hard lines, enforced lethally.
- **Ottoman Empire:** Official religious prohibition, open elite practice, celebrated in poetry. Hypocrisy that makes ours look amateur.
- **1970s America:** More sexually open than us. Less porn, more actual sex. Key parties in suburbia.
- **Medieval Catholic Europe:** 80 permitted sex days per year if you followed all rules. Nobody did. Confession records prove everything was happening.

## Side threads worth noting

- Spousal homicide rates as a proxy for marital misery: might correlate more with famine/stress than with legal restrictions on divorce. Interesting but probably not graphable with good data.
- The question of whether women in "oppressive" cultures were as miserable as modern projection assumes. Answer: the median marriage was probably functional, but the floor was much lower without exit options. The distribution was wider, not uniformly shifted.
- Non-PIV sex as contraception: if oral/manual sex was common in a culture, birth interval data tells you nothing about sexual frequency. Need vocabulary and instructional evidence instead.
