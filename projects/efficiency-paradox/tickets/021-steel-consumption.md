# Steel: US Apparent Steel Consumption Per Capita 1990–2022

## What to Research

Total US steel consumption (apparent consumption = production + imports - exports), annually from 1990–2022. This is the **consumption metric** for the steel domain.

## Where to Look

1. **Primary:** USGS Minerals Yearbook (Iron and Steel chapter) and Mineral Commodity Summaries — report apparent consumption annually.
2. **Secondary:** World Steel Association (worldsteel) statistical yearbook — has country-level production, imports, exports, and apparent consumption.
3. **Tertiary:** AISI annual statistical reports — US production figures. Combine with Census Bureau trade data (imports/exports of steel mill products) for apparent consumption.
4. **Fallback:** US Census Bureau Foreign Trade data (steel imports and exports by HS code) + USGS production data.

## Scope

- **Apparent consumption** = US steel production + steel imports − steel exports. This captures total steel used in the US regardless of where it was produced.
- Include all steel mill products (crude steel equivalent is ideal; finished steel products is acceptable if noted).
- Do NOT use production alone — US imports significant steel (especially post-2000 with China's rise).

## Output Format

**Coverage:** All steel mill products, crude steel equivalent (or finished products — specify)

## Data Gaps

- All three series should be complete annually. Steel is well-tracked.
- 2009 will show a massive dip (financial crisis — US apparent consumption fell ~40%). This is real data.
- 2018-2022 may show effects of Section 232 tariffs (25% steel tariff). This reduced imports but may have shifted to domestic production rather than reducing consumption.

## Methodology Notes

- "Apparent consumption" is a standard metric in commodity analysis. It assumes all imported steel is consumed domestically and all exported steel is consumed abroad. In reality, some imported steel is re-exported, but this is minor.
- US steel consumption peaked in the late 1990s-early 2000s, declined, and has been roughly flat at lower levels since ~2010.
- Per-capita consumption is the relevant metric for our graph (divide by US population from ticket 001).
- Steel intensity of the economy (steel per GDP) has been falling for decades — the economy uses less steel per dollar of output (services replace manufacturing). This is structural de-industrialization, not efficiency improvement.

## What "Good Data" Looks Like

- 1990: ~95-100 million short tons apparent consumption
- 2000: ~120-130 million short tons (peak)
- 2006: ~115-125 million short tons
- 2009: ~60-70 million short tons (recession crash)
- 2019: ~95-100 million short tons
- 2020: ~80-85 million short tons (COVID)
- Per capita: roughly stable at ~350-400 lbs/person (2019) vs. ~380-400 lbs/person (1990). The big story here is that per-capita steel consumption is roughly flat — efficiency gains in production haven't led to more consumption because steel demand is driven by construction and manufacturing, which have their own dynamics.
