# Ticket 042: Pre-Columbian US Population — Baseline for Land Carrying Capacity

## What to research

Pre-Columbian Native American population estimates provide a lower bound for what the land can support with sophisticated but non-industrial agriculture. How many people lived in what is now the US before European contact?

## Specific data needed

1. **Population estimates:**
   - The range is genuinely enormous: 2-18M for the area of the modern US
   - Mooney (1928): ~1.15M (now considered far too low)
   - Dobyns (1966): ~12-18M (now considered possibly too high)
   - Ubelaker (1988): ~5-7M (moderate estimate, widely cited)
   - Thornton (1987): ~7M
   - Mann, Charles — "1491": summarizes the debate, leans toward higher estimates
   - Current scholarly consensus: probably 5-15M, with recent work trending higher

2. **Agricultural practices:**
   - Not just "hunter-gatherers" — many nations practiced intensive agriculture
   - Eastern Woodlands: corn, beans, squash ("Three Sisters") supporting large populations
   - Southwest: irrigation agriculture (Hohokam, ancestral Puebloans)
   - Pacific Northwest: salmon-based food system supporting high density
   - Great Plains: primarily hunting/gathering, lower density

3. **Regional population density:**
   - Which regions supported the most people?
   - How does this map to modern US population distribution?
   - Are the high-density pre-Columbian regions the same ones that would support post-collapse populations?

4. **Why this is a LOWER bound, not a ceiling:**
   - Pre-Columbian populations didn't use draft animals (no horses until European introduction)
   - Didn't have steel tools, crop rotation knowledge from Europe/Asia, or access to Old World crops (wheat, rice)
   - A post-collapse population with books, some retained knowledge, and Eurasian crop varieties could exceed pre-Columbian density
   - But also: post-collapse population lacks the centuries of accumulated ecological knowledge Native Americans had

5. **Post-contact population collapse as analogue:**
   - European diseases killed 80-95% of Native American population within 150 years
   - This IS a population collapse with a survival curve
   - Timeline and pattern of decline (though disease-driven, not infrastructure-collapse-driven)
   - Recovery? (Population of Native Americans didn't return to pre-contact levels until possibly the 21st century)

## Where to look

1. **Mann, Charles C. — "1491: New Revelations of the Americas Before Columbus"** — accessible summary
2. **Ubelaker, Douglas (1988)** — North American population estimates (Smithsonian)
3. **Dobyns, Henry (1966)** — high-end estimates
4. **Thornton, Russell (1987) — "American Indian Holocaust and Survival"**
5. **Denevan, William (1992) — "The Native Population of the Americas in 1492"**
6. **Milner, George — pre-Columbian population estimation methods**

## Result format

Key Data Points:
- `pre_columbian_population_low` / `high` / `central`
- `regional_density_per_sq_mile` for major regions (Northeast, Southeast, Plains, Southwest, Pacific NW)
- `agricultural_vs_huntergatherer_population_split`
- `post_contact_decline_pct`
- `post_contact_recovery_time_years`

## What good data looks like

- Presents the full scholarly range (2-18M) with the current consensus (probably 5-15M)
- Notes WHY the range is so wide (methodological differences, not ignorance)
- Connects to carrying capacity: pre-Columbian population IS a data point for "what the land supports without modern inputs" but it's a lower bound (no Old World crops/animals/tools)
- The post-contact decline (80-95%) is itself a data point for "what happens when a population faces a civilizational disruption" — though driven by disease, not infrastructure collapse
