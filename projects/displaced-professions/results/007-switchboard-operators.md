# Switchboard / Telephone Operators

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Telephone operator / switchboard operator
- **Country/Region:** United States
- **Peak Year:** ~1970 (telephone operators in telecom); ~1950 for total including private switchboard operators
- **Peak Workforce:** ~420,000 telephone operators (BLS); ~1,350,000 total including private switchboard operators in 1950 (Conversable Economist / Richmond Fed)
- **Primary Mechanism:** gradual_erosion
- **Cause Tags:** tech_tool, tech_platform
- **Killing Event:** Automated telephone switching systems (dial phones for local calls from the 1920s, direct distance dialing for long-distance from the 1950s–70s, digital electronic switching from the 1960s) progressively eliminated the need for human operators over a 60+ year span.

## Workforce Timeline

Two overlapping categories complicate this timeline: (1) telephone operators employed by phone companies (Bell System/AT&T and independents), and (2) private switchboard operators employed at offices, hotels, hospitals, and factories. Pre-1950 census data primarily captures group 1. The 1950 estimate from the Richmond Fed captures both. BLS post-1970 data tracks them separately under SOC 43-2021 (Telephone Operators) and SOC 43-2011 (Switchboard Operators, Including Answering Service).

**Note on the growth paradox:** Telephone operator employment *grew* from 88,000 in 1910 to 420,000 in 1970 — five decades after automated dial switching was introduced — because telephone adoption outpaced automation. Each new subscriber added call volume faster than dial technology could absorb it. Long-distance calls still required operators until direct distance dialing became universal in the late 1960s.

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** ~1970 (for telecom telephone operators); private switchboard operators began declining from ~1950
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~5 (telephone operators, ~1975)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** ~8 (telephone operators, ~1978)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** ~11 (telephone operators, ~1981)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** ~14 (telephone operators, by 1984 at ~40,000)
- **Decline Shape:** cliff (once decline started in the 1970s, the collapse was rapid — but this followed 50 years of growth *despite* the automating technology existing; the overall 1920s–2020s arc is more of an s_curve)
- **Current Status:** remnant (<10% peak) — ~1,460 telephone operators remain (BLS 2023); ~18,800 switchboard operators in ancillary roles (hospitals, answering services)

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: estimated

Consistent wage data spanning the full 1920–2020 period does not exist for this occupation. Pre-1950 data comes from employer records and labor history; post-2000 data from BLS OES. The middle decades (1950–1990) are reconstructed from fragmentary sources and general clerical wage trends.

- **Income at Peak:** ~$6,500/year in 1970 (~$52,000 in 2024 dollars) — estimated from Bell System clerical wages (BLS Industry Wage Survey, Communications)
- **Income at Trough:** ~$35,750/year in 2019 dollars for remaining operators (BLS OES, SOC 43-2021) — nominal decline but remaining operators held relatively steady real wages
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** N/A — the profession shrank rather than seeing wage collapse; displaced workers moved to comparable-wage clerical roles
- **Inflation Adjustment Method:** CPI-U

**Key observation:** The profession shrank drastically but remaining operators' real wages held roughly steady. The income story here is not a wage collapse — it's mass job elimination with modest-to-no wage penalty for remaining workers and for those who transitioned to comparable roles.

## Where They Went

| Destination | Est. % of Displaced | Basis | Notes |
|-------------|-------------------|-------|-------|
| Secretaries / typists / clerical | ~35% | Inferred (Feigenbaum & Gross 2024) | Largest absorption category; middle-skill office jobs expanded as operators declined |
| Customer service representatives | ~20% | Inferred (BLS successor occupation) | Natural successor role; same phone-based customer interaction skills |
| Other telephone company roles (supervisors, billing, engineering support) | ~15% | Inferred (Bell System internal mobility) | Operators moved "up through the ranks into higher-level positions as chief operators" and into accounting/financial departments |
| Food service and lower-skill service | ~10% | Tracked (Feigenbaum & Gross 2024) | Younger displaced workers absorbed into growing service sector |
| Left the labor force | ~15% | Tracked (Feigenbaum & Gross 2024) | Incumbent operators disproportionately affected — "less likely to be working" a decade later |
| Dispatchers / receptionists | ~5% | Inferred (BLS occupation data) | Similar coordination/communication skills |

**Critical finding from Feigenbaum & Gross (QJE, 2024):** Future cohorts of young women were NOT harmed by the elimination of operator jobs — overall employment rates remained stable as comparable clerical and service jobs absorbed them. But *incumbent* operators (those already working when their city automated) were more likely to be unemployed or in lower-paying work a decade later. The adverse effects were "relatively modest" in magnitude but concentrated on incumbents.

## Surplus Capture

- **Capital owners:** ~40% — AT&T and phone companies captured enormous savings from eliminating labor costs. The Bell System was the largest private employer in the world; automating hundreds of thousands of positions directly boosted profits.
- **Consumers:** ~40% — Long-distance calling costs dropped dramatically. A 3-minute NYC-to-SF call cost $3.70 in 1950 (~$48 in 2024$). By 1987, a long-distance minute cost $0.30. Post-1984 AT&T breakup saw a further ~40% drop in long-distance rates. Today, long-distance is essentially free.
- **Adjacent workers:** ~10% — Technical workers (installers, maintenance, engineers) benefited from network expansion enabled by lower operating costs. Faster network growth = more technical jobs.
- **Government/public:** ~10% — Expanded telecom infrastructure generated tax revenue and enabled economic productivity gains far beyond the telecom sector itself.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
- **Bell System internal mobility programs:** AT&T, as the dominant employer, moved operators into supervisory, clerical, and administrative roles within the company. Operators who became "chief operators" oversaw automated systems rather than working switchboards directly. The company's scale allowed gradual attrition rather than mass layoffs — automation was phased city-by-city over decades, and natural turnover (operators were young women who often left for marriage) absorbed much of the displacement.
- **Julia O'Connor (1890–1972):** Rose from telephone operator to president of IBEW's National Telephone Operators' Department. Led the successful 1919 New England strike, then transitioned into broader labor organizing — using skills developed as an operator (communication, coordination, network knowledge) in a new domain.

### False Movers
- **1919 New England Telephone Strike:** 8,000 operators walked out for five days across five states, winning higher wages and collective bargaining rights. A tactical success — but the victory was against low pay, not automation. Unionization did not slow the pace of dial conversion. The operators won better terms for a shrinking profession.
- **Communications Workers of America (CWA) automation resistance:** The union developed policies against "technological unemployment" but directed most retraining resources toward male technicians, not female operators. Operators — the most displaced group — received the least institutional support.

### Deniers
- **Early Bell System management (1890s–1910s):** Despite Almon Strowger patenting automatic dial switching in 1891, AT&T delayed adoption for 30 years, partly because management believed automated systems could never match human operators' ability to handle complex routing, emergency calls, and personalized service. The first AT&T dial installation didn't happen until 1919 in Norfolk, Virginia — 28 years after the technology existed.
- **"Personal service" proponents:** Many customers and operators believed the human touch was irreplaceable. One veteran operator noted operators "got to know her 50 to 100 assigned customers by name and knew their needs." This was true — but consumers chose cheaper, faster automated service over personal relationships.

### Survival Pattern
Survivors moved into management, supervision, or technical roles overseeing the systems that replaced them — or transferred their communication and coordination skills into adjacent clerical and customer-facing roles.

## Unique Texture

1. **One in thirteen working women in 1950 was a telephone operator.** At ~1.35 million total (phone company + private), switchboard operation was one of the largest female occupations in American history — and almost entirely female (98% women at AT&T). (Conversable Economist / Richmond Fed)

2. **The profession grew for 50 years after its killing technology was invented.** Dial phones were patented in 1891 and introduced to Bell System in the 1920s, yet operator employment grew from 88,000 in 1910 to 420,000 in 1970 because telephone adoption outpaced automation. This is the longest growth-despite-automation paradox in the dataset. (Feigenbaum & Gross, QJE 2024)

3. **America's last hand-cranked switchboard retired in 1983 in Bryant Pond, Maine** — operated by Susan Glines. The full automation arc from Strowger's 1891 patent to the last manual exchange took 92 years. (JSTOR Daily)

4. **Black women entered the profession just as automation closed it.** As researcher Venus Green documented, "as soon as the job opportunity opened for large numbers of African American women, the introduction of high-speed computerized equipment closed it." The narrow window of racial integration in the 1960s–70s coincided precisely with the occupation's collapse. (JSTOR Daily / Green, "Race on the Line")

5. **Operators were forbidden from smiling.** Bell System operators faced extreme surveillance and control: strict dress codes (long black dresses, no jewelry), no whispering to colleagues, and monitoring via listening boards. One veteran recalled: "We are not allowed to speak even in a whisper... much less smile." Despite this, phone companies offered unusually progressive benefits (libraries, athletic clubs, disability plans, company dormitories) to attract middle-class women. (History.com)

## Known Data Gaps

- **1950–1970 combined workforce:** The 1950 figure (~1.35M total) and the 1970 figure (~420K telephone operators only) measure different categories. No single consistent series spans the full arc for the combined profession.
- **Income data 1940–1990:** No consistent wage series exists for telephone operators across this period. BLS Industry Wage Surveys cover communications workers broadly but don't isolate operators. Pre-BLS wages come from employer records and labor histories with uncertain representativeness.
- **Private switchboard operator timeline:** The ~1 million private switchboard operators in 1950 have no reliable annual employment series until BLS OES data begins in the 2000s. Their decline trajectory is inferred, not tracked.
- **2000 and 2010 employment figures:** BLS OES data for these specific years was not directly accessible; figures are estimates based on interpolation between known data points (1984: 40K telecom operators; 2021: 5K telephone operators + 70K switchboard operators).
- **Where-they-went for post-1970 displaced operators:** Feigenbaum & Gross tracked the 1920–1940 cohort via census linking. No comparable individual-level tracking exists for the 1970–1990 decline wave.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium
- **Workforce numbers:** medium — Pre-1950 census data is solid but measures a narrower category; post-1970 BLS data exists but category definitions shifted; the combined timeline requires bridging different measurement frameworks
- **Income data:** low — No consistent wage series exists across the full timeline; pre-1950 data is fragmentary, mid-century data is estimated from general clerical wages, and modern BLS data covers only the tail end of the profession
- **Where-they-went:** medium — Feigenbaum & Gross (2024) provides rigorous individual-level tracking for the 1920–1940 cohort using genealogy-linked census data, but the larger 1970–1990 displacement wave lacks equivalent tracking
