## Data Gaps

- **Median new home sqft:** Data begins in 1973 (no 1970–1972 values). The Census Bureau's Characteristics of New Housing survey started in 1973.
- **Median new home sqft:** Prior to 1999, values are rounded to the nearest 5 square feet per Census methodology.
- **Total households:** There is a methodological discontinuity at 2000–2001 due to CPS sample redesign incorporating 2000 Census results (the jump from 104,705 to 108,209 thousand is partially methodological).
- **Total households:** Another smaller break at 2010–2011 when estimates were revised to incorporate 2010 Census controls.
- **Average household size:** The 1993 revised estimate (2.66) differs slightly from the original (2.63) due to post-hoc population control adjustments. This file uses the original 1993 estimate for series continuity.

## Methodology Notes

- **Population:** World Bank compiles from Census Bureau intercensal estimates (revised after each decennial census) and postcensal estimates. Values are mid-year estimates.
- **Real GDP:** FRED GDPC1 series represents the annual average of quarterly seasonally-adjusted values from BEA NIPA Table 1.1.6. Chained 2017 dollars use Fisher-ideal chain weighting (not a fixed base year), so components don't sum exactly to totals.
- **Households:** From Census Bureau Current Population Survey (CPS) Annual Social and Economic Supplement (ASEC). A household is defined as all persons occupying a housing unit.
- **Average household size:** Computed by Census as household population (total population minus group quarters population) divided by total households. Group quarters includes prisons, dorms, nursing homes, and military barracks (~2.5–3% of total population).
- **Median sqft:** From the Survey of Construction (SOC). Covers single-family homes completed in that calendar year. This is a flow measure (what's being built), not a stock measure (what exists). The existing stock median is lower and changes more slowly.

## Regulatory Context

These are normalizer series — they have no direct regulatory context. They serve as denominators for the EDC calculations across all 11 domains.

## Quality Substitution Notes

- **Housing size growth** is itself a form of quality substitution relevant to the HVAC domain (ticket 012). Homes grew 49% in median size from 1990 (1,905 sqft) to 2015 (2,467 sqft), meaning per-household energy consumption rose even as per-sqft efficiency improved.
- **GDP per capita** grew from $40,282 (1990) to $66,262 (2022) in chained 2017 dollars — a 64% increase. This growth drives demand expansion that can consume efficiency gains (the income effect portion of rebound).
- **Shrinking household size** (2.63 → 2.50 persons from 1990–2022, a 5% decline) means more households per capita, which increases total housing units and associated resource consumption even at flat per-capita rates.
