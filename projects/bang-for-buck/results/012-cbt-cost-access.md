# Result: CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy) — Cost and Access Barriers for Bob

## Summary

A standard 12–16 session course of face-to-face CBT costs Bob between $480 (university training clinic, amortized over 3 years) and $4,000+ (private practice without insurance) for the full course. The realistic mid-case for Bob — a community mental health center or Open Path provider at sliding-scale rates — runs approximately $800–$1,200 for the full course, or ~$330/year amortized over 3 years. Digital/self-guided CBT drops the cost to near-zero but with substantially reduced efficacy. The critical barrier isn't cost — it's access. Over 122 million Americans live in mental health provider shortage areas, waitlists at low-cost clinics run 2–6 months, and Bob's shift work makes scheduling weekly appointments extremely difficult. Combining this cost data with the QALY evidence from ticket 011 (0.06 QALY/year), CBT for a depressed Bob is cost-effective by any standard threshold, but the activation energy to actually start and complete treatment is the highest of any intervention in this study.

## QALY Evidence

*See ticket 011 for full QALY analysis. Summary values used here:*

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.06
- **Range:** 0.03 – 0.10 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** CoBalT trial (Wiles et al. 2013, 2016); Ross et al. (2019); Cuijpers et al. (2023)
- **Study type:** RCT + decision-analytic model + meta-analysis (409 RCTs, 52,702 patients)
- **Population studied:** Primary care adults with depression (UK and US)
- **Population match to Bob:** Fair
- **Key caveats:** Conditional on having depression (~19% of Bobs) and completing treatment (~65% completion rate)

## Cost Analysis (for Bob at ~$30k household income)

### Scenario 1: Private Practice (Expensive Case)

- **Per-session cost:** $150–$250 without insurance (national average ~$175)
- **Full course (16 sessions):** $2,400–$4,000
- **Amortized over 3 years:** $800–$1,333/year
- **Notes:** This is the sticker price that deters most low-income patients from even inquiring. Geographic variation is enormous — $100–$150 in low-cost-of-living areas, $200–$350+ in major metros (NYC, LA, SF).

### Scenario 2: Sliding Scale / Community Provider (Mid Case — Most Realistic for Bob)

- **Community Mental Health Center (CMHC):** $10–$60/session on sliding scale for $30k household income. Typical: $20–$40/session.
- **FQHC behavioral health:** $5–$30/session under federal sliding fee scale (required to serve patients regardless of ability to pay, discounts for income ≤200% FPL; $30k for a single person is ~195% FPL in 2024).
- **Open Path Collective:** $30–$50/session individual therapy + $59–$89 one-time lifetime membership. Available for households under ~$100k. Many therapists offer CBT.
- **Full course (16 sessions) at sliding scale:** $320–$640 (CMHC) or $540–$860 (Open Path including membership)
- **Amortized over 3 years:** $110–$290/year
- **Notes:** This is the realistic scenario for Bob. FQHCs and CMHCs exist specifically for his income bracket. Open Path fills gaps where community centers have long waitlists.

### Scenario 3: University Training Clinic (Best Case — In-Person)

- **Per-session cost:** $5–$30 (supervised graduate student therapists)
- **Full course (16 sessions):** $80–$480
- **Amortized over 3 years:** $27–$160/year
- **Quality:** Solid — students use manualized CBT protocols under close faculty supervision. Current on latest techniques.
- **Limitation:** Only available near universities with clinical psychology programs. Limited scheduling flexibility. May not be available in Bob's area.

### Scenario 4: Telehealth Platforms

- **BetterHelp:** $260–$400/month ($65–$100/week); financial aid can reduce by 10–40%. A 4-month CBT course: $1,040–$1,600 (or $625–$960 with aid).
- **Talkspace:** $276–$436/month; insurance may cover with $15–$30 copay. A 4-month course without insurance: $1,100–$1,744.
- **Amortized over 3 years:** $210–$580/year (with financial aid)
- **Notes:** Telehealth removes transportation and scheduling barriers but introduces subscription-model costs that make a discrete 16-session course harder — platforms incentivize ongoing use, not time-limited CBT. CBT-specific matching is available but not guaranteed. Not all therapists on these platforms are trained in structured CBT.

### Scenario 5: Digital / Self-Guided CBT (Cheapest)

- **CBT apps (Woebot, MoodGYM, Sanvello):** Free to ~$15/month ($0–$180/year)
- **Workbook-based CBT (e.g., "Feeling Good" by Burns):** $15–$25 one-time
- **Computerized CBT (cCBT):** Generally free via health systems or low-cost online
- **Amortized cost:** $0–$60/year
- **Efficacy caveat:** Self-guided CBT has substantially smaller effect sizes than therapist-delivered CBT. Woebot RCT showed symptom reduction vs. control but "does not offer benefit above and beyond other self-help behavioral intervention technologies." MoodGYM cost-effectiveness: ~€3,432/QALY but only 0.01–0.05 incremental QALYs (vs. 0.057 for face-to-face). Dropout rates are much higher for e-therapy.
- **Notes:** Best as augmentation or for sub-clinical symptoms. Not equivalent to face-to-face CBT for moderate-to-severe depression.

### Scenario 6: Group CBT

- **Per-session cost:** $30–$75 (vs. $100–$250 individual)
- **Full course (typically 8–12 sessions):** $240–$900
- **Amortized over 3 years:** $80–$300/year
- **Effectiveness:** Mixed evidence. Group CBT appears cost-effective for depression (ICER as low as $3,715/QALY for group CBT by non-physicians), but less effective than individual CBT for anxiety and substance use. The Ontario Health Technology Assessment found group CBT produced 0.11 QALYs (95% CrI: 0.03–0.22) vs. usual care, nearly identical to individual CBT's 0.12 QALYs.
- **Availability:** Less widely available. CMHCs and some university clinics offer group programs.

## Time Commitment

- **Per session:** 50 minutes therapy + 15–30 min travel (in-person) or 0 travel (telehealth)
- **Between sessions:** 30–60 min/week homework (thought records, behavioral experiments, exposure tasks)
- **Active treatment phase:** 16 weeks (sessions 1–16)
  - In-person: ~2 hrs/week (session + travel + homework) × 16 weeks = 32 hours
  - Telehealth: ~1.5 hrs/week × 16 weeks = 24 hours
- **Maintenance (post-course):** 1–2 booster sessions per year (~2–4 hours/year)
- **Annualized (active phase ÷ 52 weeks):** ~0.5 hrs/week (amortized over the year)
- **During active treatment:** 1.5–2.0 hrs/week
- **Hours per week (for graph):** 1.5 (during active treatment — see ticket 011 note on amortized vs. active)
- **Category:** Medium (1–3 hr/wk)

## Access and Barriers

- **Activation energy:** High
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - **Therapist shortage:** 122+ million Americans live in Mental Health Professional Shortage Areas. Only 26.4% of workforce needs are met in designated shortage areas. Wait times at community mental health centers: 2–6 months for intake. Rural areas: wait times up to 3× longer than urban.
  - **Scheduling conflicts:** Bob likely works hourly/shift jobs. Weekly therapy during business hours (when most therapists work) requires taking time off — potentially losing income. Evening/weekend availability is extremely limited, especially at low-cost clinics.
  - **Transportation:** 12–16 round trips to a therapist's office. If Bob lacks reliable transportation, each session requires planning around buses, rides, or walking. Travel time compounds the scheduling problem.
  - **Stigma and health literacy:** Lower-income populations face greater mental health stigma. Bob may not identify his symptoms as depression, may view therapy as "for other people," or may not know CBT exists as a specific, time-limited treatment. "I need to see a therapist for 16 sessions to learn cognitive restructuring" is high-literacy framing.
  - **Emotional readiness:** CBT requires active cognitive engagement — completing homework, challenging automatic thoughts, tolerating discomfort. This is harder when you're exhausted from work, stressed about money, or struggling with executive function from untreated depression.
  - **Internet/device access for telehealth:** 22% of adults in households earning <$30k lack home broadband (Pew Research). Telehealth options require stable internet and a private space for sessions — not guaranteed in shared or unstable housing.
  - **Continuity risk:** A 16-week treatment course is vulnerable to life disruptions — job loss, car breakdown, illness, childcare emergency. Each missed session reduces treatment fidelity. Unlike a daily pill, you can't "catch up" easily.

### Access Mitigants

- **FQHC mandate:** Federally Qualified Health Centers must serve patients regardless of ability to pay and increasingly offer integrated behavioral health. ~1,400 FQHCs serve 30+ million patients nationwide.
- **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline:** Can provide referrals to local low-cost therapy options.
- **SAMHSA treatment locator:** Free tool for finding sliding-scale behavioral health providers.
- **Telehealth expansion post-COVID:** More therapists offer remote sessions, reducing transportation barrier. BetterHelp and Talkspace provide CBT-trained therapists without geographic limitation.
- **Group CBT:** Shorter courses (8–12 sessions), lower cost, and available at more CMHCs than individual CBT.

## Side Effects and Risks

- **No pharmacological side effects** — zero risk of sexual dysfunction, weight gain, emotional blunting, or withdrawal (significant advantage over SSRIs)
- **Temporary emotional discomfort** during sessions (engaging with painful thoughts) — therapeutically intentional, transient
- **Opportunity cost of time:** 24–32 hours over 16 weeks is real for someone working multiple jobs
- **Risk of poor-quality therapy:** Not all therapists deliver manualized CBT competently. Community mental health centers may have high staff turnover. Training clinic therapists are closely supervised but less experienced.
- **Financial risk if dropout:** If Bob drops out at session 6, he's paid for 6 sessions with likely incomplete benefit. Sliding-scale rates mitigate the dollar loss but the time investment is unrecoverable.

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate (full course completion):** ~65%
- **Source:** Fernandez et al. (2015) meta-analysis; BJPsych Advances review
- **Pretreatment dropout:** 15.9% (never start after initial contact)
- **During-treatment dropout:** 26.2%
- **Depression-specific:** Highest dropout of any diagnosis in CBT
- **Dropout timing:** Front-loaded in sessions 1–4. Patients reaching session 5 are likely to complete.
- **Bob-specific adherence concerns:**
  - Lower SES associated with higher therapy dropout
  - Shift work and scheduling conflicts increase missed sessions → dropout
  - Transportation barriers compound scheduling issues
  - Male gender (if applicable) associated with higher dropout in e-therapy
  - Lower educational attainment predicts higher dropout in web-based formats
- **Notes:** Unlike SSRIs (which require indefinite daily adherence at ~45% long-term), CBT is a one-time course. A completed 16-session course produces lasting benefit. The adherence challenge is completing the course, not maintaining an ongoing regimen. This is CBT's paradox: harder to start, but once done, the benefit is self-sustaining.

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 1
- **Tier rationale:** CBT has the strongest cost-effectiveness evidence base of any psychotherapy. Multiple high-quality systematic reviews, RCTs with direct QALY measurement, and US-specific decision models. Cost data is well-documented across delivery formats.
- **Key studies:**
  - Ross et al. (2019) — CBT vs. SGAs cost-effectiveness model, Annals of Internal Medicine
  - Wiles et al. (2013, 2016) — CoBalT trial with direct QALY measurement
  - Ontario Health Technology Assessment (2019) — Group vs. individual CBT cost-effectiveness, including $3,715/QALY for group CBT
  - JAMA Network Open (2024) — Computer-assisted CBT: ICER $37,295/QALY, 0.021 incremental QALYs over 6 months
  - Systematic review of 38 economic evaluations (2022) — ICER ranges across delivery formats
- **Cost data sources:**
  - Psychology Today therapist directory, therapist rate surveys (2024–2025)
  - Open Path Collective published pricing
  - HRSA FQHC sliding-fee requirements
  - BetterHelp/Talkspace published rates (2025–2026)
  - SAMHSA treatment access data
  - Commonwealth Fund behavioral health workforce analysis (2023)
  - KFF Mental Health Professional Shortage Area data
- **Limitations:**
  - Cost data for Bob's exact scenario (low-income, uninsured, specific geography) is assembled from multiple sources, not from a single definitive study
  - Sliding-scale rates vary enormously by location and clinic — the $20–$40/session estimate is a central tendency, not a guarantee
  - Waitlist data is anecdotal and regional — no national average exists
  - Telehealth platform pricing changes frequently
