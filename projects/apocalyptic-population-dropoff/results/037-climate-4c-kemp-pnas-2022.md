# Result: Climate Cascades — 4°C+ Warming (Kemp et al. PNAS 2022)

## Summary

Kemp et al. (2022) argued in PNAS that catastrophic climate scenarios (≥4°C warming) are dangerously under-studied, proposing a "Climate Endgame" research framework around four drivers: famine, extreme weather, conflict, and vector-borne diseases. Under the most extreme emission pathway (SSP5-8.5), 4°C is reached by ~2080-2100, triggering multiple tipping cascades (Amazon dieback, AMOC collapse, permafrost thaw). This scenario is fundamentally different from all others in this project: it unfolds over decades, not days or months, producing a slow grinding decline rather than a sudden catastrophe. The US is better positioned than most nations due to its temperate latitude, agricultural diversity, and wealth — but is not immune to cascading global instability. Uncertainty is enormous: Kevin Anderson's 2009 claim of "10% global survival" is an unreviewed media statement, while mainstream IPCC modeling shows severe but sub-civilizational impacts for the US specifically at 4°C.

## Scenario-Specific Analysis

### Why this scenario is fundamentally different

Every other scenario in this project involves a discrete triggering event (detonation, eruption, impact, grid failure). Climate cascades have no single start point. Warming is gradual, impacts compound over decades, and the "event" is more like slowly turning up the heat on a pot of water. The curve shape is a long, slow decline with periodic crisis spikes (extreme droughts, crop failures, heat waves), not a cliff edge followed by stabilization.

### The Kemp et al. (2022) "Climate Endgame" framework

Kemp and co-authors at Cambridge's Centre for the Study of Existential Risk argued that the IPCC has disproportionately studied moderate warming (1.5-2°C) while neglecting catastrophic tail risks at ≥3°C. They proposed four interconnected drivers of civilizational risk at extreme warming — the "four horsemen":

1. **Famine** — crop yield losses, fishery collapse, simultaneous breadbasket failures
2. **Extreme weather** — more intense hurricanes, floods, droughts, heat waves
3. **Conflict** — resource competition, mass migration, state fragility
4. **Vector-borne diseases** — expanded range of malaria, dengue, novel pathogens from permafrost thaw or zoonotic spillover

These interact: famine triggers conflict, conflict disrupts disease response, extreme weather worsens famine. The paper did not produce its own mortality model but called for a dedicated "CATACLYSM" research agenda to fill this gap.

### Tipping point cascades at 4°C

At 4°C of global warming, Armstrong McKay et al. (2022) identified at least 6 major tipping elements likely triggered:

- **Amazon rainforest dieback** (threshold ~3.5°C): forest transitions to savannah, losing ~50% of its own rainfall via evapotranspiration loss. Massive carbon release accelerates further warming.
- **AMOC collapse** (threshold ~4°C, range 1.4-8°C): shutdown of Atlantic circulation. Cools Northern Hemisphere by ~3-5°C paradoxically, but devastates European agriculture and disrupts global weather patterns. Could occur as early as 2060s per recent modeling.
- **Boreal permafrost collapse** (threshold ~4°C): releases carbon equivalent to ~2x current atmosphere. Creates self-reinforcing warming feedback.
- **Greenland Ice Sheet** (threshold ~1.5°C): already triggered. Irreversible loss over centuries-millennia. Commits world to multi-meter sea level rise.
- **West Antarctic Ice Sheet** (threshold ~1.5°C): already triggered. Marine ice sheet instability creates unstoppable grounding line retreat.
- **Boreal forest southern dieback** (~4°C): shifts biome boundary northward.

A 2021 simulation study found that ~33% of 3 million model runs produced domino effects (one tipping point triggering another) even at 2°C. Temporary temperature overshoots increase cascade risk by up to 72%.

### US-specific impacts at 4°C

**Agriculture:** US crop yields decline ~15-25% at 4°C, but with significant regional variation. Southern corn belt and Plains states see the worst losses — maize becomes non-viable above 35°C. Northern states (Minnesota, Dakotas, Montana) see expanded growing seasons. Net effect: US remains food-capable but with significantly reduced output and major geographic shifts. Unlike many developing nations, the US has the wealth and technology for partial adaptation (irrigation, new cultivars, shifted planting zones).

**Heat mortality:** Current US heat-related deaths are ~1,300/year (EPA estimate). At 4°C, the southern US (Texas, Arizona, Florida, Gulf Coast) experiences dramatically more lethal heat days per year. Hsiang et al. (2017) estimated that in the US, each 1°C of warming increases mortality by ~5.4 deaths per 100,000 per year in the hottest counties. At 4°C this compounds significantly, especially if grid strain causes rolling blackouts during heat waves (removing air conditioning from a population dependent on it). Annual excess heat deaths could reach 10,000-50,000+ in the US.

**Sea level rise:** By 2100 under SSP5-8.5: 0.65-1.6m. Long-term committed rise at 4°C: 2-6m over centuries. 40% of US population lives near a coast. Hauer (2017) projects ~13 million Americans displaced by sea level rise by 2100. This is displacement, not death — it's slow enough for managed retreat. But it represents enormous economic disruption and loss.

**Water stress:** The US Southwest (already in megadrought) faces severe and potentially permanent water scarcity. Colorado River basin, which supplies 40 million people, faces existential decline. Groundwater depletion accelerates.

**Global instability spillover:** Perhaps the biggest US risk at 4°C isn't direct domestic impacts but global cascading effects. Billions displaced globally (Xu et al. estimate ~1 billion per degree), failed states, broken supply chains, potential conflict over resources. The US is insulated but not immune.

### The Kevin Anderson "10% survival" claim

In November 2009, ahead of the Copenhagen climate summit, climate scientist Kevin Anderson stated that only ~10% of the global population (~500 million) would survive at 4°C warming. This was a media statement to The Scotsman, not derived from any published model or peer-reviewed research. Anderson's peer-reviewed work (e.g., Anderson & Bows 2011, Phil. Trans. R. Soc. A) focused on emission pathways and temperature targets, not survival rate modeling.

This estimate is an extreme outlier. Mainstream climate impact literature (IPCC AR6, Hsiang et al., Lenton et al.) projects severe but sub-civilizational impacts at 4°C for wealthy nations like the US. The 10% figure may reflect a worst-case compound scenario where all tipping cascades fire simultaneously and human adaptation capacity is zero — but no model has produced this result. It should be treated as the extreme pessimistic bound, not a central estimate.

### Mortality curve rationale

The central estimate of ~15% US cumulative excess mortality over 100 years at 4°C+ reflects:
- Gradual accumulation of heat deaths (tens of thousands per year compounding)
- Agricultural decline reducing food security for the most vulnerable populations
- Water stress in arid regions
- Periodic crisis events (extreme droughts, hurricanes, heat waves) that spike mortality
- Global instability creating supply chain disruptions and potential conflict
- Partial offset by US adaptation capacity (wealthy, technologically advanced, temperate northern zones)

The low bound (~5%) represents aggressive adaptation: crop shifts, infrastructure hardening, managed migration. The high bound (~40%) represents cascading system failures: AMOC collapse + multiple breadbasket failure + grid instability + global conflict. This high bound approaches but does not reach Anderson's 90% because the US has significant geographic and economic buffers that most nations lack.

## Data Gaps

- **No mortality model exists for 4°C+ specifically.** Kemp et al.'s entire argument is that this research hasn't been done. All mortality numbers at 4°C are extrapolations, not direct model outputs.
- **Tipping cascade interactions are modeled individually, not as compounds.** We know the thresholds for each tipping point but not how they amplify each other's mortality effects.
- **Adaptation capacity is the largest unknown.** The difference between "managed decline" and "civilizational collapse" depends almost entirely on whether institutions hold together — which is not modeled.
- **Mora et al. (2017) deadly heat threshold data** was not directly accessible for this research pass. Their finding that ~74% of world population faces ≥20 deadly heat days per year by 2100 under BAU is widely cited but specific US projections at 4°C were not located.
- **Hsiang et al. (2017)** county-level US mortality projections at high warming were referenced but the full dataset was not accessible.
- **Second-order effects dominate.** Direct heat/weather mortality in the US at 4°C is significant but not civilization-ending. The real risk is global system disruption (mass migration, conflict, broken supply chains) feeding back into US stability. This is fundamentally unmodellable with current tools.
- **Timeline uncertainty is massive.** Under current policies (~2.7°C by 2100), the US never reaches 4°C. This scenario requires either much worse policies (SSP5-8.5) or tipping cascade feedbacks that push warming beyond what emissions alone produce. The timeline could shift by decades.
