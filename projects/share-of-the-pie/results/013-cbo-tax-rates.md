# Result: CBO Effective Federal Tax Rates by Quintile, 1979–2021

## Source
- **Primary source:** Congressional Budget Office, "The Distribution of Household Income in 2021," Publication 60341, Supplemental Data Table 9: "Average Federal Tax Rates, by Income Group, 1979 to 2021" (September 2024)
- **URL:** https://www.cbo.gov/publication/60706 (report page); supplemental data XLSX from https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2024-09/60341-supplemental-data.xlsx
- **Date accessed:** 2026-04-30

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Total effective federal tax rate = total federal taxes paid / before-tax income × 100. "Before-tax income" is CBO's comprehensive income measure (market income + government transfers, before federal taxes).
- **Taxes included:** Individual income taxes, payroll taxes (employee + employer shares), corporate income taxes (allocated to capital income recipients), and federal excise taxes. This is ALL federal taxes combined.
- **Universe:** All US households, ranked by before-tax income adjusted for household size, then grouped into quintiles containing equal numbers of people (not equal numbers of households).
- **Corporate tax allocation:** CBO allocates corporate income tax to households in proportion to their capital income. This is a modeling choice — it increases effective rates at the top of the distribution. Other reasonable approaches exist.
- **Negative rates:** Effective rates can be negative when refundable tax credits (EITC, child tax credit, stimulus payments) exceed total federal tax liability. This is especially visible in 2008–2010 and 2020–2021 for the lowest quintile.
- **Rebenchmarking:** CBO re-estimates the entire 1979–2021 series in each new report. These numbers supersede earlier CBO publications. Values may differ from older CBO reports covering the same years.
- **Known methodology changes:** CBO revised its income definition in 2018 (added in-kind benefits more comprehensively). The entire series was re-estimated under the new definition, so internal consistency is maintained.

## Gaps and Caveats

- **No missing data in the CBO series.** CBO provides continuous annual estimates from 1979–2021.
- **2020–2021 pandemic anomaly:** The bottom two quintiles show extreme negative or near-zero rates due to pandemic-era fiscal policy: stimulus checks (Economic Impact Payments), expanded Child Tax Credit, expanded EITC, and other refundable credits. The middle quintile dropped from 13.0% to 7.7–7.8%. These years are real data but represent an extraordinary policy environment, not a structural shift. Annotate heavily in the graph.
- **2008–2012 Great Recession era:** Rates fell across all quintiles due to stimulus measures (ARRA 2009), payroll tax holiday (2011–2012), and the Making Work Pay credit (2009–2010). The lowest quintile went negative (-0.7% in 2009).
- **2013 jump:** Rates rose sharply when the payroll tax holiday expired, the American Taxpayer Relief Act restored higher rates on top earners, and ACA taxes (Net Investment Income Tax, Additional Medicare Tax) took effect. Top 1% jumped from 28.5% to 33.6%.
- **2018 TCJA effect:** The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act reduced rates across all quintiles. Most visible in the middle-to-upper quintiles (middle: 13.7% → 12.7%; top 1%: 31.5% → 30.3%).
- **Major tax legislation effects visible in data:**
  - 1981 ERTA (Reagan): Rates fell, especially for top earners (top 1%: 35.1% → 26.8%)
  - 1986 TRA (Reagan): Base-broadening + rate cuts; top 1% rose from 24.6% to 30.0% as loopholes closed
  - 1993 OBRA (Clinton): Top rates increased; top 1% rose from 29.6% to 33.2%
  - 2001/2003 EGTRRA/JGTRRA (Bush): Rates fell across the board; middle quintile: 17.1% → 14.2%
  - 2013 ATRA + ACA: Rates rose, especially at top; top 1%: 28.5% → 33.6%
  - 2017 TCJA (Trump): Rates fell across the board; middle quintile: 13.7% → 12.7%
- **Federal only.** These rates exclude state and local taxes, which are generally regressive (sales tax, property tax). Total tax burden analysis is in ticket 022.
- **Corporate tax allocation matters.** CBO allocates corporate income tax to capital income recipients. Alternative allocation methods (e.g., to labor, or split labor/capital) would change the top quintile rates by several percentage points. The bottom three quintiles are relatively unaffected.
- **Data vintage:** This is the September 2024 CBO release. CBO rebenchmarks the entire series periodically; numbers for any given year may differ from earlier CBO publications.
- **Key field for TypeScript data file:** `effectiveFederalTaxRateMiddle` maps to the Middle Quintile column. All quintiles are collected here for context and cross-checking, but the plan.md type definition only requires the middle quintile rate.
- **Confidence: HIGH** for 1979–2019 (consistent CBO methodology, large samples, well-understood tax code). **MEDIUM** for 2020–2021 (data is accurate but reflects one-time pandemic policy — interpret with caution for trend analysis).
