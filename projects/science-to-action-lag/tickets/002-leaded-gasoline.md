# Ticket 002: Leaded Gasoline → Neurological Damage

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that tetraethyl lead in gasoline causes population-wide lead exposure and neurological damage to binding regulation phasing out leaded gasoline.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — When did science first establish that leaded gasoline contributes to dangerous population-level lead exposure? Key figure: Clair Patterson's 1965 paper "Contaminated and Natural Lead Environments of Man" quantified the scale. Earlier: workers at Ethyl Corporation's plants suffered acute lead poisoning in the 1920s, and the public health community raised alarms.

2. **Consensus date** — When did a major scientific body formally recommend removing lead from gasoline? Look for NAS reports, EPA determinations, WHO positions.

3. **First OECD regulation** — Which country first mandated leaded gasoline phase-out? Japan began restricting in the early 1970s. The US EPA began phase-down in 1973-1975.

4. **US regulation** — EPA's phase-down began 1973 (Clean Air Act authority), accelerated 1975 (catalytic converters required unleaded gas), final ban 1996.

5. **Global completion** — Algeria was the last country to ban leaded gasoline (2021). The global story is useful context.

6. **Industry opposition** — The Ethyl Corporation (GM + Standard Oil joint venture) and the Lead Industries Association fought regulation aggressively. Robert Kehoe's "show me the harm" defense strategy was foundational.

7. **50% exposure reduction** — US blood lead levels dropped dramatically after the gasoline phase-out. CDC NHANES data tracks this precisely.

8. **Substitute availability** — Unleaded gasoline existed but required engine modifications (catalytic converters couldn't tolerate lead, creating an independent economic reason to remove it).

## Where to look

1. EPA historical documents on leaded gasoline regulation
2. Gerald Markowitz & David Rosner, "Deceit and Denial"
3. Jamie Kitman, "The Secret History of Lead" (The Nation, 2000)
4. Clair Patterson's 1965 paper and subsequent advocacy
5. CDC NHANES blood lead level data (1976-present)
6. UNEP Partnership for Clean Fuels and Vehicles (global phase-out timeline)

## What "good data" looks like

- Clear separation from the lead paint story — this is about gasoline specifically
- Evidence dates that focus on POPULATION exposure (Patterson's contamination work), not just occupational poisoning at manufacturing plants
- Global phase-out timeline as context, but primary metric uses first OECD country regulation
- Blood lead level data showing the exposure reduction curve

## Known pitfalls

- The 1920s occupational poisoning at Ethyl Corp plants is evidence of lead's toxicity, but the population exposure argument (ambient lead from car exhaust affects everyone) came decades later. Use the population-level evidence date.
- Catalytic converters (required for emissions control starting 1975) needed unleaded gas — this was an ECONOMIC driver for phase-out independent of health science. Note this in judgment calls.
- Don't conflate US phase-down (gradual, 1973-1996) with a single "ban date." Use the start of mandatory phase-out.
