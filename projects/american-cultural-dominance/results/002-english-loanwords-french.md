# English Loanwords in French

## Summary
English loanwords in French have grown from under 1% of dictionary stock in 1906 to 3.2% by 2025, but the real story is the acceleration in adoption rate: the share of new dictionary entries that are English-origin rose from ~2% in 1800 to 5% by the early 1900s, spiked to 12% during the interwar American cultural wave, and has reached 25% in the 2010s-2020s — despite France being the world's most aggressive legislative defender against anglicisms. The Toubon Law (1994) made English in public life literally illegal, yet dictionary adoption rates actually *increased* afterward. The gap between official resistance and lexicographic reality is the clearest signal of American cultural pressure on the French language.

## Penetration Score Definition
**Denominator:** Percentage of new entries in major French dictionaries (Petit Larousse illustré, Petit Robert) that are English-origin per decade. Measures the rate at which English/American culture drives French vocabulary growth.
**0% anchor:** No new English-origin words entering French dictionaries in a given decade.
**100% anchor:** Every new French dictionary entry in a given decade is English-origin.

## Methodology
The penetration score uses the **percentage of new dictionary entries that are English-origin** in major French dictionaries (Petit Larousse illustré, Petit Robert) as its metric. This measures the active rate at which American/English culture is capturing French lexical space, not just the accumulated stock.

**Why flow rate, not stock?** The cumulative stock of anglicisms (0.7% in 1906 → 3.2% in 2025) understates the felt pressure because the denominator (entire dictionary) dilutes recent borrowings. The flow rate captures the intensity of cultural pressure at each moment — a new-entry rate of 25% means American culture is driving a quarter of French vocabulary growth.

**Data sources by period:**
- **Pre-1906:** Estimated from historical accounts of borrowing waves (industrial, sporting). Low confidence but directionally reliable — lexicographic historians agree English was a minor source before 1850 and grew through the Belle Époque.
- **1906-1948:** HAL-SHS study of the Nénufar digital corpus, analyzing every edition of the Petit Larousse illustré. Direct counts of anglicism entries and new additions per revision (1925 major, 1938 semi, 1948 major).
- **1960-1990:** Interpolated from stock percentages (PLI 1.3% in 1960) and qualitative accounts. The Étiemble debate, legislative history, and computing-era borrowing provide directional anchors.
- **1998-2022:** OpenEditions/Lengas study ("Sommes-nous réellement envahis par les anglicismes?"), analyzing 9,191 new entries across both Petit Larousse and Petit Robert over 24 years. The most rigorous dataset available.

**Consistency note:** The shift from British English borrowings (pre-1920) to American English borrowings (post-1920) means early data points reflect British rather than American cultural influence. However, by the mid-20th century, American English overwhelmingly dominates. The metric consistently measures English-origin loanwords regardless of British/American source, which is appropriate since the graph tracks overall Anglophone cultural penetration with America as the primary driver from ~1920 onward.

**The 1938 anomaly:** The 11.8% spike in the 1938 PLI semi-revision (77 anglicisms out of 651 entries) is partly an artifact of a smaller revision scope — fewer total entries makes the percentage jump. The decade-smoothed estimate of ~8% for the 1930s accounts for this. The 1948 major revision (5.5% of 2,856 entries) has a lower percentage but higher absolute count (157), reflecting that large revisions dilute the anglicism share with many non-English additions.

## Notes
- **The Toubon Law paradox is the most striking finding.** France passed the most aggressive language protection law in the Western world in 1994, created an entire government apparatus (DGLFLF, FranceTerme) to generate French alternatives, and made English in advertising/government/broadcasting illegal without French equivalents. Yet the rate of anglicism adoption in dictionaries *accelerated* from ~14% to 25% of new entries. The law constrained formal/institutional language but had no measurable effect on lexicographic reality. This suggests cultural pressure operates below the level of legislation.
- **Source shift from British to American.** Pre-1920 borrowings are overwhelmingly British (industrial, sporting, political vocabulary). Post-1920 they're increasingly American (jazz, cinema, marketing, computing, internet). By 2000, virtually all new anglicisms are American English in origin.
- **"Franglais" as a cultural marker.** The coining of "franglais" in 1959 and Étiemble's 1964 book are themselves data points — they mark the moment French cultural elites perceived English influence as threatening enough to name and resist. The concept didn't exist before the post-WW2 Americanization wave.
- **Survival rate is remarkably high.** 78% of anglicisms present in the 1906 PLI survive in the 2025 edition unchanged. Once English words enter French, they rarely leave. A few exceptions: *speaker* → *présentateur*, *lift* → *ascenseur*, *starter* → *démarreur*. But these are rare successes of the replacement policy.
- **Dictionary vs. speech.** Corpus studies show anglicisms are only ~1% of words actually used in speech/text (token frequency), even though they represent ~3% of dictionary types. Most borrowed words are specialized or low-frequency. The "25% of new entries" figure captures the bleeding edge of cultural transfer, not everyday conversation.
- **90% of French people** reportedly use some franglais (Médiaprism/Le Parisien study), but actual anglicism density in text remains under 1% — suggesting code-switching and anglicism use are socially salient but quantitatively modest.
