# Result: Major Tax and Transfer Policy Changes, 1947–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** Bipartisan Policy Center — U.S. Tax Reform Timeline, 1945–Present (https://bipartisanpolicy.org/issue-brief/u-s-tax-reform-timeline-1945-present/)
- **Secondary source:** Tax Policy Center — Major Enacted Tax Legislation summaries by decade (https://www.taxpolicycenter.org/laws-proposals/major-enacted-tax-legislation-1940-1949)
- **Tertiary sources:** SSA FICA & SECA Tax Rates (https://www.ssa.gov/oact/progdata/taxRates.html); Treasury OTA Working Paper 81 — Revenue Effects of Major Tax Bills (https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/131/WP-81.pdf); CBO historical budget data; Tax Foundation historical analyses
- **Date accessed:** 2026-05-01

## Methodology
- **Scope:** Federal tax and transfer policy changes that would visibly affect the median household's disposable income or effective tax burden. State/local tax changes excluded.
- **Year effective:** The calendar year the primary provision took effect, not the year the law was signed. Where phase-ins span years, the year of the largest single-year change is listed.
- **Impact direction:** Whether the policy increased or decreased disposable income for middle-quintile households specifically. Top-bracket-only changes are included only if they had structural effects (e.g., bracket compression affecting middle earners indirectly).
- **Magnitude:** Rough impact on middle-income households specifically: "large" (>2% of income), "moderate" (1–2%), "small" (<1%). Based on CBO/TPC distributional analyses where available; otherwise informed estimates flagged as such.
- **Revenue impact:** From BPC/Treasury OTA figures, expressed in nominal dollars of the year of enactment. These are total revenue impacts, not middle-quintile-specific.

**Key insight for the graph:** The total FICA rate quintupled from 3.0% (1950) to 15.3% (1990). For a middle-income worker earning the median, this represents one of the largest sustained increases in effective tax burden over the study period. Unlike income taxes, payroll taxes are flat-rate and regressive (capped at the taxable maximum), so they hit middle-income workers proportionally harder than high earners.

## Gaps and Caveats

- **Magnitude estimates are approximate.** "Large/moderate/small" for middle-income impact draws on CBO and TPC distributional tables where available (post-1979). For pre-1979 legislation, magnitude is estimated from statutory rate changes applied to typical middle-income earners and flagged accordingly. Confidence is **high** for post-1979 entries, **medium** for 1964–1978, and **low** for pre-1964.
- **Transfer programs vs. tax changes.** Transfer programs (EITC, CTC, TANF, ACA subsidies) are harder to size for "middle income" because they phase out below median income. Entries note where the primary beneficiaries are lower-quintile rather than middle.
- **State interactions omitted.** Federal tax changes sometimes trigger state conformity changes (e.g., SALT cap under TCJA). These second-order effects are not captured.
- **COVID-era programs were temporary.** The 2020–2021 entries represent one-time or single-year programs. The expanded CTC reverted to $2,000/child in 2022 when Congress did not extend it.
- **2025 legislation.** The "One Big Beautiful Bill Act" (signed July 2025) extended TCJA provisions and expanded CTC but falls outside the 1947–2024 study period.
- **Revenue figures are nominal.** They are not adjusted for inflation or GDP growth, so comparing 1950s figures to 2010s figures requires context. The BPC source provides these in nominal dollars of the enactment year.
- **FICA rate table draws from SSA official rate schedules.** The combined employee+employer rates are statutory rates; actual burden depends on whether employer share is passed through in lower wages (economic consensus: largely yes).

## Annotation Guide for Graph Phase

The following entries are most likely to produce **visible inflection points** in the share-of-pie ratio and should be prioritized as chart annotations:

1. **1964 — Revenue Act (Kennedy-Johnson tax cut):** Large income tax cut across all brackets. Expect upward tick in disposable income ratio.
2. **1966 — Medicare payroll tax begins:** New 0.70% payroll tax partially offsets income gains. Watch for slight drag on earnings ratio.
3. **1972 — Social Security COLA + benefit increase:** Higher payroll taxes for workers, higher benefits for retirees. Mixed effect on working-age ratio.
4. **1981 — ERTA (Reagan tax cut):** Large across-the-board cut; bracket indexing prevents future bracket creep. Expect visible upward shift.
5. **1983 — Social Security Amendments:** Payroll tax trajectory set to reach 15.3% by 1990. Significant drag on middle-income take-home pay through decade.
6. **1986 — Tax Reform Act:** Rate cuts offset by base broadening. Roughly neutral for middle income but visible rate change.
7. **1993 — OBRA-93 (Clinton):** Top rate increase mostly hits upper brackets, but EITC expansion is the largest anti-poverty expansion since Food Stamps. Bottom quintile gains.
8. **2001/2003 — Bush tax cuts:** Visible cut for middle-income via new 10% bracket and CTC increase.
9. **2010 — ACA:** Premium subsidies are an in-kind transfer not captured in income data. Net investment income tax hits top quintiles.
10. **2011–2012 — Payroll tax holiday:** Directly visible as ~2% boost in take-home pay for all workers.
11. **2018 — TCJA:** Doubled standard deduction is the main middle-income lever. Visible in after-tax income data.
12. **2020–2021 — COVID relief:** Massive one-time transfers will show as anomalous spike in transfer income and disposable income ratios.
