## Sources

1. President's Science Advisory Committee, "Restoring the Quality of Our Environment," Report of the Environmental Pollution Panel, November 1965. Warned that CO2 from fossil fuels would cause measurable climate changes by 2000.
2. Charney, J.G. et al., "Carbon Dioxide and Climate: A Scientific Assessment," National Academy of Sciences, 1979. Estimated equilibrium climate sensitivity of 3°C (±1.5°C) for doubled CO2 — a range that has held for 45+ years.
3. IPCC First Assessment Report, 1990. Stated that human activities were increasing greenhouse gas concentrations but concluded that "unequivocal detection" of the enhanced greenhouse effect was "not likely for a decade or more."
4. IPCC Second Assessment Report (SAR), 1995. The landmark "balance of evidence suggests a discernible human influence on global climate" finding, adopted at the Madrid plenary in November 1995.
5. United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), opened for signature June 4, 1992 at the Rio Earth Summit. Entered into force March 21, 1994. Framework only — no binding emission limits.
6. Kyoto Protocol, adopted December 11, 1997. First legally binding emission reduction targets for developed nations. Entered into force February 16, 2005. 36 participating countries met their first commitment period targets.
7. Paris Agreement, adopted December 12, 2015. Entered into force November 4, 2016. Legally binding treaty with universal participation but nationally determined contributions (NDCs). Goal: limit warming to well below 2°C, pursue 1.5°C.
8. EPA, "Endangerment and Cause or Contribute Findings for Greenhouse Gases Under Section 202(a) of the Clean Air Act," December 15, 2009 (74 FR 66496). Prerequisite for US vehicle GHG standards.
9. EPA, "Light-Duty Vehicle Greenhouse Gas Emission Standards and Corporate Average Fuel Economy Standards; Final Rule," May 7, 2010 (75 FR 25324). First binding US federal CO2 regulation.
10. EPA, "Final Rule: Rescission of the Greenhouse Gas Endangerment Finding and Motor Vehicle Greenhouse Gas Emission Standards Under the Clean Air Act," February 2026. Reversed the Endangerment Finding and all associated GHG standards.
11. Banerjee, N., Song, L., Hasemyer, D., "Exxon: The Road Not Taken," Inside Climate News, 2015. Documented Exxon's internal climate research from 1977-1982 that accurately predicted global warming while the company later funded external climate denial.
12. Supran, G., Rahmstorf, S., Oreskes, N., "Assessing ExxonMobil's global warming projections," Science, Vol 379, Issue 6628, 2023. Showed Exxon's internal models from 1977-2003 predicted warming of ~0.20°C/decade — remarkably accurate.
13. Global Climate Coalition (1989-2002): largest industry group opposing climate regulation. Lobbied against Kyoto Protocol, helped pass the Byrd-Hagel Resolution (1997, 95-0 Senate vote) that effectively blocked US Kyoto ratification.
14. IEA, Global Energy Review 2025. Global fossil fuel CO2 emissions reached 37.2 Gt in 2025, a new record, with 0.7% annual growth. Atmospheric CO2 reached 422.5 ppm in 2024, 50% above pre-industrial levels.

## Judgment calls

**Early evidence year (1965 vs. alternatives):** Arrhenius (1896) calculated the greenhouse effect of CO2 but this was theoretical physics, not empirical observation of ongoing warming — excluded per plan guidelines. Callendar (1938) compiled temperature data showing warming and linked it to rising CO2, but his work was largely dismissed by contemporaries. Keeling began continuous CO2 measurements in 1958, providing the empirical backbone. The 1965 PSAC report was chosen because it is the first credible government scientific report that (a) established the causal link between fossil fuel CO2 and climate change with evidence, (b) quantified the expected increase, and (c) formally warned a head of state. Revelle and Suess (1957) could also be argued but was a journal paper, not a formal assessment. The Charney Report (1979) was the first comprehensive NAS assessment with quantitative sensitivity estimates, but by that point the PSAC had already established the link 14 years earlier.

**Consensus year (1995 vs. 1990):** The IPCC FAR (1990) explicitly said unequivocal detection was "not likely for a decade or more" — this is NOT a consensus statement that human influence had been detected. The SAR (1995) crossed that threshold with "the balance of evidence suggests a discernible human influence." This is the correct consensus date. The stronger statements in TAR (2001: "most of the warming is likely due to") and AR4 (2007: "very likely") represent increasing confidence, not first consensus.

**First regulation year (1997 Kyoto vs. alternatives):** The UNFCCC (1992) was a framework convention with no binding emission limits — it committed parties to "aim" to return emissions to 1990 levels but this was not enforceable. Kyoto (1997) was the first treaty with legally binding quantified emission reduction targets. Paris (2015) is broader and universal but its NDCs are nationally determined and not externally enforceable. Using the plan's proxy table for international treaties ("Year treaty signed"), 1997 is correct.

**US regulation year (2010):** The US signed but never ratified Kyoto. The Endangerment Finding (2009) was a legal prerequisite, not a regulation itself. The vehicle GHG standards finalized in May 2010 were the first actual binding federal regulation limiting CO2. However, these were rescinded in February 2026, leaving the US with no binding federal CO2 regulation. This is noted but 2010 remains the correct historical date for "first binding US regulation."

**Regulation status (in-progress):** Despite Kyoto and Paris, global CO2 emissions continue to rise (37.2 Gt in 2025, a record). The US has reversed its regulations. The regulatory framework exists but has not achieved meaningful exposure reduction. This is categorized as "in-progress" rather than "completed" because the harm has not been meaningfully addressed.

**Category note:** Climate change is categorically different from every other hazard in this dataset. Regulation requires global economic restructuring — replacing the energy system that powers civilization — not banning a single chemical or withdrawing a single product. The 32-year lag from evidence to first binding regulation reflects political and economic complexity at a scale incomparable to other entries. The fossil fuel industry's organized opposition campaign (estimated billions of dollars over decades) is the largest in history, but even without it, the structural difficulty of decarbonizing the global economy would make this the longest-lag case in the dataset.

**Industry opposition:** Rated "strong" — this is the gold standard. Exxon's internal scientists accurately modeled climate change from 1977 onward while the company spent decades funding external climate denial. The Global Climate Coalition (1989-2002) coordinated industry opposition across fossil fuels, automobiles, utilities, and chemicals. Koch Industries, API, and successor organizations continued after the GCC disbanded. The Byrd-Hagel Resolution (1997), passed 95-0 in the US Senate with GCC lobbying, effectively killed US participation in Kyoto before it was even signed.

**Substitute availability (partial):** Renewable energy (solar, wind) and nuclear power exist and are increasingly cost-competitive. However, full replacement of fossil fuels at current global scale — including heavy transport, industrial heat, aviation, and petrochemical feedstocks — remains incomplete. "Partial" reflects the 2026 reality: substitutes exist for electricity generation but not yet for all fossil fuel uses.
