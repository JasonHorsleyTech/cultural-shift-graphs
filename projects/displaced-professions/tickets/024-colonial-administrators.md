# Ticket 024: Colonial Administrators (Decolonization)

## Event
The displacement of European colonial civil servants during decolonization, ~1945–1970. When colonies gained independence, the administrative apparatus that governed them — district officers, colonial judges, police commanders, agricultural officers — lost their positions and returned to the metropole (or didn't).

## Mechanism
**Discrete shock** (regime)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Focus on 2–3 well-documented cases rather than trying to cover all colonies:

**British India (1947):**
- Indian Civil Service (ICS): ~1,000 British officers at peak (the "steel frame of India")
- Indian Police Service, Indian Political Service: additional thousands
- Military officers, railway administrators
- Total British civilian administration in India: estimate

**French Algeria (1962):**
- Pieds-noirs (European settlers): ~1 million, many in administrative/professional roles
- French colonial civil service in Algeria
- The exodus: ~900,000 left Algeria for France in 1962

**Belgian Congo (1960):**
- Belgian administrators: ~10,000 at independence (verify)
- Near-total evacuation in weeks during the Congo Crisis
- Lumumba's assassination and the chaos that followed

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Kirk-Greene, "Britain's Imperial Administrators, 1858–1966" (2000) — ICS data
2. Kirk-Greene, "On Crown Service" (1999)
3. Shepard, "The Invention of Decolonization: The Algerian War and the Remaking of France" (2006) — pieds-noirs
4. Stora, "Algeria 1830–2000: A Short History" (2001)
5. Young, "The African Colonial State in Comparative Perspective" (1994)
6. Stengers & Vansina, "King Leopold's Congo" and related Belgian Congo histories

### Income Data
**Known caveats:** Colonial salaries were typically generous (including overseas allowances, housing, servants) but varied enormously by rank and colony. Post-return income in the metropole was generally lower because overseas premiums disappeared.

- ICS officer salaries vs. equivalent UK civil service grades
- Pieds-noirs economic status in France post-return (many arrived as refugees)
- Track what "going home" meant economically for each group

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Kirk-Greene (2000, 1999) — ICS salary data
2. Colonial Office records (UK National Archives)
3. Shepard (2006) — pieds-noirs economic integration in France

### Where They Went
**British ICS officers:**
- UK civil service (Home Civil Service) — absorbed some but not all
- Business/corporate sector (expertise in Indian markets)
- Academic/advisory roles (development consulting)
- Many struggled with the transition — trained for a world that no longer existed

**Pieds-noirs:**
- Settled primarily in southern France (Marseille, Toulouse, Montpellier)
- Many arrived with nothing — refugee integration took years
- Eventually became politically influential (National Front support base)

**Belgian Congo administrators:**
- Returned to Belgium — a small country suddenly absorbing its colonial workforce
- Belgian civil service, business

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Kirk-Greene — post-colonial career tracking
2. Shepard — pieds-noirs integration
3. Colonial office de-staffing records

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** ICS officers who pivoted to development consulting (World Bank, UN). Those who cultivated relationships with incoming nationalist leaders.
- **False movers:** Officers who believed the timeline would be longer and stayed too long. Pieds-noirs who organized the OAS resistance — violent opposition to Algerian independence that alienated both sides.
- **Deniers:** "The empire will endure." Churchill's resistance to Indian independence. French settlers' belief that Algeria was permanently French.
- **Survival pattern:** Transferable skills (administration, law, finance) and early acceptance of the political reality.

### Unique Texture
- The speed varied enormously: India's transition was planned over years; Congo's was chaotic within weeks
- The pieds-noirs as displaced *settlers*, not just administrators — 1 million people "going home" to a country many had never lived in
- The "wind of change" speech (Macmillan, 1960) — the moment the British government acknowledged the end
- The Congo Crisis as the worst-case scenario: no transition planning, violence, collapse of services
- The moral complexity: these were the administrators of colonial exploitation, but they were also individual workers losing their livelihoods
