# English Replacing French as Diplomatic and Business Lingua Franca

## Summary
English displaced French as the world's lingua franca for diplomacy, international institutions, science, and business over roughly 100 years (1919–2020). The inflection point was the Treaty of Versailles (1919), the first major treaty with an official English version alongside French. Post-WWII American dominance accelerated the shift through the UN, Bretton Woods institutions, ICAO, and corporate globalization. By the 2020s, English accounts for 85%+ of EU Commission source documents, 97% of scientific publications, and is the mandatory or dominant working language of virtually every major international organization — even as French retains ceremonial status.

## Penetration Score Definition
**Denominator:** Percentage of international diplomatic, institutional, scientific, and business communication conducted in English (composite of four sub-indicators: treaty/diplomatic language, international organization working language, scientific publication language, and corporate/business lingua franca usage).
**0% anchor:** English has no role in international diplomacy, institutions, or business. French (or another language) is the sole lingua franca across all domains.
**100% anchor:** English is the sole language of international diplomacy, institutional operations, scientific publishing, and global business. French and other languages have been completely displaced from all international communication.

## Methodology
The penetration score is a composite of four sub-indicators, each tracking English's displacement of French (and other languages) in a major domain of international communication:

1. **Diplomatic/treaty language** (~25% of composite): Tracks the language of major international treaties and diplomatic communications. French was the sole language of diplomacy from ~1714 to 1919. The 1919 Treaty of Versailles marks the transition to bilingual. Post-1945 English becomes dominant through US-led institutional design.

2. **International organization working language** (~25% of composite): Tracks the de facto working language of major international organizations (UN, IMF, World Bank, NATO, ICAO, EU institutions). Scored by the percentage of organizations where English is the primary (not just official) working language, weighted by organizational significance.

3. **Scientific publication language** (~25% of composite): Percentage of indexed scientific papers published in English. Best-documented sub-indicator with data from Gordin, Ammon, Web of Science, and Scopus. Trend: ~33% (1900) → ~60% (1950) → ~90% (2000) → ~97% (2020).

4. **Business/corporate lingua franca** (~25% of composite): Percentage of major multinational corporations using English as primary corporate language. Estimated from corporate adoption milestones and business English market data. Slowest to shift — business remained multilingual longer than science or diplomacy.

The composite equally weights all four sub-indicators. For years where only some sub-indicators have data, the composite averages available indicators. EU Commission translation statistics (available 1997–2020) serve as a hard anchor for the institutional sub-indicator in the modern era.

**Key limitation:** The pre-1919 scores are estimated from qualitative historical evidence rather than quantitative data. The transition from "French as sole diplomatic language" to "English as dominant" is well-documented at the milestone level but not as a continuous percentage series.

## Notes
- **The Treaty of Versailles (1919) is the single most important inflection point.** Woodrow Wilson and Lloyd George insisted on an official English text alongside French. The French delegation resisted but ultimately conceded. This broke 200+ years of French monopoly in diplomatic language.
- **Post-WWII institutional design locked in English dominance.** The US designed the UN, IMF, World Bank, and NATO — all with English as a primary working language. This was structural, not just cultural: the institutions that govern global affairs operate in English.
- **The EU is the most ironic case.** English became the dominant drafting language of EU institutions even though only Ireland and Malta have English as an official national language (post-Brexit). By 2020, 85.5% of EU Commission documents were originally drafted in English — a figure that *increased* after Brexit removed the EU's largest native-English-speaking member.
- **Science was a three-horse race until WWI.** The roughly equal English/French/German split before 1914 is often forgotten. The deliberate boycott of German science after WWI, combined with the flight of European scientists to the US before and during WWII, concentrated scientific publishing in English. This was driven as much by geopolitics as by any inherent linguistic advantage.
- **French retains institutional footholds.** The Organisation Internationale de la Francophonie (88 member states), the African Union (French is a working language), and some UN bodies still operate significantly in French. The score plateaus below 100% because complete displacement is unlikely — French has enough institutional and demographic weight (especially in West Africa) to maintain a permanent minority position.
- **Business was the last domain to flip.** Diplomacy and science shifted to English decades before corporate boardrooms did. The corporate English mandate wave (ABB 1988, Nokia, Samsung, Renault, Nordea 2000, Rakuten 2010) is a late-20th/early-21st century phenomenon.
- **"English" here is Anglo-American, not purely American.** British Empire trade and colonialism planted English globally before American dominance. However, post-1945 the driver is overwhelmingly American: American institutional design, American scientific funding, American corporate power, and American cultural exports. The metric captures both British and American contributions but the 20th-century acceleration is American-driven.
