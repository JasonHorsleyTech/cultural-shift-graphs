# Ticket 008: Human Computers

## Event
The displacement of human "computers" — people who performed mathematical calculations by hand — by electronic computers, ~1945–1960. A platform shift that displaced workers regardless of skill level, since the machine was faster at everything.

## Mechanism
**Tipping point** (tech_platform)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track employment of human computers in the US and UK:

- Contexts: military ballistics (Aberdeen Proving Ground, Bletchley Park), aerospace (NACA/NASA JPL), census (Census Bureau), insurance/actuarial, astronomical observatories
- Peak: difficult to pin down. Thousands employed during WWII. Estimate total US human computer employment at peak.
- Key dates: ENIAC (1945), UNIVAC I at Census Bureau (1951), IBM mainframes widespread (late 1950s), NASA transition to electronic computers (early 1960s)
- By ~1960, the profession was effectively extinct as a standalone job title

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Grier, "When Computers Were Human" (2005) — the definitive history
2. Shetterly, "Hidden Figures" (2016) — NASA's human computers
3. Light, "When Computers Were Women" (1999) — gender and early computing
4. NACA/NASA personnel records (if accessible via archives or secondary sources)
5. US Census Bureau history — UNIVAC transition
6. Hicks, "Programmed Inequality" (2017) — UK computing transition

### Income Data
- Government pay scales for "computer" or "mathematician" positions (Civil Service grades)
- Compare human computer wages to early electronic computer programmer wages
- Were programmers paid more or less than human computers initially?

**Known caveats:** "Human computer" was not always a distinct job title — many were classified as "mathematician," "clerk," or "calculator." Tracking the profession requires looking at function, not job title.

**Source hierarchy:**
1. US Civil Service Commission pay scales (GS grades for mathematician/computer positions)
2. Grier (2005) for wage and employment data
3. NASA/NACA historical personnel data
4. UK Civil Service pay records for Bletchley Park and related

### Where They Went
This is a well-documented and largely *positive* transition:
- Many human computers became the first electronic computer programmers
- Some became systems analysts, operations researchers
- At NACA/NASA: human computers transitioned to programming (the Hidden Figures story)
- At Census Bureau: many retrained on UNIVAC

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Grier (2005) — tracks career transitions
2. Shetterly (2016) — individual career stories
3. Abbate, "Recoding Gender" (2012) — women in early computing
4. Light (1999)

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** The women of NACA/JPL who learned FORTRAN and became programmers (Katherine Johnson, Dorothy Vaughan, Mary Jackson). Specific transition dates and roles.
- **False movers:** Were there human computers who retrained in something other than electronic computing and regretted it?
- **Deniers:** Any documented resistance to electronic computers? Mathematicians who argued machines couldn't do "real" math?
- **Survival pattern:** Learning to program the replacement technology — the most direct "become the operator of the thing that replaced you" story in the dataset.

### Unique Texture
- The profession was heavily female — computing was "women's work" before it became a prestige male profession
- The gender flip: computing went from ~majority female (1945) to ~majority male (1970)
- Speed of transition: ~15 years from ENIAC to near-total displacement
- The irony: human computers were often the first programmers — they displaced *themselves*
- Comparison to modern AI: software engineers building AI tools may be displacing themselves, just as human computers who programmed ENIAC did

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Estimated total human computer employment at peak (even rough order of magnitude)
- Timeline of key electronic computer installations and corresponding transitions
- At least 3 specific individual transition stories (names, dates, before/after roles)
- Documentation of the gender dynamics
- The "they became the programmers" transition quantified as much as possible
