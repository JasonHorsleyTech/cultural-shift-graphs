# Result: Resilient Community Archetypes — Who Survives Best?

## Summary

Approximately 10–18 million Americans (3–5.4% of the US population) belong to communities with significantly above-average post-collapse survival probability. The most resilient are small absolute numbers (Amish ~500K, subsistence farmers ~1.5M) while the largest group (preppers at ~15M with 31+ days supply) has the most uncertain long-term survival. Overlap between categories (e.g., Mormon homesteaders, prepper farmers) means the unique total is toward the lower end of that range.

## Community Profiles

### 1. Amish / Old Order Mennonite (~500K)

**Population:** 411,000 Amish (Young Center 2025) + ~72,000–100,000 Old Order Mennonite = ~485,000–510,000 total.

**Why they're resilient:** Already practicing pre-industrial agriculture. Use draft animals, hand tools, seed saving. Strong community mutual aid (barn raisings, shared labor). Education system trains for manual trades. Grow most of their own food. 30–40% more energy-efficient per unit of food than conventional farms.

**Estimated 1-year survival:** 75–92%. The high estimate reflects that they already live without grid dependency. The low end accounts for: (a) modern Amish are not fully self-sufficient — they buy flour, sugar, and many goods from stores; (b) concentrated geography (PA/OH/IN) makes them vulnerable to regional scenarios (nuclear strike on eastern US); (c) small communities can't defend against large desperate populations.

**Primary limitation:** Small absolute numbers. Geographic concentration. Not fully food-independent (most supplement with purchased goods). Would face intense pressure from surrounding desperate populations.

### 2. Active Military + Dependents on Bases (~2.9M)

**Population:** 1.28M active duty + 770K reserves/Guard + ~1.6M dependents = ~3.7M total affiliated. Of these, approximately 2.5–2.9M live on or immediately adjacent to military installations at any given time.

**Why they're resilient:** Organized command structure with clear hierarchy. Armed and trained. Medical facilities on base. Some strategic food reserves (21-day war reserves per CFR). Infrastructure (power generation, water treatment, communications) often independent of civilian grid. Experience with logistics and rationing.

**Estimated 1-year survival:** 45–75%. The wide range reflects scenario-dependent factors: (a) in a nuclear scenario, military bases are primary targets — many die immediately; (b) in non-nuclear scenarios, bases become strongholds but supplies last only weeks without resupply; (c) mandate to maintain civil order may draw resources outward; (d) organized structure enables food production transition better than any other group.

**Primary limitation:** Bases are nuclear first-strike targets. 21-day supply reserves are designed for deployment, not siege. Mission requirements may conflict with base survival (soldiers ordered to maintain order elsewhere). Dependents increase resource burden.

### 3. Mormon (LDS) Community (~6.8M, ~1.2M well-prepared)

**Population:** ~6.8M US members. Of these, an estimated 15–30% maintain meaningful food storage (3+ months), yielding ~1.0–2.1M well-prepared individuals.

**Why they're resilient:** Cultural tradition of food storage (church teaches 3-month minimum, 1-year optimal). Bishop's Storehouse system provides community-level food reserves. Ward structure (150–400 members each) provides pre-existing mutual aid network. Geographic concentration in Utah/Idaho/Arizona creates critical mass. Home canning and preservation traditions.

**Estimated 1-year survival for well-prepared subset:** 50–75%. For the broader LDS membership without storage: probably 30–50% (still above baseline due to community organization). Ward structure means even those without personal storage get shared resources.

**Primary limitation:** Compliance with food storage counsel is far lower than commonly assumed — possibly only 15–20% have meaningful supplies per ward coordinator estimates (vs. the commonly cited 30–60%). Storage runs out (1 year maximum for the most prepared). Utah/Idaho concentration means regional catastrophe could eliminate much of the community.

### 4. Small-Scale Farmers / Homesteaders (~1.4M)

**Population:** 1.88M total US farms; 86% are "small" (~1.6M). But "small farm" = less than $350K GCFI, which includes hobby farms and land speculators. Truly subsistence-capable farms (with livestock, diverse crops, water access, enough land): estimated 200,000–500,000 operations. At ~4 people per household = 800,000–2,000,000 people.

**Why they're resilient:** Already producing food. Own land. Have agricultural knowledge and tools. Many have livestock (protein source independent of supply chain). Water access. Rural location means lower population density competition for resources.

**Estimated 1-year survival:** 55–80%. Highly variable by farm type. A diversified homestead with chickens, garden, water well, and stored grain is essentially disaster-proof for a year. A "small farm" that grows soybeans for export with zero self-provisioning capability is no better than suburban.

**Primary limitation:** Most "small farms" are not subsistence operations — they grow commodity crops and buy groceries like everyone else. True subsistence capability requires: diverse crops OR livestock + garden, water source independent of municipal supply, stored seeds, preservation capability. Maybe 15–25% of small farms meet this threshold.

### 5. Fishing Communities (~750K)

**Population:** 21,900 direct fishing/hunting workers (BLS 2024). Broader fishing-dependent communities: estimated 500,000–1,000,000 people in communities where fishing is primary economy. Alaska subsistence communities: ~120,000 rural residents getting 35–62% of calories from wild harvest.

**Why they're resilient:** Access to renewable protein source not dependent on agriculture or supply chains. Boats provide mobility. Coastal location gives access to multiple food sources (fish, shellfish, seaweed, waterfowl). Alaska data shows subsistence fishing can provide 242% of protein requirements for rural populations. Skills are directly transferable to post-collapse caloric production.

**Estimated 1-year survival:** 45–70%. Fishing alone provides protein but limited calories (only 35% of caloric needs in Alaska subsistence data). Need supplemental carbohydrate sources. Equipment maintenance (nets, boats, engines) becomes harder without supply chain. Coastal vulnerability to some scenarios (tsunami, storm surge).

**Primary limitation:** Fish alone doesn't provide complete nutrition — need carbohydrate supplementation. Modern commercial fishing depends heavily on fuel, ice, and equipment maintenance. True subsistence fishing with primitive methods has much lower yield. Seasonal variation in many fisheries means gaps.

### 6. Survivalist / Prepper Community (~15M broad, ~2M serious)

**Population:** FEMA data shows 14.9M Americans with 31+ days self-sufficiency (2020). 6.7M "ultra-highly resilient" with 97+ days. Estimated 1–3M with 1+ year of supplies and serious training/skills. Chris Ellis (Cornell/military researcher) estimates 20–23M total with some level of preparation.

**Why they're resilient:** Pre-positioned supplies (food, water filtration, medical, ammunition). Many have planned for specific scenarios. Skills training (hunting, foraging, first aid). Often rural or semi-rural. Many have alternative energy (solar, generators with stored fuel). Some have community networks (mutual assistance groups, ham radio networks).

**Estimated 1-year survival:** 35–65%. Wide range because: (a) supplies are finite — a 90-day supply just delays the problem; (b) individual/family focus means less community resilience than Amish or LDS; (c) "prepper" is self-identified with no quality control — ranges from a closet of canned goods to a fully stocked hardened bunker; (d) those who planned for community cooperation do much better than lone wolves.

**Primary limitation:** Supplies run out. Many preppers plan for short disruptions (weeks-months), not civilizational collapse (years). Individual/family orientation means no division of labor, no community agriculture, no mutual defense beyond family. Conflicts between well-supplied preppers and desperate neighbors. Skills vary enormously — many have supplies but not agricultural knowledge for long-term food production.

### 7. Native American Reservations (~1M total, ~300K with agricultural tradition)

**Population:** ~1M living on reservations. Of these, approximately 200,000–400,000 live in communities with living agricultural traditions or significant subsistence hunting/fishing practices (primarily Southwest tribes like Navajo/Hopi/Zuni for agriculture, and northern tribes for hunting/fishing).

**Why they're resilient:** Traditional ecological knowledge spanning millennia. Some communities maintain traditional agriculture (Three Sisters planting, dry farming techniques). Land ownership is communal — no fragmentation. Community social structure predates and doesn't depend on modern institutions. Some reservations are remote enough to avoid refugee pressure.

**Estimated 1-year survival:** 35–65%. Extremely variable between communities. Hopi communities with active dry-farming traditions are very different from urban-adjacent reservations with no agricultural base. Many reservations already experience food desert conditions — only 10 grocery stores in the entire 27,000 sq mi Navajo Nation.

**Primary limitation:** Modern reservation populations largely depend on imported food — 22% food insecurity rate already exists in Apache County (Navajo Nation). Traditional agricultural knowledge exists but is not widely practiced at subsistence scale. Many reservations are in marginal land (intentionally — that's why they were assigned there). Extreme poverty limits stored supplies. Healthcare access already poor. Wide variation makes generalization dangerous.

## Overlap Analysis

Categories are not mutually exclusive. Key overlaps:
- **Mormon + farmer:** Utah/Idaho LDS members who farm. Significant overlap.
- **Prepper + farmer:** Many homesteaders identify as preppers. Major overlap.
- **Prepper + LDS:** Mormon food storage tradition means many LDS members are counted as preppers.
- **Prepper + military (veteran):** Veteran preppers are overrepresented in the serious prepper community.
- **Native American + fishing:** Alaska Native subsistence communities fit both categories.

Estimated unique overlap reduction: ~15–25% of the raw sum. Raw total ~14.8M, unique-adjusted ~10–15M.

## Total Resilient Population

**Central estimate:** ~12 million Americans (3.6% of 335M) have significantly above-average 1-year survival probability in a civilization-collapse scenario.

**Range:** 8–18 million (2.4–5.4%).

This aligns with the intuition that in most collapse scenarios, the US eventually stabilizes at some equilibrium population. These ~12M are the "seed population" most likely to make it through the first year, around which larger surviving communities eventually form.

## Data Gaps

- **LDS food storage compliance:** The 15–20% figure comes from a single ward coordinator's estimate. No systematic church-wide survey is publicly available. The often-cited "60% compliance" appears to be aspirational, not measured.
- **True subsistence capability of small farms:** USDA tracks farm income, not self-sufficiency potential. No dataset distinguishes "could feed themselves for a year" from "grows specialty lettuce for farmers markets."
- **Prepper supply quality:** FEMA measures days of self-sufficiency but not nutritional completeness. A year of ramen ≠ a year of balanced nutrition.
- **Post-supply behavior:** All survival estimates assume the transition from stored supplies to production. No data exists on how well preppers/LDS/military adapt once supplies run out.
- **Community vs. individual resilience:** The biggest gap. Amish thrive because of community structure. A lone prepper with a year of food but no community is in deep trouble in year 2. This is poorly quantified.
- **Scenario dependency:** Military bases are nuclear targets. Coastal communities are tsunami-vulnerable. Estimates assume a "generic" collapse; real scenarios would dramatically shift which groups survive.
