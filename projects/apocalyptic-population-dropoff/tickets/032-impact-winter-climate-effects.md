# Ticket 032: Impact Winter — Climate and Crop Effects from Large Asteroid

## What to research

After a 10 km asteroid impact (assuming NOT a direct US hit), the dust and soot in the stratosphere produce an "impact winter" analogous to nuclear winter. How severe and how long?

## Specific data needed

1. **Impact winter climate modeling:**
   - Dust and soot injection: how much material reaches the stratosphere?
   - Temperature drop: Chicxulub models suggest 10-20°C cooling for years
   - Duration: how many years until recovery? (Chicxulub: photosynthesis may have been shut down for months)
   - Sunlight reduction: near-total darkness for weeks to months?

2. **Comparison to nuclear winter:**
   - Impact winter is likely MORE severe than nuclear winter (more material, more energy)
   - Duration may be longer (larger particles, more material to settle)
   - But mechanism is similar: reduced sunlight → crop failure → famine

3. **Chicxulub-specific data:**
   - Best evidence for what actually happened 66 million years ago
   - Geological record: iridium layer, soot layer, charcoal
   - Estimated duration of darkness/cooling from geological proxies
   - Marine and terrestrial extinction patterns (what survived and how?)

4. **US crop production under impact winter:**
   - Near-zero for 1-3 years (similar to worst nuclear winter estimates or worse)
   - Marine fisheries: deep impact on ocean ecosystems
   - When does recovery begin? (Possibly faster than nuclear winter because there's no ongoing radiation)

5. **Acid rain:**
   - Impact vaporizes rock → sulfur/nitrogen compounds → acid rain
   - Effects on soil, freshwater, remaining vegetation
   - How long does acid rain persist?

6. **Famine mortality:**
   - Given near-zero food production for 1-3 years, what's the survival path?
   - Stored food + alternative food (mushrooms, etc.) — same as nuclear winter (ticket 016)
   - Key difference: no radiation contamination, infrastructure intact in non-impact zones

## Where to look

1. **Schulte et al. (2010)** — Chicxulub impact review
2. **Brugger et al. (2017)** — asteroid impact winter modeling
3. **Bardeen et al. (2017)** — impact winter climate simulation
4. **Kaiho & Oshima (2017)** — soot from Chicxulub fires
5. **Vellekoop et al. (2014)** — post-impact environmental recovery
6. **Pierazzo et al. (2003)** — hydrocode impact simulations

## Result format

**Mortality Timeline** from month 1 through year 10 (continuing from ticket 031):

| time_hours | cumulative_deaths_pct | deaths_pct_low | deaths_pct_high | primary_cause | source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Use the "far-side impact" scenario (US not directly hit but facing impact winter).

Key Data Points:
- `temperature_change_c` for years 1-10
- `sunlight_reduction_pct` for years 1-5
- `crop_production_pct` for years 1-10

## What good data looks like

- Notes that a 10 km impactor is a mass extinction event — this is the extreme end of our scenarios
- Impact winter may be more severe than nuclear winter but with potentially faster recovery (no radiation)
- Final mortality for far-side impact: probably 70-95% over 5 years
- Near-side impact: >99% — essentially not survivable for the US
