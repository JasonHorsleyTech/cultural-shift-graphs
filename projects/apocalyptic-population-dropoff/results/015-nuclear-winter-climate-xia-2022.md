# Result: Nuclear Winter — Climate Modeling (Xia et al. 2022)

## Summary

A full-scale US-Russia nuclear exchange (150 Tg soot) would produce catastrophic global cooling of -7°C to -16°C (depending on model and region), reduce solar radiation by up to 85%, cut precipitation by 45-68%, destroy 75% of the ozone layer for 15 years, and cool ocean surfaces by ~6°C. Two leading climate models (Robock/ModelE and Coupe/WACCM4) agree on the severity, differing mainly in magnitude. The Los Alamos group (Reisner et al.) disputes the soot injection assumptions but their dry-plume methodology has been challenged by subsequent research.

## Model Comparison: Severe vs. Moderate Predictions

### Severe nuclear winter (Robock/Xia/Coupe consensus)

| parameter | value | source |
|---|---|---|
| Stratospheric soot penetration | ~80% of total | Assumed from fire/firestorm dynamics |
| Global temp change (peak) | -8 to -16°C | ModelE: -8°C avg; WACCM4: larger |
| Duration to recovery | 10-15 years | Robock 2007, Xia 2022 |
| Soot lifetime in stratosphere | 8-10 years | Self-lofting maintains altitude |
| Methodology | Firestorm assumed; soot lofted by pyroconvection + self-heating | Standard since Turco/Toon 1983 |

### Moderate "nuclear autumn" (Reisner et al. counterpoint)

| parameter | value | source |
|---|---|---|
| Stratospheric soot penetration | ~6% of total | Reisner et al. 2018 |
| Total soot produced | 3.7 Tg (vs 5 Tg assumed by Robock for same scenario) | Reisner et al. 2018 |
| Stratospheric soot (worst case) | 0.2 Tg | Reisner et al. 2018 |
| Global temp change | minimal (not specified; "no nuclear winter effect") | Reisner et al. 2018 |
| Key methodological difference | Dry convection only; no latent heating from moisture | Reisner et al. 2018 |
| Critical limitation identified | Dry simulations shown invalid for firestorm plume lofting | Tarshish & Romps 2022 |

### Scientific debate status

The Rutgers/NASA group (Robock, Xia, Toon, Coupe) has **stronger evidential support** for several reasons:

1. **Wet lofting validated:** Tarshish & Romps (2022) demonstrated that moisture-driven (latent heat) lofting is essential for firestorm plume modeling. Reisner's dry-only simulations miss this mechanism entirely.
2. **Historical precedent:** Firestorms occurred in ~50% of past nuclear city detonations (Hiroshima, Hamburg, Dresden). Reisner's model predicted they wouldn't form.
3. **Scale argument:** The Los Alamos work modeled a regional scenario (100 × 15-kt weapons). For a full US-Russia exchange (4,400 weapons, 100+ kt each), the case for massive firestorms is much stronger.
4. **Model resolution:** Reisner modeled suburban areas with golf courses, not dense urban cores where firestorm conditions are most likely.

However, the debate is **not fully resolved** — there is legitimate uncertainty of a factor of 2-3x in cooling magnitude, which is why this project uses separate curves for "winter" and "autumn" scenarios.

## UV Radiation After Smoke Clears

A counterintuitive effect: as the smoke dissipates (years 3-7+), the destroyed ozone layer allows extreme UV radiation through to the surface. This creates a "double punch" — first darkness and cold, then UV bombardment.

| timeframe | ozone_loss_pct | uv_effect | source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Year 1-2 | Building (smoke blocks UV initially) | Net UV at surface reduced (smoke absorbs it) | Bardeen et al. 2021 |
| Year 3-5 | 75% peak global loss | UV Index >35 tropics, extreme midlatitudes | Bardeen et al. 2021 |
| Year 5-10 | 50-60% (slow recovery) | Still dangerous; sunburns in minutes | Bardeen et al. 2021 |
| Year 10-15 | 20-30% (recovering) | Elevated but survivable | Bardeen et al. 2021 |
| Year 15+ | Near baseline | Normal | Bardeen et al. 2021 |

Normal UV Index: 1-11 depending on latitude/season. Values >11 are "extreme." Values of 35-45 are unprecedented in human experience — exposed skin would burn in minutes.

## Data Gaps

- **Year-by-year granularity:** The published papers present results primarily as figures/graphs rather than tabulated data. The year-by-year numbers above are composited from multiple sources and include some interpolation between reported data points.
- **US-specific regional data:** Most modeling reports global or zonal averages. Coupe 2019 provides Iowa-specific data; broader US regional breakdown (coasts vs. interior, south vs. north) is limited.
- **PAR vs. total shortwave:** Xia reports "85% solar radiation reduction" but doesn't always distinguish between PAR (plant-relevant wavelengths) and total shortwave. For agricultural modeling purposes, crop impact is the relevant output.
- **Recovery shape:** Whether recovery is linear or has a "snapback" once soot clears a threshold is not well characterized. The transition from cold/dark to UV-exposed is a qualitative shift, not a gradual improvement.
- **Interaction effects:** Ozone loss + cooling + precipitation loss compound in ways not fully modeled in any single study. Each paper focuses on one dimension.
- **Reisner full-scale scenario:** Reisner et al. only modeled regional exchanges. They have not published full US-Russia exchange results with their methodology, making direct comparison at the 150 Tg scale impossible.
