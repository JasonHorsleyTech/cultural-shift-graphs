# Ticket 019: Tobacco Smoking → Lung Cancer

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that tobacco smoking causes lung cancer to binding regulation restricting tobacco advertising, sales, and use. This is THE canonical science-to-action lag case — the most studied and most cited.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Doll & Hill (1950) and Wynder & Graham (1950) published the first major epidemiological studies linking smoking to lung cancer. Earlier observations exist (Müller 1939 in Nazi Germany). What's the earliest credible epidemiological evidence?

2. **Consensus date** — The US Surgeon General's Report (January 11, 1964) is the definitive consensus statement for the US. UK Royal College of Physicians report came earlier (1962). Look for the first major government-affiliated scientific body consensus statement.

3. **First OECD regulation** — Multiple layers: health warnings on packages, broadcast advertising bans, indoor smoking restrictions. Which OECD country enacted the FIRST binding regulation that meaningfully restricted tobacco? Italy banned cigarette advertising on TV in 1962. UK required health warnings in 1965. US banned broadcast advertising in 1971.

4. **US regulation** — Federal Cigarette Labeling and Advertising Act (1965, required health warnings). Public Health Cigarette Smoking Act (1970, banned broadcast ads effective 1971). MANY subsequent actions. Use the first binding federal action.

5. **Industry opposition** — The most well-documented case of organized industry opposition in history. Tobacco Industry Research Committee (later Council for Tobacco Research), hiring of doubt-manufacturing PR firms, suppression of internal research. Strong opposition sustained for 50+ years.

6. **50% exposure reduction** — US smoking rate peaked ~42% in 1965, dropped below 21% (50% of peak) around 1998-2000. CDC data tracks this precisely.

7. **Substitute availability** — No direct substitute (nicotine replacement came later, vaping much later). Smokers had to quit, not switch. This distinguishes tobacco from chemical-substitute cases like CFCs.

## Where to look

1. US Surgeon General's report (1964) and subsequent reports
2. UK Royal College of Physicians, "Smoking and Health" (1962)
3. Doll & Hill (1950) — British Medical Journal
4. Oreskes & Conway, "Merchants of Doubt" — detailed tobacco industry strategy
5. Allan Brandt, "The Cigarette Century" — comprehensive history
6. CDC smoking prevalence data (1965-present)
7. US legislative history: Federal Cigarette Labeling Act (1965), Public Health Cigarette Smoking Act (1970)

## What "good data" looks like

- Precise dates for consensus statements (Royal College 1962, Surgeon General 1964)
- The FIRST binding regulation in any OECD country, not just the US
- Smoking rate decline data for the secondary metric (50% exposure reduction year)
- Clear documentation of industry opposition scale and duration

## Known pitfalls

- The "lag" for tobacco depends ENORMOUSLY on what you count as "regulation." Health warnings (1965)? Ad bans (1971)? Indoor smoking bans (2004-2010)? 50% smoking rate reduction (~2000)? The graph needs the FIRST binding action, but note the decades-long regulatory ratchet.
- Secondhand smoke is a SEPARATE ticket (020). This ticket is about direct smoking and cancer.
- The Müller (1939) German study predates Doll & Hill but was ignored after WWII. Whether to count it as "early evidence" is a judgment call — document reasoning.
- Category: behavioral-mandate (regulations restrict advertising and public behavior, not a product ban — tobacco is still legal).
