# Result: Mean Personal Income Per Capita (BEA), 1947–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** FRED series A792RC0A052NBEA (BEA NIPA Table 7.1, line 4 — Per capita personal income, current dollars)
- **Secondary source:** None needed; single authoritative series
- **Date accessed:** 2026-04-29

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Total personal income divided by midyear population. Personal income includes wages & salaries, supplements to wages (employer contributions), proprietors' income, rental income, personal dividend and interest income, and transfer payments (Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, unemployment insurance, veterans' benefits, etc.). It excludes corporate retained earnings and government revenue not distributed as transfers.
- **Universe:** Entire US resident population (not just workers or adults). This is a mean measure — total income spread across every man, woman, and child.
- **Known methodology changes:** BEA periodically revises the entire series via comprehensive revisions (most recent: 2023). Each revision rebenches the full historical series for consistency. The downloaded data reflects the latest vintage as of April 2026.
- **Important note:** Because this includes transfer payments, it will be higher than a pure "market income per capita" measure. This is intentional — it serves as the numerator for the "mean sanity check" line, which should track relatively close to GDP per capita (personal income is the largest income-side component of GDP).

## Gaps and Caveats
- **No missing data.** Complete annual series, 78 rows, 1947–2024.
- **Monotonically increasing in nominal terms** except for recession-related dips: 1949 ($1,415 vs $1,456), 1954 ($1,861 vs $1,875), 2009 ($39,271 vs $40,801). These dips are real — nominal personal income can decline when job losses + wage cuts outweigh inflation.
- **2020–2021 spike:** Per capita personal income jumped from $55,560 (2019) to $59,160 (2020) to $64,658 (2021) due to massive transfer payments (stimulus checks, expanded unemployment benefits, PPP). This is NOT an error — BEA personal income includes all transfers. The 2022 figure ($66,259) represents a partial normalization.
- **Confidence: HIGH across entire series.** BEA NIPA data is the gold standard for national accounts. The series is internally consistent (same methodology, periodically re-benchmarked as a whole). No source switches or splicing required.
- **Ratio to GDP per capita:** Should hover around 70–85% throughout the series. Personal income is smaller than GDP because it excludes corporate retained earnings, the statistical discrepancy, and some production taxes. The ratio has drifted upward over time as transfer payments (especially Medicare/Medicaid) have grown as a share of personal income.
