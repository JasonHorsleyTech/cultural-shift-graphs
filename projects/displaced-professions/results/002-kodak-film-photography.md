# Kodak / Film Photography Workers

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Film photography manufacturing, processing, and support workers (Eastman Kodak and broader film photography industry)
- **Country/Region:** United States (headquartered in Rochester, NY; global operations)
- **Peak Year:** 1988
- **Peak Workforce:** 145,300 worldwide (Kodak alone) (Rochester Business Journal timeline; Kodak annual reports)
- **Primary Mechanism:** tipping_point
- **Cause Tags:** tech_platform, consolidation
- **Killing Event:** Digital cameras crossed a quality/price threshold around 2003, making film photography obsolete for consumers; digital camera sales surpassed film, Kodak stopped making film cameras (2004), and filed for bankruptcy (2012).

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1989 (workforce began declining from 1988 peak, though the digital tipping point accelerated decline from ~2003)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~6 years (reached ~108,000 by ~1994, after 10,000 cut under Fisher's restructuring starting 1993)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** ~14 years (reached ~70,000 by end of 2002)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** ~16 years (reached ~40,900 by 2004)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** ~22 years (reached 18,800 by 2010)
- **Decline Shape:** stepped — slow erosion through the 1990s (restructuring, diversification sell-offs) followed by a steep cliff from 2003-2012 once the digital tipping point hit. The 1990s decline was ~45% over 11 years; the 2003-2012 decline was another ~75% of remaining workforce in 9 years.
- **Current Status:** remnant (<10% peak) — 3,500 employees (2.4% of peak) in a completely restructured company focused on printing technology and advanced materials, not photography.

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: estimated

Kodak was famously a high-compensation employer. Direct BLS historical wage data for SOC 51-9151 was not accessible in archived form, so income estimates rely on a mix of company-level and regional data.

- **Income at Peak:** Kodak manufacturing workers earned above-market wages plus an annual wage dividend (bonus) of ~15% of base salary, with extensive benefits (healthcare, credit union, pension). Estimated total compensation for production workers: ~$45,000-$55,000/year in late 1980s dollars (~$100,000-$120,000 in 2024 dollars). (Dollars & Sense 1998; Baltimore Sun 1997)
- **Income at Trough:** Post-displacement, many blue-collar ex-Kodak workers "slipped into low-wage service jobs." Rochester's replacement jobs were described as "low-paying gigs that offer little security." (Rochester Beacon 2019; Baltimore Sun 1997)
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** N/A (workforce was still declining 10 years after digital tipping point; no aggregate income recovery data available for displaced workers specifically)

## Surplus Capture

- **Capital owners:** ~50% — Kodak's shareholders initially captured decades of film monopoly profits. After the collapse, the surplus shifted to digital platform companies (Apple, Samsung, Canon) whose shareholders captured the value of digital imaging. Kodak's patent portfolio — sold for ~$525 million during bankruptcy — represents decades of innovation value captured by acquirers.
- **Consumers:** ~40% — The primary beneficiary. Digital photography eliminated film/processing costs (~$0.50-$1.00/photo in film era → $0 marginal cost), democratized photography, and enabled smartphone cameras. The average American now takes ~2,000+ photos/year vs. ~100 in the film era.
- **Adjacent workers:** ~5% — Some benefits to digital camera industry workers, smartphone hardware engineers, and software developers, but digital cameras are far less labor-intensive to produce than film and chemical-based products.
- **Government/public:** ~5% — Loss of Kodak's massive tax base in Rochester was devastating. Kodak successfully pressured Rochester to reduce its taxes before declining, setting a precedent for other major employers.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
1. **Fujifilm (corporate pivot, 2000-2010):** Under CEO Shigetaka Komori, Fujifilm leveraged its chemical expertise to pivot into medical imaging, LCD protective films (achieving 70% market share with FUJITAC), pharmaceuticals (acquired Toyama Chemical in 2008), and cosmetics (Astalift skincare line launched 2007, built on collagen expertise from gelatin used in film). Revenue grew 57% from 2000-2010. Maintained ~72,000-81,000 employees throughout, vs. Kodak's collapse from 145,300 to 3,500. The most striking corporate adaptation story in modern business history.
2. **Ex-Kodak engineers who left early for digital companies:** Individual-level data is sparse, but Rochester's small tech ecosystem was partly seeded by ex-Kodak engineers who recognized the digital shift and left before the collapse.

### False Movers
1. **Kodak's digital camera division:** Kodak actually held #1 US digital camera market share (21.3%) in 2005 — but lost $60 per camera sold in 2001 and couldn't make the economics work. Digital cameras were a "crappy business" (CEO Perez, 2006) with razor-thin margins where anyone could compete. Having invented the digital camera didn't translate to owning the market.
2. **Kodak's Ofoto acquisition (2001):** Kodak bought the photo-sharing site but used it to drive users toward ordering physical prints — the exact opposite of where the market was going. They had Instagram before Instagram existed and used it to sell more film-era products.

### Deniers
1. **Steve Sasson's 1975 digital camera presentation:** Kodak management told Sasson "that's cute, but don't tell anyone about it" when he demonstrated the first digital camera prototype. While partly because the technology wasn't market-ready (0.01 megapixels, 23-second capture time), the instinct to suppress was telling. (Snopes rates the "suppression" narrative as more nuanced than popularly claimed — Kodak did continue digital R&D — but the initial reaction was defensive.)
2. **CEO Antonio Perez's belated acknowledgment:** "We were 20 years late. Film obviously was not lasting as long as the company thought it was going to last. In fact, it was disappearing very rapidly — maybe even faster than any other technology that we know that has disappeared." This quote captures the institutional denial that persisted even among leaders who intellectually understood the threat.

### Survival Pattern
Workers who survived best were those with transferable technical skills (engineers, chemists) who left Kodak early and moved into healthcare, optics, or tech — the long-tenured production workers who stayed loyal to the end fared worst.

## Unique Texture

1. **Kodak invented the thing that killed it.** Steve Sasson built the first digital camera at Kodak in 1975. The company held over 1,000 digital imaging patents, sold during bankruptcy for ~$525 million — a fraction of their development cost. (IEEE Spectrum; Kodak bankruptcy filings)

2. **Kodak paid a quarter of metropolitan Rochester's entire salary bill** at its peak and may have accounted for half of total economic activity. The city's GDP growth from 2007-2018 was effectively 0% while the nation grew 16%. Chemical manufacturing sector GDP in Rochester fell 74%. (Rochester Beacon 2019)

3. **Fujifilm vs. Kodak is a natural experiment in corporate adaptation.** Both companies had ~60-66% of revenue from film in 2000. Fujifilm pivoted and maintained ~75,000 employees with growing revenue. Kodak went bankrupt. The divergence: Fujifilm invested in unrelated industries using core chemical competencies; Kodak tried to stay in imaging and sold its healthcare division for $2.35 billion in 2007 — a move Fujifilm's CEO called a "fatal mistake." (PetaPixel; Fortune)

4. **The racial dimension of displacement.** Early Kodak layoffs disproportionately hit young, unskilled/semi-skilled workers, "in many cases black or Hispanic." Black unemployment in Rochester doubled from 8% to 16% between 1970-1990 during Kodak's first wave of cuts. Child poverty rates reached 48% for Black children and 58% for Latino children. (Dollars & Sense 1998)

5. **The speed was deceptive.** From peak (145,300 in 1988) to bankruptcy (2012) was 24 years — but the real collapse was compressed into ~9 years (2003-2012) once digital crossed the quality threshold. The 1990s decline felt like normal corporate restructuring; the 2000s decline was catastrophic and irreversible. (RBJ; Kodak bankruptcy filings)

## Known Data Gaps

- **Year-by-year Kodak global headcount 1989-1998:** Kodak 10-K filings exist but SEC EDGAR pages returned 403 errors during research. The 1988 peak and 1999 figure of 80,650 are confirmed, but intervening years are interpolated.
- **BLS historical wage data for SOC 51-9151:** Archived BLS OES pages for 2002-2015 returned 403 errors. The occupation's median wage, employment counts, and historical trend data could not be directly accessed. Employment decline of 84.9% (2007-2020) is confirmed from BLS publications but individual-year data points are missing.
- **Individual worker income tracking:** No panel data following specific displaced Kodak workers over time. Income trajectory section relies on regional averages and journalistic accounts rather than longitudinal studies.
- **Kodak-specific average compensation by year:** SEC filings report total compensation expense but not per-worker averages in a consistent format across the full timeline.
- **Rochester MSA unemployment rate time series:** FRED data exists but the page returned 403 during research. The general pattern (Rochester lagging national recovery) is confirmed from multiple secondary sources.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium-high
- **Workforce numbers:** high — Kodak peak (145,300) and post-2010 figures are well-documented from SEC filings and business journalism; mid-period (1990s-2000s) has gaps but the trajectory is clear.
- **Income data:** low — No direct BLS wage series was accessible; Kodak compensation estimates are assembled from journalistic accounts and current salary data rather than historical wage surveys.
- **Where-they-went:** medium — Rochester's economic transformation is well-documented at the sector level (healthcare/education replacing manufacturing), but individual-level tracking of displaced workers doesn't exist. Percentage allocations are informed estimates based on sector employment growth data.
