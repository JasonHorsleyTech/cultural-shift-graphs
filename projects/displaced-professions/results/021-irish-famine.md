# Irish Potato Blight Farm Laborers

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Cottiers and agricultural laborers (smallholders under 5 acres)
- **Country/Region:** Ireland (all island, with worst impact in western and southern counties)
- **Peak Year:** 1845 (immediately pre-blight; 1841 census is the measured peak)
- **Peak Workforce:** ~1,000,000 (596,000 cottiers + 408,000 smallholders in 1841 Census) (Irish Census 1841; Fitzsimons, "Tracing Landless Labourers")
- **Primary Mechanism:** discrete_shock
- **Cause Tags:** environmental, resource
- **Killing Event:** Phytophthora infestans destroyed the potato crop — the sole food source for the cottier and laborer class — across consecutive seasons 1845–1849, killing ~1 million and driving ~2 million to emigrate within a decade.

## Workforce Timeline

The "workforce" here is the cottier/laborer class — those holding under 5 acres or landless, who depended on potato subsistence. Farm holdings data tracks their disappearance more reliably than occupation counts.

| Year | Workforce | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| 1841 | ~1,004,000 (596K cottiers + 408K smallholders) | Irish Census 1841 |
| 1845 | ~1,000,000 (estimated, pre-blight) | Extrapolation from 1841 Census |
| 1847 | ~700,000 (estimated — "Black '47" peak mortality and emigration) | Mokyr (1983); Ó Gráda (1999) |
| 1851 | ~300,000 (farms under 5 acres dropped from 45% to 15% of all holdings) | Irish Census 1851; Wesley Johnston |
| 1861 | ~200,000 (estimated — continued emigration, further consolidation) | Irish Census 1861 |
| 1871 | ~150,000 (estimated — decline continued, total holdings fell steadily) | Irish Census 1871 |
| 1881 | ~100,000 (estimated — cottier class functionally extinct as a category) | Irish Census 1881 |

**Note:** Exact occupational counts for the cottier class are only available from the 1841 Census. Post-famine estimates are derived from farm holding size distributions (the percentage of holdings under 5 acres) and overall population decline. The 1851 figure is the most reliable post-famine data point: farms under 5 acres fell from 45% to 15% of all holdings, representing roughly a 67% absolute decline given that total holdings also shrank.

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1845
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~2 (by 1847 — mass death and emigration had removed ~250,000)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** ~4 (by ~1849 — cumulative deaths, emigration, and eviction)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** ~6 (by 1851 — Census confirms ~70% reduction in small holdings)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** ~30 (by ~1875 — continued emigration and consolidation)
- **Decline Shape:** cliff (initial 1845–1851 collapse was near-vertical, followed by long tail of continued emigration)
- **Current Status:** extinct (the cottier/laborer class no longer exists as an occupational category; Irish agriculture is now mechanized large-scale farming)

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: unavailable

As flagged in plan.md, income data for pre-famine Irish subsistence farmers is near-meaningless. The cottier class operated largely outside the cash economy — they grew potatoes for food and paid rent in labor. Wage data exists for the minority who did occasional cash work.

- **Income at Peak:** 6d/day average when cash work available (~£7–8/year at ~250 working days, but most cottiers only had intermittent cash work) in 1841 (Fitzsimons; Ó Gráda)
- **Income at Trough:** 0 (starvation/death) or 8d–10d/day on public works (below subsistence for a family) in 1847 (Parliamentary Papers)
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** N/A — the cottier class did not recover; those who survived transitioned to different economic roles
- **Inflation Adjustment Method:** N/A — subsistence economy makes monetary comparison meaningless

**Population/emigration as severity proxy (per plan.md guidance):**

## Surplus Capture

- **Capital owners:** 60% — Landlords benefited enormously from farm consolidation. Holdings over 15 acres went from 19% to 51% of all farms between 1841 and 1851. Livestock exports to Britain quadrupled from 200,000 animals/year (1840s) to 800,000 (1900s). The shift from tillage to pasture was more profitable per acre but required far fewer workers.
- **Consumers:** 25% — British consumers benefited from cheap Irish food exports (grain, livestock, dairy). Food continued to be exported from Ireland throughout the famine.
- **Adjacent workers:** 5% — Remaining agricultural workers on larger farms had somewhat more stable employment, but wages remained low. The shift benefited skilled graziers over unskilled laborers.
- **Government/public:** 10% — The British Treasury spent ~£8 million on famine relief but recovered much through Poor Law taxes on Irish ratepayers. The Encumbered Estates Act (1849) facilitated land sales that generated stamp duties and legal fees.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
1. **The grazier class** — Larger farmers (30+ acre holdings) who had already begun shifting from tillage to pastoral farming before the famine accelerated the transition. Families like the Scullys in Tipperary consolidated land during the famine at distressed prices and became wealthy cattle ranchers. Their pre-existing capital and diversified agriculture (not solely potato-dependent) let them survive and expand.
2. **Early emigrants** — Those who emigrated in 1845–46, before the worst of the famine and before the "coffin ship" era, had better outcomes. Passage cost ~55–100 shillings depending on destination. Families who pooled resources and left early established footholds that later arrivals used as support networks in American cities.

### False Movers
1. **Tenants who borrowed to pay rent in 1845–46** — Expecting the blight to be a single bad year (the 1845 crop was only partially affected), many cottiers borrowed at usurious rates to pay rent and hold their plots. When the 1846 crop failed completely, they had both no food and crushing debt, making their eviction and destitution worse.
2. **Public works scheme participants** — The government's public works program (peak: 715,000 employed, March 1847) paid 8d–10d/day for stone-breaking and road-building, but this was below subsistence wages for a family. Workers weakened by hunger died on the job. The "reasonable pivot" — work for wages rather than starve — failed because the wages couldn't buy enough food at famine-inflated prices.

### Deniers
1. **Charles Trevelyan** (Secretary of the Treasury, administrator of famine relief) described the famine as "a direct stroke of an all-wise and all-merciful Providence" and "the sharp but effectual remedy by which the cure is likely to be effected." He opposed closing ports to prevent food exports, writing: "We beg of you not to countenance in any way the idea of prohibiting exportation."
2. **The prevailing laissez-faire ideology** — British officials and economists argued that market interference would make things worse. The Times of London editorialized that the Irish had brought the famine on themselves. This "denial" wasn't ignorance of the crisis but ideological refusal to intervene adequately.

### Survival Pattern
Those who survived had at least one of: capital (even small), farm size above subsistence threshold (~15+ acres), geographic mobility (means to emigrate), or social networks outside the affected class.

## Unique Texture

1. Ireland's 1841 population of 8.18 million has never been reached again — 180+ years later, the entire island has only ~7.0 million people. No other event in this dataset produced a demographic wound that lasted nearly two centuries. (Irish Census; CSO)

2. Ireland was a net food exporter throughout the famine. An average of £100,000 worth of food was exported monthly while people starved. Armed escorts accompanied food shipments to ports to prevent seizure by the starving populace. (Donnelly, "The Great Irish Potato Famine," 2001; UK Parliamentary Papers)

3. Farm holdings under 5 acres went from 45% to 15% of all Irish farm holdings in a single decade (1841–1851) — the most extreme land consolidation event in modern European history, achieved through mass death and eviction rather than market forces. (Irish Census 1841, 1851)

4. The Choctaw Nation, themselves survivors of the Trail of Tears just 14 years earlier, sent $170 (equivalent to several thousand dollars today) to Irish famine relief in 1847. This act of solidarity between displaced peoples is commemorated by the "Kindred Spirits" sculpture in Midleton, County Cork, unveiled in 2017. (Smithsonian National Museum of the American Indian)

5. Workhouse deaths rose from 14,662 in 1846 to 68,890 in 1848 — a nearly 5x increase — as institutions designed for ~100,000 capacity were forced to hold 932,000 inmates. (Parliamentary Papers; Ó Gráda)

## Known Data Gaps

- **Individual cottier/laborer counts post-1841:** The 1841 Census is the only one that enumerated the cottier class specifically. Post-famine censuses used different occupational categories, making direct comparison impossible. Farm holding size is the best available proxy.
- **Cash income data:** Cottiers operated largely outside the cash economy. The 6d/day figure represents occasional hired work, not annual earnings. No reliable income series exists for this class.
- **Mortality by occupation:** The ~1 million death figure is for the general population. No breakdown by occupational class exists, though the cottier and laborer class suffered disproportionately (concentrated in western counties with highest mortality).
- **Individual-level tracking of emigrants:** Passenger lists exist but are incomplete. Tracking individual cottiers from Irish farms to American occupations is not systematically possible.
- **Exact emigration by year and destination before 1851:** Pre-famine emigration statistics are estimates; systematic record-keeping began mid-famine.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium
- **Workforce numbers:** medium — 1841 Census data is solid; post-famine estimates rely on farm holding proxies and population data rather than direct occupational counts
- **Income data:** low — subsistence economy makes monetary income data near-meaningless; population loss is the better severity metric as noted in plan.md
- **Where-they-went:** medium-high — emigration and mortality figures are well-studied by multiple scholars; destination breakdown is supported by US/UK census data on Irish-born population
