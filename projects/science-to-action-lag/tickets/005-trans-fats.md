# Ticket 005: Trans Fats → Cardiovascular Disease

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that artificial trans fats increase cardiovascular disease risk to binding regulation banning or restricting trans fats in food.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — First scientific suggestion that trans fats increase coronary disease risk. The 1956 suggestion by Kummerow is early. Renewed scrutiny in early 1990s — Mensink & Katan (1990), Willett et al. (1993). When did the evidence become credible (not just suggestive)?

2. **Consensus date** — When did a major health body formally state trans fats are harmful? EFSA opinion (2004)? WHO recommendation? Look for the earliest formal consensus statement from a recognized body.

3. **First OECD regulation** — Denmark effectively banned artificial trans fats in 2003 (Executive Order No. 160, limiting industrial trans fats to 2% of total fat). This is the first OECD ban. Confirm date and scope.

4. **US regulation** — FDA labeling mandate 2003 (effective 2006). FDA "not GRAS" determination 2015. Effective ban 2018 (compliance deadline extended to 2019). The US trajectory is a long ramp from labeling to ban.

5. **EU regulation** — EU limit of 2g per 100g of fat in 2021. Individual member states acted earlier.

6. **Industry opposition** — Food industry resisted bans but less aggressively than tobacco/lead. Reformulation was feasible (switch to other oils). Moderate opposition.

7. **Substitute availability** — Yes. Other vegetable oils and fully hydrogenated fats were commercially viable substitutes.

## Where to look

1. WHO report on trans fats (REPLACE program)
2. FDA regulatory history for trans fat labeling and GRAS determination
3. Denmark's Executive Order No. 160 (2003)
4. PubMed — "trans fat cardiovascular disease history" and "trans fat regulation"
5. Mozaffarian et al. (2006) — comprehensive review of trans fat health effects

## What "good data" looks like

- Clear distinction between the 1956 early evidence (suggestive) and 1990s evidence (convincing) — document both with rationale for which to use
- Regulation dates that distinguish between labeling requirements (not a ban), concentration limits (Denmark model), and outright GRAS revocation (US model)
- Confirmation that Denmark 2003 is the first binding OECD regulation, not just the first voluntary guideline

## Known pitfalls

- The lag swings wildly depending on whether you start from 1956 (~47 years to Denmark) or early 1990s (~10 years). This is exactly the kind of judgment call that needs to be documented. Use the 1990s as "early evidence" (when convincing studies emerged) and note the 1956 paper as historical context.
- US FDA labeling requirement (2006) is NOT a ban — it's disclosure. The first binding US restriction is the 2018 ban. Don't confuse these.
