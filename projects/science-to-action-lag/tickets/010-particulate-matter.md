# Ticket 010: Particulate Matter (PM2.5) → Respiratory / Cardiovascular Disease

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that fine particulate matter (PM2.5) causes respiratory and cardiovascular disease to binding air quality standards for PM2.5.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — General air pollution health effects were documented from the London Great Smog (1952, ~12,000 deaths) and Donora, PA smog event (1948). But PM2.5 SPECIFICALLY (fine particles under 2.5 micrometers) emerged as a distinct concern from the Harvard Six Cities Study (Dockery et al., 1993). When did the evidence specifically implicating fine particles become credible?

2. **Consensus date** — When did a major body formally state PM2.5 causes disease? WHO guidelines? EPA scientific advisory? The distinction between total suspended particulates (TSP), PM10, and PM2.5 matters — PM2.5 is the most harmful fraction.

3. **First OECD regulation** — The US set the first PM2.5 National Ambient Air Quality Standard (NAAQS) in 1997. Were other OECD countries earlier? Earlier regulations addressed "particulates" or PM10 but not PM2.5 specifically.

4. **US regulation** — EPA PM2.5 NAAQS set 1997, tightened 2006, 2012, 2024. The original standard is the first binding regulation.

5. **Broader context** — The US Clean Air Act (1970) addressed air pollution broadly, including particulate matter (as TSP). The PM2.5-specific science and regulation came much later. How to handle this layering?

6. **Industry opposition** — Industry groups (particularly power generation, mining) fought PM2.5 standards. The American Trucking Associations challenged the 1997 standard (Supreme Court upheld EPA's authority in 2001). Moderate to strong opposition.

7. **50% exposure reduction** — US PM2.5 concentrations have declined substantially since 1999. EPA Air Quality data tracks trends.

## Where to look

1. EPA NAAQS history for particulate matter standards
2. Dockery et al. (1993) — Harvard Six Cities Study
3. Pope et al. (2002) — American Cancer Society study confirming PM2.5 mortality link
4. WHO Air Quality Guidelines (2005, 2021)
5. Supreme Court — Whitman v. American Trucking Associations (2001)

## What "good data" looks like

- Clear distinction between evidence for AIR POLLUTION generally (1940s-1950s smog events) and PM2.5 SPECIFICALLY (1990s). The metric should use PM2.5-specific evidence because that's what the regulation targets.
- If using PM2.5-specific evidence (~1993) and PM2.5-specific regulation (1997), the lag is only ~4 years — an unusually fast response. This may be because earlier general particulate regulations paved the way.
- Air quality improvement data showing actual PM2.5 reduction trends

## Known pitfalls

- The 1970 Clean Air Act addressed particulates broadly but didn't distinguish PM2.5 from coarser particles. Using 1970 as the regulation date would mean the regulation PRECEDED the PM2.5-specific science. Use PM2.5-specific milestones.
- PM2.5 standards keep tightening as science shows harm at lower levels (no safe threshold). Use the FIRST standard, not the most recent.
- Category: exposure-limit (ambient air quality standard).
