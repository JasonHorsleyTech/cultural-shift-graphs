# Ticket 022: Elevator Operators

## Event
The displacement of elevator operators by automatic elevators, ~1950s–1970s in the US. A gradual erosion accelerated by the 1945 NYC elevator operators' strike, which demonstrated that buildings could function (briefly) without them and motivated investment in automatic systems.

## Mechanism
**Gradual erosion** (tech_tool)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track US elevator operator employment from ~1940 to ~1980:

- Peak: estimates vary widely. Some sources cite ~100,000 in the US by the 1940s (verify). NYC alone had thousands.
- Key dates: Otis Autotronic elevator (1948), NYC elevator strike (1945 and 1957), most new buildings automatic by 1960s
- The transition was building-by-building — operators in existing buildings persisted long after new buildings went automatic
- By the 1980s: essentially extinct except as novelty (a few luxury buildings, historic hotels)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. US Census occupational data via IPUMS (look for "elevator operator" or "elevator tender")
2. BLS historical occupational data
3. Bernard, "Elevator Operators in New York City" (if academic studies exist)
4. New York Times and other newspaper reporting on elevator automation
5. Otis Elevator Company history / annual reports
6. Building Service Employees International Union (BSEIU, now SEIU) records

### Income Data
- Elevator operators were typically unionized in major cities (BSEIU)
- Track union wages from 1945–1970
- Compare to building maintenance/janitorial wages (the likely successor occupations within the building services category)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BSEIU/SEIU contract data
2. BLS wage data for building service occupations
3. Census earnings data

### Where They Went
- Building security / lobby attendant / doorman (stayed in the building, different role)
- Janitorial / maintenance work
- General service sector
- Parking garage attendants (similar skill level, similar hours)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS employment data for building services occupations
2. SEIU membership data
3. Census occupational transition data

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Operators who became building superintendents or security staff. Buildings that retained operators as "starters" (lobby personnel directing people to elevators) during the transition.
- **False movers:** The 1945 NYC strike was meant to improve conditions but instead demonstrated that buildings could function without operators, accelerating automation.
- **Deniers:** Resistance to automatic elevators on safety grounds ("passengers can't be trusted to operate elevators"). Building codes that required operators (eventually repealed).
- **Survival pattern:** Transition to other building service roles rather than leaving the building.

### Unique Texture
- The 1945 NYC elevator strike paralyzed the city — and inadvertently proved the technology was ready
- Elevator operators as the "last human in the machine" — they literally pushed buttons that a machine could push
- The safety perception: passengers initially feared automatic elevators. The "close door" button was partly psychological — many didn't actually do anything.
- The luxury residual: a handful of NYC buildings still employ operators as a prestige amenity
- The union dimension: BSEIU fought hard against automation but eventually pivoted to representing other building workers

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Census employment estimates at 2–3 points between 1940 and 1980
- NYC-specific data (where the concentration was highest)
- Timeline of automatic elevator adoption
- The 1945/1957 strike stories documented
- Wage data for operators vs. replacement occupations
