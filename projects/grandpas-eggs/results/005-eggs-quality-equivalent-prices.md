# Eggs — Quality-Equivalent Prices

## Summary

The quality-equivalent egg (Certified Humane Pasture-Raised, per the rubric in 003) did not exist as a retail category until ~2007-2009. Before ~1995, there was only one tier — the default egg was the quality-equivalent. Between 1995 and 2007, organic eggs served as the closest available proxy (they address feed and antibiotics but not necessarily pasture access). True pasture-raised eggs entered mainstream retail around 2009 when Vital Farms landed Whole Foods as its first major account. Prices have ranged from ~$5/dozen at market lows (2017) to ~$8-9/dozen at peaks (2023, 2025). Notably, pasture-raised egg prices are far less volatile than conventional eggs — during avian flu spikes, conventional prices surge toward pasture-raised levels while pasture-raised prices hold relatively steady. The premium over conventional has ranged from 2x to 5x depending on market conditions.

## Methodology

### Defining "quality-equivalent" across eras

The quality-equivalent product changed identity over time because the market did:

1. **Pre-1995:** No quality tiers. The standard egg *was* the quality-equivalent (hens had outdoor access through the 1950s-60s, then battery cages became universal). From ~1970-1995, the 1955-quality egg essentially disappeared from retail. Line A = Line B during this period, but both represent a *degraded* product vs. 1955.

2. **1995-2007:** Organic eggs served as the closest available proxy. USDA National Organic Program (2002) required organic feed and "access to outdoors," but the outdoor-access standard was weak. Organic addresses feed quality and antibiotics but not necessarily pasture access. Still, it was the best available retail alternative.

3. **2007-present:** Pasture-raised eggs (Certified Humane standard: 108 sq ft/bird outdoor pasture) are the true quality-equivalent per the rubric. Vital Farms (founded 2007, first retail 2009) pioneered the category. By ~2015, pasture-raised was a distinct retail category at major grocers.

### Price data approach

- **1950-1990:** BLS Average Price series (APU0000708111) for Grade A Large eggs. This is the only product available — it IS both Line A and Line B.
- **1995-2006:** Organic egg prices estimated from USDA ERS organic premium data (66%-173% over conventional) applied to BLS conventional prices. Limited hard data for this period.
- **2007-2014:** Academic research (Lusk et al. via ResearchGate) provides organic and noncage prices. Pasture-raised data is sparse — Vital Farms was in limited distribution. Used organic as proxy for 2007-2012, transitioning to pasture-raised estimates for 2013-2014 as the category matured.
- **2015-2024:** Scratch & Peck Feeds decade review provides premium egg price ranges. Cross-referenced with USDA AMS Egg Markets Overview reports for specific tier pricing in 2023+.
- **2025-2026:** USDA AMS Egg Markets Overview reports provide granular tier-by-tier advertised retail prices.

### Key structural breaks

- **2015 HPAI outbreak:** >50M birds culled. All egg prices spiked. Premium eggs reached $5-$7/dozen.
- **2022-2023 HPAI outbreak:** Worst in US history. >58M commercial hens lost. Conventional prices spiked to near-pasture-raised levels. This temporarily *compressed* the quality-equivalent premium — conventional hit $4.82/dozen in Jan 2023 while pasture-raised held at ~$5-$7.
- **Price convergence events:** During avian flu crises, the gap between Line A and Line B narrows dramatically because conventional supply drops while pasture-raised supply (smaller flocks, different biosecurity) is less affected. NBC News (Feb 2025): "Pasture-raised egg prices have held steady while conventional egg costs explode."

The premium ratio is highly volatile because conventional prices swing wildly while pasture-raised prices are relatively sticky. In stable markets, pasture-raised costs 3-4x conventional. During supply crises, the gap compresses to 1.5-2x.

## Known gaps

- **1995-2003:** No systematic retail price data for organic or premium eggs. The USDA AMS organic poultry report started in January 2004. Prices before this are estimates based on later-observed premium structures.
- **2007-2014 pasture-raised:** Pasture-raised was not tracked as a distinct category by USDA during this period. Vital Farms and similar brands existed but in limited distribution. Prices are estimated from organic data and industry reports.
- **The BLS APU series does not break out by tier.** It tracks "Grade A, Large" eggs as a weighted average across all production methods. Since conventional is ~80% of the market, this series effectively tracks conventional prices.
- **Advertised vs. actual retail prices.** USDA AMS "Egg Markets Overview" tracks advertised (promotional) prices at major retailers, which may be lower than everyday shelf prices. Actual average retail prices for pasture-raised may be $1-$2 higher than advertised prices.
- **Regional variation is enormous.** Pasture-raised eggs at a NYC Whole Foods may be $9-$10/dozen while the same product at a Texas H-E-B is $5-$6. National averages smooth this out.
