# Human Computers

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Human computer (mathematical calculator)
- **Country/Region:** United States, United Kingdom
- **Peak Year:** 1945
- **Peak Workforce:** ~2,000–3,000 across US facilities (estimated aggregate; see breakdown below) (Grier 2005, NASA historical records, various institutional records)
- **Primary Mechanism:** tipping_point
- **Cause Tags:** tech_platform
- **Killing Event:** Electronic digital computers (ENIAC 1945, UNIVAC 1951, IBM mainframes late 1950s) rendered hand calculation obsolete within ~15 years of their introduction.

## Workforce Timeline

No single source tracks total human computer employment across all US/UK facilities. The profession was distributed across military, aerospace, census, insurance, and academic institutions, often under different job titles ("mathematician," "clerk," "calculator"). The following timeline aggregates the best-documented facilities to reconstruct the arc.

**Major facility-level data:**
- NACA Langley: 5 (1935) → 75 (1942) → ~400 (1946) → declining through 1960s (NASA History Office)
- Aberdeen Proving Ground/BRL: ~100 women computers during WWII (Army historical records)
- Moore School (UPenn): ~200 women computing ballistics tables during WWII (Army/IEEE records)
- Mathematical Tables Project (WPA): ~120 computers + 450 clerks, 1938–1948 (Grier 2005)
- JPL: ~28+ documented in 1953 computing group photo (NASA JPL archives)
- Los Alamos: Small teams, ~5–6 in T-5 group; supplemented by IBM machines from late 1943 (Nuclear Museum)
- Bletchley Park (UK): 273 women operating Colossus machines; ~7,500 women total at Bletchley (75% of workforce), though most were not "computers" in the strict mathematical sense (Bletchley Park Trust)

**Aggregate US estimate rationale:** At WWII peak (~1944–1945), the known major facilities account for ~800–1,000 human computers. Adding smaller installations (other military proving grounds, insurance companies, observatories, university computing offices, the Census Bureau), an aggregate of 2,000–3,000 is a defensible order-of-magnitude estimate. Grier (2005) describes computing as an established profession with "large computing offices" at dozens of institutions by the 1940s.

|------|-----------|--------|

| 1970 | 0 | Extinct |

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1946 (post-war drawdown + ENIAC operational)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~4 (by 1949, post-war cuts and early electronic computers)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** ~7 (by 1952, UNIVAC at Census Bureau, IBM installations spreading)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** ~12 (by 1957, IBM mainframes widespread)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** ~15 (by 1960)
- **Decline Shape:** s_curve (slow initial decline 1946–1950 as post-war drawdowns happened gradually, then accelerating through 1950s as electronic computers proliferated, then flattening at the bottom with a small remnant through early 1960s)
- **Current Status:** extinct

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: estimated

Human computers were federal civil servants or held equivalent institutional positions. Direct pay data comes from NACA personnel records and federal pay scales.

- **Income at Peak:** $1,440–$3,200 USD/year in 1945 (SP-3 Junior Computer at $1,440; SP-8 Chief Computer at $3,200) (NASA History Office — Langley records)
- **Income at Trough:** N/A — profession went extinct; workers transitioned to other roles
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** ~110–150% for those who transitioned to programming; those who left the workforce saw 0% recovery
- **Inflation Adjustment Method:** CPI (BLS historical CPI series)

Context: A junior engineer at NACA started at $2,600/year — nearly double the SP-3 computer rate of $1,440, despite computers often having comparable education. The SP-3 rate of $1,440/year in 1945 is roughly $24,400 in 2024 dollars. By 1969, female computer specialists (the successor role) earned a median of $7,763/year, while male computer specialists earned $11,193 — a 44% gender gap persisted through the transition (US Census/BLS data).

At Harvard Observatory in the 1880s–1900s, human computers earned $0.25/hour (~$520/year), and some women volunteered without pay (Harvard Observatory records).

Note: The income trajectory is misleading in isolation because the "survivors" who stayed in computation transitioned to higher-graded programmer/analyst roles. The profession didn't experience wage decline — it experienced job-title extinction with upward reclassification for those who adapted.

## Surplus Capture

- **Capital owners:** 30% — Government agencies and corporations captured massive computation cost savings. A single IBM 704 (1954) could do the work of hundreds of human computers. Hardware vendors (IBM, Remington Rand) captured enormous value selling machines.
- **Consumers:** 40% — Faster computation enabled the space program, nuclear weapons development, weather prediction, census processing — broad public benefits. Electronic computers were ~1,000x faster than human computers for many calculations.
- **Adjacent workers:** 25% — The new programmer/analyst class benefited from higher wages and more interesting work. Electronic computing created far more jobs than it destroyed — the computing workforce expanded dramatically from thousands to millions by the 1970s.
- **Government/public:** 5% — Tax revenue from the growing computing industry; reduced cost of government computation.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
1. **Dorothy Vaughan** (NACA/NASA, 1949–1971): As supervisor of the West Area Computers, Vaughan saw electronic computers coming and taught herself FORTRAN before it was required. She then taught her entire team, ensuring they could transition to the new Analysis and Computation Division when NACA became NASA in 1958. Her team was absorbed intact because they were already fluent in the language of the new machines.

2. **Betty Holberton** (BRL → Remington Rand → NBS, 1945–1983): One of the original six ENIAC programmers, Holberton moved directly from human computing to programming ENIAC, then helped develop UNIVAC, and later contributed to the development of COBOL and FORTRAN standards. She turned displacement into a 40-year career at the frontier of the replacement technology.

### False Movers
1. **UK women who retrained as general clerks:** After WWII, many British women who had operated Colossus and Bombe machines at Bletchley Park were forced into redundancy. Some retrained as general administrative clerks — a reasonable-seeming move that meant they missed the computing revolution they had helped start. The Official Secrets Act prevented them from even discussing their relevant experience.

2. **Jean Bartik's departure (1951):** After pioneering stored-program computing on ENIAC and contributing to UNIVAC, Bartik left the field when Remington Rand's acquisition made conditions intolerable. She spent 16 years out of the workforce raising children. When she returned in the mid-1960s, mini-computers had transformed the industry and she had to restart at a lower level. Her departure was individually rational but a loss compared to peers who stayed.

### Deniers
At Los Alamos in late 1943, Richard Feynman organized a competition between human computers (T-5 group) and IBM tabulating machines (T-6 group). The human computers lost because "the machines, unlike people, did not get tired after multiple shifts." Some human computers initially believed their intuitive understanding of calculations would keep them relevant — that machines could crunch numbers but couldn't replicate the judgment a human computer brought to spotting errors and anomalies. The machines proved them wrong within months.

### Survival Pattern
Survivors learned to program the machine that replaced them — the most direct "become the operator of the thing that killed your old job" story in any displacement event.

## Unique Texture

1. **The kilogirl:** In the early 1940s, a member of the Applied Mathematics Panel coined "kilogirl" as a unit of computing power — approximately 1,000 hours of human computing labor. The term was used without irony in technical discussions. A single IBM 704 (1954) had the computing power of roughly 100–200 kilogirls. (Grier 2005)

2. **The gender flip:** In 1945, human computing was overwhelmingly female — "women's work" that male engineers considered beneath them. By 1970, computer programming was overwhelmingly male and considered prestigious. The same intellectual activity underwent a complete gender reversal as it gained status and pay. In 1969, female computer specialists earned a median of $7,763 vs. $11,193 for men — a 44% gap in the profession women had essentially founded. (US Census Bureau; Light 1999)

3. **They displaced themselves:** The six women who programmed ENIAC — Kay McNulty, Betty Jennings, Betty Snyder, Marlyn Wescoff, Fran Bilas, and Ruth Lichterman — were selected from a pool of ~100 women already employed as human computers at the BRL. They literally programmed the machine that made their previous job obsolete, and in doing so invented subroutines, nesting, and other fundamental programming techniques. (IEEE; Army historical records)

4. **Speed of replacement at Los Alamos:** When Feynman's IBM machines competed against human computers in late 1943, the machines won on endurance, not speed per operation. But by 1950, electronic computers were ~1,000x faster per operation. The gap went from marginal to astronomical in 7 years. (Nuclear Museum)

5. **The Bletchley silence:** 273 women who operated Colossus at Bletchley Park — arguably the world's first electronic computers — were bound by the Official Secrets Act and could not mention their computing experience for decades. Many left the field entirely because they couldn't explain their most relevant qualifications. Britain's early lead in computing was partly squandered because its most experienced operators were legally prohibited from applying their knowledge. The secrecy wasn't lifted until the 1970s. (Bletchley Park Trust; Hicks 2017)

## Known Data Gaps

- **Total workforce count is an estimate, not a measurement.** No single source tracks all human computers across all US institutions. The 2,000–3,000 peak figure aggregates facility-level data with generous inference for undocumented sites. The true number could be 1,500 or 5,000 — the order of magnitude is right but precision is impossible.
- **Pre-GS pay scales are sparse.** The SP (Specialist) classification used before 1949 has limited surviving documentation. The SP-3/SP-8 figures come from NACA Langley records and may not reflect pay at other agencies.
- **Individual-level career tracking is anecdotal.** The "where they went" percentages are reconstructed from institutional records and concurrent labor force trends, not from a panel study tracking individual human computers. The famous names (Johnson, Vaughan, Holberton) are survivors who stayed — we know less about those who left.
- **UK data is harder to aggregate** because of Bletchley Park secrecy, different civil service structures, and the systematic purge of women from computing roles post-war.
- **Insurance and private-sector human computers** are almost entirely undocumented. The profession existed in actuarial and financial settings but left few records.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium
- **Workforce numbers:** low-medium — Aggregate is an informed estimate from facility-level data; no census or BLS category existed for "human computer" as an occupation.
- **Income data:** medium — Federal pay scales are documented but the SP-3/SP-8 range comes from one facility (NACA Langley); private-sector pay is unknown.
- **Where-they-went:** medium — The programming transition is well-documented for famous individuals and NACA/NASA specifically, but the full workforce distribution is inferred from labor force trends, not tracked.
