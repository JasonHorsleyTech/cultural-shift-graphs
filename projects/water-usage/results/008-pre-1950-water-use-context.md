# Pre-1950 Water Use Context

## Summary

Before USGS began systematic national water-use reporting in 1950, the US underwent a dramatic transformation from hand-carried well water to pressurized municipal systems serving millions. Per-capita municipal water use exploded from ~10 gal/day (hand-carried from wells) to 60+ gal/day once piped water arrived, to 140+ gal/day as indoor plumbing spread — all before 1900. Total national water withdrawal likely grew from roughly 50 Bgal/day in 1900 to 170 Bgal/day in 1950, driven by irrigation expansion in the West and the spread of indoor plumbing nationwide.

**The pattern:** Hand-carried water ≈ 5-10 gal/person/day. Add a public well or standpipe ≈ 15-25. Add piped water to the building ≈ 40-60. Add flush toilets and bathtubs ≈ 100-150. Add lawn watering, washing machines, and unmetered waste ≈ 200-300. Peak consumption in Chicago hit 288 gal/person/day in 1930 before metering and efficiency brought it back down.

**Why this matters for water use:** Flush toilets are the single biggest driver of residential water consumption growth. A flush toilet uses 3-7 gallons per flush, adding 20-40 gal/person/day. When 80% of homes lacked flush toilets in 1920, residential per-capita water withdrawal was a fraction of 1950 levels.

**Key context:** Irrigated acreage grew 7x from 1890 to 1950 (3.6M to 25M acres). Almost all early growth was private — the Bureau of Reclamation handled only ~5-13% of total irrigated acreage even by mid-century. Mormon settlers in Utah (1847) pioneered organized western irrigation; the Reclamation Act of 1902 federalized it.

| 1910 | ~60-70 | Low | Irrigation alone roughly doubled (7.5M to 13.7M acres); industrial growth rapid | Derived |

| 1950 | 170 | High | First USGS systematic estimate | USGS Circular 115 (1951) |

**Derivation note:** Picton (1952) estimated US water use from 1900 to 1950 — the earliest known attempt at a national historical series. The USDA Forest Service reported that from 1900 to 1940, "population increased by roughly 1.7 million persons per year while withdrawals increased by about 2.4 billion gallons per day per year." Working backward from the 1950 USGS baseline of 170 Bgal/day, and accounting for accelerated growth during the 1940s war-industrial buildup, the 1900 figure was likely in the 40-50 Bgal/day range. These are order-of-magnitude estimates, not measured values.

## Key Findings

1. **Per-capita water use scaled directly with infrastructure, not population.** NYC designed the Croton Aqueduct for 22 gal/person/day but actual use hit 60 within 17 years. Chicago went from 33 to 144 gal/person/day in 26 years (1856-1882) as indoor plumbing spread. The pattern is universal: build the infrastructure, and consumption follows.

2. **Flush toilets were the single biggest inflection point for residential water use.** In 1920, 80% of US homes lacked flush toilets. By 1950, 71% had them. Each toilet added 20-40 gal/person/day. This adoption curve — not population growth — drove the residential water use explosion between 1920 and 1960.

3. **Irrigation grew 7x in 60 years (1890-1950), almost entirely through private initiative.** The Reclamation Act of 1902 gets the historical credit, but even by mid-century the Bureau of Reclamation accounted for only ~13% of total irrigated acreage. Private farmers drove the expansion.

4. **The US likely tripled or quadrupled its total water withdrawal between 1900 and 1950** — from roughly 40-50 Bgal/day to 170 Bgal/day. This growth was split roughly equally between irrigation expansion and industrial/municipal growth.

5. **The "explosion" part of the story is real.** For most of human history, a person used 5-10 gallons of water per day. By 1950, the average American was responsible for over 1,100 gallons per day (170 Bgal/day ÷ 151M population) when you include agricultural and industrial use. That's a 100-200x increase in per-capita water mobilization within a century — one of the most dramatic resource-use inflections in human history.
