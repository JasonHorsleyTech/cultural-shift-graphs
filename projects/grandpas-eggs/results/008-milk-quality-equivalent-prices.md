# Milk — Quality-Equivalent Prices

## Summary

The quality-equivalent for milk is **organic grass-fed whole milk** (matching the 1955 baseline of pasture-based, hormone-free, minimally processed whole milk — see rubric 006). The price series has three distinct eras: (1) before divergence (~pre-1997), quality-equivalent = conventional (same price, covered by ticket 007); (2) 1997–2010, standard organic milk was the closest available proxy since grass-fed didn't exist as a retail category; (3) 2011–present, 100% grass-fed organic whole milk became the true quality-equivalent when Organic Valley launched Grassmilk nationally.

The trajectory: organic milk went from an estimated ~$5.50/gallon in the late 1990s (natural food stores only, ~110% premium over conventional) to ~$7.60/gallon by 2008 as it entered mainstream supermarkets. When grass-fed organic entered the market in 2011–2012, the quality-equivalent price jumped to ~$10.00/gallon and has since climbed to ~$14.00/gallon by 2025. A consumer in 2025 pays roughly $14/gallon for milk that matches the nutritional and production profile of what cost ~$2.60/gallon (conventional) in 1997 — before quality tiers existed.

There is a **structural break in 2011** when the series transitions from standard organic (best available proxy) to grass-fed organic (true quality-equivalent). The graph phase should annotate this.

## Methodology

### Three-era approach

The quality-equivalent milk price series uses different data sources and products across three eras, reflecting the real-world evolution of the dairy market:

1. **Pre-1997:** No quality tiers existed at retail. The conventional product was the quality-equivalent. Prices reported in ticket 007.

2. **1997–2010 (standard organic as proxy):** Organic milk became available at mainstream retail beginning in the late 1990s. While standard organic does not perfectly match the 1955 baseline (USDA organic allows substantial grain feeding, which changes fatty acid profiles), it was the *closest available retail product* to 1955-style milk. For 1997–2005, organic prices are estimated from documented premium ratios applied to BLS conventional prices. For 2006–2010, USDA AMS retail organic milk surveys provide the base data.

3. **2011–present (grass-fed organic as true quality-equivalent):** Organic Valley Grassmilk launched nationally in 2011 as the first widely available 100% grass-fed organic milk. This matches the 1955 baseline on the key nutritional dimensions (omega-3 content, CLA levels, omega-6:omega-3 ratio) far better than standard organic. However, systematic retail price tracking does not exist for grass-fed organic specifically — USDA AMS tracks organic as a whole but does not break out grass-fed. Prices for 2011–2025 are based on limited actual retail observations supplemented by estimated premiums over tracked organic averages.

### Premium estimation

**1997–2005 organic premium over conventional:** Based on USDA ERS research using Nielsen supermarket scanner data, the national average organic whole milk premium was ~98% above conventional in 2004. Earlier years (1997–2003) likely had higher premiums (~105–110%) because organic was sold primarily through natural food stores with higher markups. By 2004–2005, mainstream grocery adoption had moderated premiums.

**2011–2025 grass-fed premium over standard organic:** Based on available data points:
- 2012: OV Grassmilk $5.00/hg vs organic avg ~$3.85/hg = ~30% premium
- 2013: OV Grassmilk $5.50/hg vs organic avg ~$3.82/hg = ~44% premium
- 2025: OV Grassmilk ~$7.00/hg (midpoint) vs organic avg $5.19/hg = ~35% premium

The grass-fed premium over standard organic fluctuates between ~30–50%, with ~40–45% as a reasonable central estimate. This premium has been relatively stable — grass-fed remains a niche product (~1% of fluid milk) with limited scale economies.

### Per-gallon conversion

Organic and grass-fed organic milk are sold predominantly in half-gallon (64 oz) containers. Per-gallon prices in the data table are calculated as 2× the half-gallon retail price. This slightly overstates the per-gallon cost versus a true gallon container (which would have a modest volume discount), but gallon containers are rare-to-nonexistent for grass-fed organic brands.

## Known Gaps

- **1997–2005 organic prices:** No systematic retail price tracking existed. Estimates rely on applying documented premium ratios to conventional prices. Actual prices varied enormously by region and retail channel (natural food store vs. early mainstream).
- **2006–2007 organic prices:** USDA AMS began tracking in January 2006, but the NODPA national average retail series begins with confirmed data at 2008. Estimates for 2006–2007 are interpolated.
- **2011–2025 grass-fed organic prices:** No government or industry body tracks grass-fed organic milk prices separately from standard organic. Only two confirmed retail price observations exist (2012 and 2013, from a single Whole Foods blog). All other grass-fed prices are estimated from standard organic base + premium ratio. Current (2025) prices are based on the quality rubric's survey of current retail.
- **Regional variation:** All prices are national averages. Grass-fed organic milk shows extreme regional variation — from ~$5.58/half gallon (Central Market store brand, Texas) to $7.99/half gallon (Maple Hill at Kroger, Northeast). The series uses a mid-range estimate appropriate for a national average.
- **The "silent degradation" gap (1955–1997):** The quality of conventional milk degraded substantially during this 40-year period (larger herds, confinement, grain-heavy diets) but there was no premium product available to purchase. The quality-equivalent was being *created as a concept* by the widening gap between what farms produced and what 1955 milk had been. This ticket cannot capture that hidden divergence — it only begins when a purchasable alternative appeared.
