# Prohibition-Era Alcohol Workers

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Brewers, distillers, bartenders, saloon keepers, coopers, bottlers, liquor retailers
- **Country/Region:** United States
- **Peak Year:** 1914 (pre-wartime restrictions and state dry laws)
- **Peak Workforce:** ~300,000–500,000 across all alcohol-related occupations (see breakdown below) (US Census 1910; Bureau of Internal Revenue)
- **Primary Mechanism:** discrete_shock
- **Cause Tags:** regulatory
- **Killing Event:** The 18th Amendment to the US Constitution banned the manufacture, sale, and transportation of alcoholic beverages, effective January 17, 1920.

### Peak workforce breakdown
The alcohol industry workforce spanned multiple occupational categories that Census data tracked separately:
- **Brewery/distillery manufacturing workers:** ~25,000–30,000 semi-skilled operatives (1910 Census manufacturing data, Perez et al.)
- **Liquor retail establishments:** 170,000+ liquor stores/saloons shut down (Bureau of Internal Revenue). At ~300,000 saloons nationwide (~1 per 150–200 Americans), bartenders and saloon staff likely numbered 200,000+.
- **Adjacent trades:** Coopers, truckers, bottlers, barrel-makers, glass manufacturers — uncounted but significant.
- The alcohol industry was described as the "fifth largest industry in the nation" before Prohibition (PBS/Ken Burns).

The 300,000–500,000 estimate encompasses production, distribution, retail, and closely adjacent trades. The core manufacturing workforce (breweries + distilleries) was ~25,000–30,000; the retail/service workforce was far larger.

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1916 (state-level dry laws began closing saloons; national Prohibition effective 1920)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~2 (1916–1918, state dry laws + wartime restrictions)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** ~4 (by 1918, wartime grain restrictions + state laws had halved the industry)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** ~6 (by 1920, Prohibition wiped out virtually all legal operations)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** ~6 (1920 — near-total elimination of legal alcohol employment)
- **Decline Shape:** cliff (the regulatory shock was phased over ~4 years of state laws, then completed instantly with the 18th Amendment)
- **Current Status:** This is the only displacement in the dataset that was **reversed**. The industry returned after repeal (1933) but in a consolidated form — 684 breweries in 1940 vs. 1,568 in 1910. The saloon as an institution never recovered; it was replaced by bars, cocktail lounges, and restaurants with liquor licenses.

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: estimated

Pre-Prohibition brewery workers were well-paid skilled laborers, often unionized. Income data during Prohibition is effectively meaningless for the legal economy (the profession ceased to exist), and post-repeal data is confounded by the Great Depression.

- **Income at Peak:** ~$19/week (~$988/year) for brewery workers circa 1910; laborers at $2.25/day (~$700/year) (Beer History / BLS historical bulletins)
- **Income at Trough:** $0 in legal alcohol work (1920–1933). Former alcohol workers were employed in "lower skilled, lower paying occupations" per Census-linked data (Perez et al.)
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** N/A — the profession was illegal for 13 years. By 1940 (7 years post-repeal, 20 years post-peak), former alcohol workers had returned to statistically equivalent occupational skill levels (Perez et al.)
- **Inflation Adjustment Method:** CPI (Historical Statistics of the United States)

**Context:** Average manufacturing wages in 1914 were ~$580/year (BLS). Brewery workers at ~$988/year earned roughly 70% above average manufacturing — these were premium blue-collar jobs, reflecting unionization and the skill required for brewing.

|------|--------------|---------------------|--------------|--------|
| 1910 | ~$988/yr | $988 (base) | 100% | Census / BLS bulletins |

Note: The 1940 "recovery" finding from Perez et al. measures occupational skill level, not specifically brewing wages. Many former alcohol workers had moved to other industries at equivalent skill/pay by 1940.

## Surplus Capture

This is an unusual case — the "efficiency gain" was not economic but moral/political (temperance movement). There was no genuine productivity surplus to distribute; instead, the displacement destroyed economic value.

- **Capital owners:** ~20% — Brewery owners who diversified (Anheuser-Busch, Coors, Pabst) preserved capital through alternative products. Many smaller owners lost everything.
- **Consumers:** ~0% negative — Consumers lost legal access to alcohol. Those who continued drinking paid black-market premiums (bootleg liquor was more expensive and often dangerous — ~1,000 Americans died annually from tainted liquor).
- **Adjacent workers:** ~15% — Workers in ice cream, soft drink, and alternative industries benefited from brewery conversions and reduced competition from saloons.
- **Government/public:** ~-65% — Massive net loss. The federal government lost $11 billion in tax revenue over 13 years while spending $300 million+ on enforcement. New York lost 75% of state revenue from liquor taxes. Organized crime captured most of the "surplus" — Al Capone alone earned an estimated $60M/year from bootlegging.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
1. **Anheuser-Busch** pivoted aggressively starting before Prohibition, launching Bevo (a non-alcoholic cereal beverage) in 1916, selling 2.2 million cases in six months. During Prohibition, they produced 25+ products including infant formula, frozen eggs, corn syrup, truck bodies, and even police vans for Prohibition enforcement officers. They invested $10 million in a bottling facility in 1918. When repeal came, they had 50,000 barrels ready for distribution on March 22, 1933.

2. **Coors Brewing Company** (Colorado had banned beer 4 years before national Prohibition) pivoted to malted milk for candy companies and soda fountains, and launched the Coors Porcelain Company, manufacturing tea sets, dinnerware, spark plugs, and lab equipment for Thomas Edison. CoorsTek still operates today as a standalone ceramics company — an entire business born from a brewery's survival strategy.

### False Movers
1. **Near-beer breweries** — Many breweries bet heavily on near beer (0.5% ABV), expecting it to sustain operations. By 1925, near beer was commercially dead — bootlegging made real beer readily available, and nobody wanted the legal substitute. Breweries that hadn't diversified beyond near beer were trapped.

2. **Miller Brewing Company** — Nearly failed during Prohibition despite producing near beer (Vivo), soft drinks, malted milk, and malt syrup. The family tried to sell the company in 1925 and couldn't find a buyer. They survived only through real estate sales and bond investments — essentially running down capital while waiting for repeal.

### Deniers
The brewing industry broadly assumed Prohibition would never actually pass (the constitutional amendment process seemed too difficult), and then after it passed, assumed it would be quickly repealed. They were right about repeal but catastrophically wrong about timing — it took 13 years. Many breweries didn't diversify because they expected to resume operations within 2–3 years.

### Survival Pattern
The breweries that survived Prohibition had two things in common: diversification into non-alcohol products using existing infrastructure (refrigeration → dairy, grain processing → food products), and sufficient capital reserves to sustain 13 years of reduced operations.

## Unique Texture

1. **This is the only profession in the dataset that came back from the dead.** After 13 years of total prohibition, the industry resumed — but in radically consolidated form. The 1,300+ pre-Prohibition breweries shrank to fewer than 100 survivors, then rebounded to only 756 by 1934. The industry returned; the small-brewery ecosystem did not (until the craft beer revolution 50+ years later). (EH.net)

2. **Bootlegging was 5% of US GDP.** The illegal alcohol economy commanded an estimated $3 billion by 1927 — creating a parallel economy of brewers, distillers, truckers, and bartenders doing the exact same jobs illegally. Organized crime essentially became the "successor industry." (SSRN / Britannica)

3. **Pharmacists tripled in New York during Prohibition.** The medicinal whiskey loophole was so lucrative that the number of registered pharmacists in New York State tripled. Walgreens expanded from 20 stores to 525 during the 1920s, in part thanks to medicinal alcohol sales. (PBS/Ken Burns; Wikipedia)

4. **Pabst sold 8 million pounds of cheese.** The Milwaukee brewery, unable to sell beer, manufactured "Pabst-ett" processed cheese spread — until Kraft sued them. Coors made porcelain. Anheuser-Busch built police vans. The survival strategies were wildly creative. (Mob Museum; History.com)

5. **Saloons never came back.** There were ~300,000 saloons in 1916. After repeal, they were replaced by bars, cocktail lounges, and restaurants with liquor licenses — different institutions with different economics and labor models. The job of "bartender" returned, but the all-male neighborhood saloon as a social institution was permanently gone. (Contemporary accounts; Mob Museum)

## Known Data Gaps

- **Total alcohol-related workforce:** No single source gives a reliable total across production, distribution, retail, and service sectors. The 300,000–500,000 estimate is assembled from separate brewery worker counts, saloon counts, and liquor store counts — not a single Census enumeration.
- **Bootlegging employment:** Inherently unmeasurable. The $3B/5% GDP figure suggests massive employment, but no reliable headcount exists for illegal workers.
- **Income during Prohibition for displaced workers:** The Perez et al. study tracks occupational skill level (a proxy for income), not actual wages. Individual-level income data for former alcohol workers during 1920–1933 does not exist.
- **Post-repeal wages vs. pre-Prohibition wages:** The 1940 Census was the first to collect individual income data. Pre-Prohibition income comes from union records and BLS surveys of establishments, not individual Census respondents. The two are not directly comparable.
- **Saloon employee counts:** The ~300,000 saloon figure is well-attested, but employees per saloon varied enormously (1–2 for a corner bar to dozens for a beer hall). Total saloon employment is estimated at 200,000+ but could be higher.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium
- **Workforce numbers:** medium — Brewery counts are solid (Census of Manufactures); total industry employment is assembled from multiple sources with different methodologies. The retail/service side (saloons, liquor stores) relies on establishment counts × estimated employees.
- **Income data:** low — Pre-Prohibition wage data exists for unionized brewery workers but not for the broader alcohol workforce. No income data exists during Prohibition (the legal profession didn't exist). Post-repeal recovery is measured by occupational skill proxy, not wages.
- **Where-they-went:** low-medium — The Perez et al. Census-linked study is the best available evidence for occupational outcomes, but it tracks only manufacturing workers identifiable in the 1910 Census. The massive bootlegging economy is acknowledged but unquantifiable. The 13-year gap makes individual tracking extremely difficult.
