# 008: Pagers / Beepers

**System:** Pagers (numeric and alphanumeric)
**Time range:** 1980s–2000s
**Expected data quality:** Low to moderate — niche system, shorter-lived, fewer studies

## Research task

Document the pager's brief lifecycle. Pagers are interesting as a system with a very short window of mainstream use that still managed to attract spam. Numeric spam to pagers was particularly problematic in Asia (Japan, China) in the 1990s, and some telemarketing via pager existed in the US.

## Key years to sample

- 1980 (pagers mostly used by doctors/emergency services)
- 1985 (pagers spreading to business professionals)
- 1990 (pagers becoming consumer devices, teenage culture)
- 1994 (peak US pager subscribers — ~61 million)
- 1997 (mobile phones beginning to displace pagers)
- 2000 (pager use declining sharply)
- 2005 (pagers mostly limited to healthcare)
- 2010 (near-extinct in consumer use)

## Data sources to check

- **CTIA** and wireless industry historical data
- **FCC records** on paging services
- **Japanese telecommunications studies** — pager spam was a documented problem in Japan
- **Trade publications** from the pager industry era
- **Academic papers** on pager usage patterns
- **Pew Research** early technology adoption surveys

## Dimensions to collect

1. **Spam percentage** — % of pages received that were unsolicited. Harder to quantify for US market. Better data likely exists for Japan.
2. **Total message volume** — total pages sent per year
3. **Primary users** — % of US population using pager as primary contact method
4. **Total users** — US pager subscribers per year
5. **User cost per message** — pager service monthly fee, cost per page
6. **User time per message** — time to send a numeric/alphanumeric page (phone a number, enter digits)
7. **Infrastructure cost per message** — paging network cost per message
8. **Platform ARPU** — paging service revenue per subscriber
9. **Barrier to reach** — phone-number (need the pager number)
10. **Reach per message** — one-to-one
11. **Gatekeeping** — open (anyone can page any pager number)
12. **Years to spam saturation** — was the system displaced by mobile phones before spam could kill it?
13. **Regulatory response** — any specific pager spam regulation
14. **Defenses** — limited (no caller ID on most pagers, no filtering)

## Special notes

Pagers may be another control case like the telegraph — displaced by a better technology (mobile phones) before spam fully destroyed them. The comparison between pager spam in the US (relatively low) and in Japan/China (a major problem) could illustrate how market density affects spam viability. If US data is thin, the Japan comparison still makes the system worth including.
