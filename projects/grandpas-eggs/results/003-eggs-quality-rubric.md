# Eggs (Dozen) — Quality Rubric

## The 1955 baseline product

In 1955, a dozen eggs meant eggs from a small-to-medium farm flock (typically 400–1,500 hens) where birds had outdoor yard access with a coop for roosting. Hens foraged on grass, insects, and seeds supplemented with grain. The dominant commercial breed was the White Leghorn (for white eggs) or various dual-purpose breeds like Plymouth Rocks and Rhode Island Reds (for brown eggs), but these were standard breeds — not the highly optimized hybrids that would come later. Hy-Line, the first hybrid breeding company, was founded in 1936, and hybrid lines were gaining ground in the 1950s, but the full genetic optimization hadn't yet occurred.

Hens produced roughly 200 eggs per year. Eggs were not graded by production method because there was essentially one production method. The USDA graded eggs by size and shell quality (Grade AA, A, B), not by how the hen was raised — there was no need, since nearly all hens had outdoor access and ate a mixed diet.

Sub-therapeutic antibiotics had just been FDA-approved in 1951 and were beginning to enter poultry feed, but adoption was not yet universal in the mid-1950s. Battery cages existed but were not yet the dominant housing system — that transition accelerated through the late 1950s and became standard by the mid-1960s.

A consumer in 1955 walked into a grocery store and bought "eggs." No label distinctions. The product they received was nutritionally and productively similar to what we would today call "pasture-raised" — hens with outdoor access, mixed forage-and-grain diet, moderate stocking density, standard (not hyper-optimized) breeds.

## Quality attributes

| Attribute | 1955 Baseline | 2025 Cheapest (Conventional) | 2025 Quality-Equiv (Pasture-Raised) | Direction |
|-----------|---------------|------------------------------|--------------------------------------|-----------|
| **Hen housing density** | Outdoor yard access, fenced runs; ~2–4 sq ft per bird indoors, free range outdoors | Battery/enriched colony cages, 67–76 sq inches (~0.5 sq ft) per bird, no outdoor access | Certified Humane pasture-raised: 108 sq ft per bird outdoors, rotated pasture | Degraded |
| **Feed type** | Forage (grass, insects, seeds) supplemented with farm grain; varied natural diet | All-grain commercial feed (corn/soy based) with synthetic vitamin/mineral supplements | Forage + supplemental grain; organic feed if organic-certified | Degraded |
| **Nutritional profile (omega-3)** | High omega-3 from forage diet; estimated omega-6:omega-3 ratio ~5:1–10:1 | Low omega-3; omega-6:omega-3 ratio ~15:1–20:1 (up to 51:1 in some studies) | 2–3× more omega-3 than conventional; ratio ~6:1–11:1 | Degraded |
| **Nutritional profile (vitamins)** | High vitamin A, E, D, and beta-carotene from outdoor forage and sun exposure | Baseline USDA nutrient levels | 38% more vitamin A, 2× vitamin E, 3–4× vitamin D, 7× beta-carotene vs. conventional (Mother Earth News 2007; Penn State 2010) | Degraded |
| **Breed productivity** | ~200 eggs/hen/year; standard breeds with moderate productive lifespan | ~296–301 eggs/hen/year; Hy-Line W-36 and similar hybrids optimized for output | Varies; some producers use heritage breeds (~250 eggs/year), many use commercial hybrids | Improved (for output) |
| **Food safety / consistency** | Higher Salmonella risk from floor/yard contact; seasonal variation in supply; less standardized | USDA-inspected, refrigerated supply chain; Salmonella Enteritidis reduction programs since 1990s; consistent year-round supply | USDA-inspected; outdoor access introduces some pathogen exposure but managed through rotation and flock size | Improved |
| **Antibiotic use** | Minimal in mid-1950s (FDA approved sub-therapeutic use in 1951; adoption was growing but not universal) | Routine sub-therapeutic use through 2010s; declining since FDA Guidance for Industry #213 (2017) phased out growth-promotion use; therapeutic use continues | No antibiotics (organic certification requires it; most pasture-raised brands prohibit routine use) | Mixed |

## Quality-equivalent product definition

The modern product that most closely matches the 1955 baseline egg is **Certified Humane Pasture-Raised eggs** — specifically brands like **Vital Farms** or **Pete & Gerry's Organic Pasture-Raised**.

Why pasture-raised and not just "free-range" or "organic"?

- **"Cage-free"** only means uncaged indoors — no outdoor access, high indoor stocking density. Not equivalent.
- **"Free-range"** (USDA definition) requires "access to the outdoors" but sets no minimum space, duration, or vegetation requirements. Many free-range operations provide a small porch or door that most hens never use. Not equivalent.
- **"Organic"** requires organic feed, no antibiotics, and "access to the outdoors" — but the outdoor-access requirement has been notoriously weak and contested (the USDA's Organic Livestock and Poultry Practices rule was proposed in 2016, withdrawn in 2018, and reinstated later). Organic alone doesn't guarantee meaningful pasture access.
- **"Pasture-raised" (Certified Humane)** requires 108 sq ft of outdoor pasture per bird, year-round outdoor access (min 6 hours/day weather permitting), and rotated vegetation. This most closely replicates the 1955 production method: hens on grass, eating a mixed diet of forage and grain, with room to roam.

The ideal match is **pasture-raised organic** (pasture access + organic feed + no antibiotics), but pasture-raised non-organic is also a reasonable proxy since the 1955 baseline used conventional (non-organic) grain. For price research purposes, use Certified Humane Pasture-Raised as the quality-equivalent, noting that pasture-raised organic commands an additional premium.

## Divergence timeline

The US egg market did not split into quality tiers all at once. The story is a collapse-and-rebuild:

| Period | What happened | Market tiers |
|--------|---------------|--------------|
| **Pre-1955** | Most eggs came from small flocks with yard access. No label distinctions needed. | One tier |
| **1955–1965** | Battery cages adopted rapidly. Farms consolidated. The "default" egg shifted from pasture-access to caged. | One tier (transitioning downward) |
| **1965–1990** | Battery cages dominant (~95% of US production by the 1980s). Cheap eggs everywhere. The 1955-quality egg essentially disappeared from retail. | One tier (caged) |
| **1990–2002** | Organic food movement gains mainstream retail presence. Small organic egg brands appear in natural food stores. Whole Foods Market expands (founded 1980, IPO 1992). | Two tiers emerge (conventional + organic/specialty) |
| **2002** | USDA National Organic Program takes effect. "Organic" becomes a regulated label. Consumer awareness grows. | Two clear tiers |
| **2007–2010** | "Cage-free" and "free-range" labels proliferate. Mother Earth News publishes pastured egg nutrition study (2007). Pasture-raised brands like Vital Farms founded (2007). | Multiple tiers forming |
| **2008** | California Proposition 2 passes — bans battery cages (effective 2015). First major legislative action. | — |
| **2015–2016** | Over 200 major food companies pledge to go cage-free by 2025. Cage-free/free-range/pasture-raised become distinct retail categories. | Full tier spectrum established |
| **2018–2022** | California Proposition 12 passes (2018), banning battery cage eggs from sale in CA (effective 2022). Massachusetts, Washington, Oregon, Colorado follow with similar laws. | Regulatory floor rising |

**Key divergence date for this project: ~1995.** Before 1995, there was effectively one product at retail (conventional/caged). After 1995, premium alternatives began appearing, and by 2005 they were widely available in mainstream grocery stores. For the price data tickets, report a single price before 1995 and split into two lines afterward.

## Overall assessment

**Quality degraded.** The cheap version (conventional caged eggs) is worse on a majority of attributes compared to the 1955 baseline:

- Housing density: dramatically worse (0.5 sq ft cage vs. outdoor yards)
- Feed: worse (all-grain commercial vs. forage + grain)
- Nutritional profile: worse (lower omega-3, vitamins A/D/E, beta-carotene)
- Breed diversity: worse (monoculture of optimized hybrids vs. varied standard breeds)

The cheap version is genuinely better on two attributes:
- Food safety and supply consistency (modern inspection, refrigeration, Salmonella programs)
- Productive output per hen (300 vs. 200 eggs/year — relevant to affordability, not quality-to-consumer)

The quality gap is real and measurable. A consumer in 1955 received a nutritionally superior product (by modern pasture-raised standards) as the default — the only — option. Today, that same quality requires seeking out a premium product at 2–4× the price of conventional eggs. The gap between Line A (cheapest) and Line B (quality-equivalent) is the quality tax.
