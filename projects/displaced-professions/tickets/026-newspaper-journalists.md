# Ticket 026: Print Newspaper Journalists

## Event
The ongoing decline of print newspaper journalism employment as digital media captured advertising revenue, ~2000–present. A tipping point driven by the collapse of classified advertising (Craigslist), display advertising migration to digital, and the free-content expectation of the internet.

## Mechanism
**Tipping point** (tech_platform)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track US newspaper newsroom employment from ~1990 to present:

- Peak: ~56,900 newsroom employees (ASNE/Pew, 2008 estimate) or ~71,000 (some counts include all editorial). Earlier peak may have been higher.
- Decline has been relentless: roughly half of newspaper journalism jobs eliminated since 2008
- Current: ~30,000–31,000 (Pew Research Center, 2020 data)
- Track newspaper industry employment more broadly (NAICS 51111)
- Key dates: Craigslist national expansion (2004–2006), 2008 recession (accelerator), digital paywalls (2011+)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Pew Research Center "State of the News Media" reports (annual)
2. BLS QCEW/CES data, NAICS 51111 (Newspaper Publishers)
3. American Society of News Editors (ASNE) newsroom census
4. BLS OES data, SOC 27-3022 (Reporters, Correspondents, and Broadcast News Analysts)
5. Newspaper Association of America (now News Media Alliance) — advertising revenue data

### Income Data
- BLS median wages for reporters/correspondents over time
- Newspaper industry average wages from CES data
- The two-tier outcome: elite journalists at surviving papers (NYT, WaPo, WSJ) are doing well. Local journalists have been devastated.
- Track from 2000–2023

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS OES median annual wage for SOC 27-3022
2. CPI-U for inflation adjustment
3. Pew Research Center income/compensation data for journalists
4. News Guild (union) contract data

### Where They Went
- Digital media / online journalism (but these jobs often pay less)
- Communications / PR / corporate content (the "dark side" transition)
- Freelance journalism (lower income, no benefits)
- Government/nonprofit communications
- Entirely different careers
- The "news desert" phenomenon: many communities lost their newspaper entirely

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Pew Research Center surveys of journalists
2. University of North Carolina "News Deserts" project
3. BLS employment data for public relations and communications
4. Academic studies on journalist career transitions

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** The New York Times (digital transformation, subscription model — reached 10M+ subscribers). Washington Post (Bezos acquisition and digital investment). Local journalists who built successful substacks or newsletters.
- **False movers:** Newspapers that gave away content for free online expecting digital ads to replace print ads (they didn't — CPM rates are far lower). Tribune Publishing's various digital strategies.
- **Deniers:** "Newspapers are essential — they'll always exist." The slow response to classified ad loss. Belief that paywalls would never work (they did, but only for a few).
- **Survival pattern:** Brand strength (NYT, WSJ), subscription models, and/or niche expertise.

### Unique Texture
- Classified advertising revenue: ~$19.6B (2000) → ~$2.7B (2012). Craigslist killed it.
- Total newspaper ad revenue: ~$65B (2000) → ~$9B (2020). One of the most dramatic revenue collapses in media history.
- The "news desert" phenomenon: 1,800+ US newspapers have closed since 2004. Communities without local journalism.
- The "pivot to video" — Facebook-driven advice that turned out to be catastrophically wrong for newsrooms that followed it
- The democracy dimension: the decline of local journalism correlated with lower voter turnout and increased corruption in local government (academic studies exist)

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Annual newsroom employment from 2000–2023
- Newspaper advertising revenue (classified and total) from 2000–2020
- Newspaper closure/merger counts over time
- Wage data for journalists
- The NYT subscriber growth as a counterpoint to industry decline
