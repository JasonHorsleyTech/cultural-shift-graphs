# Exploration Pass 1: Grounding the Question

## The question in one line

When a profession dies, does the *type* of death predict the *shape* of the decline — and does that shape tell you anything useful about what to do if it's happening to you?

---

## Two concrete examples that make this tangible

### Example A: Handloom weavers vs. power looms (Tech displacement, 1810–1860)

This is the textbook slow death. The numbers:

- **Peak:** ~250,000–300,000 handloom weavers in England, ~1810–1813 (Bythell, *The Handloom Weavers*, 1969). Cotton weavers alone were ~250K.
- **The weapon:** Power looms. ~2,400 in Britain in 1813; ~100,000 by 1833; ~250,000 by 1850.
- **The wage collapse:** A skilled handloom weaver earned ~25 shillings/week in 1800. By 1820: ~9–10s. By 1830: ~5–6s. By 1840: ~4–5s. That's an 80% pay cut over 40 years while still technically employed. The 1838 Royal Commission confirmed starvation wages.
- **The headcount:** 200K by 1830, 100–125K by 1840, 40–50K by 1850, 10–15K by 1860.
- **Who died first:** Coarse cloth weavers — the easiest to automate. Fine fabric and specialty weavers survived longest. The hierarchy collapsed bottom-up.
- **Where they went:** Factory work (power loom tending, ~10–12s/week — better than late handloom wages), general labouring, mining, construction. Many entered the Poor Law system. Handloom weavers were disproportionately represented in workhouse admissions in Lancashire in the 1830s–40s. Non-trivial number simply died in poverty.

**Napkin math:** A handloom weaver in 1800 earning 25s/week is roughly £65/year. A factory power loom tender in 1840 earned ~10–12s/week, or ~£28/year. The "replacement job" paid ~43% of the peak job. But the handloom weaver who waited until 1840 to switch was already earning 4–5s/week (~£12/year), so the factory job was actually a *raise* for the holdouts. The lesson: the transition penalty was enormous for early movers (50% pay cut), but waiting made it even worse. The optimal move was to switch around 1820 when handloom wages were still ~10s and factory jobs paid similar — you'd have taken a lateral move instead of a step down.

**Shape of the curve:** Slow exponential decay over ~50 years. Bottom of the market eroded first. The last 10% held on for decades doing specialty work. Classic tech displacement pattern.

### Example B: USSR collapse (Regime collapse, 1989–1998)

This is the cliff. The numbers:

- **GDP:** Russia's GDP fell ~40% from 1989 to the 1998 trough (World Bank).
- **Real wages:** Fell to 34–40% of 1991 levels by 1998. Did not recover to 1991 levels until approximately 2007. That's a **16-year income recovery**.
- **Life expectancy:** Russian male life expectancy dropped from 64.2 years (1989) to 57.6 years (1994) — a 6.6-year drop in 5 years. This is one of the largest peacetime demographic catastrophes ever recorded.
- **Unemployment:** Peaked at ~13.3% (ILO methodology) in 1998, with 9–10 million officially unemployed — from near-zero under the Soviet system. But "hidden unemployment" was massive — 20–30% of the workforce was effectively underemployed (on payroll but unpaid for months).
- **Hardest hit:** Military-industrial complex (~5 million jobs lost), heavy manufacturing, collective agriculture, state R&D. Defense spending fell from ~15–20% of GDP to 3–4%.
- **Shadow economy:** Grew to 40–50% of GDP by mid-1990s (Schneider & Enste 2000). "Shuttle traders" (*chelnoki*) — petty cross-border traders — numbered an estimated 10–30 million at peak. ~800,000+ private security guards emerged as a new profession.

**Napkin math:** A Soviet engineer earning the equivalent of $300/month in 1989 (typical skilled worker) was earning the real equivalent of $100–120/month by 1998. If they were one of the lucky ones who pivoted to private security or shuttle trading, they might have made $150–200/month in unstable cash income. The "replacement" was informal, precarious, and often dangerous.

**Shape of the curve:** Near-vertical cliff. Employment didn't erode over decades — it shattered in months. Recovery was slow, chaotic, and profoundly uneven. The top 1% captured absurd wealth (oligarchs); the median worker lost 60% of their real income for a decade and a half.

### The contrast is the thesis

These two examples, side by side, are the whole argument:

| | Handloom weavers | USSR collapse |
|---|---|---|
| Duration of decline | ~50 years | ~2 years |
| Warning time | Decades | Months |
| Who died first | Bottom of the hierarchy | Everyone simultaneously |
| Income at trough | ~20% of peak (4–5s vs 25s) | ~35% of peak |
| Recovery time | ~10 years to factory wages | ~16 years to pre-collapse wages |
| Optimal move | Switch early (~1820) | Be politically connected or leave |

If this pattern generalizes across examples — tech is slow and bottom-up, regime collapse is instant and total, resource depletion has a tipping point, cultural shift is glacial — that's a genuinely useful framework for someone staring down AI displacement.

## Two more examples to broaden the picture

### Example C: Travel agents vs. the internet (Tech displacement, modern)

- **Peak:** ~124,000 travel agents in the US, ~2000 (BLS SOC 41-3041).
- **Current:** ~56,400 (BLS, May 2023). Down 55% from peak.
- **Kill shot:** Airlines eliminated base commissions in 2002 (Delta first, all majors followed within months). Commissions dropped from 10% → 8% → 0%.
- **Key disruptions:** Expedia (1995), Travelocity (1995), Priceline (1998), Orbitz (2003).
- **Who survived:** Luxury/experiential agents (average trip value $10K–25K+, commission from hotels/resorts), corporate TMCs. Budget/domestic leisure agents (~60–70% of the pre-internet base) were essentially eliminated.
- **Earnings twist:** Median travel agent income went from ~$26K (2000) to ~$47K (2023). Headcount collapsed 55%, but survivors earn 80% more. Pure survivor bias — the cheap-ticket agents left, the luxury advisors remained and thrived.

**This is the handloom pattern in modern dress.** Bottom of the market dies, top survives, and the "average" stat improves because the denominator changed. The 22-year decline timeline is compressed vs. handlooms (50 years) but the shape is identical.

### Example D: Newfoundland cod moratorium (Resource depletion, 1992)

- **Pre-moratorium:** ~40,000 people employed in Newfoundland cod (20K fishers, 20K plant workers) — ~10% of provincial employment.
- **Displacement:** 30,000–40,000 workers displaced overnight. Largest mass layoff in Canadian history.
- **Government response:** NCARP ($772M, 1992–94, ~26K recipients) → TAGS ($1.9B budgeted, 1994–98, ~40K recipients at peak). Combined federal spending exceeded $3B.
- **Population collapse:** Newfoundland dropped from ~580K (1992) to ~508K (2007) — 12% decline. Outport communities lost 30–50% of their population. Net outmigration of 60–70K people, primarily to Alberta.
- **Where they went:** Alberta oil sands ("Newfie goes West"), crab/shrimp fisheries (temporary boom), retraining programs (low completion rates), early retirement/income support. Women plant workers had especially poor outcomes.
- **Income:** Average fishing household income dropped 30–40% post-moratorium. Communities remained below provincial average a decade later.

**Shape of the curve:** Resource depletion looks like regime collapse — a cliff, not a slope. The moratorium was July 2, 1992. One day you had a job, the next you didn't. But unlike USSR, the *resource* was the constraint, not the system — so the industry didn't recover even when people were willing to work. The cod still haven't fully recovered 30+ years later. This is a one-way door.

## Proposed metric: what exactly to measure and graph

### The meta-graph (the money shot)

A scatter plot. Every displaced profession is one bubble.

- **X axis:** Years from peak employment to "effectively dead" (< 10% of peak workforce in that occupation). Log scale recommended — the range spans from ~1 year (cod moratorium, USSR) to ~50 years (handlooms).
- **Y axis:** Income recovery at 10 years post-peak. Median real income of workers in that occupation (or its closest successor occupation) as a percentage of the peak-era median. 100% = full recovery. Range: probably 30–120%.
- **Bubble size:** Peak workforce as percentage of the national labor force at that time. Makes handloom weavers (huge % of 1810 British labor) visually dominant vs. travel agents (tiny % of 2000 US labor).
- **Color:** Collapse type (9 categories from the question).

**What this graph would show:** Clusters. If the hypothesis holds, tech displacements cluster in the upper-right (slow death, decent recovery), regime collapses cluster in the lower-left (fast death, poor recovery), resource depletions cluster in the left-center (fast death, moderate recovery via government intervention), etc.

**The optional AI dot:** A speculative marker for "software engineers, 2025–????" — placed according to which cluster the user thinks AI displacement most resembles. This is the payoff of the whole project: "Based on all of human history, here's where you probably are."

### Per-event panels (secondary views)

As described in question.md — workforce over time, Sankey flows, income trajectories, foresight timelines. These are powerful but they're the *detail*, not the *summary*. The meta-scatter is the hero.

### Unit decisions

- **Workforce:** Percentage of national workforce at that time (not absolute numbers — comparing 250K handloom weavers in 1810 Britain to 40K cod fishers in 1992 Newfoundland requires normalization).
- **Income:** Real income, inflation-adjusted, indexed to the year of peak employment (peak = 100).
- **Time:** Years relative to peak (0 = peak, positive = years after peak).
- **"Effectively dead" threshold:** < 10% of peak workforce remaining in the named occupation. Acknowledge in methodology that this is arbitrary — some professions (blacksmith) never hit 0%, they just become niche.

## Data sources with longitudinal potential

### 1. IPUMS (Integrated Public Use Microdata Series) — Minnesota Population Center
US census microdata from 1850 to present. Occupational codes allow tracking of specific professions over time. This is the single best source for US-based events (travel agents, typists, etc.). Free for academic use.

### 2. Historical Statistics of the United States: Millennial Edition (Cambridge, 2006)
US workforce breakdowns from 1790 to 2000. Economic data (wages, prices, GDP). Essential for pre-BLS events. Available through university libraries.

### 3. UK Parliamentary Papers / Census Returns (1801–1911)
British census data includes occupational classifications. The Select Committee on Hand-Loom Weavers (1834–35) and Royal Commission (1838–41) are goldmines for the weaver case. Available through UK Parliamentary Archives and Hathi Trust.

### 4. BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OES)
US data from ~1988–present. Clean, consistent, updated annually. Covers modern events (travel agents, typists/word processors, telephone operators). Best source for recent tech displacement.

### 5. World Bank / OECD / Rosstat
Post-Soviet transition data. World Development Indicators (WDI) for GDP, employment, life expectancy. OECD Economic Surveys for Russia. Rosstat for detailed Russian labor statistics.

### 6. Statistics Canada / DFO
Newfoundland cod data. Census data (1991–2006) for population and employment changes. DFO stock assessments for the ecological side.

### 7. Academic monographs per event
Each event has its "definitive study" — Bythell for handlooms, Sinclair for cod, Brainerd for post-Soviet wages. These are where the texture comes from.

## What I'm unsure about — where the framing is wobbly

### 1. "Where did they go?" is the weakest data

We can measure profession decline (census shows fewer weavers each decade). But tracking where *those specific people* went requires longitudinal panel data — following individuals over time. This barely exists before 1970. Even after 1970, panel studies (PSID, NLSY) are US-only and may not capture specific displacement events well.

**Workaround:** Instead of tracking individuals, track *concurrent growth in successor industries* in the same geography. If Lancashire loses 50K weavers and gains 40K factory workers in the same decade, the inference is strong even without individual tracking. This is honest but should be labeled as inference, not direct measurement.

### 2. The 9-category taxonomy may not hold

Many events are multi-causal:
- **Whaling** = tech (kerosene replaced whale oil) + resource depletion (whale populations crashed)
- **Post-WWI servants** = war (men didn't come back) + cultural shift (returning soldiers wouldn't go into service)
- **US textiles** = globalization + tech (automation of remaining jobs)

If most events are 2–3 types blended, the "collapse type predicts collapse shape" thesis weakens. The categories might be *contributing factors* rather than *types*.

**Possible fix:** Assign a primary and secondary tag. If the thesis only works for primary type, that's still useful. If the shape is determined by whichever factor moves fastest (tech is slow, regulation is fast, so a tech+regulation event looks like regulation), that's an even more interesting finding.

### 3. Comparing across centuries is risky

A handloom weaver in 1820 and a travel agent in 2005 lived in fundamentally different labor markets:
- Social safety nets didn't exist in 1820; unemployment insurance, retraining programs exist now
- Geographic mobility: a weaver might never travel more than 20 miles; a modern worker can relocate across the country
- Skills transferability: "weaving" had zero overlap with factory work; "booking travel" has some overlap with customer service, event planning
- The very concept of "a career" and "retraining" is modern

The meta-scatter will plot these on the same axes. Is that valid? Probably yes if we're comparing *shapes* rather than *absolute outcomes*. The claim is that tech displacement is always slow and bottom-up regardless of era — and the data does support that (handlooms in 1820 and travel agents in 2000 show strikingly similar patterns). The safety net changes the *severity* but not the *shape*.

### 4. "Effectively dead" is doing a lot of work

At what point is a profession dead?
- There are still blacksmiths, but nobody *becomes* a blacksmith as a career path.
- There are still travel agents, but the industry is half its peak size — is it "dead" or "contracted"?
- Newfoundland cod fishing was banned, not market-killed. Is that "dead" or "suspended"?

The 10%-of-peak threshold is defensible but arbitrary. The report should be transparent about this. An alternative: instead of a binary alive/dead, track workforce as a continuous percentage and let the graph speak for itself.

### 5. Survivor bias in "foresight stories"

The question.md asks about adapters, false movers, and deniers. But we only know about successful adapters because they survived. The weaver who switched to factory work in 1820 and died of a factory accident in 1825 doesn't appear in any "success" narrative. The Soviet engineer who pivoted to shuttle trading and got robbed doesn't show up in the "adapters" column.

The foresight stories are compelling narrative but should be flagged as inherently biased. The graph itself (quantitative, population-level) is more honest than the stories.

---

## Bottom line: is this graphable?

**Yes.** The meta-scatter is achievable and would be genuinely striking. The per-event panels are more ambitious but the data exists for at least 10–12 well-documented events.

**Biggest risk:** The "where did they go" and "income recovery" data may be too thin for pre-1900 events, forcing us to rely on inference and estimates. The graph should be honest about confidence levels — maybe solid dots for well-sourced data and hollow dots for estimates.

**Biggest opportunity:** The side-by-side of handlooms (50-year slow death) and USSR (2-year cliff) is so visually stark that even with imperfect data, the *shape* argument is immediately obvious. Lead with the shape, add nuance as the data allows.

**Next step:** Stress-test the metric and the taxonomy. Try to break the "type predicts shape" hypothesis. Find events where tech displacement was fast, or where regime collapse was slow. If the thesis survives that, it's worth building.
