# Ticket 009: Benzene → Leukemia

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that benzene causes leukemia to binding regulation setting enforceable exposure limits.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Santesson documented leukemia in benzene-exposed workers in 1897. Thorpe & Trotter (1928) firmly established the link in British medical literature. The American Petroleum Institute internally acknowledged benzene as leukemogenic by 1948. What's the earliest credible epidemiological evidence?

2. **Consensus date** — When did a major body formally classify benzene as a human carcinogen? IARC classification? NAS report? NIOSH/OSHA determinations?

3. **First OECD regulation** — Which OECD country first set a binding workplace or environmental exposure limit for benzene? Look for the earliest enforceable standard, not just recommended limits.

4. **US regulation** — OSHA set the first US workplace exposure limit in 1971 (10 ppm), tightened to 1 ppm in 1987 after a Supreme Court case. The 1987 standard is arguably the first "meaningful" limit. Document both.

5. **Industry opposition** — The petroleum and chemical industries fought tighter benzene standards, particularly the OSHA 1 ppm standard (legal battles went to the Supreme Court in 1980). Level of opposition?

6. **Substitute availability** — Toluene and other solvents replaced benzene in many applications. Was substitution feasible at the time of regulation?

## Where to look

1. IARC Monograph on benzene
2. OSHA regulatory history for benzene standard (29 CFR 1910.1028)
3. Supreme Court — Industrial Union Department v. American Petroleum Institute (1980) — the "benzene case" that reshaped OSHA's risk assessment requirements
4. PubMed — "benzene leukemia history" or "benzene occupational exposure regulation"
5. Robert Infante's work on benzene epidemiology

## What "good data" looks like

- Evidence spanning from 1897 (earliest case reports) through 1948 (industry internal acknowledgment) — choose the year when epidemiological evidence was convincing, not just case reports
- The 1971 vs. 1987 OSHA standard distinction matters — the 10 ppm standard was later shown to be inadequate. Document both but choose the first binding limit as the regulation date.
- The Supreme Court case (1980) is important context — it changed how OSHA had to justify regulation going forward

## Known pitfalls

- Benzene is primarily an occupational hazard. The "general population" exposure (from gasoline vapors, cigarette smoke) exists but regulation focused on workplace limits. This is a case where the exposure-limit category's workplace-only exception applies — occupational regulation counts because the hazard IS primarily occupational.
- The 10 ppm standard (1971) was "meaningful" at the time but is considered dangerously high by modern standards. Use it as the regulation date but note the inadequacy.
- The "benzene case" at the Supreme Court (1980) made it harder for OSHA to regulate future chemicals — a perverse outcome of this specific regulation fight. Note this in judgment calls.
