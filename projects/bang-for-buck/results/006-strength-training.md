# Result: Strength Training / Gym Membership

## Summary

Regular strength training (2-3 sessions/week, ~60 min/week optimal) reduces all-cause mortality by 15% (RR 0.85), with a maximum 27% reduction at ~60 min/week per dose-response analysis. Two independent 2022 meta-analyses (Shailendra et al., Momma et al.) converge on the 15% figure across 10-16 studies and 263,000+ participants. Beyond mortality, strength training significantly reduces depression symptoms (SMD 0.66), improves insulin sensitivity (34% lower T2D risk), and preserves bone density and functional capacity — all particularly relevant for Bob's elevated obesity and depression risk. The QALY estimate is derived from mortality reduction scaled against the walking benchmark, plus a quality-of-life component. Cost is anchored on Planet Fitness ($229/year) as the budget-gym standard, with bodyweight training as a zero-cost alternative that likely confers similar but less-studied benefits. Adherence is the major weakness: gym dropout rates are high, with roughly 50% of new members quitting within 6 months.

## QALY Evidence

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.07
- **Range:** 0.03 – 0.11 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** Shailendra et al. (2022), Momma et al. (2022) — two independent meta-analyses
- **Study type:** Systematic reviews and meta-analyses of prospective cohort studies (observational)
- **Population studied:** General adult populations, predominantly middle-aged and older adults from US cohorts
- **Population match to Bob:** Fair — evidence is from general populations, not specifically low-income or high-BMI. However, metabolic benefits may be amplified in obese/sedentary individuals. Depression benefits confirmed regardless of health status.
- **Key caveats:**
  - All mortality evidence is observational — no RCTs exist for strength training on all-cause mortality
  - Cannot fully separate strength training from concurrent aerobic activity (people who lift weights often also walk/run)
  - Self-reported "muscle-strengthening activities" definition varies across studies — ranges from gym-based weight training to manual labor
  - No direct QALY measurement studies exist for resistance training alone — estimate is derived from mortality reduction and QoL proxies
  - The U-shaped dose-response (benefits diminish above ~60-140 min/week) suggests a ceiling effect

### Evidence Stream 1: All-Cause Mortality Meta-Analyses

**Shailendra et al. (2022)** — *American Journal of Preventive Medicine*
- 10 studies in meta-analysis
- Any resistance training vs none: **RR 0.85** (95% CI: 0.77–0.93) — 15% reduction in all-cause mortality
- CVD mortality: **RR 0.81** (95% CI: 0.66–1.00) — 19% reduction
- Cancer mortality: **RR 0.86** (95% CI: 0.78–0.95) — 14% reduction
- Dose-response (4 studies): nonlinear, **maximum 27% reduction at ~60 min/week** (RR 0.74, 95% CI: 0.64–0.86), diminishing at higher volumes

**Momma et al. (2022)** — *British Journal of Sports Medicine*
- 16 studies (2012–2020), 7 studies on all-cause mortality with **42,133 deaths among 263,058 participants**
- Muscle-strengthening activities: **RR 0.85** (95% CI: 0.79–0.93) — 15% reduction in all-cause mortality
- Lowest RR at ~40 min/week (17% reduction)
- Protective effect persisted up to ~140 min/week
- Combined with aerobic exercise (3 studies, 581,194 participants): **RR 0.60** (95% CI: 0.54–0.67) — 40% lower mortality risk

### Evidence Stream 2: Depression Benefits

**Gordon et al. (2018)** — *JAMA Psychiatry*
- 33 RCTs, 1,877 participants
- Resistance training significantly reduced depressive symptoms: **SMD 0.66** (95% CI: 0.48–0.83) — moderate-to-large effect
- Effect was significant regardless of health status, total training volume, or whether strength improved
- Not dependent on achieving objective strength gains — the act of training itself produces benefit

**Anxiety:** Gordon et al. (2017) found resistance training reduced anxiety symptoms (SMD 0.31, 95% CI: 0.17–0.44), with larger effects in healthy participants.

### Evidence Stream 3: Metabolic Benefits

- **Health Professionals Follow-up Study:** Men who did resistance training had **34% lower T2D risk**, independent of aerobic exercise
- Resistance training improves GLUT4 translocation, insulin sensitivity, and reduces HbA1c
- ACSM/ADA position statement recommends resistance training at least 2x/week for T2D prevention and management

### QALY Synthesis

The best estimate of **0.07 QALY/year** reflects:
- **Mortality component: ~0.05 QALY/year** — Proportionally scaled from the walking benchmark (walking: 11% mortality reduction → ~0.05 QALY/year from mortality; strength training: 15% → ~0.065, conservatively rounded to 0.05 given weaker causal evidence)
- **Quality-of-life component: ~0.02 QALY/year** — Depression symptom reduction (SMD 0.66), improved functional capacity, bone density preservation, metabolic improvements. Estimated conservatively because no direct QALY measurement exists for these benefits in isolation.
- **The low estimate (0.03)** assumes substantial confounding inflates the mortality reduction and minimal QoL gains
- **The high estimate (0.11)** assumes the mortality reduction is causal, Bob's worse baseline amplifies marginal gains, and depression/metabolic benefits add meaningfully

## Cost Analysis (for Bob at ~$30k household income)

- **Annual direct cost:** $229 (Planet Fitness gym-based scenario)
- **One-time startup cost:** $50–100 (gym bag, lock, basic workout clothes, water bottle)
- **Startup cost amortized over 3 years:** $17–33/year
- **Total annual cost (best estimate):** $250
- **Cost range:** $0 – $400 (low – high)
- **With insurance (if applicable):** Some insurers offer gym reimbursement (Silver Sneakers, Blue365); could reduce to $0–100/year
- **Without insurance:** $250/year
- **Cost notes:**
  - **Planet Fitness Classic:** $15/month + $49 annual fee = $229/year. This is the cheapest national gym chain and the most realistic option for Bob. Price was raised from $10/month in May 2024.
  - **YMCA with financial assistance:** Sliding scale based on income; at $30k household income, Bob would qualify for significant discounts. Typical assisted rate: $10–30/month = $120–360/year. Requires application and tax return documentation.
  - **Home equipment:** Resistance bands ($15–30) + used dumbbells ($50–100) = ~$65–130 one-time, amortized to $22–43/year. No ongoing costs.
  - **Bodyweight only (push-ups, squats, lunges):** $0. Evidence suggests bodyweight training produces meaningful strength and health gains (ACSM 2026 guidelines confirm), though most mortality meta-analyses studied gym-based or weighted resistance training specifically.
  - **Low estimate ($0)** = bodyweight-only program following free online routines
  - **Mid estimate ($250)** = Planet Fitness membership + minimal startup gear
  - **High estimate ($400)** = YMCA or mid-tier gym without financial assistance

## Time Commitment

- **Hours per week:** 2.5 (3 sessions × 50 min average, including warm-up/cool-down)
- **Category:** Medium (1–3 hr/wk)
- Note: Travel to gym adds ~0.5–1.5 hrs/week (15–30 min round trip × 3 sessions). Bodyweight training at home eliminates travel time. Optimal dose per mortality evidence is only ~40–60 min/week total, but practical sessions including warm-up and rest periods run 45–60 min each.

## Access and Barriers

- **Activation energy:** Medium
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - **Knowledge gap:** Knowing what exercises to do, proper form, how to progress — without a personal trainer ($40–80/session), this is a real barrier. Free resources exist (YouTube, apps) but require initiative.
  - **Gym intimidation:** "Gymtimidation" is well-documented, especially for obese and inexperienced individuals. Planet Fitness specifically markets against this but the barrier persists.
  - **Physical discomfort at high BMI:** Joint stress, limited range of motion, embarrassment. Beginners with BMI 35+ need modified exercises and slower progression.
  - **Transportation:** Nearest affordable gym may require a car or bus ride. Rural areas may have no gym within reasonable distance.
  - **Schedule constraints:** Shift work makes consistent 3x/week scheduling difficult. Peak gym hours (5–8 PM) conflict with common shift patterns.
  - **Home alternative reduces several barriers** but introduces new ones (space, motivation without social environment, limited progression)

## Side Effects and Risks

- **Musculoskeletal injury:** Low risk when properly supervised. Injury rates for resistance training are 0.0035 per 100 participant hours — far lower than most sports (rugby: 0.80). However, unsupervised beginners with poor form have elevated risk, especially for low back and shoulder injuries.
- **Delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS):** Near-universal in beginners; peaks 24–72 hours after initial sessions. Can discourage continuation but is temporary (1–2 weeks of regular training).
- **Rhabdomyolysis:** Extremely rare but serious. Risk is elevated in deconditioned individuals who do too much too soon. Preventable with gradual progression.
- **Cardiovascular events during training:** Acute blood pressure spikes during heavy lifts; generally safe for healthy individuals but warrants caution with uncontrolled hypertension (relevant for Bob's demographic).
- **Net risk assessment:** Low with proper progression. Risk-benefit ratio is strongly favorable.

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate:** 0.35
- **Source:** Composite from multiple gym membership retention studies and exercise adherence literature
- **Notes:**
  - ~50% of new gym members quit within the first 6 months (IHRSA industry data; multiple gym retention studies)
  - Annual retention for established members is ~71%, but this includes long-term members; for new members specifically, 12-month adherence is substantially lower
  - The 35% estimate reflects: of those who start a gym-based strength training program, approximately 35% are still training consistently at 12 months
  - Lower than walking (50%) due to higher activation energy, gym access requirements, knowledge barrier, and DOMS/discomfort in early weeks
  - Adherence improves with: training partners, simple programs, visible progress (strength gains come faster than cardio improvements), and habit stacking
  - Home bodyweight programs may have different adherence profiles (lower barrier but less social accountability)

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 2
- **Tier 2** = Some RCTs, or strong observational data, or good data but population mismatch
- **Key studies:**
  - Shailendra P, Baldock KL, Li LSK, et al. (2022). Resistance Training and Mortality Risk: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis. *Am J Prev Med*, 63(2):277-285.
  - Momma H, Kawakami R, Honda T, et al. (2022). Muscle-strengthening activities are associated with lower risk and mortality in major non-communicable diseases. *Br J Sports Med*, 56(13):755-763.
  - Gordon BR, McDowell CP, Hallgren M, et al. (2018). Association of Efficacy of Resistance Exercise Training With Depressive Symptoms. *JAMA Psychiatry*, 75(6):566-576.
  - Gordon BR, McDowell CP, Lyons M, et al. (2017). The Effects of Resistance Exercise Training on Anxiety. *Sports Med*, 47(12):2521-2532.
- **Limitations:**
  - All mortality evidence is observational — healthy-user bias cannot be excluded (people who strength train may have other healthy behaviors)
  - "Muscle-strengthening activities" is self-reported and heterogeneous across studies — includes everything from gym weight training to manual occupational labor
  - Cannot isolate strength training mortality benefit from concurrent aerobic activity — most people who lift also do some cardio
  - No direct QALY measurement studies exist for strength training; QALY estimate is synthesized from mortality reduction and domain-specific QoL studies
  - Depression RCTs (Gordon 2018) are well-designed but short-term (median 16 weeks) — long-term mental health QALY impact extrapolated
  - Evidence rated "very low certainty" by Momma et al. due to indirectness
  - Tier 2 rather than Tier 1 because: (a) QALY estimate requires more inferential leaps than walking, (b) confounding with aerobic activity is unresolved, (c) no direct QALY measurement exists
