# Result: Yoga

## Summary

Regular yoga practice (3 sessions/week, ~30–45 min each) shows moderate health benefits across pain reduction, mental health, blood pressure, and quality of life — but unlike walking and strength training, it lacks significant all-cause mortality evidence after adjustment. The QALY estimate of 0.03/year is derived from several condition-specific cost-effectiveness analyses (chronic low back pain, depression, musculoskeletal conditions) rather than mortality reduction. Three RCTs with direct QALY measurement converge on gains of 0.02–0.04 QALY/year for people with relevant conditions. Yoga is uniquely cheap — YouTube-based home practice with a $15 mat is genuinely effective — making its cost-effectiveness ratio excellent despite moderate QALY gains. The major caveat for Bob is cultural: yoga participation skews heavily female (77%), white, and higher-income. Only 10.4% of adults below 200% FPL practice yoga, and real-world adherence for Bob's demographic is likely the lowest of any intervention in this study.

## QALY Evidence

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.03
- **Range:** 0.01 – 0.05 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** Groessl et al. (2020), Aboagye et al. (2017), Skjøth et al. (2024)
- **Study type:** Multiple RCTs with embedded economic evaluations
- **Population studied:** Veterans with chronic low back pain (US), NHS employees with musculoskeletal conditions (UK), older adults with multiple long-term conditions (UK)
- **Population match to Bob:** Fair — Bob shares elevated chronic pain and mental health burden with study populations, but studies skew older, female, and/or higher-SES. No studies specifically target low-income sedentary men.
- **Key caveats:**
  - All QALY estimates are condition-specific (low back pain, musculoskeletal conditions, depression) — no general-population QALY study exists for yoga
  - National prospective cohort study (Jeter et al. 2015, n=61,530) found yoga was NOT significantly associated with reduced all-cause mortality after adjustment
  - Yoga's QALY pathway is quality-of-life improvement (pain, depression, flexibility, BP), not mortality reduction
  - Bob doesn't necessarily have all the conditions where yoga shows strongest gains, though his demographic has elevated prevalence
  - Most yoga RCTs have small-to-moderate sample sizes (150–454 participants)

### Evidence Stream 1: Direct QALY Data from RCTs

**Groessl et al. (2020)** — *Value in Health*
- 150 military veterans with chronic low back pain
- 12-week intervention (2×/week, 60-min sessions), 12-month follow-up
- Incremental QALYs gained: **0.043 over 12 months**
- Intervention cost: $307/participant
- ICER: **$4,488/QALY** (healthcare perspective); dominant from societal perspective
- 89% probability of cost-effectiveness at $50,000 WTP threshold

**Aboagye et al. (2017)** — *Occupational Medicine*
- 151 NHS employees with musculoskeletal conditions (93% female, mean age 44)
- 8-week intervention, 6-month follow-up
- EQ-5D-5L: Yoga group improved from 0.836 to 0.850; control declined from 0.815 to 0.782
- Incremental QALYs: **0.016 over 6 months** (~0.032 annualized)
- ICER: **£2,103/QALY** (healthcare perspective); dominant from societal perspective
- 95% probability of cost-effectiveness at £20,000 WTP threshold

**Skjøth et al. (2024)** — *Age and Ageing* (chair-based yoga)
- 454 older adults (≥65 years) with ≥2 long-term conditions, 15 primary care practices
- 12-week intervention, 12-month follow-up
- Adjusted mean EQ-5D-5L difference: **0.020** (95% CI: −0.006 to 0.045, p=0.14 — not significant)
- ICER: **£4,546/QALY**
- 79% probability of cost-effectiveness at £20,000 threshold

### Evidence Stream 2: All-Cause Mortality

**Jeter et al. (2015)** — *Complementary Therapies in Medicine*
- National prospective cohort study, n=61,530
- Yoga participation was NOT significantly associated with reduced all-cause mortality in adjusted models
- Unadjusted models suggested a protective effect, but this disappeared after controlling for confounders (income, education, physical activity, BMI)
- This is a critical distinction from walking and strength training, which retain significant mortality associations after adjustment

### Evidence Stream 3: Quality-of-Life Components

**Blood pressure:** Meta-analyses show systolic BP reduction of 4.6–7.9 mmHg, diastolic 3.4–4.1 mmHg (Cramer et al. 2014; 2026 Frontiers review). Strongest with 3×/week practice including breathing exercises. Clinically meaningful for Bob's demographic (elevated hypertension prevalence).

**Depression/anxiety:** Meta-analysis (Cramer et al. 2013) found moderate effect on depression (SMD ~0.69) vs. active controls. HRQoL meta-analysis (Büssing et al. 2019) found ES = 0.51 (95% CI: 0.25–0.77) for health-related quality of life.

**Chronic low back pain:** Strong evidence from multiple RCTs. Yoga performs comparably to physical therapy (Saper et al. 2017) at lower cost.

### QALY Synthesis

The best estimate of **0.03 QALY/year** reflects:
- **Quality-of-life component: ~0.03 QALY/year** — Derived from averaging the three direct QALY measurements (0.032, 0.043, 0.020), with a slight downward adjustment because Bob doesn't universally have the conditions studied. This captures pain reduction, mental health improvement, BP reduction, and functional capacity gains.
- **Mortality component: ~0.00 QALY/year** — No significant all-cause mortality evidence after adjustment.
- **The low estimate (0.01)** assumes Bob doesn't have LBP or depression, and benefits are limited to modest cardiovascular and flexibility gains.
- **The high estimate (0.05)** assumes Bob has one or more conditions where yoga excels (LBP, depression), and his worse baseline amplifies marginal gains.

## Cost Analysis (for Bob at ~$30k household income)

- **Annual direct cost:** $50 (home practice with YouTube/free apps)
- **One-time startup cost:** $20 (basic yoga mat from Amazon/Walmart)
- **Startup cost amortized over 3 years:** $7/year
- **Total annual cost (best estimate):** $60
- **Cost range:** $5 – $300 (low – high)
- **With insurance (if applicable):** Some insurers cover yoga classes for chronic pain management; YMCA partnerships exist. Could reduce structured class costs to $0–100/year.
- **Without insurance:** $60/year (home practice); $240–420/year (YMCA with financial assistance)
- **Cost notes:**
  - **Home practice (YouTube):** $0 ongoing. "Yoga With Adriene" has 12M+ subscribers, full-length free classes at every level. This is a genuinely viable primary practice modality — not a consolation prize.
  - **Basic mat:** $15–30 at Amazon, Walmart, or Target. Adequate for beginners. Premium mats ($50–120) are unnecessary.
  - **Yoga apps (paid):** Down Dog $8/month ($96/year); Yoga With Kassandra $14/month ($168/year). Glo $18/month ($216/year). Budget option: many free apps exist.
  - **YMCA with financial assistance:** Sliding scale at $30k income likely yields $20–35/month ($240–420/year). Includes yoga classes plus full facility access.
  - **Community/donation-based yoga:** Variable availability. Some studios offer $5–10 community classes or donation-based sessions, but availability in low-income areas is limited.
  - **Studio classes:** $15–25/class, $80–200/month unlimited. Out of Bob's range without assistance.
  - **Low estimate ($5)** = already owns or borrows a mat, free YouTube classes
  - **Mid estimate ($60)** = new mat amortized + free YouTube or cheap app
  - **High estimate ($300)** = YMCA with financial assistance or paid app subscription

## Time Commitment

- **Hours per week:** 2.0 (3 sessions × 40 min average)
- **Category:** Medium (1–3 hr/wk)
- Note: Home practice eliminates travel time, making yoga one of the most time-efficient structured exercise options. Beginner sessions can be as short as 20 min (YouTube offers 10-, 20-, 30-, 45-, and 60-min options). The 2.0 hours/week estimate assumes 3 × 40-min sessions, which is the dose supported by cardiovascular and mental health evidence.

## Access and Barriers

- **Activation energy:** Low (for home practice) / Medium (for classes)
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - **Cultural/gender perception:** Yoga is perceived as a "women's activity" in many male demographics. CDC NHIS 2022: 23.3% of women vs. 10.3% of men practice yoga. This is the single biggest barrier for Bob if male.
  - **Income/class perception:** Only 10.4% of adults below 200% FPL practice yoga vs. 23.0% at 400%+ FPL. Yoga studios are concentrated in affluent areas and signal a lifestyle Bob may not identify with.
  - **Physical limitations at high BMI:** Obese beginners need modified poses. Chair yoga and beginner-specific content exists, but standard studio classes may not accommodate well. YouTube content actually handles this better (private, self-paced, modification-focused channels exist).
  - **Flexibility self-consciousness:** "I can't do yoga because I'm not flexible" is the #1 stated barrier — ironic since inflexibility is exactly what yoga addresses.
  - **Home practice requires self-motivation:** No social accountability of a class environment. Compensated by zero cost and zero commute.
  - **Space requirements:** Needs a mat-sized clear floor area. Feasible in most housing but can be a real constraint in very small spaces.

## Side Effects and Risks

- **Musculoskeletal injury:** Low risk overall. Systematic review (Cramer et al. 2015) found injury rates comparable to other exercise modalities. Most common: shoulder, knee, and wrist strain from weight-bearing poses.
- **Risk for obese beginners:** Higher risk of joint strain in weight-bearing poses. Modifications are essential. Chair yoga and beginner-specific sequences mitigate this.
- **Rare serious events:** Vertebral artery dissection from extreme cervical rotation (headstand, shoulder stand) — extremely rare and avoidable by excluding advanced inversions.
- **Net risk assessment:** Very low with appropriate modifications. Yoga is one of the safest exercise modalities, especially for beginners following guided video content that emphasizes modifications.

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate:** 0.25
- **Source:** Estimated from RCT dropout meta-analysis (Cramer et al. 2016) adjusted for real-world conditions and Bob's demographic barriers
- **Notes:**
  - RCT dropout rates average 11.42% (95% CI: 10.11%, 12.73%) across 168 RCTs — translating to ~89% retention. But RCT conditions (structured, supervised, free) overstate real-world adherence.
  - CDC data shows only 10.4% of adults below 200% FPL practice yoga — implying very low uptake and persistence in Bob's bracket.
  - The 25% estimate reflects: of those who start a yoga program, approximately 25% are still practicing regularly at 12 months. This is lower than strength training (35%) because cultural/gender barriers create additional friction beyond typical exercise dropout.
  - Home practice may paradoxically have both lower and higher adherence — lower because no accountability, higher because zero-barrier access means resumed practice after gaps is trivial.
  - Adherence improves with: consistent scheduling, beginner-specific content, visible flexibility gains (faster feedback than weight loss or cardiovascular improvements).

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 2
- **Tier 2** = Some RCTs, or strong observational data, or good data but population mismatch
- **Key studies:**
  - Groessl CE, Liu L, Chang DG, et al. (2020). Cost-effectiveness of Yoga for Chronic Low Back Pain in Veterans. *Value in Health*, 23(10):1307-1315.
  - Aboagye E, Karlsson ML, Hagberg J, Jensen I. (2017). Cost-effectiveness of yoga for managing musculoskeletal conditions in the workplace. *Occup Med*, 67(9):687-695.
  - Skjøth MM, et al. (2024). Effectiveness and cost-effectiveness of chair-based yoga in older adults with multiple long-term conditions. *Age and Ageing*.
  - Cramer H, Lauche R, Langhorst J, Dobos G. (2013). Yoga for depression: A systematic review and meta-analysis. *Depress Anxiety*, 30(11):1068-83.
  - Jeter PE, Slutsky J, Singh N, Khalsa SBS. (2015). Yoga participation and all-cause mortality: National prospective cohort study. *Complement Ther Med*, 23(6):757-762.
  - Cramer H, et al. (2016). A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis Estimating the Expected Dropout Rates in Randomized Controlled Trials on Yoga Interventions. *Evid Based Complement Alternat Med*.
- **Limitations:**
  - Direct QALY evidence exists but is condition-specific — no general-population QALY study for yoga
  - No significant all-cause mortality association after adjustment, unlike walking and strength training
  - Study populations are predominantly female, white, and higher-SES — significant mismatch with Bob
  - Most studies are small-to-moderate in size (150–454 participants)
  - Yoga interventions are heterogeneous — "yoga" ranges from gentle chair-based to vigorous vinyasa, making cross-study comparison imprecise
  - Blinding is impossible in exercise RCTs; expectation effects may inflate benefit estimates
  - Tier 2 rather than Tier 1 because: (a) no mortality evidence, (b) significant population mismatch, (c) condition-specific rather than general-population QALY data, despite having multiple RCTs with direct QALY measurement
