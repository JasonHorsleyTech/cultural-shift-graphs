## Methodology
- **Definition:** Population aged 25–64 as a percentage of total population, both sexes combined.
- **Universe:** Resident population (plus Armed Forces overseas for PE-11 files, 1947–1979). All races, all states included (except Alaska and Hawaii excluded pre-1960 in PE-11 data — negligible effect on percentages since AK+HI were <0.4% of total population).
- **Calculation:** Sum of single-year-of-age population counts for ages 25 through 64, divided by total population, times 100.
- **Timing:** July 1 estimates for all years (mid-year population).
- **Known methodology changes:**
  - 1960: Alaska and Hawaii added to PE-11 estimates (previously excluded).
  - 1980: Transition from PE-11 series to intercensal PQI format. Population universe shifts to resident population only (excludes armed forces overseas). Discontinuity is minimal.
  - 1990: Transition to PMP intercensal format with expanded race/ethnicity detail.
  - 2000: Transition to new intercensal dataset format with multi-race categories.
  - 2010: Transition from intercensal (backward-revised) to postcensal (forward) estimates.
  - 2020: New postcensal series anchored to 2020 Census.

## Gaps and Caveats
- **No missing years.** Complete annual data from 1947 through 2024.
- **Pre-1960 excludes Alaska and Hawaii.** The PE-11 estimates for 1947–1959 exclude AK and HI, which were not yet states. Combined AK+HI population was ~630,000 in 1950 (~0.4% of total), with a broadly similar age structure, so the effect on the working-age percentage is negligible (<0.1 pp).
- **Population universe varies slightly across series.** PE-11 (1947–1979) counts "resident population plus Armed Forces overseas." Later series count resident population only. Armed forces overseas numbered ~1–3 million in the late 1940s–1950s (primarily ages 18–30), declining to ~300K by the 1970s. This slightly inflates the under-25 denominator in early years, meaning the working-age share may be ~0.1–0.3 pp lower in 1947–1955 than it would be under a pure resident definition.
- **Series transitions at 1980, 1990, 2000, 2010, 2020.** Each decade uses a different Census dataset. Intercensal estimates (1980–2010) are backward-revised to match both bounding censuses and are highly reliable. Postcensal estimates (2011–2024) are forward projections from the 2020 Census, subject to later revision. All transitions are smooth (no jumps >0.5 pp at boundaries), indicating good consistency.
- **Confidence: High across all years.** Census population estimates by age are among the most reliable government statistics. The annual estimates are benchmarked to decennial census counts and use administrative records (births, deaths, immigration) for intercensal years.

## Interpretation

The working-age (25–64) share tells a clear demographic story driven by the Baby Boom:

1. **1947–1950 (~50–51%):** Post-war starting point. Pre-baby-boom age structure still dominant. The 25–64 cohort was about half the population.
2. **1950–1967 (decline to ~44%):** The Baby Boom (1946–1964) flooded the under-25 population. By 1967, the working-age share hit its nadir as boomers were all still under 25 while being a massive share of total population.
3. **1968–2011 (rise to ~53%):** Baby Boomers entered the 25–64 age range starting in the early 1970s. Combined with declining birth rates (fewer children), the working-age share rose steadily for four decades, peaking around 2010–2011.
4. **2012–2024 (decline to ~51%):** Baby Boomers began turning 65 in 2011 (born 1946). The working-age share has been declining since, and Census projections suggest it will continue falling to ~48–49% by the 2030s.

**Implication for "share of the pie":** When comparing working-age median earnings to GDP per capita, the changing denominator matters. In 1967, the median worker was one of only 44% of the population in working age — GDP per capita was "diluted" across many children. By 2010, 53% were working age. This 9-percentage-point shift means GDP per capita rose partly because a larger fraction of the population was of working age, not just because productivity increased. Conversely, the post-2011 decline means GDP per capita growth is being achieved despite a shrinking working-age share.
