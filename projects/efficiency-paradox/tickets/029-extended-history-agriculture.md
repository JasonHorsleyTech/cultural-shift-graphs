# Extended History: Agriculture Yields and Inputs 1950–1990

## What to Research

Agricultural yield improvements and input changes during the Green Revolution era (1950–1990), before the primary study window. This shows the period of most dramatic yield gains and how inputs responded.

## Where to Look

1. **Primary:** USDA NASS historical data (crop yields by year — available from 1866 for many crops).
2. **Secondary:** USDA ERS "Agricultural Productivity in the US" — total factor productivity indices going back to 1948.
3. **Tertiary:** USDA ERS — fertilizer use data (total nitrogen, phosphate, potash applied, from ~1960).
4. **For land use:** USDA ERS Major Land Uses (decadal estimates of cropland).

## Scope

- 1950–1990, annual where possible (decadal for fertilizer/land use if annual unavailable).
- Key metrics:
  1. Corn yield per acre (the poster child of the Green Revolution in the US)
  2. Total cropland acreage
  3. Total commercial fertilizer applied (nitrogen specifically)
  4. Total farm energy use (diesel, electricity for irrigation)

**Source:** USDA ERS Fertilizer Use data (derived from AAPFCO data)

| Year | Value | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|

## Data Gaps

- Fertilizer data pre-1960 is sparse (commercial fertilizer use was much lower before Green Revolution).
- Farm energy data pre-1965 may be rough estimates.
- Accept decadal values for inputs if annual isn't available.

## Methodology Notes

- Green Revolution in the US = hybrid corn (1930s-50s), synthetic nitrogen fertilizer (post-WWII), mechanization, irrigation expansion, pesticides (1950s-60s).
- The key insight for this period: yields tripled, but so did fertilizer use. Land use was roughly flat (some decline due to urbanization and CRP precursor programs). The "efficiency" of land use improved enormously, but it was achieved by substituting chemicals and energy for land — not a "free lunch."
- This is a different story from the primary window (1990-2020) where yields continued rising but fertilizer use plateaued (precision agriculture, improved nutrient management).

## What "Good Data" Looks Like

- Corn yield: ~38 bu/acre (1950) → ~120 bu/acre (1990). Roughly 3x in 40 years.
- Cropland: ~380M acres (1950) → ~340M acres (1990). About 10% decline.
- Nitrogen fertilizer: ~1 million tons (1950) → ~11 million tons (1990). About 11x increase.
- The punchline: land efficiency tripled, and we did use slightly less land — but the gains came from massive increases in other inputs (fertilizer, energy, water). It's not "efficiency freed up land" so much as "we substituted chemicals for land."
