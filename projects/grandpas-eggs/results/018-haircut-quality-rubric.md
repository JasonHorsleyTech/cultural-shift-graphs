# Men's Haircut — Quality Rubric

## The 1955 baseline product

A standard men's haircut at a neighborhood barbershop, performed by a state-licensed barber. The 1950s were the golden age of American barbershops — nearly every neighborhood had one, and men visited every 2–3 weeks. The typical service included:

- **Haircut** with hand-operated clippers and steel shears. Popular styles: crew cut, flat top, pompadour, side part, ducktail. These required genuine skill and precision.
- **Razor neck cleanup** with a straight razor, stropped before use.
- **Finishing touches**: talcum powder on the neck, pomade or hair tonic applied, sometimes a hot towel.
- **Duration**: approximately 20–30 minutes in the chair.
- **Setting**: leather chairs, checkered floors, large mirrors, magazines (Field & Stream, newspapers). The barbershop functioned as a social hub — conversation was part of the service.

Cost was roughly $1.00–$1.50 (varying by city; some small towns charged as little as $0.50–$0.75). There was essentially one tier of service: your neighborhood barber.

## Quality-equivalent product definition

A standard men's haircut at an **independent neighborhood barbershop** in the $25–$40 range (national average ~$30–$35). This tier provides:

- 20–30 minutes of service
- Razor neck cleanup with a straight or safety razor
- Some finishing (styling product, hot towel at some shops)
- A personal relationship with the barber (regulars get consistent service)

This is NOT the premium/upscale barbershop ($50–$80+) with craft cocktails, elaborate hot towel rituals, and luxury branding. That product goes well beyond what the 1955 baseline was. The quality-equivalent is the workhorse independent shop that still operates like barbershops always have.

Brand/tier examples: independent neighborhood barbershops, Floyd's 99 Barbershop (mid-tier chain), Sport Clips (borderline — includes some extras). NOT Great Clips or Supercuts (too stripped down). NOT premium shops like Blind Barber or high-end grooming lounges (too upscale).

## Divergence timeline

**Pre-1975: Single tier.** There was essentially one product — the neighborhood barbershop haircut. Prices were locally set, often by barber associations or even state price boards. No meaningful quality differentiation.

**1960s–1970s: Industry disruption (but not yet a quality split).** The counterculture's long-hair movement devastated barbershops. Twenty thousand barbers left the profession nationwide. Unisex salons rose, offering longer, styled cuts that traditional barbershops resisted. This was a demand disruption, not yet a quality tier split.

**1975–1985: The budget tier emerges.** Supercuts opened in 1975, charging $6 for a no-appointment, no-frills haircut. Great Clips followed in 1982. These chains industrialized the haircut: standardized processes, faster throughput (15 min target vs. 25 min), cosmetologists instead of traditional barbers, minimal finishing services. This is when Line A and Line B begin to diverge — not on the technical quality of the cut itself, but on the service scope and experience.

**2010s–present: Premium tier emerges above the old baseline.** A barbershop renaissance created a new top tier — upscale shops with craft beer, hot towel rituals, leather chairs, and $50–$80+ haircuts. This pushed the old-school neighborhood barbershop into the middle tier, which is where the quality-equivalent sits.

**Divergence year for pricing purposes: ~1975** (founding of Supercuts). Before this, report a single price. After, the budget chain price and the standard barbershop price begin to separate.

## Overall assessment

**No change.** This is Baumol's cost disease in its purest form. The core product — one human cutting another human's hair — is fundamentally unchanged. A barber in 2025 cannot cut hair meaningfully faster than a barber in 1955; there have been no productivity breakthroughs equivalent to what happened in manufacturing or agriculture.

The budget tier (Great Clips, Supercuts) has trimmed service scope and time — less finishing, no razor work, 12–20 minutes instead of 25. But the actual haircut is arguably equal or better, thanks to superior electric clippers with precision guards. The quality "gap" is in the experience wrapper, not the haircut itself.

The price increase from ~$1.25 (1955) to ~$20 (2025 budget chain) or ~$35 (2025 quality-equiv) is almost entirely explained by the fact that barber wages must keep pace with wages in sectors that DID see productivity growth. A barber in 2025 needs to earn a living in a 2025 economy, but still produces only one haircut per 20 minutes — exactly as in 1955.

**Why this matters for the graph:** The haircut should show Line A and Line B nearly overlapping, with both rising steeply in labor-minutes terms (or modestly, since wages rose too). The productivity benchmark line will plunge downward (representing what the haircut "should" cost if it benefited from economy-wide productivity gains), while the actual price holds steady or rises in labor-minutes — visually demonstrating that the service sector didn't participate in the productivity revolution. This is the Baumol's control case that makes the quality-tax story in other commodities more convincing by contrast.
