# UV Radiation (Tanning Beds) → Skin Cancer

## Data

- slug: uv-tanning-beds
- hazard: Tanning beds (artificial UV)
- harm: Melanoma and other skin cancers
- category: behavioral-mandate
- early_evidence_year: 1986
- early_evidence_desc: Multiple case-control studies (Gallagher et al., Holman et al., 1986) first reported statistically significant associations between sunlamp/sunbed use and cutaneous melanoma risk, building on earlier suggestive evidence from Klepp & Magnus (1979) and Adam et al. (1981)
- consensus_year: 2009
- consensus_desc: IARC reclassified UV-emitting tanning devices from Group 2A ("probably carcinogenic") to Group 1 ("carcinogenic to humans"), based on a meta-analysis showing 75% increased melanoma risk when tanning bed use begins before age 30
- first_regulation_year: 1997
- first_regulation_country: France
- first_regulation_desc: Decree 97-617 prohibited commercial tanning bed use by persons under 18, with mandatory health warnings on all tanning devices — the first binding restriction on artificial UV tanning in any OECD country
- us_regulation_year: 2010
- us_regulation_desc: Affordable Care Act imposed a 10% federal excise tax on indoor tanning services (effective July 2010); FDA reclassified tanning devices from Class I to Class II in 2014 with mandatory cancer risk warnings and recommendation against use by under-18s. No federal ban exists.
- regulation_status: completed
- industry_opposition: moderate
- substitute_available: partial
- harm_visibility: medium
- exposure_reduction_50_year: 2015
- lag_from_evidence: 11
- lag_from_consensus: -12
- notes: Unusual case where first binding regulation (France 1997 minors ban) preceded formal scientific consensus (IARC 2009) by 12 years. Regulatory responses range from total commercial bans (Brazil 2009, Australia 2014-2015) to age restrictions (France 1997, UK 2010, Germany 2012) to mere taxation and warnings (US). The US has no federal ban on tanning beds; the ACA tax and FDA reclassification are the strongest federal actions. Indoor tanning among US high school students declined from 15.6% in 2009 to 5.6% in 2017, driven by both regulation and public awareness campaigns.

## Sources

1. Gallagher RP et al. (1986). "Sunlight exposure, pigmentation factors, and risk of nonmelanocytic skin cancer." Archives of Dermatology, 122(12):1455-60 — early case-control study examining artificial UV exposure and skin cancer risk.
2. Holman CD et al. (1986). "Risk factors for cutaneous malignant melanoma in Western Australia." International Journal of Cancer — case-control study including sunlamp use as a risk factor.
3. Autier P et al. (1994). EORTC multicenter case-control study examining sunbed use and cutaneous malignant melanoma in Belgium, France, and Germany.
4. Westerdahl J et al. (1994). "Use of sunbeds or sunlamps and malignant melanoma in southern Sweden." American Journal of Epidemiology — key Swedish case-control study.
5. International Agency for Research on Cancer (2009). "A review of human carcinogens—Part D: Radiation." Lancet Oncology 10(8):751-2. IARC Working Group reclassified UV-emitting tanning devices to Group 1 carcinogen.
6. IARC Working Group (2006). Meta-analysis of 19 studies: 15% increased melanoma risk among ever-users of indoor tanning; 75% increased risk when first use before age 30. Published as Boniol et al. in the BMJ, 2012.
7. France Decree No. 97-617 (1997). Decree relating to the sale and availability to the public of certain tanning devices using ultraviolet radiation — banned under-18 access, required health warnings.
8. Brazil ANVISA Resolution RDC 56/09 (November 2009). Complete ban on commercial tanning beds for cosmetic purposes — first country to implement a total ban.
9. Australian state governments (2014-2015). Commercial solarium bans enacted state by state; nationwide ban effective January 2016.
10. US Affordable Care Act, Section 5000B (2010). 10% federal excise tax on indoor tanning services, effective July 1, 2010.
11. US FDA (2014). Final rule reclassifying UV lamps for tanning from Class I to Class II (Federal Register 79 FR 31205, June 2, 2014). Required black-box warning and recommendation against under-18 use.
12. FTC (2010). Settlement with Indoor Tanning Association over deceptive advertising claiming tanning beds were safe. FTC charged ITA with making false health and safety claims.
13. CDC Youth Risk Behavior Survey. Indoor tanning prevalence among US high school students declined from 15.6% (2009) to 5.6% (2017). Among white female students: 37.4% (2009) to 10.1% (2017).
14. American Suntanning Association — industry lobbying group that claims to have defeated over 100 under-18 tanning ban bills in US state legislatures since 2013.

## Judgment calls

1. **Early evidence year (1986 vs earlier):** The natural UV-skin cancer link was recognized much earlier (early 1900s), but this ticket is specifically about artificial UV from tanning devices. Klepp & Magnus (1979) and Adam et al. (1981) are earlier references but are either case reports or less rigorous. The 1986 case-control studies by Gallagher et al. and Holman et al. represent the first formal epidemiological evidence specifically implicating artificial UV exposure (sunlamps/sunbeds) in melanoma risk. I chose 1986 over 1979 because the earlier work was suggestive rather than establishing a credible epidemiological link.

2. **France 1997 as first regulation:** The ticket flags whether a minors-only ban counts as "meaningful regulation reducing population exposure." I judged it does count because: (a) it was a binding law, not a guideline; (b) teenagers and young adults are the highest-use demographic and the group where early exposure most increases lifetime melanoma risk; (c) France's approach was subsequently adopted by dozens of countries, becoming the dominant regulatory model. The alternative — using Brazil's 2009 total ban — would miss over a decade of binding OECD regulation. Note that Brazil was not an OECD member in 2009 (it acceded in 2024), so even if we wanted the first total ban in an OECD country, that would be Australia (2014-2015).

3. **US regulation (2010 vs null):** The US has no federal tanning bed ban. The ACA's 10% excise tax (2010) is a binding federal regulation that does reduce exposure by increasing cost. The FDA's 2014 reclassification added mandatory warnings and a recommendation (not a ban) against under-18 use. I used 2010 as us_regulation_year because the tax is a binding federal action with measurable impact on demand, even though it is far weaker than an outright ban. A case could be made for "null" if the standard is a ban.

4. **Category (behavioral-mandate vs product-withdrawal):** The regulatory landscape includes both age-based access restrictions (behavioral mandates) and outright bans (product withdrawals). Since the first and most common form of regulation is age-based access restriction (France 1997, UK 2010, many US states), I classified this as behavioral-mandate. If the focus were on Brazil's or Australia's total bans, product-withdrawal would be appropriate.

5. **Negative lag_from_consensus (-12 years):** First regulation preceded formal consensus, which is unusual. France acted on accumulating evidence in the 1990s rather than waiting for IARC's definitive classification. This makes tanning beds one of the rare cases where precautionary regulation outpaced formal scientific consensus, at least in the narrow form of minors-only bans.

6. **Industry opposition (moderate):** The Indoor Tanning Association actively lobbied against regulation, used misleading advertising (resulting in FTC enforcement), and the American Suntanning Association claims to have defeated 100+ state bills. However, the tanning industry ($5B in the US) is far smaller than tobacco, fossil fuels, or chemical industries, and the opposition, while sustained, was not on the scale of multi-decade, science-denying campaigns by those industries.

7. **Exposure reduction 50% year (2015):** CDC YRBS data shows US high school indoor tanning prevalence dropped from 15.6% (2009) to approximately 7-8% by 2015 (and to 5.6% by 2017). The 50% reduction point from 2009 peak levels was approximately 2015. This represents youth specifically; adult usage also declined but data is less granular.
