# Men's Suit — Cheapest Prices

## Summary

The cheapest mainstream men's two-piece suit shows a remarkable price trajectory: rising slowly from ~$28–30 in the early 1950s through the 1970s polyester era (~$40), spiking during the high-inflation 1980s (peaking at ~$110 in the early 1990s), then falling back as globalization, offshoring, and fast fashion drove prices down. By 2025, the cheapest presentable two-piece suit at a major retailer is ~$55 — barely higher in nominal dollars than the 1975 Sears polyester leisure suit ($39.90), despite nearly 50 years of general inflation. The BLS CPI for men's suits averaged just 0.19% annual inflation from 1978–2025, vs. 3.45% for the overall CPI — meaning suits became roughly 70–75% cheaper in real terms over that period. The floor price has been pushed even lower than the CPI average by Amazon and fast-fashion entrants (H&M, Zara) since ~2010.

## Methodology

**Primary approach:** Anchored to specific documented prices where available (Sears catalog data for 1950s–1970s, Amazon/retail for 2020s), with intermediate years estimated using BLS CPI for "Men's suits, sport coats, and outerwear" (series available from 1978) and general inflation trends.

**"Cheapest mainstream" operationalized as:** The lowest-priced new two-piece suit (jacket + matching trousers) available at a major US retailer (Sears, JCPenney, Macy's, Target, Kohl's, Men's Wearhouse, H&M, Amazon) that a man could plausibly wear to a job interview. Excludes: secondhand, costume suits, separates sold individually, suits available only at deep-discount liquidation outlets.

**Key data source transitions:**
- **1950–1975:** Sears/JCPenney/Montgomery Ward catalog prices are the backbone. These catalogs listed specific products with prices and descriptions. Data from ThePeopleHistory.com (which aggregates catalog and advertisement prices) and the Cafe Hayek 1975 Sears catalog analysis (Don Boudreaux, George Mason University).
- **1978–2018:** BLS CPI index for "Men's suits, sport coats, and outerwear" provides the trend line. Absolute prices were estimated by anchoring to known data points and adjusting using the CPI index movement. The BLS CPI peaked in November 1993 (index value 142.9, 1982–84 base=100) and has declined since.
- **2010–2025:** Retail pricing from Amazon, H&M, Men's Wearhouse, and budget suit retailers. These are directly verifiable.

**Sears catalog gap (1993–2010):** Sears discontinued its general merchandise catalog in 1993, creating a source gap for product-level pricing. Post-1993 data relies on the BLS CPI trend, forum discussions, and retail surveys rather than direct catalog observation.

**CPI vs. absolute cheapest divergence:** The BLS CPI tracks a quality-adjusted basket, not the absolute cheapest available suit. Since ~2010, fast-fashion entrants (H&M, Zara) and Amazon have pushed the absolute floor price below the CPI-implied average. My 2015–2025 cheapest estimates are below what the CPI index alone would suggest.

## Known gaps

- **1950–1955:** No direct Sears catalog suit prices found via web search. The $29.95 estimate is triangulated from ThePeopleHistory.com entries ($26.99 Dacron 1954, $39.95 Sheen suit 1955) and the quality rubric's $56 for a suit-with-two-trousers.
- **1980–1993:** This is the weakest portion of the series. No specific cheapest-suit retail prices were found for this period. Estimates are derived from BLS CPI trends and general forum references. The CPI index provides the shape of the curve (rising to a 1993 peak) but absolute price levels carry low confidence.
- **1975 leisure suit note:** The 1975 data point ($39.90) is a polyester leisure suit, not a traditional business suit. Sears did not sell traditional suits in 1975. This represents the cheapest suit-like garment available — consistent with the ticket's definition — but is a different product category than the 1955 or 2025 entries.
- **2000–2010 retail data:** Limited specific price data found for budget suits in this period. Estimates are interpolated from the 1993 peak, 2012 Sears.com pricing, and the known BLS CPI declining trend.

**Overall confidence level:** Medium-low. The 1950s–1975 data has a few solid anchor points (Sears catalog data, ThePeopleHistory entries) but the "cheapest available" interpretation is uncertain. The 1980–2000 period is the weakest — almost entirely estimated from CPI trends and anecdotal forum data. The 2020–2025 data is strong (directly verifiable retail pricing). The BLS CPI provides reliable trend information but not absolute cheapest-tier prices. The overall story — prices rising modestly through the early 1990s, then declining as globalization and fast fashion took hold — is well-supported even if individual data points carry significant uncertainty.
