# Professional Athlete — Sports Entertainment

Athletes are among the highest-paid workers anywhere. But sports franchise revenues are even more extreme. A $50 ticket to an NFL game, with 70,000 fans, is $3.5M in gate revenue for one game — and that's before TV deals, merchandise, sponsorships, and luxury suites.

**Fundamental worker:** Professional athlete (pick ONE league — NFL, NBA, or MLB — whichever has the most accessible historical data for both player compensation and ticket prices)
**Customer cost proxy:** Average ticket price for the chosen league. Or total franchise revenue per game (gate + media + merch) divided by games played, if that data is available.

**Interesting angles:**
- The salary cap vs. revenue split: NFL players get ~48% of revenue, NBA players get ~50%. These are negotiated collectively. Has the player share changed over time? (It's actually been remarkably stable since collective bargaining began — but what about before free agency?)
- Pre-free-agency era (before 1976 in MLB, 1993 in NFL): players were paid a fraction of their value. The ratio was probably much worse.
- Minor league / lower league athletes make poverty wages while the parent organization makes billions. The ratio for a AAA baseball player vs. MLB ticket prices is a horror show.
