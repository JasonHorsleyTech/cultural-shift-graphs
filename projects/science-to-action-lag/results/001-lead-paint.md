## Sources

1. Gibson, J.L. (1904). "A plea for painted railings and painted walls of rooms as the source of lead poisoning amongst Queensland children." Australasian Medical Gazette. First scientific publication linking lead paint in homes to neurological damage in children.
2. Byers, R.K. & Lord, E.E. (1943). "Late effects of lead poisoning on mental development." American Journal of Diseases of Children, 66(5), 471-494. First study demonstrating persistent neurological/behavioral deficits in children recovered from lead poisoning.
3. Needleman, H.L. et al. (1979). "Deficits in psychologic and classroom performance of children with elevated dentine lead levels." New England Journal of Medicine, 300(13), 689-695. Landmark study quantifying IQ loss (6-7 points) from subclinical lead exposure.
4. ILO White Lead (Painting) Convention No. 13 (1921). International Labour Organization convention recommending prohibition of white lead in painting. Over 50 countries ratified; US declined.
5. Lead-Based Paint Poisoning Prevention Act (1971), Public Law 91-695. US federal law restricting lead paint in federally-funded housing.
6. Consumer Product Safety Commission, 16 CFR Part 1303 (1977, effective 1978). Federal ban on lead-containing paint for consumer use.
7. Markowitz, G. & Rosner, D. (2013). "Lead Wars: The Politics of Science and the Fate of America's Children." University of California Press. Documents Lead Industries Association's multi-decade campaign against regulation.
8. CDC NHANES data on blood lead levels in children (1976-2016). Documents 94.5% decline in blood lead levels from 1976-1980 baseline to 2011-2016.
9. Wikipedia, "Lead paint" and "Lead-based paint in the United States" — verified key dates against primary legislative sources.

## Judgment calls

**Early evidence year (1904):** Gibson's 1904 publication is the first credible scientific work linking lead paint specifically in homes to neurological damage in children. Earlier reports of lead toxicity exist (occupational poisoning of painters dates to antiquity), but Gibson was first to establish the household paint → child neurological harm pathway with clinical evidence. Alice Hamilton's work (1910s-1920s) was primarily occupational/industrial and focused on workers, not residential child exposure.

**Consensus year (1970):** This is the formal consensus on childhood neurological harm specifically. The ILO convention (1921) represents earlier international consensus on general lead paint toxicity (primarily occupational), but the specific recognition that household lead paint damages children's neurological development was formalized by the Surgeon General in 1970. The choice of 1970 over 1943 (Byers & Lord) reflects that a single study doesn't constitute consensus — it took decades for the medical establishment to formally accept what Byers demonstrated.

**Negative lag_from_consensus (-61):** The first regulation (France, 1909) preceded formal consensus on childhood neurological damage (1970) by 61 years. This is because the early bans were based on general lead toxicity evidence (worker poisoning), not the specific childhood neurological mechanism established later. This makes lead paint unusual — early-acting countries regulated on precautionary/general-toxicity grounds, while late-acting countries (especially the US) demanded specific evidence of the harm mechanism before acting. The US lag from consensus is +8 years (1978-1970), which better represents the "evidence → action" gap for the childhood neurological framing.

**First regulation (France, 1909):** Belgium and Austria also banned white lead in interior paint the same year (1909). I selected France as it is most frequently cited as the primary example and the law covered both interior and exterior use. All three are OECD members.

**Substitute availability:** Titanium dioxide-based paints became commercially available in the 1920s-1930s. By the time of the US ban (1978), zinc oxide and titanium dioxide alternatives had been on the market for decades. The Lead Industries Association's extended campaign succeeded despite viable alternatives existing for 40+ years.

**Exposure reduction year (1988):** NHANES data shows geometric mean blood lead in US children aged 1-5 dropped from ~15 μg/dL (1976-1980) to ~3.6 μg/dL (1988-1991). The 50% mark was crossed approximately 1988. IMPORTANT CAVEAT: this decline is driven by both the lead paint ban (1978) AND the leaded gasoline phaseout (1973-1996). The two interventions are inseparable in the biomonitoring data. The paint ban's isolated contribution cannot be quantified from NHANES alone.

**Industry opposition (strong):** The Lead Industries Association operated from 1928 to at least the 2000s. Key actions: blocked US ratification of the 1921 ILO convention; framed childhood lead poisoning as a "slum problem" (1957 annual meeting); challenged EPA air quality standards in court (Lead Industries Association v. EPA, 1980); funded research to raise doubt about low-level effects. This is among the most sustained and well-documented industry opposition campaigns in regulatory history, comparable to tobacco.

**Harm visibility (low):** Acute lead poisoning is visible, but the population-level harm — subclinical IQ reduction of 6-7 points, behavioral changes — is invisible to individuals and only detectable through epidemiological studies. Parents cannot see their child losing IQ points. This invisibility is a key reason the industry campaign succeeded for decades.
