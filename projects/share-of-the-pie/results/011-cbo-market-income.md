# Result: CBO Market Income by Quintile, 1979–2021

## Source
- **Primary source:** Congressional Budget Office, "The Distribution of Household Income in 2021" (Publication 60341), September 2024. Supplemental data tables.
- **Companion report:** CBO, "Trends in the Distribution of Household Income From 1979 to 2021" (Publication 60342), September 2024.
- **Data file:** CBO supplemental data spreadsheet (60341-supplemental-data.xlsx), accessed via Google Sheets mirror.
- **Date accessed:** 2026-04-30

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Market income = labor income + business income + capital income (including capital gains) + income from other sources (employer-sponsored retirement, other). Excludes ALL government transfers (Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, SNAP, unemployment insurance, etc.) and excludes federal taxes.
- **Universe:** All U.S. households. CBO ranks individuals by their household's size-adjusted income (equivalized), then divides the population into quintiles of equal numbers of people (not households). The number of households per quintile varies because household sizes differ across the distribution.
- **Unit:** Average (mean) household market income per income group, in constant 2021 dollars. Inflation adjustment uses the BEA price index for personal consumption expenditures (PCE). Values are rounded to the nearest $100.
- **Size adjustment note:** CBO uses income equivalized by household size (dividing by the square root of household size) for ranking purposes only. The dollar values reported here are total household income, NOT per-person or equivalized income. This is important: a $70,400 middle-quintile figure is the average total income for households ranked in the middle fifth, not individual income.
- **Quintile construction:** Unlike Census quintiles (which rank households), CBO quintiles rank people — each quintile has ~equal numbers of people. Larger households tend to sort into different quintiles than they would under Census ranking. This makes CBO quintiles not directly comparable to Census quintiles.

## Gaps and Caveats
- **No missing years.** Complete annual series from 1979–2021 (43 data points per quintile).
- **Constant 2021 dollars.** All values are inflation-adjusted using the PCE price index, NOT CPI. PCE typically shows lower inflation than CPI, so growth rates will differ slightly from CPI-adjusted series. The plan.md specifies "nominal dollars unless stated otherwise" but CBO only publishes this data in constant dollars (their primary presentation). The graph phase should note the base year and avoid double-deflating.
- **These are means, not medians.** CBO reports average (mean) income per quintile, not median. Within each quintile, the mean can be pulled by outliers, especially in the top quintile and top 1% (where capital gains create massive spikes in individual years — note the $3.1M top 1% figure in 2021 vs $2.1M in 2019).
- **CBO rebasing.** The September 2024 report uses 2021 as the base year. Earlier CBO reports used different base years (e.g., 2019 dollars). Values here supersede any earlier-base-year figures.
- **Market income vs. other CBO measures:** Market income EXCLUDES all government transfers (Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, SNAP, unemployment, etc.) and EXCLUDES taxes. It is the "what did the market give you?" baseline. This is different from CBO's "income before transfers and taxes" (which adds social insurance benefits to market income) and "income after transfers and taxes" (the comprehensive measure). Ticket 012 covers after-tax income; ticket 013 covers tax rates.
- **Equivalized ranking, unequalized dollars:** CBO ranks people by household income divided by the square root of household size for sorting purposes. But the dollar values in the table are total household market income (unequalized). Do not divide these by household size for per-person figures — CBO already handles composition effects in how it ranks households.
- **2020–2021 pandemic distortion:** The 2020 bottom-quintile dip ($15,200, down from $18,500 in 2019) reflects pandemic labor market effects. The 2021 top-1% spike ($3.1M) reflects massive capital gains from asset price inflation. These are real values in the data, not measurement errors, but they are not representative of long-run trends.
- **Capital gains realization timing.** The top quintile and top 1% are heavily affected by capital gains realization timing, creating year-to-year volatility (e.g., 1986 Tax Reform Act drove massive realizations in 1986, visible as a spike). This is real income by CBO's definition but should be interpreted with the understanding that timing of realizations is partly discretionary.
- **Confidence: HIGH.** This is CBO's definitive data product, based on merged IRS tax return data and Census survey data. The methodology is transparent and has been refined over decades.

## Key patterns for the graph phase
- Middle quintile real market income grew ~21% over 42 years (1979–2021): $58,300 → $70,400. That's about 0.4% real annual growth.
- Top quintile grew ~154%: $158,700 → $403,400.
- Top 1% grew ~403%: $618,100 → $3,110,500.
- Bottom quintile grew ~39%: $11,100 → $15,400, but the trajectory is not monotonic — it was stagnant 1979–1994, rose in the late 1990s boom, fell in recessions, and remains volatile.
- The ratio of top quintile to bottom quintile widened from 14.3:1 (1979) to 26.2:1 (2021).
