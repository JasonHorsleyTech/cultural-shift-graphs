# English Loanwords in Spanish

## Summary
English loanwords in Spanish trace a distinctive arc from near-zero in 1800 — when French dominated foreign borrowing via the Bourbon court — through a slow British-driven trickle in the late 19th century (sports, industry), a post-WWII American surge (film, music, consumer brands), and an explosive internet-era acceleration that made English the near-exclusive source of new foreign vocabulary by 2000. The key inflection points are: the 1880s Industrial Revolution wave (sports, transport), the 1950s American cultural expansion into Latin America (Hollywood, consumer brands), and the 1990s internet revolution (tech vocabulary almost entirely English). According to Pedrero (2007), the DRAE accepted only 6 anglicisms from 1780–1852 but 509 from 1956–2001, a 250x increase in rate. By 2017, Rodríguez González's *Gran diccionario de anglicismos* cataloged 4,500+ anglicisms in active Spanish use. A crucial geographic gradient exists: Mexican Spanish absorbs far more English than Peninsular Spanish, with the US-Mexico border zone producing Spanglish (~35 million speakers in the US). Spain's RAE actively resists anglicisms and French was taught in Spanish schools until the 1980s — making the eventual English dominance of foreign borrowing all the more striking.

## Penetration Score Definition
**Denominator:** Percentage of new foreign-language loanwords entering standard Spanish that are English-origin, per decade. Measures how much of the foreign linguistic pressure on Spanish comes from English/American culture versus other sources (historically French, Italian, Arabic).
**0% anchor:** No new foreign borrowings entering Spanish are English-origin (pre-industrial Spain, when French and Italian monopolized foreign influence).
**100% anchor:** Every new foreign borrowing entering Spanish is English-origin (no words from French, Japanese, Korean, or any other language entering the lexicon).

## Methodology
The penetration score estimates the percentage of new foreign-language borrowings entering standard Spanish that originate from English/American English. This is a **flow** metric — it measures the share of incoming foreign vocabulary, not the accumulated stock.

**Why flow rate, not stock?** Spanish carries a massive historical layer of ~4,000 Arabic-origin words from the Moorish period (711–1492), plus ~2,000+ French borrowings and ~1,000+ Italian words. Using accumulated stock as the denominator would permanently dilute English's share because of this ancient linguistic substrate. The flow rate captures the intensity of American cultural pressure at each moment — when 90% of new foreign borrowings are English-origin, American culture is driving virtually all of Spain's linguistic evolution from external sources.

**Primary data sources:**
1. **Pedrero González (2007), "Tipología de los anglicismos léxicos en el DRAE":** Provides the most rigorous historical data on anglicism incorporation into the official Spanish dictionary. Four stages: 1780–1852 (1–6 anglicisms), 1869–1899 (81), 1914–1947 (63), 1956–2001 (509). This 250x increase in incorporation rate (from ~0.08/year to ~11/year) anchors the acceleration curve.
2. **Rodríguez González (2017), *Gran diccionario de anglicismos*:** Comprehensive dictionary cataloging 4,500+ anglicisms in active Spanish use across 1,142 pages. Covers technology, fashion, sports, music, business, and daily life. Demonstrates that actual usage far exceeds RAE-accepted vocabulary (~860 cumulative DRAE anglicisms vs. 4,500+ in active use).
3. **CREA corpus studies (2001–2004):** Corpus de Referencia del Español Actual analysis of 5.8 million words from Spanish newspapers. Found anglicism frequency of 3.85/1,000 words in El País, up to 10.11/1,000 in cultural press. 2,198 distinct anglicisms identified across 93 thematic areas.
4. **Pratt (1980), *El anglicismo en el español peninsular contemporáneo*:** Landmark study defining the scope and typology of anglicisms in Peninsular Spanish. Based on Pratt's Oxford PhD thesis (1977). Established the baseline for pre-1980 Peninsular anglicism levels.
5. **Lorenzo (1996), *Anglicismos hispánicos*:** Comprehensive 710-page study of English influence on Spanish across all Hispanic countries. Provided taxonomies still used in contemporary research.
6. **DRAE 23rd edition (2014):** First edition to systematically mark 201 unassimilated anglicisms in italics, continuing a practice from the 22nd edition. Provides an official benchmark for the scale of recognized English borrowing.

**Conversion from raw data to flow rate:**
- Pre-1870 scores are estimated from the near-total French dominance of foreign borrowing and the documented negligible English presence (1–6 anglicisms over 73 years).
- 1870–1950 scores interpolate between the Pedrero stage data and known cultural history (British industrial influence → American post-WWII expansion).
- Post-1950 scores are calibrated from: (a) the 509 DRAE additions 1956–2001 vs. declining French influence, (b) the CREA corpus frequency data, (c) the 4,500+ active anglicisms documented by Rodríguez González, (d) the known shift from French to English in Spanish education during the 1980s.

**Consistency:** The same proxy (English share of new foreign borrowings) is used across the full range. Pre-1870 data is sparse but directionally reliable based on documented French dominance. Post-1950 data is well-anchored by multiple independent sources.

**Comparison to other languages in this series:** Spanish's penetration curve is *later-starting but steeper* than German's (ticket 003). Germany had direct American occupation after 1945, creating immediate deep anglicization; Spain under Franco was relatively insulated until the 1960s–70s. But once Spanish education switched from French to English in the 1980s, the adoption rate surged past Germany's and accelerated through the internet era. Latin American Spanish, especially Mexican, runs ~5–10 points ahead of Peninsular Spanish due to geographic proximity to the US.

## Notes
- **The French-to-English transition is the story.** Before the 18th century, Italian was the primary source of foreign loanwords in Spanish. From the 18th century through the mid-20th century, French dominated — the Bourbon dynasty, French fashion, French intellectual culture, and French taught as the primary foreign language in Spanish schools. English's rise is really the story of English *displacing French* as the dominant external linguistic influence. This transition happened later in Spain (1980s school curriculum change) than in Latin America (1950s–60s cultural shift), creating a measurable geographic lag.
- **The geographic gradient is itself evidence.** Mexican Spanish has absorbed far more English than Peninsular Spanish, with Argentine Spanish falling somewhere between. This gradient — from the US border southward and across the Atlantic — maps directly to cultural proximity. The US-Mexico border zone has produced Spanglish, a code-switching phenomenon involving ~35 million US speakers. The gradient supports a "radiation from source" model of American cultural influence.
- **DRAE acceptance massively undercounts actual usage.** The RAE accepted ~660 anglicisms by 2001, but Rodríguez González documented 4,500+ in active use by 2017 — a ratio of roughly 7:1. The RAE is explicitly conservative and prescriptivist, actively promoting Spanish alternatives (correo electrónico over email, enlace over link). This means the official dictionary is a *lower bound* on penetration, not a representative measure. The flow rate in the data table reflects estimated actual usage, not just RAE acceptance.
- **The 1914–1947 dip is real.** Pedrero's data shows only 63 DRAE anglicism additions in 1914–1947, fewer than the 81 in 1869–1899. This reflects multiple factors: Spain's neutrality in WWI (less American contact than Germany had), the Spanish Civil War (1936–39) disrupting cultural exchange, and Franco's autarky (post-1939 nationalism and insularity). Meanwhile, Latin American usage was accelerating — the DRAE just wasn't capturing it.
- **RAE vs. street: the acceptance lag.** The RAE periodically accepts anglicisms it previously rejected. This lag between street usage and official acceptance creates a measurable "resistance gap." Words like parking, marketing, and fútbol were in common use for decades before RAE acceptance. Recent editions have accelerated acceptance — the 23rd edition (2014) for the first time marked 201 unassimilated anglicisms in italics, acknowledging their status as active vocabulary even if not fully "Spanish."
- **Technology vocabulary as the modern beachhead.** In the internet era, technology vocabulary is almost exclusively English-origin: app, blog, chat, click, email, hashtag, influencer, link, like, online, post, selfie, spam, streaming, tweet, web, wifi, software, hardware. Even when Spanish alternatives exist (ratón for mouse, enlace for link), the English terms dominate in actual usage, especially in Latin America. The CREA corpus found that technology was among the top thematic areas for anglicism frequency.
- **Spanglish as the extreme case.** The US-Mexico border zone has produced Spanglish — not just borrowing but systematic code-switching and grammatical hybridization. Terms like "textear" (to text), "faxear" (to fax), "hackear" (to hack), and "hacer click" show English verbs being morphologically integrated into Spanish grammar. This goes beyond vocabulary borrowing into structural linguistic influence, which some linguists consider a deeper form of language contact.
- **The 90% ceiling.** Even at peak dominance, English doesn't reach 100% of new foreign borrowings. Japanese (anime, manga, sushi, emoji, karate), Korean (K-pop terminology), and occasional French, Italian, and Arabic terms continue entering Spanish. The ~10% non-English slice represents the irreducible diversity of global cultural influence — a useful reminder that even at peak Americanization, other cultures maintain linguistic beachheads.
