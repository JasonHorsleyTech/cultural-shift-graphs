# Ticket 023: CFCs → Ozone Depletion

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) deplete the ozone layer to binding international regulation phasing out CFC production. This is widely considered the great SUCCESS story of science-to-action.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Molina & Rowland published their paper on CFC-ozone interaction in Nature in June 1974. This is the foundational scientific evidence. Was there any earlier evidence?

2. **Consensus date** — The NAS issued a report confirming the CFC-ozone link in 1976. The Antarctic ozone hole was discovered by Farman, Gardiner & Shanklin in 1985, dramatically confirming the theory. Which serves as the consensus date?

3. **First OECD regulation** — The US, Canada, Sweden, and Norway banned CFC aerosol propellants in 1978. The Montreal Protocol was signed in 1987 and is the landmark international regulation. Which is "first binding regulation"?

4. **US regulation** — EPA and FDA banned CFC use in aerosol spray cans in 1978. The US ratified the Montreal Protocol. Full CFC phase-out completed 1996.

5. **Montreal Protocol details** — Signed September 16, 1987. Entered into force January 1, 1989. Has been ratified by all 197 UN member states (the only treaty with universal ratification). Phase-out schedules for different substances.

6. **Why this was fast** — DuPont had CFC substitute patents (HFCs/HCFCs) ready. The ozone hole was visible and alarming. The number of CFC manufacturers was small. Industry switched from opposition to support once substitutes were viable.

7. **50% exposure reduction** — CFC production/consumption data from UNEP. When did global CFC consumption drop to 50% of peak?

## Where to look

1. Molina & Rowland (1974) — Nature paper
2. NAS report on halocarbons and stratospheric ozone (1976)
3. Farman, Gardiner & Shanklin (1985) — Nature paper on ozone hole
4. Montreal Protocol text and UNEP Ozone Secretariat
5. Richard Benedick, "Ozone Diplomacy" — the definitive account of Montreal Protocol negotiations
6. EPA regulatory history for CFC aerosol ban (1978)

## What "good data" looks like

- The 1974 → 1978 → 1987 → 1996 timeline is clean and well-documented
- DuPont's role: from initial opposition (funded counter-research) to support (once they had substitute patents). Industry opposition was SHORT-LIVED — strong initially, then reversed.
- The 1985 ozone hole discovery as a catalyst — it made the abstract threat VISIBLE
- CFC production data showing rapid decline after Montreal Protocol

## Known pitfalls

- The 1978 US aerosol ban and the 1987 Montreal Protocol are DIFFERENT regulatory actions. The 1978 ban covered one use (aerosols) in one country. The Montreal Protocol covered all uses globally. Use the 1978 ban as "first regulation" and the Montreal Protocol as the broader regulatory milestone.
- Category: international-treaty (the Montreal Protocol is the defining regulation, even though national bans preceded it).
- industry_opposition: moderate (DuPont fought initially but switched when substitutes were viable — unlike tobacco's sustained multi-decade opposition).
- substitute_available: yes (HFCs/HCFCs, though these later turned out to be potent greenhouse gases — a "regrettable substitution" but one that enabled the political deal).
