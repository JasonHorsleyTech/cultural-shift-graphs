# Ticket 021: Irish Potato Blight Farm Laborers

## Event
The displacement of Irish agricultural laborers following the Great Famine (An Gorta Mór), 1845–1852. An environmental catastrophe that killed ~1 million people and drove ~1 million more to emigrate, permanently reshaping Ireland's population and labor force.

## Mechanism
**Discrete shock** (environmental)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track Irish population and agricultural employment from ~1840 to ~1870:

- 1841 Census: Ireland population ~8.2 million. Majority agricultural laborers and smallholders.
- 1845: Phytophthora infestans arrives. First blight of potato crop.
- 1846–47: "Black '47" — worst year of famine
- 1851 Census: population ~6.6 million (1.6 million fewer — deaths + emigration)
- 1861 Census: population ~5.8 million (continued emigration)
- Agricultural laborer class essentially destroyed as a category — consolidation of farms into larger holdings

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Irish Census returns 1841, 1851, 1861, 1871
2. Ó Gráda, "Black '47 and Beyond: The Great Irish Famine" (1999)
3. Mokyr, "Why Ireland Starved: A Quantitative and Analytical History" (1983)
4. Donnelly, "The Great Irish Potato Famine" (2001)
5. UK Parliamentary Papers on the Irish Famine (relief expenditures, workhouse data)
6. Kinealy, "The Great Calamity: The Great Irish Famine 1845–52" (1994)

### Income Data
**Known caveats:** Pre-famine Irish agricultural laborers were largely subsistence farmers and cottiers. "Income" in a market sense barely applies — they grew potatoes for food and might earn small cash wages during harvest. Use population and land-holding data as proxies instead.

- Pre-famine: cottiers held ~1–5 acres, paying rent in labor. Agricultural laborers earned 6d–1s/day when work was available.
- During famine: public works wages (8d–10d/day for stone-breaking), soup kitchen rations
- Post-famine: farm consolidation → fewer, larger farms → higher per-farm income but FAR fewer people

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Mokyr (1983) — detailed economic analysis
2. Ó Gráda (1999) — wages and prices data
3. Poor Law Commission records — relief expenditures
4. Griffith's Valuation (1847–64) — land holding data

### Where They Went
- Death (~1 million, from starvation and disease)
- Emigration to US (~1 million during 1845–55, primarily to Boston, New York, Philadelphia)
- Emigration to UK (Liverpool, Glasgow, London)
- Emigration to Canada, Australia
- Workhouses (at peak, ~250,000 inmates)
- Urban migration within Ireland (Dublin, Cork, Limerick)
- Remaining agricultural work on consolidated larger farms

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Irish emigration statistics (passenger lists, port records)
2. US Census data on Irish-born population (1850, 1860)
3. UK Census data on Irish-born in England/Scotland
4. Miller, "Emigrants and Exiles: Ireland and the Irish Exodus to North America" (1985)

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Larger farmers (graziers) who consolidated land during and after the famine. Emigrants who established themselves in American cities.
- **False movers:** Tenants who borrowed to pay rent during the first year of blight expecting the next harvest to recover. Government public works schemes that paid below subsistence wages.
- **Deniers:** British officials (Charles Trevelyan) who attributed the famine to Irish "moral failings" and resisted adequate relief. The ideology that market forces should not be interfered with.
- **Survival pattern:** Capital (even small amounts), geographic mobility (ability to emigrate), and farm size (larger holdings survived).

### Unique Texture
- The population trajectory: Ireland's population in 1845 (~8.2M) was not reached again — it's still only ~5.1M (Republic) + ~1.9M (NI) today. The most extreme long-term demographic impact of any event in this dataset.
- The "coffin ships" — emigrant ship mortality rates
- The political dimension: Ireland was exporting food during the famine (grain exports continued under British policy)
- The workhouse system overwhelmed — designed for ~100K capacity, forced to accommodate ~250K
- The cultural memory: the famine defined Irish identity, Irish-American identity, and Anglo-Irish relations for generations
- The Choctaw Nation's donation to Irish famine relief ($170 in 1847, equivalent to ~$5,000 today) — themselves survivors of the Trail of Tears

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Irish population at each census from 1841–1871
- Emigration numbers by destination (US, UK, Canada, Australia)
- Mortality estimates (even rough ranges)
- Pre-famine vs. post-famine land holding patterns (farm sizes, number of holdings)
- Workhouse admission data
