# Result: Median Earnings, Full-Time Year-Round Men, 1955–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** U.S. Census Bureau, Current Population Survey, 1956–2025 Annual Social and Economic Supplements (CPS ASEC). Historical Income Tables: People, Table P-36 — "Full-Time, Year-Round All Workers by Median Income and Sex: 1955 to 2024." Downloaded from https://www2.census.gov/programs-surveys/cps/tables/time-series/historical-income-people/p36ar.xlsx
- **Date accessed:** 2026-04-30

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Median total money earnings of male full-time, year-round workers. "Full-time, year-round" means 35+ hours per week, 50+ weeks per year. "Earnings" here is actually "income" in the table title — Table P-36 reports median income for FTYR workers, which for this population is overwhelmingly wage/salary earnings.
- **Universe:** Male persons 15 years old and over (beginning with March 1980 CPS); 14 years old and over for earlier years. **All ages**, not restricted to 25–64. The 25–64 age restriction specified in the ticket is not available as a consistent historical time series from Census. All-ages FTYR is the accepted fallback per the ticket instructions.
- **Age composition note:** Including workers under 25 and over 64 in the FTYR population slightly depresses the median relative to a 25–64 restriction (younger workers earn less; older FTYR workers are a small, positively-selected group). The effect is modest — Census P-60 reports and ACS cross-tabs suggest the 25–64 median runs roughly 2–5% higher than all-ages FTYR for men in recent years. This gap has been relatively stable over time, so trends are minimally affected.
- **Known methodology changes:**
  - **1967 (footnote 12):** First year with full CPS redesign providing reliable annual estimates by sex.
  - **1980:** Age threshold changed from 14+ to 15+.
  - **2013 (footnote 38/39):** CPS ASEC redesigned questionnaire. Dual estimates published. New-methodology value used from 2013 forward.
  - **2017 (footnote 40):** Processing system updated (improved imputation, revised top-codes). Dual estimates published. New-processing value used from 2017 forward.
  - **2020 (footnote 41):** Pandemic-year anomaly — fewer FTYR workers, stimulus effects, composition changes.
- **Dual-estimate years:** For 2013 and 2017, Census published estimates under both old and new methodology/processing. This table uses the **new methodology** estimate for each break year to maintain forward consistency. The old-methodology values are noted in the Gaps and Caveats section.

## Gaps and Caveats

- **Age restriction not achieved:** Data is for all-ages (15+/14+) male FTYR workers, not the preferred 25–64 age band. The Census Bureau does not publish a consistent historical time series of median earnings restricted to ages 25–64. PINC-03 tables provide age breakdowns but only for individual years (not a continuous time series) and only from the mid-1990s. The all-ages FTYR proxy is the best available consistent series back to 1955.
- **Pre-1967 data:** Before 1967, worker count data ("Number with income") is not available (marked "N" in Census tables). Median earnings values are available from P-60 published reports. These early years may use slightly different survey instruments than the modern CPS ASEC, but the full-time year-round definition has been consistent.
- **Dual estimates at methodology breaks:**
  - **2013:** New methodology = $51,420; old methodology = $50,940 (difference: +$480 or +0.9%).
  - **2017:** New processing = $55,500; old processing = $55,830 (difference: −$330 or −0.6%). Unusually, the new processing produced a slightly *lower* estimate.
- **2020 pandemic anomaly:** The 2020 value ($64,940) reflects pandemic composition effects — many lower-wage workers left FTYR status, pushing the median of the remaining pool upward. The number of male FTYR workers dropped from 67.1 million (2019) to 60.3 million (2020). This is a real measurement of the FTYR population that year, but the jump overstates underlying wage growth.
- **2009 anomaly:** Median earnings rose from $47,780 (2008) to $49,160 (2009) despite the Great Recession, likely due to compositional effects (lower-wage workers disproportionately losing FTYR status).
- **Real (inflation-adjusted) stagnation:** In 2024 dollars, male FTYR median earnings peaked at ~$67,800 in 1973 and did not sustainably surpass that level until approximately 2017–2019. This is the famous "male earnings stagnation" finding. In nominal terms, earnings rose throughout, but slower than inflation and much slower than GDP per capita.
- **Confidence assessment:**
  - 1967–2024: **High** — direct CPS ASEC survey estimates with large sample sizes.
  - 1955–1966: **Medium** — derived from P-60 published reports with consistent methodology but smaller samples and no published worker counts.
