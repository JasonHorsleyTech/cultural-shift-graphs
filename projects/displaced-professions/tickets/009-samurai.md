# Ticket 009: Samurai Class (Meiji Restoration)

## Event
The abolition of the samurai class following the Meiji Restoration of 1868. A feudal warrior aristocracy of ~2 million people (with families) was dissolved by government decree over roughly a decade (1868–1877), culminating in the Satsuma Rebellion.

## Mechanism
**Discrete shock** (regime, cultural)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track the dissolution of the samurai class:

- Pre-Meiji: ~400,000–500,000 samurai warriors (estimates vary). With families: ~1.8–2 million people (~5–6% of Japan's ~33 million population).
- 1869: Return of domains to the emperor (hanseki hokan)
- 1871: Abolition of domains (haihan chiken) — samurai stipends begin converting
- 1873: Conscription law — commoner army replaces samurai military function
- 1876: Sword ban (haitōrei) and compulsory commutation of stipends to bonds
- 1877: Satsuma Rebellion — the last armed samurai resistance, crushed
- By 1880s: samurai as a legal class effectively extinct

### Income Data
Samurai were paid in rice stipends (kokudaka system), not money wages.

- Stipend structure: ranged from ~30 koku/year (low-ranking) to thousands for daimyo
- 1 koku ≈ enough rice to feed one person for one year
- Commutation to bonds (1876): bonds worth 5–14 years of stipend, depending on rank
- Many sold bonds immediately at a discount

**Known caveats:** Converting rice stipends to a modern income equivalent requires many assumptions. Report the stipend data in its own units and provide rough monetary equivalents with clear methodology. Don't pretend this is precise.

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Jansen (2000) — stipend structure and commutation terms
2. Yamamura (1966) — economic analysis of stipend commutation
3. Crawcour, "The Tokugawa Period and Japan's Preparation for Modern Economic Growth" (1974)

### Where They Went
- Government bureaucracy (many upper samurai became Meiji civil servants, police, teachers)
- Military officers in the new conscript army
- Business/entrepreneurship — some used commutation bonds as capital
- Police force (many former samurai)
- Teaching (national education system created 1872)
- Farming — some lower samurai returned to agriculture
- Poverty — many lower samurai lost everything

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Jansen (2000) — post-samurai career paths
2. Sonoda, "The Decline of the Japanese Warrior Class" (1990)
3. Studies of Meiji-era bureaucratic staffing

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Samurai who entered government service early (Ito Hirobumi, Yamagata Aritomo — both lower samurai who became prime ministers). Samurai who became entrepreneurs (Iwasaki Yataro founded Mitsubishi).
- **False movers:** Samurai who invested commutation bonds in failed agricultural or industrial ventures.
- **Deniers:** Saigo Takamori and the Satsuma Rebellion — the most dramatic "last stand" in the dataset. Also earlier revolts (Saga Rebellion 1874, Shinpūren Rebellion 1876).
- **Survival pattern:** Lower samurai who embraced modernization fared better than upper samurai who clung to status. Political connections to the new government were decisive.

### Unique Texture
- The class was ~5–6% of the population — proportionally one of the largest displaced groups in the dataset
- The Satsuma Rebellion (1877): 30,000 samurai vs. a conscript army. The conscripts won.
- Many samurai families retained social prestige for generations even after losing legal status
- The "shizoku" designation on family registers — a lingering class marker
- Comparison to other aristocratic dissolutions (French Revolution, Russian Revolution)
