# Ticket 011: American Whalers

## Event
The collapse of the American whaling industry, ~1846–1880s. A tipping point driven by the convergence of kerosene replacing whale oil and whale population depletion. The whaling fleet dropped ~80% from peak (735 ships, 1846) to near-zero over ~30 years.

## Mechanism
**Tipping point** (tech_platform, resource)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track US whaling employment from ~1820 to ~1900:

- Growth phase: American whaling fleet expanded dramatically 1820s–1840s
- Peak: 735 whaling ships (1846). Estimate total employment (sailors + onshore workers — coopers, chandlers, rope-makers, try-works)
- Key dates: Drake's oil well (1859), kerosene widely available (1860s), spring steel replaces whalebone/baleen (1870s–1880s)
- New Bedford, MA as the whaling capital — track its economic trajectory
- Residual whaling continued into the 1920s but on a tiny scale

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Starbuck, "History of the American Whale Fishery" (1878) — fleet data
2. Davis, Gallman & Gleiter, "In Pursuit of Leviathan" (1997) — economic analysis
3. Dolin, "Leviathan: The History of Whaling in America" (2007)
4. New Bedford Whaling Museum archives — ship registers, crew lists
5. US Census occupational data for whaling ports (New Bedford, Nantucket, Sag Harbor)

### Income Data
- Whaling crew wages operated on a "lay" system (share of profits, not fixed wages)
- Average lay for different ranks (captain, mate, harpooner, ordinary seaman)
- Compare whaling income to merchant marine or factory wages of the same era
- Track whether whaling became less profitable before workers left (likely yes — whale oil prices relative to kerosene)

**Known caveats:** The lay system makes "income" variable and voyage-dependent. A good voyage could pay very well; a bad one could leave sailors in debt. Report the range and typical outcomes.

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Davis et al. (1997) — detailed economic data on voyages, lays, profitability
2. Whaling crew lists and voyage accounts (New Bedford Whaling Museum)
3. Historical commodity prices for whale oil vs. kerosene

### Where They Went
- Merchant marine / commercial shipping
- Factory work (New Bedford's textile mills grew as whaling shrank)
- Fishing (commercial fishing in New England expanded)
- Migration west (California Gold Rush overlapped with whaling decline)
- Some Pacific island whalers became traders or settled in Hawaii

**Source hierarchy:**
1. US Census data for New Bedford / Bristol County, 1850–1900
2. Davis et al. (1997) for occupational transitions
3. New Bedford economic history studies

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Whaling firms that pivoted to merchant shipping or textile investment. Any specific captains or owners who saw the writing on the wall.
- **False movers:** Arctic whaling expeditions of the 1870s — chasing the last whales in increasingly dangerous conditions. The 1871 disaster (33 ships trapped in Arctic ice).
- **Deniers:** Belief that petroleum was a fad and whale oil would remain the premium illuminant.
- **Survival pattern:** Capital owners who diversified early. Sailors had fewer options.

### Unique Texture
- The dual cause: tech (kerosene) AND resource depletion. Which came first? Did whales get scarce before kerosene appeared, or did kerosene arrive just in time?
- The 1871 Arctic disaster — 33 ships abandoned in ice, 1,219 sailors rescued. The most dramatic single event in the industry's decline.
- New Bedford was the richest city per capita in the US in the 1840s–50s. Track what happened to it.
- Nantucket's transformation from whaling center to resort island
- Whaling as an early example of a multi-racial workforce (significant Black, Cape Verdean, and Pacific Islander participation)

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Fleet size annually from 1820–1900
- Total estimated employment (at-sea + onshore) at peak
- Whale oil vs. kerosene price data showing the substitution
- New Bedford economic indicators (population, property values, industry composition)
- The 1871 disaster as a specific narrative data point
