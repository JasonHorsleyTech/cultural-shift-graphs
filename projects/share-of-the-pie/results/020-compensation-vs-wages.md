# Result: Total Compensation vs Wages — Benefits Share of Compensation, 1947–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** BEA NIPA Table 2.1 "Personal Income and Its Disposition" — Line 2 (Compensation of employees, received), Line 3 (Wages and salaries), Line 4 (Supplements to wages and salaries). Series codes: A033RC (compensation), A034RC (wages), A038RC (supplements). Accessed via DBnomics mirror of BEA data.
- **Cross-reference:** BLS Employer Costs for Employee Compensation (ECEC) quarterly reports (for definitional context, not direct data).
- **Date accessed:** 2026-04-30

## Methodology
- **What is measured:** Total employee compensation decomposed into (a) wages and salaries and (b) supplements to wages and salaries, for the entire U.S. economy. "Benefits share" = supplements / total compensation × 100.
- **BEA "supplements" include:** Employer contributions for government social insurance (employer FICA — Social Security + Medicare, unemployment insurance, workers' compensation) and employer contributions for employee pension and insurance funds (health insurance premiums, defined-benefit pension funding, 401(k) matching, life insurance, etc.).
- **BEA "supplements" exclude:** Paid leave (vacation, holidays, sick days), overtime premiums, shift differentials, and nonproduction bonuses — these are classified as "wages and salaries" by BEA.
- **Universe:** All employees in the U.S. economy (private + government). This is the "received" concept (compensation received by U.S. residents, excluding compensation paid to rest-of-world).
- **No methodology breaks:** The BEA compensation series is continuous from 1929 to present with no definitional changes affecting the supplements/wages split.

### Important definitional note: BEA vs. BLS ECEC

The ticket's "what good data looks like" benchmarks (~30% by 2024) correspond to the **BLS ECEC** definition of "benefits," which is considerably broader than BEA "supplements." BLS ECEC counts paid leave, supplemental pay (overtime premiums, bonuses), and other items as "benefits," whereas BEA counts those as "wages and salaries." As of 2024:

| Measure | Benefits share |
|---------|---------------|
| BEA NIPA supplements share | ~17.6% |
| BLS ECEC benefits share (private industry) | ~29.5–29.9% |

The ~12 percentage point gap is mostly paid leave and supplemental pay. For this project's purpose — measuring the "invisible" portion of compensation that doesn't show up in paychecks — the BEA definition is arguably more appropriate, since paid leave is experienced directly by the worker even though it isn't cash. The BEA supplements capture specifically what employers spend on workers that workers never see: FICA, health premiums, pension funding.

## Gaps and Caveats

- **No missing data.** BEA NIPA provides continuous annual data for all three components from 1929 onward. The 1947–2024 range is fully covered.
- **Aggregate, not per-worker.** These are total economy figures. Per-worker values can be derived by dividing by total employment (from BLS or BEA), but the percentage (benefits share) is the same regardless.
- **All employees, not just private sector.** Includes government workers. Government compensation has a somewhat higher benefits share than private sector (government pensions are more generous). This is appropriate for the project since GDP per capita is also whole-economy.
- **1948 dip:** The benefits share briefly fell from 7.0% (1947) to 6.1% (1948) as postwar wages surged faster than benefits expanded. This is real, not an error.
- **1990s dip:** Benefits share peaked at 19.3% in 1993, then fell back to 17.5% by 1998–2000. This reflects the managed-care revolution (HMOs temporarily slowed health cost growth) and the late-1990s wage boom (dot-com era wage growth outpaced benefits growth).
- **2005–2010 peak:** Benefits share peaked at 19.6% in 2010 as health costs accelerated while wages stagnated during the Great Recession.
- **2021–2024 decline:** Benefits share fell from 18.4% (2020) to 17.6% (2024) despite rising health costs, because the post-pandemic labor market drove exceptionally strong wage growth that outpaced benefits growth.
- **BEA vs. BLS ECEC definitional gap:** See methodology note above. The BLS ECEC shows benefits at ~30% of compensation, but uses a broader definition. The two series are not comparable. This file uses BEA NIPA throughout.

### Confidence assessment
- **1947–2024: HIGH.** BEA NIPA is the gold standard for national accounts. The compensation series has no methodology breaks and is regularly revised for consistency. The decomposition into wages and supplements is derived from tax records (W-2s, FICA filings) and employer surveys, making it highly reliable.

### Key narrative for the graph
The benefits share of compensation roughly tripled from ~7% (1947) to ~19–20% (peak, 2005–2010), then stabilized. This means that roughly 1 in 5 dollars employers spend on workers goes to benefits the worker never sees as cash — up from 1 in 14 in 1947. The growth was driven by three forces: (1) Social Security/Medicare tax increases (FICA rate rose from 1% to 7.65%), (2) the employer health insurance cost explosion (tickets 018–019), and (3) expansion of employer pension contributions. The recent plateau suggests that health cost growth, while still significant, is being offset by periods of strong wage growth and employer cost-shifting strategies (higher deductibles, HDHPs).
