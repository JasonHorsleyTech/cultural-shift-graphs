# Samurai Class (Meiji Restoration)

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Samurai (hereditary warrior-aristocrat class)
- **Country/Region:** Japan (nationwide)
- **Peak Year:** ~1868 (final year of Tokugawa shogunate; class had been stable for ~250 years)
- **Peak Workforce:** ~1,900,000 including families (~400,000–500,000 warriors proper; 1,282,167 shizoku registered in 1872 census, plus dependents bringing total to ~1.9 million) (1872 Jinshin koseki census; Jansen 2000)
- **Primary Mechanism:** discrete_shock
- **Cause Tags:** regime, regulatory, cultural
- **Killing Event:** The Meiji Restoration (1868) abolished feudal domains and the hereditary warrior class through a rapid sequence of edicts between 1868–1876, ending with compulsory commutation of stipends to bonds and the sword ban.

## Workforce Timeline

The samurai class was not a "workforce" in the modern sense — it was a hereditary legal status encompassing warriors, administrators, and their families. The relevant metric is the legal dissolution of the class and its economic supports.

| 1868 | ~1,900,000 (samurai + families, ~5–6% of ~33M population). Full stipends and privileges intact. | Jansen (2000); Totman (2005) |

| 1947 | Shizoku designation formally abolished in revised civil code after WWII. | Wikipedia — Shizoku |

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1869 (hanseki hokan — first loss of domain autonomy)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~3 (by 1871, domain system abolished — administrative/governance role eliminated for most)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** ~5 (by 1873, military function eliminated by conscription)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** ~8 (by 1876, economic foundation destroyed — stipends commuted, swords banned)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** ~9 (by 1877, last armed resistance crushed)
- **Decline Shape:** cliff (a sequence of discrete legislative shocks over 8 years, each removing a pillar of the class)
- **Current Status:** extinct

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: estimated

Samurai were paid in rice stipends (kokudaka system), not money wages. Converting to modern equivalents requires many assumptions. Income data below uses the stipend/bond structure directly.

- **Income at Peak:** Ranged enormously by rank. Low-ranking samurai: ~30–70 koku/year (1 koku ≈ enough rice for one person for one year ≈ roughly 1 ryō gold). High-ranking hatamoto: 100–9,500 koku. Daimyō: 10,000+ koku. Modal income for rank-and-file: 30–70 koku/year. (Nihonto.com; Jansen 2000)
- **Income at Trough:** Bond interest income only. A low-ranking warrior receiving bonds worth ~100 yen got 7% annual interest = 7 yen/year — roughly one-third of Tokyo's lowest worker wages in the 1880s. (Chitsuroku-shobun, Japanese Wiki Corpus)
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** Highly variable. Only 37.6% secured government employment, military positions, or other stable income by 1883. Two-thirds faced economic ruin. (1883 Japanese government statistics via Japanese Wiki Corpus)
- **Inflation Adjustment Method:** Not applicable — pre-modern currency system. Rice stipend values reported in koku; bond values in nominal yen.

## Surplus Capture

Who benefited from eliminating the samurai class and its 30%+ budget drain?

- **Capital owners:** ~30% — Samurai commutation bonds became startup capital for 153 national banks (1876–1879), fueling Japan's industrialization. Zaibatsu founders (many ex-samurai themselves) captured enormous value. Bond buyers purchased discounted bonds from desperate samurai.
- **Consumers:** ~15% — Lower taxes eventually, cheaper goods from industrialization, but benefits took decades to materialize.
- **Adjacent workers:** ~10% — Commoner conscripts gained social mobility through military service; farmers gained ownership rights (1873 land tax reform).
- **Government/public:** ~45% — The freed 30%+ of the national budget was redirected to industrialization, railroads, military modernization, and public education. This was the primary purpose of the abolition.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
1. **Itō Hirobumi** (1841–1909): A low-ranking samurai (chūgen — foot soldier class) from Chōshū domain. Studied naval science in London in 1863 as one of the "Chōshū Five." Embraced Western modernization early. Became Japan's first Prime Minister (1885) and served four terms. His low rank paradoxically freed him from attachment to the old system.
2. **Iwasaki Yatarō** (1835–1885): From a family that had sold its samurai status generations earlier. Ran the Tosa domain's trading office, then converted it into the private shipping company Mitsubishi (1873). Won government shipping contracts during the 1874 Taiwan expedition. Built one of Japan's largest zaibatsu. Exploited the transition rather than resisting it.

### False Movers
1. **Samurai turned farmers:** The government offered land, equipment, and up to 7 acres per family for agricultural ventures. Many samurai had no farming experience and failed. Hokkaido colonization schemes had particularly high failure rates due to harsh climate and unfamiliar work.
2. **Bond-funded businesses:** Many samurai invested commutation bonds in commercial ventures without any business experience. "Samurai ineptitude at commerce became proverbial." By 1882, 90% of samurai in Tottori Prefecture had already sold their bonds just to survive — those who tried entrepreneurship first often lost everything before selling.

### Deniers
1. **Saigō Takamori** (1828–1877): The most famous denier. Former Restoration hero who had helped overthrow the shogunate, then turned against the Meiji government's modernization. Led 20,000 samurai in the Satsuma Rebellion (1877) against 65,000+ imperial conscripts. Killed at the Battle of Shiroyama. His rebellion proved conclusively that the samurai military model was obsolete.
2. **Shinpūren (Divine Wind League):** Ultra-conservative samurai in Kumamoto who refused all Western influence — some reportedly refused to use Western-made umbrellas. On October 24, 1876, 200 members attacked the Kumamoto garrison with swords. Killed ~300 soldiers in the initial surprise attack but were defeated by the next morning. Most surviving rebels committed seppuku.

### Survival Pattern
Lower-ranking samurai who embraced modernization early and secured government employment fared far better than high-ranking samurai who clung to status — political connections to the new regime were decisive, not old-regime rank.

## Unique Texture

1. **The 30% budget drain:** Samurai stipends consumed over 30% of the Meiji government's entire budget in 1869, making abolition as much a fiscal necessity as a political choice. The government was literally going bankrupt paying a warrior class it no longer needed. (Japanese Wiki Corpus — Chitsuroku-shobun; Jansen 2000)

2. **90% bond sell-off:** By 1882 — just six years after commutation — 90% of former samurai in Tottori Prefecture had already sold their bonds, presumably at deep discounts, just to cover basic survival expenses. The bonds were meant as a long-term economic bridge; they became fire-sale assets. (1882 Tottori Prefecture report via Japanese Wiki Corpus)

3. **The conscripts beat the samurai:** The Satsuma Rebellion's decisive outcome — 20,000 traditional samurai warriors defeated by a conscript army of commoner farmers — was Japan's proof-of-concept that modern military organization trumped hereditary martial skill. It was the samurai class's own military obsolescence made visible on a battlefield. (Ravina 2004)

4. **153 banks from samurai bonds:** Between 1876 and 1879, 153 national banks were established, many capitalized with samurai commutation bonds. The bonds that impoverished individual samurai collectively bootstrapped Japan's modern banking system. (Shizume & Tsurumi, Waseda working paper)

5. **From warriors to the entire Meiji elite:** Despite abolishing the samurai class, nearly 80% of public sector jobs in the new government were initially held by former samurai. The class was destroyed on paper but its upper echelon simply became the new bureaucratic elite. (Sonoda 1990)

## Known Data Gaps

- **Individual-level tracking:** No panel data tracking where specific samurai families ended up. The 37.6% stable employment figure (1883) and the occupational breakdown are estimates based on aggregate government statistics and academic inference, not individual records.
- **Income comparability:** Rice stipends (koku) cannot be meaningfully converted to a single modern wage equivalent. The interest rate on bonds (5–10%) relative to contemporary wages is the best proxy for income collapse severity.
- **Regional variation:** The 90% bond sell-off figure comes from Tottori Prefecture specifically and may not be nationally representative. Outcomes varied significantly by domain — samurai from the four leading Restoration domains (Satsuma, Chōshū, Tosa, Hizen) had far better access to government positions.
- **Lower samurai vs. upper samurai breakdown:** The class was internally stratified across 100+ ranks. Outcomes diverged dramatically by rank, but systematic data on outcomes by rank level is sparse outside of specific domain studies.
- **Exact casualty figures for minor rebellions:** Saga Rebellion (1874), Shinpūren Rebellion (1876), and other pre-Satsuma revolts have incomplete casualty records.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium
- **Workforce numbers:** medium — 1872 census provides a solid baseline (1,282,167 shizoku registered), but "with families" estimates vary. The class boundary was always somewhat fuzzy at the lower end.
- **Income data:** low — rice stipend system is fundamentally non-comparable to modern wages. Bond terms are well-documented but income outcomes are fragmentary.
- **Where-they-went:** medium — the 37.6% stable employment figure (1883) and 80% public sector share are well-sourced; the detailed occupational breakdown is scholarly inference rather than census data.
