# State College — Cheapest Prices (Published Tuition and Required Fees)

## Summary

Average published tuition and required fees at public four-year institutions for in-state students rose from ~$243/year in 1963 to ~$9,750 in 2022-23 — a 40× nominal increase. Growth was modest through the 1970s (~5-7% annual), accelerated sharply in the 1980s (~10% annual), and surged again after the 2008 Great Recession when states slashed higher education budgets and never fully restored them. Since ~2016, growth has moderated to 2-3% per year. Three structural breaks dominate: the end of the state-funding golden age (~1980), the federal loan expansion era (~1993-2005), and the post-2008 state disinvestment shock.

## Methodology

### Primary series: NCES Digest of Education Statistics, Table 330.10

This table reports **average undergraduate tuition and required fees** for full-time students at public four-year degree-granting postsecondary institutions. The series runs continuously from academic year 1963-64 through 2022-23 (the latest available as of the 2023 Digest).

The NCES average is an **unweighted institutional average** — each institution counts equally regardless of enrollment size. This is the longest consistent series available for this commodity.

### College Board overlap (2023-2025)

For years after 2022-23, I used the College Board's *Trends in College Pricing* reports. The College Board publishes an **enrollment-weighted average** where larger institutions carry more weight. This produces systematically higher figures (~12% higher) because large flagship state universities tend to charge higher tuition.

- College Board 2020-21: $10,560 vs. NCES 2020-21: $9,374 (12.6% higher)
- College Board 2022-23: ~$10,940 (implied) vs. NCES 2022-23: $9,750 (12.2% higher)

For the final three years in the data table, I applied the College Board's reported year-over-year growth rates to the NCES base to maintain series consistency. The College Board's own enrollment-weighted figures are noted for reference.

### Pre-1963 estimates

No nationally representative series for public four-year tuition exists before 1963-64. The three pre-1963 data points are back-extrapolated from the 1963-64 anchor using:
- Individual institutional records (Colorado State University: $80-90 in 1950-51, $117 in 1955-56, $141 in 1960-61)
- The general growth trajectory of 4-6% per year in the pre-1963 era
- Context that the GI Bill era (1944-1956) kept tuition artificially low as states competed for federal veteran enrollment dollars

These estimates are rough (±30%) and should be treated as order-of-magnitude guidance only.

### Structural breaks annotated

1. **GI Bill era (1944-1956):** Effectively free tuition for veterans distorted the market. Post-GI Bill, tuition resumed normal growth.
2. **State funding golden age (1960s-1970s):** States funded 70-80% of public university costs. Tuition covered a small fraction. Growth was modest (5-7% annual, roughly tracking inflation).
3. **State funding cuts begin (~1980):** Recession of 1980-82 triggered first major round of state higher-ed budget cuts. Tuition growth jumps to 10-13% annual through the 1980s. This is the inflection point. Tuition more than doubled in the 1980s ($738 → $1,780).
4. **Federal loan expansion (~1993):** Student Loan Reform Act of 1993 expanded Direct Lending. Higher Education Act reauthorizations in 1992 and 1998 raised loan limits. Whether this enabled further tuition increases is debated (Bennett Hypothesis). Evidence is mixed — a 2016 NBER paper found demand-side shocks (grants and loans) explained most of the tuition increase, while three earlier government-commissioned studies found no link.
5. **Post-2008 Great Recession:** States cut educational appropriations 24% between 2008-2012 (CBPP). Overall state funding for public colleges was still $6.6 billion below 2008 levels as of 2018 (inflation-adjusted). Published tuition at public four-year colleges rose 37% ($2,708) from the 2008 baseline. This was the sharpest sustained acceleration in the series — tuition rose from $6,312 to $8,070 in just four years (2008-2012).
6. **Post-2016 plateau:** Growth has moderated to 2-3% annual, with some years barely exceeding inflation. Political pressure, state tuition freezes, and demographic enrollment declines have constrained pricing power.

### Year-labeling convention

Each data point represents an academic year. The "Year" column uses the calendar year of the fall semester (e.g., "2022" = academic year 2022-23). This matches the convention used in the foundation wage data (ticket 001), enabling direct division to compute labor-minutes.

## Known gaps

- **Pre-1963:** No national series. Three estimated data points (1950, 1955, 1960) are rough and should not be over-weighted.
- **1964-1967:** The NCES series skips from 1963-64 to 1968-69. Intermediate years are linearly interpolated.
- **2023-2025:** NCES series ends at 2022-23. Recent years are estimated by applying College Board growth rates to the NCES base. Confidence is medium.
- **Methodology shift risk:** If the graph phase needs the College Board's enrollment-weighted series for consistency with other College Board publications, those figures are: 2023-24: $11,310; 2024-25: $11,610; 2025-26: $11,950. These are ~12% higher than the NCES-consistent estimates in the main table.
