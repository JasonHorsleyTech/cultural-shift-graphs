# Ticket 037: Climate Cascades — 4°C+ Warming (Kemp et al. PNAS 2022)

## What to research

Kemp et al. (2022) in PNAS argued that catastrophic climate scenarios (4°C+ warming) are under-studied. What does the research say about population effects at extreme warming levels?

## Specific data needed

1. **Kemp et al. (2022) — "Climate Endgame":**
   - Their framework for catastrophic climate scenarios
   - Temperature thresholds for different severity levels
   - Population estimates at 4°C+ (they reference estimates ranging from 10% survival to extinction)
   - What drives mortality at these temperatures? (Heat, agriculture, sea level, cascading systems)

2. **When does 4°C happen?**
   - IPCC AR6 scenarios: which SSP pathways reach 4°C and when?
   - Current trajectory estimates
   - The "long time horizon" problem: this scenario plays out over 50-100 years, not months

3. **Agricultural impact at 4°C:**
   - Crop yield models at 4°C warming
   - Which US regions become too hot for current crops?
   - Shift in arable zones (northward migration of agricultural viability)
   - Net effect: US food production at 4°C — some areas gain, some lose

4. **Heat mortality:**
   - Mora et al. (2017) — "deadly heat" threshold
   - At 4°C, what fraction of the US population lives in zones with lethal heat events?
   - Air conditioning dependency: if grid strain leads to rolling blackouts during heat waves, excess deaths spike

5. **Sea level rise:**
   - At 4°C, expected sea level rise (1-3 meters by 2100? Higher with ice sheet collapse?)
   - US coastal population at risk
   - Timeline: this is slow — decades of gradual inundation

6. **The cascade risk:**
   - Lenton et al. (2019) — tipping point interactions
   - AMOC collapse, Amazon dieback, permafrost carbon release
   - Each tipping point triggers the next — but over decades
   - This is fundamentally different from the other scenarios: slow grinding decline, not sudden catastrophe

7. **Anderson estimate ("10% survival at 4°C"):**
   - Kevin Anderson has made this claim. What's it based on?
   - Is this a peer-reviewed estimate or a public statement?
   - How does it compare to other estimates?

## Where to look

1. **Kemp et al. (2022) — PNAS** — primary framework
2. **IPCC AR6 — WG1 (Physical Science), WG2 (Impacts)** — temperature projections and impact modeling
3. **Lenton et al. (2019) — Nature** — tipping point cascades
4. **Mora et al. (2017) — Nature Climate Change** — deadly heat thresholds
5. **Hsiang et al. (2017) — Science** — US economic/mortality impacts by temperature
6. **Anderson, Kevin** — climate scientist public statements and papers

## Result format

**Mortality Timeline** — but this is a DECADES-LONG curve, not months:

Use time_hours still for consistency, but the relevant points are years 10, 20, 30, 50, 100.

Key Data Points:
- `temperature_at_4c_year` — when 4°C is reached under different SSP pathways
- `us_agricultural_change_pct_at_4c`
- `us_population_in_lethal_heat_zone_pct`
- `sea_level_rise_m_at_4c`

## What good data looks like

- Acknowledges that this scenario is fundamentally different: decades, not months
- The uncertainty range is genuinely enormous (Anderson says 10% survival; IPCC models show significant but not civilizational impact)
- Notes that at 4°C, the world is not "suddenly apocalyptic" but rather "grinding decline with periodic crises"
- This curve should be visually distinct on the graph — dashed line, different panel, or annotation
