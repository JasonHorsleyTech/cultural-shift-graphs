# Result: Walking (30 min/day) — Cost, Safety, and Access for Bob

## Summary

Walking is genuinely the cheapest form of exercise available, but "free" is slightly misleading. Realistic all-in annual cost is $75–235 for shoes, socks, and basic weather gear — still trivially cheap compared to every other intervention. The safety profile is extraordinary: walking produces roughly 0.1–1 injuries per 1,000 hours, making it 10–50x safer than running per hour of exercise. It is the first-line exercise recommendation for obese individuals (BMI 30+) from ACSM, AHA, and federal guidelines. The real barriers are environmental and psychological: pedestrian fatality rates in low-income neighborhoods are 4.6x higher than in affluent areas, roughly 40–50% of the US population faces 60+ extreme weather days per year, and sustained daily commitment is non-trivial for someone who is depressed or fatigued. Adherence is nonetheless higher than for gym-based exercise (~75% in structured programs vs ~50% gym retention at 6 months).

## Cost Analysis (for Bob at ~$30k household income)

### Direct Financial Costs

**Footwear:**
- Budget walking shoes (Skechers, New Balance 411, ASICS Gel-Contend): $30–60/pair
- Replacement frequency at 30 min/day (~730 miles/year): every 300–500 miles = 1.5–2.5 pairs/year
- Annual shoe cost: **$45–150/year**
- BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey: the lowest income quintile spends approximately $300–400/year total on footwear (all types). Dedicated walking shoes are a subset of existing footwear spending, not purely incremental — Bob already owns shoes. The incremental cost may be as low as $0 if existing shoes are adequate, or the full $45–150 if he needs dedicated walking shoes.

**Other gear:**
- Athletic socks: $10–15/year
- Sunscreen (store brand, daily use): $10–20/year
- Basic rain jacket (Walmart): $15–30 one-time ($5–10/year amortized over 3 years)
- Hat and gloves: $10–20 one-time ($3–7/year amortized)
- Reflective vest (for evening/night walking): $5–10 one-time ($2–3/year amortized)

### Cost Summary

- **Annual direct cost:** $75
- **One-time startup cost:** $60–120 (shoes + basic gear)
- **Startup cost amortized over 3 years:** $20–40/year
- **Total annual cost (best estimate):** $75
- **Cost range:** $0 – $235 (low – high)
  - **Low ($0):** Bob walks in shoes he already owns, doesn't buy any gear
  - **Mid ($75):** One pair of budget walking shoes/year + minimal accessories
  - **High ($235):** 2.5 pairs of mid-range shoes/year + full weather gear + sunscreen
- **With insurance (if applicable):** N/A
- **Without insurance:** N/A — walking requires no medical involvement
- **Cost notes:** The $0 figure is defensible for the graph because most people own suitable footwear and can walk without additional purchases. The $75 mid-estimate assumes Bob needs at least one dedicated pair of budget walking shoes per year plus socks. At 0.25% of $30k gross income, even the high estimate is negligible. For the graph data table, using $0 as the midpoint (consistent with ticket 004) is reasonable since the marginal cost above what Bob would spend on shoes anyway is near-zero.

## Time Commitment

- **Hours per week:** 3.5 (30 min × 7 days)
- **Annual hours:** ~182
- **Category:** High (3+ hr/wk)
- **Note:** 150 min/week (5 days × 30 min = 2.5 hr/wk) is sufficient for the evidence-based dose. The ticket frames it as daily for simplicity, but the QALY evidence applies to 150+ min/week regardless of distribution across days.

## Access and Barriers

- **Activation energy:** Low
- **Barriers for Bob:**

### 1. Pedestrian Safety in Low-Income Areas

This is the most serious barrier. Data from Smart Growth America's Dangerous by Design 2024 report:

- Pedestrian fatality rate in census tracts with household income $15k–$25k: **4.90 per 100,000**
- Pedestrian fatality rate in tracts with income >$100k: **1.07 per 100,000**
- That is a **4.6x higher death rate** in Bob's likely neighborhoods
- Low-income areas (17% of population) account for **30% of all pedestrian deaths**
- Black Americans are killed at **2x the rate** of white Americans; Native Americans at **4x the rate**
- Infrastructure gaps are the root cause: low-income areas have fewer sidewalks, crosswalks, and streetlights
- 7,522 pedestrians killed in 2022 — a 40-year high, representing a 75% increase since 2010
- Much of the increase is attributed to larger vehicles (SUVs, pickups) and higher speed limits

**Mitigation:** Daylight walking, walking against traffic on streets without sidewalks, reflective clothing, choosing routes with sidewalks even if longer. These reduce but do not eliminate the disparity.

### 2. Sidewalk and Infrastructure Gaps

- No single national database tracks sidewalk coverage, but estimates suggest ~50% of urban residential streets lack sidewalks
- Suburban and rural roads overwhelmingly lack pedestrian infrastructure
- Low-income neighborhoods consistently have worse pedestrian infrastructure than affluent areas
- Walk Score data confirms pre-WWII urban cores are significantly more walkable than post-WWII suburbs
- For low-income older adults, each 10-point increase in Walk Score is associated with 45% greater odds of walking for transportation (PMID: 28395672)

### 3. Weather and Climate

Extreme weather makes outdoor walking impractical for significant portions of the year in many US regions:

| Region | Impractical Days/Year | Primary Barrier |
|--------|----------------------|-----------------|
| Sun Belt (Phoenix, Las Vegas) | 90–120+ | Extreme heat (100°F+ for 4–5 months) |
| Gulf Coast (Houston, Miami) | 60–90 | Heat + humidity (heat index 103°F+) |
| Upper Midwest (Minneapolis, Chicago) | 50–80 | Extreme cold + ice (Jan avg low 9°F, ~100 snow-cover days) |
| Southeast (Atlanta) | 20–30 | Mix of heat/cold extremes |
| Pacific Coast (LA, San Diego) | 5–15 | Mild climate year-round |

**Approximately 40–50% of the US population** lives in areas with 60+ days/year where outdoor walking is impractical due to extreme heat (>95°F heat index), extreme cold (<15°F wind chill), ice, or heavy precipitation.

NWS heat index thresholds: 91–103°F = "Caution" (fatigue/sunstroke possible); 103–124°F = "Danger" (heat cramps/exhaustion likely). Full sunshine adds up to 15°F to the heat index.

**Indoor alternatives:**
- Mall walking: free, organized programs exist in many malls, but requires transportation
- Used treadmills: $100–300 on Facebook Marketplace; new budget models $200–500
- Community centers: many offer free or low-cost indoor walking tracks
- School gymnasiums: some open to community use during off-hours

### 4. Obesity and Physical Limitations

Walking is explicitly recommended as first-line exercise for obese individuals by ACSM, AHA, and the 2018 Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans. However:

- Obese individuals exert **60% more vertical ground reaction force** than normal-weight individuals during walking (Messier, 2010)
- A person going from 120 lbs to 285 lbs adds ~660 lbs of force across the patellofemoral joint with each step
- Walking produces peak knee forces of **2.6–3.2x body weight** (instrumented knee implant study, PMID: 24465856), compared to slow jogging at 22–54% higher forces
- Walking is still substantially safer than any impact exercise for heavy individuals
- "Exercise is a safe intervention in patients with knee OA with few contraindications or adverse events" (Messier, 2010, PMID: 20699166)
- Obese children show 1.98x greater patellofemoral joint forces than healthy-weight children (PMID: 31374438)
- **Very heavy individuals (300+ lbs)** may benefit from starting with shorter durations (10–15 min) and building up gradually, but walking is not contraindicated

**Contraindications (rare):**
- Severe osteoarthritis with acute joint inflammation (temporary)
- Unstable cardiac conditions (contraindicates ALL exercise)
- Active diabetic foot wounds/ulcers
- None of these are walking-specific; they apply to all exercise

### 5. Psychological Barriers

- For someone with depression (12–14% prevalence in Bob's income bracket), sustained daily commitment requires activation energy that depression directly undermines
- Fatigue, low motivation, and anhedonia can make "just go for a walk" feel insurmountable
- No social accountability unless Bob finds a walking partner or group
- One walking study participant withdrew as "overweight and self-conscious" — body image can be a barrier even for walking
- Shift work (more common in low-income jobs) limits daylight walking hours and disrupts routine

### 6. Crime and Perceived Safety

- Crime rates and perceived safety are significant barriers in low-income urban neighborhoods
- Fear of crime reduces walking more than actual crime rates do
- Women, elderly, and people with visible disabilities face heightened perceived vulnerability
- This is a genuine barrier but difficult to quantify precisely — it varies enormously by specific neighborhood

## Side Effects and Risks

- **Musculoskeletal injury:** Walking injury rate is **0.1–1 per 1,000 hours** of exercise, roughly 10–50x lower than running (6.5–17.8 per 1,000 hours). In a systematic review of 42 walking studies covering 74,000+ participant hours (Hanson & Jones, PMC4453623), adverse events were: one fall with brief absence, one calf injury, one exercise-induced hypotension, four falls on roots/wet ground (Parkinson's disease study). Most studies reported zero adverse events.
- **Pedestrian-vehicle collision:** Real risk, especially in low-income areas (4.6x higher fatality rate). Mitigated by daylight walking, sidewalks, and reflective gear. Annual pedestrian fatality rate nationwide is ~2.3 per 100,000 population.
- **Falls:** Low risk for adults under 65; moderate risk for older adults on uneven terrain or ice.
- **Overexertion in extreme weather:** Heat stroke, hypothermia. Mitigated by avoiding extreme conditions.
- **Net risk assessment:** Negligible for the exercise itself. Environmental risks (traffic, crime) are the primary concerns and are addressable but not eliminable.

**Injury rate comparison:**

| Activity | Injuries per 1,000 hours | Source |
|----------|-------------------------|--------|
| Walking | 0.1–1.0 | Parkkari et al. 2004, Hanson & Jones |
| Recreational running | 6.5–7.7 | Videbaek et al. 2015, Naderi et al. 2024 |
| Novice running | 17.8 | Videbaek et al. 2015 |
| Contact/team sports | 6.6–18.3 | Parkkari et al. 2004 |

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate:** 0.50
- **Source:** Composite. Structured walking programs average ~75% adherence (Hanson & Jones systematic review of 42 studies, 1,843 participants). General unsupervised exercise adherence: ~50% dropout in first 3–6 months. Gym membership: 50% quit within 6 months, only 18–20% attend regularly 3+ times/week (Glofox 2024 industry data). For Bob specifically — unsupervised, with environmental and psychological barriers — 50% at 12 months is a reasonable estimate.
- **Notes:**
  - Walking has higher adherence than gym-based exercise because it requires no equipment, no travel, no skill, no social anxiety about gym environments
  - Adherence improves with: walking partners/groups, pedometer/step tracker use, flexible scheduling, habit-stacking (walking commute, dog walking)
  - Adherence drops with: weather extremes, injury, unsafe walking routes, depression, shift work
  - Low-income-specific barrier: lack of transportation to organized walking programs (walking clubs, mall walking). Walking from home eliminates this but removes social support
  - 29% of English adults do less than 30 min of moderate activity per week; 8% don't walk continuously for even 5 minutes over a 4-week period — baseline activity levels are very low for many people

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 1
- **Rationale:** Cost and access data draws on large national datasets (Smart Growth America, BLS, CDC). Safety data comes from a systematic review of 42 walking studies covering 74,000+ participant hours. Joint force data comes from instrumented knee implant studies. The evidence base for walking safety and accessibility is mature and consistent.
- **Key studies:**
  - Smart Growth America (2024). *Dangerous by Design 2024.* Pedestrian fatality rates by income.
  - Hanson S, Jones A. Systematic review of walking program safety. PMC4453623. 42 studies, 74,000+ participant hours.
  - Parkkari J et al. (2004). Finnish population cohort injury study. PMID: 15088246.
  - Videbaek S et al. (2015). Running injury systematic review. PMID: 25951917.
  - Messier SP (2010). Obesity, osteoarthritis, and exercise. PMID: 20699166.
  - Bergmann G et al. Instrumented knee implant forces. PMID: 24465856.
  - Kujala UM et al. (2006). Finnish knee injury study. PMID: 16739094.
  - Naderi A et al. (2024). Recreational running injury rate. PMID: 38311884.
- **Limitations:**
  - Sidewalk coverage statistics lack a comprehensive national dataset — estimates are pieced together from local studies
  - Weather impact on walking is estimated from climate data, not directly studied as a barrier to walking programs
  - Crime/perceived safety impact is qualitative rather than quantified
  - Adherence data is from general populations, not specifically low-income brackets
