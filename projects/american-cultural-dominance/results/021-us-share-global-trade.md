# US Share of Global Merchandise Exports

## Summary
The United States rose from a minor agricultural exporter (~4% of world exports in 1800) to the world's dominant trader after World War II (~22% in 1948), then saw steady relative decline as Europe and Japan rebuilt and later as China emerged. From the post-war peak of ~22%, US share fell to ~12% by 1980, stabilized through the 1990s, then dropped sharply after China's WTO accession (2001) to ~8% today. The arc is unmistakable: explosive rise through industrialization and two world wars, followed by a long structural decline in relative share even as absolute exports grew massively.

## Penetration Score Definition
**Denominator:** US merchandise exports as a percentage of total world merchandise exports.
**0% anchor:** The US exports zero goods internationally — no American products reach any foreign market.
**100% anchor:** Every single good exported anywhere in the world originates from the United States.

## Methodology
The penetration score is US merchandise exports divided by world total merchandise exports, expressed as a percentage. This is a natural 0-100 metric that requires no transformation.

**1960-2023:** Calculated directly from World Bank data (indicator TX.VAL.MRCH.CD.WT) for both US and World totals. These are merchandise exports in current US dollars, sourced from WTO and UN Comtrade via the World Bank.

**1948-1958:** Based on WTO/GATT historical trade statistics, which are the standard reference for post-war trade shares. The 1948 figure of ~22% is among the most widely cited statistics in trade history.

**Pre-1948:** Estimates derived from multiple sources: Angus Maddison's historical GDP reconstructions, Paul Bairoch's work on industrialization and trade shares, NBER historical trade data, and US Census Bureau historical trade records. These are less precise but directionally consistent across sources. The methodology prioritizes the overall arc — small agricultural exporter → industrial powerhouse → post-war hegemon → relative decline — over point precision.

**Consistency:** The denominator (world merchandise exports) is held constant throughout: total value of goods exported by all countries worldwide. Services trade is excluded for consistency, as services trade data doesn't exist before the 1970s.

**Cultural relevance:** Trade share matters for cultural penetration because American products carry American branding, packaging, language, and lifestyle associations. Ford trucks, IBM mainframes, Boeing jets, Apple iPhones — each export is a cultural vector. The US also disproportionately exports branded consumer goods and technology, which have outsized cultural impact relative to commodity exports.

## Notes
- **Post-WW2 peak is structural, not organic.** The 22% share in 1948 reflects European/Japanese destruction, not solely American strength. As competitors rebuilt (Marshall Plan, Japanese miracle), US share naturally declined. The "decline" narrative is misleading — US absolute exports grew massively; it's just that the rest of the world grew faster.
- **China's WTO accession (2001) is the sharpest inflection.** US share fell from 12.1% (2000) to 8.5% (2005) — a 3.6-point drop in 5 years, the steepest decline in the dataset. China went from ~4% to ~8% of world exports in the same period.
- **Services trade not captured.** The US is the world's largest services exporter (financial services, IP licensing, entertainment, software). If services were included, US share would be significantly higher in the modern era (~11-12% of total trade). This metric specifically tracks merchandise to maintain historical consistency.
- **US trade-to-GDP ratio is low.** The US has always been less trade-dependent than most countries due to its enormous domestic market. Even at the 1948 peak, exports were only ~5% of US GDP. This means US cultural influence through trade has always been "understated" relative to its economic weight.
- **The 1990s stability is notable.** Despite NAFTA, WTO formation, and the Asian financial crisis, US share barely budged from 11-12% for the entire decade. This was the last period of relative US trade hegemony before the China shock.
- **Pre-1948 estimates carry significant uncertainty.** Historical trade data before WTO/GATT records is reconstructed from fragmentary national statistics. The directional trends are reliable; exact percentages are approximate.
