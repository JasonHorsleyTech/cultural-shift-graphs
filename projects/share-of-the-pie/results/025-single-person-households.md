# Result: Single-Person Household Share, 1960–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** U.S. Census Bureau, Table HH-4 "Households by Size: 1960 to Present," Current Population Survey (CPS), Annual Social and Economic Supplement (ASEC). Downloaded from https://www2.census.gov/programs-surveys/demo/tables/families/time-series/households/hh4.xls
- **Date accessed:** 2026-05-01

## Methodology
- **Definition:** One-person households as a percentage of all households. A one-person household is a housing unit occupied by exactly one person.
- **Universe:** All occupied housing units (households) in the United States, as estimated by the CPS ASEC.
- **Known methodology changes:**
  - CPS redesign in 1994 (new questionnaire and processing). The 1993 and 2011 data have revised ("r") estimates reflecting updated controls.
  - ACS integration post-2010 affected population controls.
  - 2014 marked with "s" (supplementary sample or revised weighting).
  - Pre-1967 data is from the CPS March supplement but with less consistent methodology.

## Gaps and Caveats
- **1993 break:** The 1993 data has a revised figure (shown here) reflecting the CPS redesign. The original 1993 estimate was slightly higher (23,642 thousand, 24.5%). The revised figure is used for consistency with the post-redesign series.
- **2011 break:** Revised population controls applied; the revised figure (33,020 thousand) is used rather than the original (32,723 thousand).
- **2021 revised:** The 2021 row uses the revised estimate (36,497 thousand) rather than the original (36,972 thousand).
- **2000–2001 jump:** The total household count jumped from 104.7 million to 108.2 million between 2000 and 2001, likely reflecting updated Census 2000 population controls applied to CPS estimates. This affects the level but not the trend.
- **Confidence assessment:**
  - 1960–1966: Medium (pre-ASEC era, smaller sample sizes)
  - 1967–2024: High (full CPS ASEC methodology, large sample)
- **The percentage column is computed** (one-person / total × 100) and may differ by 0.1 pp from published Census figures due to rounding of the underlying thousands.

## Key Trends
- The share more than doubled from 13.1% (1960) to 29.1% (2024).
- The fastest growth occurred 1965–1980 (15.0% → 22.7%), driven by young adults leaving home earlier, rising divorce rates, and elderly women living longer.
- Growth slowed dramatically after 1990 (~25%) and has only gained about 4 percentage points in the 34 years since.
- The 2020s show a modest acceleration (28.2% in 2020 → 29.1% in 2024), likely reflecting aging baby boomers entering widowhood/solo living.
