# Ticket 005: Walking (30 min/day) — Cost and Access for Bob

## Objective

Quantify the real-world costs, safety considerations, and access barriers of a daily 30-minute walking habit for someone at the 25th percentile of household income.

## What to research

1. **Direct financial cost** — genuinely $0, or are there hidden costs? Dedicated walking shoes, weather gear, sunscreen, etc. What's the realistic minimum annual cost?

2. **Time cost** — 30 min/day × 365 = 182 hours/year. Document this as the weekly hours figure. Do NOT monetize it — just report the hours.

3. **Safety costs by geography**
   - Pedestrian fatality rates in low-income areas vs. national average (GHSA data)
   - Sidewalk availability in low-income neighborhoods
   - Crime rates and perceived safety for walking in Bob's likely neighborhoods
   - Injury rates from walking (trips, falls, dog attacks, traffic)

4. **Weather and climate barriers**
   - % of US population in areas where outdoor walking is impractical for >60 days/year (extreme heat, cold, ice, rain)
   - Indoor alternatives and their costs (mall walking = free but requires transport; treadmill = $200-500+)

5. **Accessibility**
   - Can a 300+ lb person walk 30 min/day? Joint stress, mobility limitations
   - Walking with chronic pain or disability

6. **Activation energy** — what does it actually take to start? No referral, no equipment, no appointment. But sustained daily commitment is psychologically non-trivial for someone who is depressed/fatigued.

## Where to look

1. **GHSA (Governors Highway Safety Association)** — pedestrian fatality data by income area
2. **Smart Growth America** — Dangerous by Design reports, walkability metrics
3. **NOAA climate data** — extreme weather days by region
4. **BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey** — footwear spending by income quintile
5. **CDC injury data** — walking-related injury rates

## Output format

Use the intervention result format from plan.md. This ticket focuses on the cost/access sections. For QALY data, reference ticket 004's results (or use "see ticket 004" as placeholder).

## What "good data" looks like

- Safety data specific to lower-income neighborhoods, not national averages
- Actual financial cost breakdown (even if it's $20-50/year for shoe wear)
- Honest assessment of barriers without overstating them — walking IS genuinely low-barrier for most people
