# Bread (Loaf, White) — Cheapest Prices

## Summary

White bread prices were remarkably stable from 1950 through the early 1970s, rising from $0.14/lb to just $0.25/lb over two decades. The 1973-74 wheat crisis (triggered by massive US grain exports to the Soviet Union) caused the first structural break, pushing prices from $0.25 to $0.35 in two years. Late-1970s inflation drove another jump to $0.51 by 1980. From 1980 to 2019, prices rose slowly and steadily from $0.51 to $1.30/lb — a remarkably boring commodity. Post-COVID inflation in 2022-2024 pushed prices to a record $2.03/lb (January 2024) before a modest retreat.

## Methodology

### Primary series: BLS Average Price (APU0000702111)

The core data source from 1980 onward is the Bureau of Labor Statistics Average Price series for "Bread, white, pan, per lb (453.6 gm)" in U.S. City Average (series APU0000702111). BLS representatives collect monthly retail prices in 75 urban areas for the Consumer Price Index program. Annual figures are the arithmetic mean of 12 monthly observations. This series tracks all white pre-packaged regular bread regardless of size, including frozen and fresh.

### Pre-1980 data

The BLS APU series in its current form begins January 1980. For 1950-1978, data was sourced from BLS historical average price compilations as reported in Stacker's "The cost of goods the year you were born" (citing BLS data). These pre-1980 prices measure the same product (white pan bread, per pound, retail) through earlier survey methodology. The splice is smooth — 1978 ($0.36) through the estimated 1979 ($0.41) to 1980 ($0.51) follows a plausible trajectory during a period of ~11-13% annual inflation.

Cross-validation: The fiftiesweb.com historical price compilation reports identical 1950s values ($0.14 in 1950, $0.18 in 1955, $0.20 in 1959), described there as "per loaf." Since a standard loaf was 1 lb in that era, per-loaf and per-pound figures are equivalent.

### 1979 estimate

BLS average prices were not published from July 1978 through December 1979 while the average price methodology was revised. The 1979 value ($0.41) was estimated by applying the BLS CPI white bread index ratio for 1978-1979 (76.808 / 68.625 = 1.119, i.e., ~12% increase) to the 1978 price of $0.36. This yields $0.403, rounded to $0.41. The estimate is consistent with the trajectory from $0.36 (1978) to $0.51 (1980 annual average) during a period of severe general inflation.

### "Cheapest" vs. BLS average

The BLS APU series tracks the average price of all white pan bread sampled, which includes store brands, national brands, and all sizes. White bread has far less quality-tier stratification than eggs — the BLS average IS effectively the cheapest mainstream product for most of this history. The cheapest white bread (store brand) runs ~10-20% below the BLS average in recent years, but the trend and trajectory are identical. For consistency with other commodities in this project, the BLS average is used throughout.

### Pre-quality-divergence note

Before the 2000s artisan bread movement, there was essentially one tier of white bread. Line A (cheapest) and Line B (quality-equivalent) are the same for the entire 1950-~1995 period. The quality-equivalent ticket (011) will focus on post-divergence pricing.

### Loaf size normalization

A "standard loaf" was 1 lb in the 1950s-1970s. Modern cheap bread is often sold in 20 oz loaves, but the BLS tracks price per pound, which controls for this. All data is reported per pound throughout.

### Structural breaks

1. **1973-74 wheat crisis:** The Soviet Union secretly purchased 19 million tons of US grain in 1972 (the "Great Grain Robbery"). Wheat futures tripled. Bread prices jumped from $0.25/lb (1972) to $0.35/lb (1974) — a 40% increase in two years. Prices never returned to pre-1973 levels.
2. **1978-1980 inflation surge:** General inflation of 11-13% per year pushed bread from $0.36 to $0.51 over two years.
3. **2007-2008 commodity boom:** Global wheat futures peaked in February 2008. Bread prices rose from $1.08 (2006) to $1.37 (2008) — 27% in two years. Unlike eggs, bread prices showed sticky post-spike behavior: they plateaued at $1.37-$1.44 through 2015 rather than falling back.
4. **2022-2024 post-COVID inflation:** Supply chain disruptions, labor cost increases, and the Russia-Ukraine war (Ukraine produces ~10% of global wheat) pushed prices from $1.30 (2019) to a record monthly high of $2.03 in January 2024 — a 56% increase. Prices moderated slightly through 2024-2025 but remained elevated.

## Known gaps

- **1979:** BLS average prices not published during methodology revision. Estimated from CPI index. Confidence: medium.
- **Pre-1950 data:** BLS has bread price data back to at least 1930 (Stacker source shows $0.09/lb in 1930), but not included per ticket scope.
- **Cheapest vs. average post-2010:** The BLS series may overstate the cheapest white bread by 10-20% in recent years, since store-brand bread (e.g., Walmart Great Value at ~$1.28/loaf or ~$0.80/lb for a 24 oz loaf in 2024) is cheaper than the BLS all-white-bread average. No nationally consistent series tracks only the cheapest white bread.
- **2025 partial year:** Only January-May data available. Annual average may shift as more months are collected.
