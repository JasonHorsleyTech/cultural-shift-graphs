# Ticket 027: Sugar → Obesity / Metabolic Disease

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that excess sugar consumption drives obesity, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic syndrome to binding regulation restricting sugar in food (taxes, reformulation mandates, marketing restrictions).

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — John Yudkin published "Pure, White and Deadly" in 1972, arguing sugar was a primary driver of heart disease. His work was marginalized by Ancel Keys's fat hypothesis and industry-funded counter-research (Sugar Research Foundation). The modern revival: Lustig's viral lecture (2009), growing evidence through the 2000s-2010s. When did credible scientific evidence establish the causal link between added sugar and metabolic disease?

2. **Consensus date** — WHO guideline recommending <10% of energy from free sugars (2015). Earlier WHO/FAO joint reports? Scientific Advisory Committee on Nutrition (UK, 2015)? When did a major body formally recommend reducing sugar intake based on disease evidence?

3. **First OECD regulation** — Denmark had a general confectionery tax from the 1930s (not science-based). Mexico enacted a sugar-sweetened beverage tax in 2014 (explicitly health-motivated). UK Soft Drinks Industry Levy (2018). Hungary's "chips tax" (2011) included sugary drinks. Find the first HEALTH-MOTIVATED binding tax or restriction in an OECD country.

4. **US regulation** — No federal sugar tax or restriction. Some cities have enacted soda taxes (Berkeley 2014, Philadelphia 2017). FDA requires added sugars on nutrition labels (2020, voluntary compliance earlier). What's the strongest US federal action?

5. **Industry opposition** — The Sugar Research Foundation (now Sugar Association) funded research to deflect blame from sugar to fat in the 1960s (Kearns et al., 2016 exposed this). Coca-Cola funded the Global Energy Balance Network to emphasize exercise over diet. Strong, sustained, tobacco-style opposition.

6. **Ongoing status** — This is an in-progress case. Regulation is patchy and weak globally. No major economy has banned sugar or imposed comprehensive restrictions.

## Where to look

1. WHO Guideline on Sugar Intake (2015)
2. Yudkin, "Pure, White and Deadly" (1972)
3. Kearns, Schmidt & Glantz (2016) — JAMA Internal Medicine, exposing sugar industry funding of counter-research
4. Mexico sugar tax research and legislation
5. UK Soft Drinks Industry Levy (2018) and Public Health England data on reformulation
6. Lustig, "Fat Chance" (2012) and related publications

## What "good data" looks like

- The Yudkin (1972) vs. modern consensus (2015) timeline shows a ~43-year gap where evidence was suppressed/disputed
- Distinguish between general confectionery taxes (not health-motivated) and health-specific sugar policies
- The industry disinformation campaign parallels tobacco — document specific campaigns
- regulation_status: in-progress. This is a live case.

## Known pitfalls

- Sugar is not a toxin — it's a macronutrient consumed in excess. "Regulation" for sugar is fundamentally different from banning a toxic chemical. The strongest regulatory tool is taxation, not prohibition. This affects the category (dietary-policy) and the interpretation of the lag.
- The 1972 Yudkin evidence was CORRECT but was successfully disputed for decades. Is the early_evidence_year 1972 (Yudkin) or ~2010 (when modern consensus re-formed)? Document the reasoning — this is one of the most important judgment calls in the dataset.
- Labeling requirements (US added sugar labels) are disclosure, not restriction. Don't count them as "binding regulation that reduces exposure."
