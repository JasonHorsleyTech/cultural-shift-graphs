# Result: Median Income for Households with Householder Age 25–64

## Source
- **Primary source:** U.S. Census Bureau, Current Population Survey, 1968 to 2025 Annual Social and Economic Supplements (CPS ASEC), Historical Income Table H-10: "Age of Householder—All Households by Median and Mean Income: 1967 to 2024"
- **URL:** https://www.census.gov/data/tables/time-series/demo/income-poverty/historical-income-households.html (Table H-10AR)
- **Direct file:** https://www2.census.gov/programs-surveys/cps/tables/time-series/historical-income-households/h10ar.xlsx
- **Date accessed:** 2026-04-30

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Median household income for households where the householder falls in the specified age bracket. "Householder" is the person in whose name the home is owned or rented.
- **Universe:** All households in the United States with householder in the given age range. Householders 15 years old and over beginning with March 1980; householders 14 years old and over for previous years.
- **Income concept:** Total money income — includes wages, salaries, self-employment income, interest, dividends, rents, Social Security, public assistance, pensions, and other money income. Excludes capital gains and non-cash benefits.
- **Dollar basis:** Current (nominal) dollars — not inflation-adjusted.
- **Census does not publish a combined 25–64 median.** Data is reported in four separate age brackets: 25–34, 35–44, 45–54, 55–64. The graph phase can use the 45–54 bracket as the "prime working age" proxy, or compute a weighted average from the bracket medians and population counts.

### Known methodology changes
- **2020 (footnote 41):** Updated CPS ASEC processing system implemented.
- **2017 (footnote 40):** Updated CPS ASEC processing system implemented. Two estimates exist for 2017 — the table below uses the newer methodology.
- **2013 (footnote 39):** Redesigned income questions implemented. Two estimates exist for 2013 — the table below uses the newer methodology.
- **2010 (footnote 37):** Implementation of 2010 Census-based population controls.
- **2009 (footnote 36):** Population control changes.
- **2004 (footnote 35):** Implementation of 2004-based population controls.
- **2000 (footnote 30):** Implementation of Census 2000-based population controls and other changes.
- **1993–1995, 1987, 1985, 1984:** Various footnotes for survey/processing changes. See Census footnotes page for full details.

Where the Census provides two estimates for the same year (old and new methodology), the table below uses the **newer methodology** estimate for that year. This creates small discontinuities at break years.

## Gaps and Caveats

- **No combined 25–64 median available.** Census publishes medians for each age bracket separately. A simple average of the four bracket medians is NOT a true median for the combined 25–64 group. Options for the graph phase: (a) use 45–54 as the "prime working age" proxy (highest-earning bracket, often used in literature), (b) compute a population-weighted average of bracket medians (approximate but reasonable), or (c) show all four brackets as separate lines.
- **Methodology breaks in 2013, 2017, and 2020.** The table uses the newer methodology at each break point. This may introduce small discontinuities — typically 1–3% jumps. The 2013 redesigned income questions generally produced higher income estimates than the old questions.
- **"Householder" is somewhat arbitrary for married couples.** The Census designates one person as the householder (the person in whose name the home is owned/rented). The householder's age doesn't perfectly capture the ages of all earners in the household.
- **Income concept does not include employer benefits.** This is total money income only — employer-provided health insurance, retirement contributions, and other non-cash compensation are excluded. This is important context when comparing to GDP per capita.
- **2020 anomaly:** Stimulus payments (Economic Impact Payments) are not counted as income in the CPS, but pandemic unemployment benefits are. The 2020 figures reflect both pandemic job losses and enhanced UI benefits.
- **Confidence: HIGH for 1967–2024.** This is the official Census Bureau CPS ASEC data, the gold-standard source for household income by demographics. Sample sizes are large (~100K households). The main uncertainty is around methodology break years.
