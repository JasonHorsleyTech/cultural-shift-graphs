# Result: Post-Collapse Violence and Social Dynamics

## Summary

Violence in societal collapses is almost always secondary to disease and starvation as a cause of death, except in cases of deliberate genocide. In the DRC, less than 10% of 5.4 million excess deaths were from violence; in Somalia's 1991-92 famine, direct combat killed ~25,000 while famine killed ~250,000. The critical exception is Rwanda, where organized genocide made violence the primary killer (~90%+ of deaths). For a US grid-down scenario, the unprecedented civilian firearms density (~400M guns) is a genuine wildcard with no historical precedent — it likely increases both the lethality of interpersonal violence and the effectiveness of community defense simultaneously, producing a wide uncertainty band. The best estimate for a violence mortality amplifier is 1.15–1.40x on top of starvation/disease deaths, with a plausible range of 1.05–1.50x.

## Historical Violence as Fraction of Collapse Deaths

### Rwanda (1994) — Violence-dominant collapse
- ~800,000–1,074,000 killed in ~100 days, primarily by direct violence (machetes, firearms)
- Violence accounted for ~93% of excess deaths during the genocide period
- This was organized, ethnically-targeted genocide — not resource competition
- Disease became significant only in refugee camps after the genocide
- **Not a good proxy for resource-scarcity collapse**, but shows the ceiling for violence-driven mortality

### Siege of Leningrad (1941-1944) — Starvation-dominant collapse
- 630,000–1,500,000 deaths out of a population of 2.9 million
- Vast majority died of starvation and cold; violence was a minor contributor
- Murder for ration cards documented: 1,216 cases in first 6 months of 1942 alone
- Shelling/bombing added military violence but primary killer was hunger
- Violence share of total deaths: ~1-5%
- **Best proxy for prolonged siege/grid-down starvation scenario**

### DRC (1998-2007) — Disease-dominant collapse
- 5.4 million excess deaths (IRC surveys)
- Less than 10% attributed to violence; IRC found only 0.4% of all deaths directly attributable to violence
- Primary killers: malaria, diarrhea, pneumonia, malnutrition
- Nearly half of deaths were children under 5
- **Best proxy for prolonged infrastructure collapse with cascading public health failure**

### Somalia (1991-1993) — Mixed violence/famine
- ~25,000 killed in Mogadishu fighting (4 months of combat, 1991-92)
- ~250,000 died in the resulting famine
- Violence share: ~9% of total excess mortality
- Warlords controlled harbors and airports for resource extraction within months
- Armed factions weaponized food — blocking aid deliveries was a deliberate strategy
- **Good proxy for armed-group resource control dynamics**

### Bosnia (1992-1995) — Organized warfare
- ~100,000 killed, primarily through direct violence
- 64,036 Bosniak deaths: 52% civilians, 48% soldiers
- Violence share: ~85% (this was active warfare with ethnic cleansing)
- Siege of Sarajevo combined violence + starvation but violence dominated
- **Proxy for organized ethnic/factional warfare, less applicable to resource-scarcity collapse**

### Syria (2011-2021) — Violence-dominant with infrastructure cascade
- ~600,000+ killed; ~307,000 civilians
- Majority from direct violence (bombardment, combat), but disease and healthcare collapse contributed significantly
- Healthcare infrastructure deliberately targeted
- Violence share: ~70% of excess mortality
- **Shows how destruction of healthcare multiplies indirect deaths**

## The Arms Factor: US Civilian Firearms as Unprecedented Variable

### The numbers
- ~400–500 million civilian firearms in the US (Small Arms Survey, updated estimates)
- ~32% of US adults (111.4 million) own firearms; 46% of households have at least one
- 1.5 guns per US resident; 2 per adult
- Americans own ~46% of the world's civilian firearms while being ~4% of world population
- No collapsed society in history has had remotely comparable civilian armament

### Arguments that firearms INCREASE violence mortality
- Firearms make interpersonal conflict far more lethal (gunshot fatality rate ~22% vs ~1.4% for knife wounds)
- Low-skill violence: unlike edged weapons, firearms require minimal training to inflict mass casualties
- Ammunition availability: most US gun owners maintain stockpiles; usable for months to years
- Escalation dynamics: when everyone is armed, conflicts that would otherwise resolve through intimidation or fistfight become lethal
- Research in normal conditions: higher gun ownership correlates with higher homicide rates in most (not all) studies

### Arguments that firearms DECREASE violence mortality
- Deterrence: armed households are harder to raid, creating a defensive equilibrium
- Community defense: armed neighborhoods can deter warlord-style takeover attempts
- Hunting: firearms enable food procurement (game), reducing starvation-driven resource competition
- Force equalization: firearms prevent physically strong individuals/groups from dominating weaker ones
- Historical precedent: Swiss civilian armament model suggests armed populations can be stable

### Net assessment
The evidence is genuinely ambiguous. Normal-conditions research on gun ownership and crime is methodologically contested (RAND, NRC reviews find no clear causal relationship). In a collapse scenario, both effects operate simultaneously. The most likely outcome: firearms increase the lethality of individual violent encounters while providing some community-level deterrent effect. The net impact is probably modestly violence-increasing (more lethal per encounter outweighs partial deterrence), but the uncertainty band is enormous.

## Resource Competition Dynamics

### Timeline of armed group formation
Historical analogues suggest:
- **Days 1-7**: Opportunistic looting and individual/small-group violence. Primarily in urban areas with high population density and visible resources.
- **Days 7-30 (168-720 hours)**: Organized gangs form around existing social networks (pre-existing criminal organizations, neighborhood groups, military/police units). Control of food stores, pharmacies, fuel stations.
- **Weeks 4-12 (720-2160 hours)**: Territorial control emerges. Groups consolidate around defensible resources — water treatment, warehouses, agricultural land. This matches Somalia (months) and Libya (weeks to months) timelines.
- **Months 3-12**: Warlord-style governance solidifies in areas with sufficient population and resources. Trade networks form between controlled territories.

### Key dynamics
- **Weaponized food access**: Somalia's warlords demonstrated that controlling food distribution (including blocking aid) is the primary power mechanism in famine conditions
- **Infrastructure control**: Warlords in Somalia fought primarily over harbors and airports — the chokepoints for resource flow
- **In a US context**: grocery distribution centers, water treatment plants, fuel depots, and agricultural regions would be the equivalents

## Cooperation Dynamics (The Counterweight)

### Disaster sociology consensus
The consistent finding across decades of disaster research (Quarantelli, Solnit, Tierney) is that the typical human response to disaster is prosocial:
- Spontaneous mutual aid, self-organization, and altruism are the norm, not the exception
- "Elite panic" (authorities overreacting with militarized response) typically causes more harm than civilian disorder
- Katrina "anarchy" was largely media myth: 4 deaths in Superdome (1 from violence), 237 looting arrests — far below normal crime rates for the population

### Critical caveat: duration and severity
The disaster sociology literature is based on **short-duration disasters with external aid available** (earthquakes, hurricanes, floods). These events last days to weeks, and affected populations know help is coming. The key findings:
- Prosocial behavior dominates when: (a) the crisis is perceived as temporary, (b) external aid is expected, (c) pre-existing social bonds are intact
- Prosocial behavior erodes when: (a) the crisis becomes indefinite, (b) no external aid is forthcoming, (c) resource scarcity becomes existential

No disaster in the modern US has tested what happens when all three erosion conditions are met simultaneously. Extrapolating from Katrina to a permanent grid-down scenario is a category error.

### Dunbar number and community formation
- Effective cooperative groups top out at ~150 people (Dunbar's number)
- Historical examples: Neolithic farming villages, Hutterite settlements split at ~150, Roman military centuries
- In collapse: expect communities of 50-150 to be the unit of cooperation
- Larger groups require hierarchy, rules, enforcement — which takes time to establish
- Smaller groups (<30) are more cohesive but less capable of defense

### Military and National Guard behavior
- US National Guard: ~450,000 members across all states
- Active duty military: ~1.3 million
- Historical pattern: military units maintain cohesion as long as chain of command holds
- When chain of command fractures: units typically fragment along pre-existing social lines (geographic, ethnic, unit loyalty)
- Some units become local protectors; others become armed factions themselves
- Yugoslavia analogy: JNA fractured along ethnic lines, with units becoming the nuclei of factional armies

## Violence Mortality Estimate for US Grid-Down Scenario

### The amplifier concept
The "violence mortality amplifier" represents additional deaths from violence as a multiplier on top of the starvation/disease baseline. A value of 1.25x means that for every 100 people who would die from starvation and disease, 25 additional people die from violence.

### Estimate: 1.25x (range: 1.05–1.50x)

**Reasoning for central estimate of 1.25x:**
- Historical analogues where violence was NOT the primary goal (DRC, Somalia, Leningrad) show violence accounting for 3-15% of total excess deaths
- But the US firearms variable has no historical precedent and likely pushes the violence fraction higher
- Urban areas would see higher violence; rural areas would approximate historical patterns
- The US has both strong community structures AND deep social divisions (political, racial, economic)
- Adjusting historical ~10% violence share upward by ~2-3x for firearms prevalence gives ~20-30%, equivalent to a 1.20-1.40x amplifier

**Reasoning for lower bound of 1.05x:**
- If prosocial behavior dominates (as disaster sociology predicts for initial period)
- If military/law enforcement maintain cohesion in most areas
- If firearms function primarily as deterrent, not as offensive tool
- Approaches the DRC pattern where violence is a tiny fraction

**Reasoning for upper bound of 1.50x:**
- If political/ethnic factionalism triggers organized violence (Bosnia/Syria pattern)
- If firearm lethality fully dominates over deterrent effect
- If urban areas descend into prolonged armed conflict over resources
- Still well below Rwanda's genocide pattern (which is a fundamentally different scenario)

### Time profile of violence
- **Hours 0-168 (week 1)**: Minimal additional violence. Shock, stored food still available, prosocial response dominates. Violence amplifier: ~1.02x
- **Hours 168-720 (weeks 2-4)**: Violence rises as stored food depletes. Looting escalates. Armed confrontations over resources begin. Amplifier: ~1.10x
- **Hours 720-2160 (weeks 4-12)**: Peak violence period. Food scarcity is acute, organized groups compete for territory. Amplifier: ~1.30-1.40x
- **Hours 2160-8760 (months 3-12)**: Violence stabilizes at moderate level as territories are established and populations thin. Amplifier: ~1.25x
- **Hours 8760+ (year 1+)**: Violence declines as surviving populations are in established communities with norms. Amplifier: ~1.10-1.15x

## Data Gaps

- **No modern precedent for nationwide permanent grid loss in a heavily armed society.** The US firearms variable is genuinely unprecedented. All historical analogues are partial at best.
- **Disaster sociology is based on short-duration, aid-available events.** Extrapolating to permanent collapse is speculative. The Katrina/Sandy/Occupy data tells us about weeks 1-2, not months 3-12.
- **Violence-cooperation interaction is non-linear.** Small increases in resource scarcity can cause phase transitions in social behavior. The tipping point is unknown.
- **Urban-rural divide is enormous but unquantified.** Urban violence could be 3-5x the rural rate in collapse, but the population-weighted average is unclear.
- **Interpersonal vs. organized violence** have fundamentally different dynamics. Interpersonal violence peaks early and declines; organized factional violence takes months to form but persists longer. The relative contribution of each is speculative.
- **The Pinker/Keeley pre-state violence data is contested.** Pinker's 15% lifetime violent death rate may overestimate by 5-10x based on recent perimortem trauma reanalysis. The archaeological record is ambiguous on whether pre-state violence was 1-3% or 10-25% of deaths.
- **Military unit fragmentation patterns** in a US context are entirely speculative. The US military has never faced a scenario where the chain of command fully collapses domestically.
