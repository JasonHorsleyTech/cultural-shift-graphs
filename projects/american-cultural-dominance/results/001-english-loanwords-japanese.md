# English Loanwords in Japanese

## Summary
English-origin loanwords in Japanese trace a dramatic arc from near-zero before 1860 to roughly 40% of all new dictionary entries today. The curve has three major inflection points: the Meiji Restoration (1868) which opened Japan to Western vocabulary, the wartime ban (1937–1945) which temporarily suppressed English loanwords, and the American occupation (1945–1952) which triggered a massive and permanent surge. Each subsequent technology wave — radio, television, computing, internet, smartphones — has ratcheted the rate higher, to the point where the Japanese government has repeatedly attempted (and failed) to stem the flow.

## Penetration Score Definition
**Denominator:** Share of new vocabulary types entering standard Japanese dictionaries per decade that originate from English. Measured via dictionary new-entry data where available, triangulated from corpus type-frequency studies and gairaigo composition data elsewhere.
**0% anchor:** Zero new English-origin words adopted into Japanese in a given decade. No English loanwords appearing in dictionary updates.
**100% anchor:** Every new word added to Japanese dictionaries in a given decade is English-origin. Total lexical capture.

## Methodology
The penetration score estimates the share of new vocabulary entering Japanese dictionaries each decade that originates from English. This is a **flow** metric — it measures the adoption rate, not the accumulated stock.

**Primary data sources, in order of reliability:**
1. **Kōjien dictionary new entries (2007):** 40% of ~10,000 new entries in the 6th edition were gairaigo (katakana loanwords). With ~90% of modern gairaigo being English-origin, this gives ~36% English-origin new entries. This is the strongest single data point.
2. **NINJAL magazine vocabulary surveys (1956, 1994):** The National Institute for Japanese Language and Linguistics measured gairaigo in magazine text. Token frequency rose from 2.9% to 12.2%; type frequency (unique words) from 9.8% to 34.9%. Type frequency better approximates vocabulary penetration.
3. **Asahi newspaper study (1952–1997):** Oshima tracked gairaigo token frequency across the Asahi Shimbun from 2.5% (1952) to 10.1% (1997) at 5-year intervals.
4. **Historical dictionary data (1889, 1911–1924):** An 1889 dictionary listed 85 Dutch and 72 English gairaigo. By 1911–1924, 51% of all gairaigo in dictionaries was English-origin.

**Conversion from stock to flow:** Available data mostly measures stock (gairaigo as % of vocabulary in use). Since gairaigo's stock share has been consistently growing, the flow rate (new entries per decade) must exceed the stock share. The penetration score is estimated as the flow rate, calibrated against the Kōjien 2007 anchor and the stock trajectory.

**English share of gairaigo over time:** In 1889, English was ~46% of Western loanwords. By 1911–1924, this rose to 51%. Post-WW2, it jumped to ~70–75% (American occupation influence). By 2000+, it reached 80–90%. This reflects the shift from British/European to overwhelmingly American English as the source.

**Consistency notes:** The methodology is consistent in measuring English-origin adoption into standard dictionaries across the full range. Pre-1880 estimates are extrapolated from the earliest dictionary data and historical context. The wartime dip (1930–1945) is well-documented but the exact magnitude is estimated — the official ban was porous in practice, especially in scientific and technical fields.

## Notes
- **The wartime dip is real but imperfect.** The 1937–1945 ban on enemy language loanwords drove official adoption to near-zero, but technical fields and informal speech retained many English terms. The ban's enforcement varied — baseball replaced "strike" and "out" with Japanese terms, but scientific vocabulary was harder to purge. The dip in the penetration score reflects official dictionary policy, not complete absence.
- **Kango vs. gairaigo for Western concepts.** During the Meiji era, most Western concepts were initially rendered as kango (Chinese-character compounds) by intellectuals — e.g., 電話 (denwa, "electric speech") for "telephone." Direct phonetic borrowing (gairaigo) became more common only in the Taisho era (1912–1926), when everyday American culture began reaching Japan through radio and cinema. This means early English influence is understated by loanword counts alone.
- **British vs. American English shift.** Pre-1945 English loanwords in Japanese often reflected British English (e.g., ボンネット/bonnetto from "bonnet" for car hood). Post-1945, American English overwhelmingly dominates (e.g., トランク/toranku from American "trunk"). This reflects the US occupation's direct cultural imprint.
- **Government resistance as evidence.** Japan's repeated attempts to limit English loanwords — the Kokugoshingikai (National Language Council), NINJAL's Loanword Committee (2002), the Gairaigo Iikae Teian paraphrase proposals (2006), and the 2013 NHK lawsuit by a 71-year-old plaintiff seeking ¥1.41 million for "emotional distress" from excessive English on television — all confirm the penetration pressure. These defensive responses have had minimal practical effect.
- **Wasei-eigo complicates measurement.** Many Japanese "English" words are wasei-eigo (made-in-Japan English) — Japanese coinages that use English morphemes but don't exist in English (e.g., サラリーマン/sararīman for "salaried worker"). These are counted as English-origin gairaigo in most studies, which slightly inflates the American influence score. However, wasei-eigo itself demonstrates how deeply English has penetrated Japanese word-formation processes.
- **The 18% figure includes mixed-origin words.** The commonly cited "18% gairaigo" for modern Japanese includes konshugo (mixed-origin compounds blending English with Japanese or Chinese elements), not just pure English loans. The BCCWJ corpus gives a lower figure of 5% token / 19% type when measured more strictly.
