# Neonicotinoids → Pollinator Collapse

## Data

- slug: neonicotinoids-pollinators
- hazard: Neonicotinoids
- harm: Pollinator population decline and colony collapse
- category: substance-ban
- early_evidence_year: 2001
- early_evidence_desc: Suchail et al. (2001) published in Pest Management Science demonstrating chronic toxicity of imidacloprid to honeybees at sub-lethal doses far below the acute LD50, establishing that field-realistic exposure levels could kill bees through cumulative chronic effects
- consensus_year: 2013
- consensus_desc: EFSA published risk assessments (January 2013) concluding that three neonicotinoids (imidacloprid, clothianidin, thiamethoxam) pose unacceptable risks to bees, forming the scientific basis for the EU moratorium
- first_regulation_year: 1999
- first_regulation_country: France
- first_regulation_desc: French Minister of Agriculture suspended use of imidacloprid (marketed as Gaucho) as a seed treatment on sunflowers, following widespread honeybee losses reported by beekeepers since 1994-1995. Extended to corn in 2003.

- us_regulation_desc: No federal ban as of 2026. EPA released proposed interim decisions (2020) with limited restrictions (PPE requirements, application timing limits, label changes) but no ban. ## Judgment calls

**Early evidence year (2001 vs. earlier):** French beekeepers observed honeybee losses on imidacloprid-treated sunflower fields starting in 1994-1995, leading the French government to commission investigations. However, these were field observations and anecdotal reports, not published scientific studies. The plan specifies "credible scientific paper or government report" and excludes anecdote. Suchail et al. (2001) was the first peer-reviewed publication demonstrating that chronic exposure to imidacloprid at field-realistic sub-lethal doses caused cumulative mortality in honeybees — a finding that fundamentally changed the understanding of neonicotinoid risk (previous risk assessments had focused only on acute toxicity). Earlier laboratory studies existed as part of the registration dossier but did not address chronic/sub-lethal effects at field-realistic concentrations.

**Regulation preceding evidence (negative lag):** This is one of the most unusual cases in the dataset. France's 1999 suspension of imidacloprid on sunflowers was a precautionary action based on field reports from beekeepers, not on published peer-reviewed science. The formal peer-reviewed evidence (2001) and scientific consensus (EFSA 2013) came AFTER the first regulatory action. This inverts the typical science-to-action pattern. The lag_from_evidence of -2 and lag_from_consensus of -14 reflect this: France acted before the science was formalized, applying the precautionary principle. However, if one uses the EU 2013 moratorium as the "first science-based regulation" (i.e., the first restriction explicitly grounded in peer-reviewed risk assessment rather than precaution), the lag from early evidence would be 12 years (2013 - 2001) and from consensus would be 0 years (2013 - 2013). Both framings are defensible; the data records France 1999 as the first binding restriction.

**Consensus year (2013):** The EFSA January 2013 risk assessment was the first formal scientific body conclusion that neonicotinoids posed "unacceptable risk" to bees. The Worldwide Integrated Assessment (van der Sluijs et al., 2015) was more comprehensive (800+ papers reviewed) but came after the EFSA conclusion. EFSA's 2018 update confirmed and strengthened the 2013 finding.

**Industry opposition (strong):** Bayer and Syngenta mounted a coordinated, multi-year campaign: (1) funded counter-studies questioning the link between neonicotinoids and bee decline; (2) filed lawsuits against the European Commission in 2013 challenging the moratorium (dismissed 2018); (3) lobbied EU Member States to block adoption of EFSA's bee guidance document; (4) obtained "emergency use" exemptions in multiple EU countries even after the ban. This level of organized, sustained opposition qualifies as "strong."

**Substitute availability (partial):** Studies show alternatives exist in ~96% of use cases, but the majority (89%) involve substituting other chemical insecticides (primarily pyrethroids), which carry their own environmental risks. Non-chemical alternatives (IPM, biological control, Bt crops) are effective in some contexts but not all. The situation is "partial" — there is no single drop-in replacement, and the alternatives require changes in farming practice.

**Harm visibility (medium):** Colony collapses and bee population declines were visible to beekeepers and became a major public news story (Colony Collapse Disorder was widely covered from 2006 onward). However, the specific causal mechanism (sub-lethal chronic effects on navigation, reproduction, and immune function) was invisible and required scientific investigation. The ecological consequences (reduced pollination services, biodiversity loss) are diffuse and statistical. CCD itself is multifactorial — neonicotinoids are one contributing factor, not the sole cause.

**Colony Collapse Disorder distinction:** CCD (named 2006) is a syndrome with multiple suspected causes including parasites (Varroa mite), pathogens, habitat loss, and pesticides. The neonicotinoid evidence is specifically about sub-lethal chronic effects (impaired navigation, reduced queen production, weakened immune response) and population-level decline, which is related to but distinct from the acute CCD phenomenon. The data here tracks the neonicotinoid-specific regulatory story, not CCD broadly.

**US regulatory status:** The US has no federal ban on neonicotinoids. The EPA's proposed interim decisions (2020) imposed some restrictions but explicitly did not ban or cancel registrations. The contrast with the EU is stark: the EU imposed a full outdoor ban in 2018 based on EFSA assessments, while the EPA's review remains ongoing as of 2026. State-level actions (Vermont outdoor use ban, California pollinator-area restrictions) do not count as federal regulation per the plan's rules.
