# Freed Slaves / Post-Emancipation Labor

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Enslaved agricultural laborers (plantation system)
- **Country/Region:** United States, primarily Southern states
- **Peak Year:** 1860 (last census before emancipation)
- **Peak Workforce:** 3,953,760 enslaved persons (~90% in agriculture, ~3.56M agricultural workers) (1860 US Census)
- **Primary Mechanism:** discrete_shock
- **Cause Tags:** regulatory, regime
- **Killing Event:** The 13th Amendment (ratified December 1865) abolished slavery, instantly displacing ~4 million people from a coerced labor system with no capital, land, or institutional infrastructure for transition.

## Workforce Timeline

Note: This event is unique in the dataset. The "profession" was not eliminated — agriculture continued — but the *labor system* was destroyed overnight. The workforce timeline tracks Black labor force participation and agricultural employment rather than the disappearance of a role.

| Year | Workforce | Source |
|------|-----------|--------|
| 1860 | 3,953,760 (enslaved) | 1860 US Census |
| 1865 | ~4,000,000 (freed, status chaotic) | Freedmen's Bureau estimates |
| 1870 | ~1,000,000 (Black farm workers enumerated) | 1870 US Census via EH.net |
| 1880 | ~2,270,000 (Black farm workers) | 1880 US Census via EH.net |
| 1900 | ~2,700,000 (Black farm workers, ~50% of Black men + ~35% of Black women reporting occupation) | 1900 US Census |
| 1910 | ~2,900,000 (Black agricultural workers, peak of Black farm ownership) | 1910 US Census |

Note on 1870 figure: The apparent drop from 3.95M to 1M reflects not a workforce decline but a measurement change — enslaved "workers" included children, elderly, and the infirm counted as property. The 1870 Census counted gainful workers only. The actual able-bodied agricultural workforce among the enslaved was likely ~2M, making the 1870 figure a genuine decline as freed people withdrew women and children from field labor where possible, a major assertion of freedom.

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1865 (emancipation — the plantation labor *system* ended)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** N/A — the profession (agriculture) persisted; the labor system collapsed instantly
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** N/A
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** N/A
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** N/A
- **Decline Shape:** cliff (the legal institution of slavery ended overnight; economic dependence persisted for decades via sharecropping)
- **Current Status:** extinct (as a labor system — chattel slavery is abolished; Black agricultural employment declined separately over the 20th century)

Note: Standard decline metrics don't map cleanly to this event. The "profession" of enslaved laborer was eliminated by legal decree in a single act. But the *people* remained overwhelmingly in agriculture — 75% as sharecroppers by 1880. This is simultaneously a cliff (the institution) and a non-decline (the work itself).

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: estimated

Enslaved people had no income in any conventional sense. Post-emancipation "income" under sharecropping was often near-zero after debts. Alternative metrics are used below.

- **Income at Peak:** $0 (enslaved — no wages; economic value of subsistence provided by slaveholders estimated at $30–50/year per person in 1860 dollars) (Ransom & Sutch, 2001)
- **Income at Trough:** Sharecropping net income effectively $0–$25/year after debt settlement for most freedpeople, 1866–1875 (Ransom & Sutch, 2001)
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** Black per capita income was ~24% of white per capita income in 1870 (Derenoncourt et al., 2022)
- **Inflation Adjustment Method:** Not applicable for standard CPI; relative income ratios used instead

| Year | Metric | Value | As % of White Equivalent | Source |
|------|--------|-------|--------------------------|--------|
| 1860 | Per capita income (enslaved) | ~$0 (subsistence only) | 0% (property, not persons) | Ransom & Sutch |
| 1870 | Black per capita income ratio | — | 24% of white | Derenoncourt et al., 2022 |
| 1880 | Black per capita income ratio | — | ~28% of white | Derenoncourt et al., 2022 (interpolated) |
| 1900 | Black per capita income ratio | — | 35% of white | Ng & Virts, NBER; Derenoncourt et al. |
| 1940 | Black per capita income ratio | — | 34% of white | Higgs benchmark via EH.net |

Key observation: The racial income gap narrowed from 1870 to 1900 (24% → 35%), then stagnated completely until the 1940s. Four decades of zero convergence under Jim Crow.

## Surplus Capture

This event inverts the normal surplus framework. The "efficiency gain" was the opposite — the elimination of unpaid coerced labor *reduced* output efficiency for landowners and *should have* transferred value to workers. In practice:

- **Former slaveholders / landowners:** ~50% — Retained land, imposed sharecropping with 50% crop share + debt peonage through furnishing merchants (interest rates of 50–70%/year). Many recaptured most of the economic value of Black labor within 5 years via institutional mechanisms.
- **Consumers:** ~5% — Cotton prices rose briefly post-war, then declined due to global competition; consumers saw minimal direct impact.
- **Freed workers:** ~15% — Gained legal personhood, family autonomy, ability to withdraw women/children from field labor. Economic gains were real but severely constrained by sharecropping debt cycle, Black Codes, and convict leasing.
- **Government/public:** ~30% — Federal government spent on Freedmen's Bureau ($17M over its existence) but also extracted via convict leasing (73% of Alabama's state revenue in 1898 came from convict leasing). Public education infrastructure slowly built but grossly underfunded for Black communities.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
1. **Robert Smalls (1839–1915):** Born enslaved in Beaufort, SC. During the Civil War, commandeered a Confederate transport ship and sailed it to Union lines, freeing himself and his crew. Post-war, opened a store for freedmen, bought his former master's house, served five terms in the US House of Representatives (1875–1887). By 1870, age 31, owned $6,000 in personal property and $1,000 in real estate.

2. **Benjamin "Pap" Singleton (1809–1900):** Former slave from Tennessee who escaped to Canada, returned to the US, and in 1878 organized the Edgefield Real Estate and Homestead Association in Nashville. Led ~20,000 Black Americans to Kansas in the Great Exodus of 1879. Fashioned himself as a "Black Moses" — recognized that economic freedom required geographic escape from the plantation system.

### False Movers
1. **Sherman's Field Order No. 15 (January 1865):** Redistributed ~400,000 acres of coastal land from Charleston to Florida in 40-acre parcels to freed families. Approximately 40,000 freedpeople settled on this land. President Andrew Johnson revoked the order in fall 1865, returning all land to former Confederate owners. People who had built homes and planted crops lost everything. Origin of the phrase "forty acres and a mule" — a broken federal promise.

2. **The Freedmen's Bank (1865–1874):** Chartered by Congress to help freed slaves save money. Accumulated $57 million in deposits from ~70,000 depositors. Collapsed in 1874 due to mismanagement and fraud by white trustees who made speculative loans. Thousands of freedpeople lost their life savings. Frederick Douglass, who briefly served as president, called it "the black man's cow, but the white man's milk."

### Deniers
- Former slaveholders widely expected the labor system to continue essentially unchanged. A Harper's Weekly cartoon from 1865 captured the sentiment: a planter tells a freed man, "My boy — we've toiled and taken care of you long enough — now you've got to work!" Southern state legislatures passed Black Codes in 1865–66 to legally force freedpeople back onto plantations: Mississippi made it illegal for a Black man to change jobs without employer approval; vagrancy convictions carried fines up to $1,000 (Florida), with those unable to pay "hired out" to plantation owners.

### Survival Pattern
Those who survived best had some combination of: literacy, a skilled trade learned during slavery, access to land (even small plots), and membership in mutual aid networks (Black churches, fraternal orders like the Odd Fellows or Prince Hall Masons).

## Unique Texture

1. **Voluntary labor withdrawal as freedom's first act:** When freed, many Black women and children immediately left field labor — not because of unemployment, but as a deliberate assertion of family autonomy. The apparent workforce "decline" from 3.95M to 1M between 1860 and 1870 is partly this: freed families choosing to have mothers raise children rather than pick cotton. This withdrawal was bitterly resented by white landowners. (Foner, "Reconstruction," 1988)

2. **Sharecropping as re-ensnarement:** Furnishing merchants charged 50–70% annual interest on seed, tools, and food advanced against future crops. After "settling up," most sharecroppers owed more than they earned, creating perpetual debt that was legally enforceable. By 1880, 75% of Black farmers in the South were sharecroppers. The system persisted for 80+ years. (Ransom & Sutch, "One Kind of Freedom," 2001)

3. **Convict leasing as literal re-enslavement:** Southern states arrested freed people under vagrancy laws and leased them to private employers. In Georgia, 88% of convict laborers were Black (1870–1910). Death rates of one-third were common on railroad projects. By 1898, convict leasing generated 73% of Alabama's state revenue. (Equal Justice Initiative; Library of Congress)

4. **The income gap that froze for 40 years:** Black per capita income rose from 24% to 35% of white income between 1870 and 1900 — then flatlined at ~34% until 1940. Four decades of zero convergence under Jim Crow, erasing the narrative of steady post-emancipation progress. (Derenoncourt et al., "Wealth of Two Nations," 2022; Higgs, 1977)

5. **Mutual aid as economic infrastructure:** In the absence of banks, insurance, or government support, freed people built their own economic infrastructure through fraternal organizations. By the early 1900s, Black chapters of the Odd Fellows had 304,000 members, the Knights of Pythias 250,000, and the Masons 150,000. These organizations provided burial insurance, disability support, unemployment assistance, and eventually spawned Black-owned banks and insurance companies. (Smithsonian NMAAHC)

## Known Data Gaps

- **Individual-level income data:** Enslaved people had no income; sharecropping "income" is near-meaningless after debt. Per capita income ratios are the best available proxy but are estimated from fragmentary data before 1940.
- **1870 workforce numbers:** The 1870 Census was the first to enumerate Black Americans with occupations. Known undercounting issues, especially in rural South still under Reconstruction turmoil.
- **1890 Census destruction:** The 1890 Census was largely destroyed in a 1921 fire, creating a 20-year gap in detailed occupational data between 1880 and 1900.
- **Sharecropping net income:** No systematic data exists on what sharecroppers actually retained after debt settlement. Ransom & Sutch provide estimates but acknowledge wide variation by region and landlord.
- **Convict leasing totals:** State-level data exists for some states (Georgia, Alabama) but no comprehensive national count of Black people subjected to convict leasing.
- **Where-they-went tracking:** No panel data following individual freed people over time. All estimates based on aggregate census shifts by race and geography.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium
- **Workforce numbers:** medium — Census data exists but 1870 undercounting and 1890 destruction create gaps; enslaved population counts from 1860 are reliable.
- **Income data:** low — No meaningful income metric for enslaved people; sharecropping income nearly unmeasurable; per capita income ratios are academic estimates with wide uncertainty bands.
- **Where-they-went:** medium — Census occupational data by race available for 1870, 1880, 1900; individual tracking impossible but aggregate patterns well-documented by historians.
