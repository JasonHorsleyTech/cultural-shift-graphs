# Refrigeration: Unit Energy Consumption by Vintage 1972–2022

## What to Research

The annual electricity consumption of a typical new refrigerator-freezer by year of manufacture, from 1972–2022. This is the **efficiency metric** for the refrigeration domain — one of the most dramatic mandated efficiency improvement stories in consumer appliances.

## Where to Look

1. **Primary:** DOE Appliance Standards Program — Technical Support Documents (TSDs) for each rulemaking include historical and projected unit energy consumption (UEC) by year and product class.
2. **Secondary:** AHAM (Association of Home Appliance Manufacturers) fact sheets and data — track average energy consumption of new models shipped.
3. **Tertiary:** ENERGY STAR data — tracks qualifying unit efficiency over time.
4. **Key data point:** The DOE rulemaking TSDs for refrigerators (1987, 1993, 2001, 2014 standards) explicitly chart historical UEC — these are the most authoritative graphs.
5. **Academic:** Meier (1997) "Refrigerator Energy Use in the Laboratory and in the Field" and related LBNL publications.

## Scope

- **Product class:** Auto-defrost refrigerator-freezer, top mount (the dominant US configuration). This is DOE Product Class 3 or 5 depending on the era.
- **Metric:** Annual energy consumption in kWh/year for a standard-size unit (~18-22 cubic feet adjusted volume).
- Also report adjusted volume of the average new unit sold (to see whether sizes grew).
- The key story: energy per unit dropped ~75% from peak (early 1970s) to 2020, while average volume increased ~15-20%. This is almost entirely regulation-driven.

## Output Format

### New Refrigerator Annual Energy Consumption

| Year | Value | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|

**Unit:** kWh per year (for typical new unit sold that year)
**Source:** DOE rulemaking TSD / AHAM data
**Coverage:** Auto-defrost top-mount refrigerator-freezer (dominant type)
**Confidence:** high (DOE has excellent data on this from standards rulemakings)

### Average Adjusted Volume of New Units Sold

| Year | Value | Notes |
|------|-------|-------|

**Unit:** cubic feet (adjusted volume = fresh food + 1.63 × freezer volume, per DOE formula)
**Source:** DOE TSD / AHAM shipment data
**Confidence:** medium-high

### DOE Minimum Efficiency Standard Level by Year

| Year | Max kWh/year allowed | Notes |
|------|---------------------|-------|

**Source:** DOE regulations (10 CFR 430.32)
**Key dates:**
- 1990: First federal standard (~900 kWh/yr for 20.6 cu ft)
- 1993: Tightened (~690 kWh/yr)
- 2001: Tightened (~480 kWh/yr)
- 2014: Tightened (~400-450 kWh/yr depending on volume)

## Data Gaps

- Pre-1972 data exists but may require digging into pre-DOE sources (NBS studies).
- "Average new unit sold" is available from AHAM/DOE, but "stock average" (what's actually running in homes) is harder — see ticket 025.
- Year-to-year values between standard changes may be estimated from manufacturer trends.

## Methodology Notes

- This is the poster child for regulatory efficiency policy. Before standards, refrigerator energy was *rising* (bigger units, more features, auto-defrost). Standards forced manufacturers to innovate.
- Peak energy use was ~1,800 kWh/year for a new fridge in ~1972-1974.
- By 2020, a new fridge of the same adjusted volume uses ~400-450 kWh/year.
- That's a ~75% reduction — while the average unit got ~15% bigger.
- California led with state standards in 1978 before federal action. Federal standards took effect in 1990.

## What "Good Data" Looks Like

- 1972: ~1,700-1,800 kWh/year
- 1980: ~1,300-1,400 kWh/year (first California standards effect)
- 1990: ~900-1,000 kWh/year (federal standard takes effect)
- 1993: ~700 kWh/year (tightened standard)
- 2001: ~500 kWh/year (tightened)
- 2010: ~450-480 kWh/year
- 2020: ~400-430 kWh/year
- Smooth declining curve with step-changes at each standard rulemaking date.
