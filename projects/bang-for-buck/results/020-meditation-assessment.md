# Result: Meditation / MBSR — Evidence Assessment

## Verdict: Include

## Rationale

Multiple published cost-effectiveness analyses report QALY gains for MBSR and MBCT programs. While most studies target clinical populations (chronic low back pain, recurrent depression, anxiety disorders, cancer survivors), the conditions studied are prevalent in Bob's demographic bracket — making the data reasonably applicable. At least 18 CEAs with QALY outcomes have been published for mindfulness-based interventions (per a 2022 systematic review in *Mindfulness*). The evidence is sufficient for a Tier 2 inclusion: real RCT-based QALY data exists, but population match to a general low-income adult is imperfect.

## Summary

Meditation and mindfulness-based interventions — particularly structured MBSR (8-week) and MBCT programs — have a growing cost-effectiveness literature. QALY gains are modest (0.01–0.04 per year depending on population and comparator), but costs are very low to zero for self-guided practice. The strongest evidence comes from MBSR for chronic low back pain (US RCT, cost-saving with 0.034 QALY gain/year) and MBCT for depression relapse prevention (dominant over antidepressants, 0.04 QALY/year gain). For Bob, who has elevated rates of chronic pain, depression, and stress, meditation represents a low-cost, low-risk intervention with modest but real QALY benefits. The main weakness is that no CEA study has measured QALYs for meditation in healthy general-population adults — all studies use clinical or at-risk populations.

## QALY Evidence
- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.02
- **Range:** 0.01 – 0.04 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** Herman et al. (2017), "Cost-Effectiveness of MBSR vs CBT or Usual Care Among Adults with Chronic Low-Back Pain," *JAMA Internal Medicine*; Pahlevan Sharif et al. (2020), "Cost-Utility Analysis of MBCT vs Antidepressant Pharmacotherapy for Prevention of Depressive Relapse," *Canadian Journal of Psychiatry*
- **Study type:** RCTs with embedded cost-effectiveness analyses
- **Population studied:** Adults with chronic low back pain (Herman: n=301, ages 20–70, US); adults with recurrent depression in remission (Pahlevan: Canadian)
- **Population match to Bob:** Fair — Bob has elevated rates of chronic pain (~30% prevalence) and depression (~12–14%), so clinical populations partially overlap with his profile, but he is not specifically selected for these conditions
- **Key caveats:**
  - The 0.034 QALY/year figure (Herman 2017) is from a chronic pain population; generalization to Bob's overall health requires caution
  - The 0.04 QALY/year figure (Pahlevan 2020) is specifically for depression relapse prevention in people with ≥2 prior episodes
  - No study measures QALY gains for meditation in healthy general adults
  - QALY instruments (EQ-5D) may underestimate mental health benefits, as responsiveness to anxiety/stress improvements is limited
  - Interaction effects with other interventions (SSRIs, CBT, exercise) are unknown

## Cost Analysis (for Bob at ~$30k household income)
- **Annual direct cost:** $50
- **One-time startup cost:** $0
- **Startup cost amortized over 3 years:** $0/year
- **Total annual cost (best estimate):** $50
- **Cost range:** $0 – $200 (low – high)
- **With insurance (if applicable):** N/A — meditation is rarely covered by insurance; some employer wellness programs include app subscriptions
- **Without insurance:** $0–$200
- **Cost notes:** The $0 floor reflects free guided meditation via apps (Insight Timer, free tier of YouTube channels) or community groups. The $70/year mid-range covers a Headspace or Calm subscription. The $200 high end includes an app subscription plus an occasional workshop or partial MBSR course fee. Full MBSR 8-week courses cost $300–$600 (e.g., UCSF charges $395), but many programs offer sliding scale or scholarships. For Bob, the most realistic path is free self-guided practice, possibly supplemented by a low-cost app. The $50 midpoint reflects a discounted app subscription or occasional donation-based class.

## Time Commitment
- **Hours per week:** 2.0
- **Category:** Medium (1-3 hr/wk)
- **Notes:** Standard MBSR prescribes 45 min/day formal practice (~5.25 hr/wk), but real-world sustained practice is typically 15–20 min/day (~2 hr/wk). The QALY evidence is based on MBSR protocol adherence, so the time estimate reflects what's needed to approach the studied benefit level. Many people meditate for less (10 min/day), but evidence for shorter durations is weaker.

## Access and Barriers
- **Activation energy:** Medium
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - Learning curve: meditation requires instruction and practice to feel effective; initial sessions often feel frustrating or pointless
  - Cultural perception: meditation may be perceived as "woo" or religious in some communities, creating social resistance
  - Quiet space: Bob may lack a quiet, private space for practice (shared housing, noisy environment)
  - No external accountability: unlike gym classes or therapy appointments, self-guided meditation has no built-in structure
  - MBSR courses (which have the strongest evidence) cost $300–$600 and require 8 weeks of weekly 2.5-hour sessions — a significant time and schedule barrier for shift workers

## Side Effects and Risks
- Occasional reports of increased anxiety or distressing experiences during meditation, particularly in people with trauma history (rare but documented)
- No serious adverse effects in published RCTs
- Risk of substituting meditation for needed medical or psychological treatment

## Adherence
- **Real-world adherence rate:** 0.30
- **Source:** Parsons et al. (2017), "Home practice in Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy and Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction: A systematic review and meta-analysis," *Journal of Consulting and Clinical Psychology*; MBSR program completion rates ~76%, but sustained daily practice at 1+ year estimated at ~30%
- **Notes:** MBSR program completion is ~76%, with 15–30% dropout. However, long-term sustained practice is much lower. Home practice during MBSR courses averages 64% of assigned time. After program completion, practice frequency drops significantly. The 30% figure reflects those who maintain a regular (3+ days/week) practice after one year. This is one of meditation's biggest weaknesses as an intervention — the evidence is based on structured programs, but most people don't maintain the practice.

## Evidence Quality
- **Tier:** 2
- **Tier justification:** Multiple RCTs with embedded CEAs report QALY outcomes. The MBSR chronic pain study (Herman 2017) is a well-designed US-based RCT with societal cost perspective. The MBCT depression studies are rigorous. However, population match to Bob is imperfect — studies target clinical conditions, not general low-income adults. The intervention itself (self-guided meditation vs. structured MBSR course) also differs from what was studied.
- **Key studies:**
  - Herman PM et al. (2017). Cost-effectiveness of MBSR vs CBT or usual care among adults with chronic low-back pain. *JAMA Internal Medicine*, 177(11):1694–1701. [QALY: 0.034/year, cost-saving, n=301, US]
  - Pahlevan Sharif S et al. (2020). Cost-utility analysis of MBCT vs antidepressant pharmacotherapy for prevention of depressive relapse. *Canadian Journal of Psychiatry*, 65(10):739–748. [QALY: 0.04/year, cost-saving, n=not specified, Canada]
  - Perez-Aranda A et al. (2019). Cost-utility of MBSR for fibromyalgia. *Mindfulness*. [MBSR dominant over psychoeducation]
  - Luciano JV et al. (2022). Economic evaluations of mindfulness-based interventions: a systematic review. *Mindfulness*, 13:2607–2625. [Systematic review of 28 economic evaluations, 18 with QALY outcomes]
- **Limitations:**
  - No QALY data for meditation in healthy general-population adults
  - Self-guided meditation (Bob's likely path) differs from structured MBSR/MBCT (what was studied)
  - EQ-5D may underestimate mental health and stress-related quality-of-life improvements
  - Long-term adherence is much lower than in-trial adherence, reducing real-world effectiveness
  - Most studies have small sample sizes (n=100–350)
  - Publication bias likely favors positive results
