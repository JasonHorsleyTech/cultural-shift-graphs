# Ticket 030: Major Tax and Transfer Policy Changes, 1947–2024

## What to research

Compile a reference list of **major federal tax and transfer policy changes** from 1947 to 2024, with the year they took effect and a rough estimate of their impact on median household effective tax rates or disposable income.

This is for chart annotations. When the share-of-pie ratio has an inflection point, it's useful to annotate whether a policy change contributed. The viewer should be able to see: "Ah, the ratio ticked up in 1965 — that's when Medicare started."

## Data source hierarchy

1. **Tax Foundation historical publications** — Comprehensive lists of major tax legislation with effective dates and estimated revenue impact.
2. **Tax Policy Center "Tax Facts" and "Historical Tax Legislation"** — Brookings/Urban Institute joint venture. Has detailed legislation summaries.
3. **CBO historical budget projections** — Revenue and spending impact of major legislation.
4. **SSA (Social Security Administration)** — History of FICA rate changes.
5. **CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid)** — Medicare/Medicaid program history.
6. **Wikipedia "History of taxation in the United States"** — Surprisingly good summary for getting started, then verify with primary sources.

## What to collect

| Column | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Year Effective | The calendar year the policy change took effect |
| Legislation | Name of the law or executive action |
| Type | "tax cut", "tax increase", "new program", "program expansion", "program cut" |
| Brief Description | One-sentence description of what changed |
| Impact Direction | "increased disposable income", "decreased disposable income", "mixed" |
| Estimated Magnitude | Rough impact on middle-income households: "large" (>2% of income), "moderate" (1–2%), "small" (<1%) |

## What to collect — must include at minimum

These are the policy changes most likely to produce visible inflection points in the data:

1. **1950s:** FICA rate increases (Social Security expansion)
2. **1964:** Revenue Act of 1964 (Kennedy-Johnson tax cut — top rate 91% → 70%)
3. **1965:** Medicare and Medicaid creation
4. **1969:** Tax Reform Act of 1969
5. **1972:** SSI creation, Social Security COLA indexing
6. **1975:** Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) created
7. **1981:** Economic Recovery Tax Act (Reagan's first tax cut — top rate 70% → 50%)
8. **1983:** Social Security Amendments (payroll tax increase, benefit taxation)
9. **1986:** Tax Reform Act (top rate 50% → 28%, but broadened base)
10. **1990:** OBRA-90 (Bush tax increase)
11. **1993:** OBRA-93 (Clinton tax increase — top rate 31% → 39.6%; EITC expansion)
12. **1996:** Welfare reform (PRWORA)
13. **2001/2003:** EGTRRA and JGTRRA (Bush tax cuts)
14. **2010:** Affordable Care Act (health insurance subsidies, individual mandate)
15. **2013:** American Taxpayer Relief Act (partial Bush tax cut expiration)
16. **2017:** Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (Trump tax cuts — top rate 39.6% → 37%)
17. **2020–2021:** CARES Act, American Rescue Plan (stimulus payments, expanded child tax credit)

## Result format

A table with the columns above, one row per policy change. Include 15–25 entries covering the most impactful changes. Don't aim for exhaustive — focus on changes that would visibly move the "share of pie" ratio.

## Known issues

- Estimating "magnitude" is inherently imprecise. Use CBO/TPC scores where available, otherwise make informed judgments and flag as estimates.
- Some policies phase in over years (ACA) or expire/get renewed (Bush cuts). Note the effective year of the biggest impact.
- Transfer programs (EITC, CTC, SNAP) affect the bottom quintiles much more than the middle. Note which income groups are most affected.
