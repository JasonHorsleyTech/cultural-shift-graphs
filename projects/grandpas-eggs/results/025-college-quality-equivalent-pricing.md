# State College — Quality-Equivalent Pricing

## Summary

The rubric (022) concluded that 2025 state college is **better on 9 of 11 scored dimensions** and worse on only 2 (tenure-track faculty share and administrative overhead). This makes college a fundamentally different case from eggs or suits: there is no meaningful "quality-equivalent" product to price because the modern product is, on balance, superior. The real story is cost explosion *despite* quality improvement — not a hidden quality tax.

The two degraded dimensions are real and worth annotating, but they don't justify a separate Line B. An honors program at a state flagship recovers 1960-level faculty attention at near-zero premium ($200–$1,600/year). A private liberal arts college recovers it at $55,000–$67,000/year — but buying that comparison means ignoring the nine dimensions where 2025 state college already exceeds 1960.

## Finding

**No meaningful quality gap — Line B ≈ Line A.** The quality-equivalent of a 1960 state college education is a 2025 state college education. The product improved on most measurable dimensions; the price exploded. The graph should show a single tuition line (not two diverging lines) with annotations for the two degraded dimensions and the structural causes of the cost explosion.

### Why Line B = Line A is the honest answer

The ticket gave three possible paths:

1. **If quality improved overall → Line B = Line A.** The rubric found 9/11 attributes improved. This path applies.
2. **If specific dimensions degraded → price the recovery.** Faculty composition degraded. Recovery costs range from ~$0 (honors program) to ~$60K (private LAC). But pricing this as "the quality-equivalent" cherry-picks the one dimension where 1960 was better and ignores everything else.
3. **Middle path → single line with annotation band.** This is the recommended approach.

A student time-traveling from 1960 would find 2025 state college unrecognizably better in almost every way — except they'd notice their organic chemistry professor is a part-timer teaching at three schools with no office hours, and the administration building has more staff than the entire faculty.

## Quality-Equivalent Cost Analysis

### Option A: Honors college at state flagship ($0–$1,600/year premium)

This is the cheapest way to recover 1960-level faculty attention at a public university:

| Institution | Honors Premium (annual) | Student-Faculty Ratio | Notes |
|------------|------------------------|----------------------|-------|
| Penn State (Schreyer) | $50/year ($25/semester) | ~12:1 in honors sections | Dedicated tenured faculty for many honors courses |
| Purdue | $200/year | ~15:1 honors, smaller sections | Honors fee covers dedicated advising |
| Oregon State | $1,560/year ($520/term) | ~15:1 honors | Differential tuition model |
| University of Oregon (Clark) | ~$3,030/year ($1,010/term) | ~14:1 honors | Highest honors premium found |
| University of Utah | $650/year ($325/semester) | Not reported | Fee supports small classes, advising |
| Grand Valley State | ~$600/year ($20/credit × 30) | Not reported | Per-credit differential |

**Typical honors class size:** 19.8 students per section (vs. potentially 100–300 in introductory lecture courses). This approximates the 1960 experience where nearly all classes were taught by full-time faculty to groups of 20–40.

**Critical caveat:** Honors programs at state universities are available to ~5–15% of undergraduates (admission is selective). The 1960 experience wasn't selective in this dimension — *all* students got tenured professors because that's who taught. The honors path recovers the faculty-attention dimension for a subset of students; it doesn't recreate the universal experience.

### Option B: Mid-tier private liberal arts college ($55,000–$67,000/year sticker)

This most closely replicates the 1960 state college *faculty* experience at scale:

| Institution | Tuition & Fees (2024-25) | Student-Faculty Ratio | Notes |
|------------|--------------------------|----------------------|-------|
| Oberlin College | $67,366 | 9:1 | Selective LAC; 97% get aid |
| Williams College | ~$65,480 | 7:1 | Top-ranked LAC |
| Amherst College | ~$66,000 | 7:1 | Top-ranked LAC |
| Average top-50 LAC | ~$59,814 | ~10:1 | College Tuition Compare |
| Average all private nonprofit | $43,350 | Varies | College Board 2024-25 |

**Net price reality:** Most private LACs discount heavily. Average institutional aid is 56.3% of sticker price (NACUBO, 2024-25). Average net tuition at private nonprofits is ~$16,000–$18,000/year after institutional grants. Still dramatically more than public tuition, but not the $60K+ sticker number.

**Why this comparison is misleading:** A private LAC in 2025 also gives you everything 2025 state college gives you (modern labs, digital libraries, career services, diverse peer group, mental health services) PLUS the small classes and tenured faculty. You're paying for the full 2025 package with 1960-level attention — not a stripped-down 1960-equivalent experience. There is no product that gives you *only* what 1960 had.

## What the graph should show for college

**Recommended approach (aligned with rubric conclusion):**

1. **Single price line** — In-state public 4-year tuition & fees, 1963–2025 (or from ~1955 with caveats). This is both Line A (cheapest) and Line B (quality-equivalent) because the modern product is equal or superior on most dimensions.

2. **Annotation band** — A shaded region above the main line showing the cost range to recover the two degraded dimensions:
   - Lower bound: honors college premium (+$200–$1,600/year) — recovers faculty attention for eligible students
   - Upper bound: private LAC tuition (~$43,000–$60,000/year) — recovers faculty attention universally but also buys 2025 improvements that 1960 didn't have

3. **Structural break annotations:**
   - **1965:** Higher Education Act — federal financial aid created
   - **1980s:** State funding begins declining as share of university budgets; tuition growth accelerates
   - **2001–2003:** Post-recession state budget cuts; first major tuition jumps
   - **2008–2012:** Great Recession triggers deep state funding cuts; steepest tuition increases in the series
   - **2020–2022:** Brief tuition deceleration during COVID

4. **Badge:** "Cost explosion" or "Baumol's+" — this case doesn't fit "quality tax" or "progress wins." It's a hybrid: the product improved, but costs exploded due to state funding withdrawal + admin bloat + amenities competition + Baumol's cost disease in a labor-intensive service.

## Methodology

**Finding follows the rubric.** The ticket required starting from the quality rubric (022) and quality metrics (024). Those documents found 9/11 attributes improved, 2 degraded. The honest conclusion is that the quality-equivalent of a 1960 state college is a 2025 state college — possibly with an honors option to recover faculty attention.

**Tuition series uses NCES Table 330.10 and College Board Trends in College Pricing.** These are the standard sources used by virtually all higher-education cost analyses. NCES Table 330.10 provides the longest consistent series (1963–2023). College Board extends to 2024-25 and 2025-26.

**Tuition & fees only, not total cost of attendance.** Room and board are excluded for consistency with the other commodities in this project (we measure the price of the product itself, not associated living expenses). Room/board costs are separable from the education product in a way that, e.g., you can't separate the shell from an egg.

**Published sticker prices, not net prices.** The plan specifies nominal published prices. Net price (after financial aid) is a different and important story — average net tuition at public 4-year institutions peaked in 2012-13 at $4,450 (2025 dollars) and declined to ~$2,300 by 2025-26 (College Board). But net price depends on individual circumstances and is not comparable across eras.

**Honors college pricing from institutional websites.** Each university publishes its honors differential. The sample is convenience-based (schools that appeared in search results), not comprehensive. The range ($50–$3,030/year) is illustrative, not exhaustive.

**Private LAC pricing from College Board aggregate data and individual institutional reports.** Individual school prices (Oberlin, Williams, Amherst) are from U.S. News/institutional websites for 2024-25.

## Known Gaps

- **Pre-1963 tuition data:** No national average exists before NCES Table 330.10's 1963-64 starting point. Individual institutional records exist but aren't aggregated.
- **Honors program outcomes:** There is very limited data on whether honors students at public universities actually receive more tenure-track instruction than regular students, or just smaller sections (possibly still taught by advanced grad students).
- **Private LAC tenure-track percentages:** Individual LAC tenure-track shares weren't available in searches. The assumption that they're higher than public university averages is reasonable (LACs don't have large adjunct pools) but not verified with specific numbers.
- **Net price history before 2006:** College Board's net price series begins in 2006-07. Pre-2006 net prices are not available from a consistent series.

## Confidence

**High** — for the overall finding (Line B ≈ Line A). This follows directly from the rubric's 9/11 improved assessment, which was based on well-documented federal data sources (NCES, AAUP).

**High** — for the public tuition series (1963–2025). NCES Table 330.10 and College Board data are the gold standard for this measurement.

**Medium** — for the private tuition series. Pre-2000 figures are from NCES; post-2000 from College Board. Both are authoritative but may not be perfectly spliced.

**Medium** — for honors college pricing. The sample is small and non-representative. Some honors colleges charge nothing extra; some charge $3,000+/year. The range captures this, but a national average isn't available.

**Low** — for pre-1963 tuition estimates. These are based on a single institution's records (Colorado State) extrapolated to a national estimate.
