# Child Laborers (Regulatory Abolition)

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Child laborers (ages 10–15) in manufacturing, mining, agriculture, and street trades
- **Country/Region:** United States (primary focus), with UK context
- **Peak Year:** 1910 (highest absolute numbers, though percentage peaked ~1900)
- **Peak Workforce:** ~2,000,000 children ages 10–15 gainfully employed (US Census)
- **Primary Mechanism:** discrete_shock
- **Cause Tags:** regulatory, cultural, tech_tool
- **Killing Event:** Cascading federal and state legislation — culminating in the Fair Labor Standards Act of 1938 — combined with rising household incomes and compulsory schooling laws to eliminate most child labor by the 1930s

## Workforce Timeline

All data is for US children ages 10–15 reported as "gainfully occupied" in decennial census returns. The EH.net compilation of census data (Whaples, Hanes) is the primary source, supplemented by Historical Statistics of the United States.

Note: The 1940 figure uses the 14–17 age bracket (10–15 data not consistently reported after FLSA changed the regulatory landscape). Industrial child labor was effectively zero by 1940; the remaining workforce was overwhelmingly agricultural, which was exempt from FLSA.

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1910 (absolute peak; percentage decline began earlier, ~1900–1910)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~10 (by ~1920, workforce dropped to ~1,060,000)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** ~15 (by ~1925, estimated ~1,000,000)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** ~20 (by ~1930, workforce ~570,000)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** ~28 (by ~1938, industrial child labor effectively eliminated)
- **Decline Shape:** s_curve (slow decline 1900–1910, accelerating 1910–1925, flattening as only agricultural exemptions remained)
- **Current Status:** remnant (<10% peak) — agricultural child labor persists legally; ~330,000 children under 16 work in US agriculture today (Child Labor Coalition, 2021)

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: estimated

Child wages are not comparable to adult displaced-worker income — these were supplemental household earnings, not primary incomes. The relevant metric is the child employment rate itself plus family income effects.

- **Income at Peak:** Children earned $2–$4/week in textile mills (~$60–$120/year); breaker boys earned $0.50–$0.80/day (~$150–$250/year); farm children contributed ~$16–$58/year in productivity (US Census, DOL survey 1917–19)
- **Income at Trough:** $0 (children removed from workforce)
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** N/A — not a meaningful metric; children transitioned to education, not alternative employment
- **Inflation Adjustment Method:** Not applied; nominal wages reported with era context

Children's wages as a share of adult wages varied by industry: textile mills ~25–33% of adult wages; mining ~40–50%; agriculture ~7–25% of adult male productivity (Craig, via EH.net). A 1917–19 Department of Labor survey found children's earnings averaged 23% of total family income among families with working children.

|------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|---------------------|--------|
| 1880 | ~20% | ~58% | Children's wages critical for immigrant/poor families | US Census |
| 1900 | 18% | ~78% | 23% of family income (DOL 1917–19 survey, retrospective) | US Census / DOL |

## Where They Went

Unique in this dataset: the "displaced workers" were children. They did not transition to other occupations — they transitioned to childhood.

| Destination | Est. % of Displaced | Basis | Notes |
|-------------|-------------------|-------|-------|
| School (compulsory education) | 70% | tracked | School enrollment rose from ~58% (1880) to ~95% (1940). Compulsory education laws passed in all states by 1918 |
| Home / family duties | 15% | estimated | Especially girls; domestic work not captured in census "gainfully occupied" |
| Agriculture (exempt sector) | 10% | inferred | Agriculture was explicitly exempted from most child labor laws, including FLSA. Over 60% of child workers were already in agriculture by 1910–20 |
| Unregulated street trades | 5% | estimated | Newsboys, bootblacks, messengers — hardest to regulate, persisted longest in cities |

## Surplus Capture

The "efficiency gains" framing doesn't apply cleanly — this was a moral/regulatory intervention, not a productivity improvement. The surplus was redistributed as follows:

- **Capital owners:** 10% — Short-term cost increase (had to hire adults at higher wages or mechanize). Long-term benefit from more skilled adult workforce. Southern textile mills mechanized to replace child workers.
- **Consumers:** 10% — Marginal price increases in textiles and other child-labor-intensive goods. Long-term consumer benefits from a more educated and productive workforce.
- **Adjacent workers (adults):** 25% — Adults gained employment opportunities previously filled by children. Adult wages in child-labor-intensive industries rose as child labor supply dried up.
- **Government/public:** 15% — Investment in public education infrastructure. Higher future tax base from educated workers.
- **The children themselves (long-term):** 40% — Enormous intergenerational human capital gains. Goldin & Katz (2008) document the "High School Movement" (1910–1940) as the single largest increase in American educational attainment, directly enabled by removing children from the workforce.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
- **The Amoskeag Manufacturing Company** (Manchester, NH) — one of the world's largest textile mills — began mechanizing spinning operations in the 1900s–1910s, reducing dependence on child workers before legislation forced the issue. By investing in ring spinning technology that required fewer but more skilled operators, they maintained productivity while gradually phasing out child employment.
- **Families who invested in education early:** Goldin & Katz document that families in the Midwest and West who enrolled children in the expanding public high school system (1910s–1920s) saw significant returns — their children entered the growing white-collar and skilled trades workforce of the 1920s–1940s.

### False Movers
- **The Keating-Owen Act (1916):** Congress passed it, Wilson signed it, and families complied — pulling children from factories. Then the Supreme Court struck it down in *Hammer v. Dagenhart* (1918), 5–4. Families that had removed children from work were suddenly unprotected. Congress tried again with a child labor tax (1919), also struck down in *Bailey v. Drexel Furniture Co.* (1922). Two federal laws, both dead.
- **The Child Labor Amendment (1924):** Proposed constitutional amendment to give Congress power to regulate child labor. It was never ratified — opposed by Southern states, the Catholic Church (which feared government overreach into family life), and the National Association of Manufacturers. It became moot when FLSA passed in 1938.

### Deniers
- **Southern mill owners** argued children's employment was beneficial: "The most beautiful sight that we see is the child at labor; as early as he may get at labor the more beautiful, the more useful does his life get to be." — Attributed to Asa G. Candler (Coca-Cola founder), widely cited in NCLC literature.
- David Clark, editor of the *Southern Textile Bulletin*, was the most vocal opponent of federal child labor regulation, arguing it was a Northern conspiracy to destroy Southern industry's competitive advantage. He funded the *Hammer v. Dagenhart* challenge.
- Parents in mill towns often opposed reform: "We need the money. The children are better off in the mill than running wild." — Common sentiment documented in NCLC investigator reports, 1908–1912.

### Survival Pattern
Families that had access to alternative adult income (either through rising wages or a second working adult) navigated the transition; families dependent on children's wages in single-industry towns suffered the most acute short-term hardship.

## Unique Texture

1. **Lewis Hine's 5,100+ photographs** for the NCLC (1908–1924) constitute the most powerful visual record of any labor displacement in this dataset. He posed as a fire inspector, Bible salesman, and industrial photographer to infiltrate mills and mines. His images of breaker boys, spinner girls, and newsboys became the primary propaganda tool of the reform movement. (Library of Congress, NCLC Collection)

2. **Two federal laws struck down before one stuck.** The Keating-Owen Act (1916) lasted only two years before the Supreme Court killed it. The child labor tax (1919) was struck down in 1922. A constitutional amendment (proposed 1924) was never ratified. It took 22 years from the first federal law to FLSA (1938) — the one that survived. (National Archives)

3. **Agriculture remains exempt today.** Children as young as 12 can legally work on US farms with parental consent. An estimated 330,000 children under 16 work in agriculture; more than half of all work-related youth fatalities occur in agriculture. The FLSA's 1938 agricultural exemption has never been closed. (DOL, GAO 2018, Child Labor Coalition 2021)

4. **The legislation probably didn't do most of the work.** Moehling (1999) found that state child labor laws explain only a small fraction of the decline. Rising household incomes, technological change reducing demand for unskilled labor, and expanding access to education were the primary drivers. The laws codified a transition already underway — but the laws mattered at the margins, especially for the poorest families and the most exploitative industries. (Moehling, *Explorations in Economic History*, 1999)

5. **Breaker boys in Pennsylvania's anthracite mines** sat hunched over coal chutes for 10 hours a day, 6 days a week, for $0.50–$0.80/day. An estimated 18,000–24,000 boys worked as breakers around 1900. Most developed permanent spinal deformities from the hunched position — "bent-backed like old men" — by their teenage years. One in four mine workers in 1908 was a boy aged 7–16. (NCLC, Pennsylvania Heritage Magazine)

## Known Data Gaps

- **1890 and 1910 participation rates by gender:** EH.net provides gendered rates only for 1880, 1900, and 1930. The intervening census years have aggregate percentages but not consistent male/female breakdowns from the same source.
- **Absolute workforce numbers for 1920 and 1930:** Calculated from participation rates × estimated child population. The census "gainfully occupied" definition changed slightly between censuses, introducing inconsistency.
- **Family income impact data:** The 1917–19 DOL survey is retrospective and limited to industrial families. No equivalent data for agricultural families, where child labor was most prevalent.
- **UK-specific workforce timeline:** UK data is not integrated into the primary timeline because the UK's child labor reform happened earlier (1833–1880s) and through different legislative mechanisms. UK context is used for comparison only.
- **Income recovery metric:** Fundamentally inapplicable — children transitioned to education, not alternative employment. This event doesn't fit the standard income trajectory framework of the dataset.
- **Street trades data:** Newsboys, bootblacks, and messengers were chronically undercounted in census data because they were self-employed and mobile.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium
- **Workforce numbers:** medium — Census data is decennial and definitions shifted; the 18% plateau from 1890–1910 is well-established, but absolute numbers are estimates derived from rates × population
- **Income data:** low — Children's wages are documented anecdotally but no systematic wage series exists; the DOL 1917–19 survey is the best available but limited in scope
- **Where-they-went:** high — School enrollment data is well-documented and the inverse correlation with child employment rates is strong and unambiguous
