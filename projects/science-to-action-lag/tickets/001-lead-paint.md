# Ticket 001: Lead Paint → Neurological Damage

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that lead paint causes neurological damage (especially in children) to binding regulation banning or restricting lead in paint, across OECD countries and the US specifically.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — When did a credible scientific study first establish that lead paint causes harm? Look for the earliest epidemiological or clinical evidence linking lead paint exposure to neurological damage in children or workers. Key names: Alice Hamilton (1910s-1920s industrial hygiene work), Randolph Byers (1943 — behavioral effects in children).

2. **Consensus date** — When did a major scientific body formally state that lead paint is a health hazard? Look for: CDC statements, NAS reports, WHO positions, Surgeon General statements. Herbert Needleman's work in the 1970s on IQ effects was pivotal.

3. **First OECD regulation** — Which OECD country first banned or meaningfully restricted lead in residential paint? Several European countries and Australia acted before the US. Look for bans, not just warnings.

4. **US regulation** — The Lead-Based Paint Poisoning Prevention Act (1971) and the CPSC ban on lead paint (1978) are the key US milestones. Confirm dates and scope.

5. **Industry opposition** — The Lead Industries Association actively fought regulation for decades. Document the scale and duration of organized opposition.

6. **50% exposure reduction** — When did blood lead levels in children drop to 50% of their peak? CDC data tracks this through NHANES. The drop was dramatic after the paint ban + leaded gas phase-out.

7. **Substitute availability** — Were lead-free paints commercially viable before the ban?

## Where to look

1. CDC/ATSDR lead poisoning prevention historical documents
2. Gerald Markowitz & David Rosner, "Lead Wars" and "Deceit and Denial" — detailed regulatory history
3. EPA historical documents on lead regulation
4. CPSC regulatory history for the 1978 ban
5. PubMed — search "lead paint regulation history" or "lead poisoning children history"
6. Wikipedia "Lead paint" article (well-sourced, verify key dates)

## Output format

Use the result file format from plan.md. Fill in all fields. Pay special attention to the judgment calls section — lead has a very long evidence history and the choice of early_evidence_year requires documentation.

## What "good data" looks like

- Clear distinction between occupational lead poisoning evidence (1900s-1920s) and childhood developmental evidence (1940s-1970s) — the graph cares about when the SPECIFIC harm (neurological damage) was evidenced, not general toxicity
- Regulation dates verified against legislative records, not secondary sources alone
- Industry opposition documented with specific organizations and campaigns

## Known pitfalls

- Lead paint and leaded gasoline are separate regulatory stories with overlapping science. This ticket is about PAINT specifically — the substance in homes, not in fuel.
- The US banned lead paint in 1978, but several countries acted decades earlier. Don't anchor on the US date as "first regulation."
- Blood lead level declines are driven by BOTH paint and gasoline regulation — hard to attribute the exposure reduction to paint regulation alone. Note this caveat.
