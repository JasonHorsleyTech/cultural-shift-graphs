# Film Screen Quotas and Import Restrictions

## Summary
Countries legislating against Hollywood imports is itself evidence of American cultural pressure — you don't build walls against something that isn't a threat. Film screen quotas began in the 1920s when European governments responded to Hollywood's post-WWI dominance, peaked in the 1960s-70s with ~15 of the top 50 economies enforcing restrictions, dipped during 1980s-90s trade liberalization, then surged dramatically after 2018 when the EU mandated 30% European content on streaming platforms. The modern count (including streaming content quotas) reaches ~32 of the top 50 economies — the highest level ever.

## Penetration Score Definition
**Denominator:** Percentage of the world's top 50 economies (by GDP) with active film screen quotas, import quotas, content quotas for streaming, or equivalent legislation specifically designed to protect domestic audiovisual production from foreign (primarily American) dominance.
**0% anchor:** No country has enacted any form of film or audiovisual content protection legislation.
**100% anchor:** All top 50 economies have active film/content protection laws targeting foreign (primarily US) dominance.

## Methodology

### What counts as a "quota"
This metric counts any active legislation in a top-50 economy that:
1. **Screen quotas:** Mandate minimum days/percentage of domestic film exhibition in cinemas (e.g., South Korea's 73-day requirement)
2. **Import quotas:** Cap the number of foreign films allowed into the market (e.g., China's 34-film limit)
3. **Content quotas for streaming:** Require minimum percentage of domestic/regional content on streaming platforms (e.g., EU's 30% European content mandate)
4. **Dubbing/distribution restrictions:** Laws that effectively limit foreign film access through dubbing requirements or distribution barriers (e.g., Mexico's ban on dubbing foreign films into Spanish)

### What doesn't count
- General film subsidies without quota requirements (many countries subsidize domestic production without restricting imports)
- Censorship that incidentally limits foreign content (e.g., content restrictions not specifically targeting foreign market share)
- Informal barriers (e.g., state-owned cinema chains preferring domestic films without legal mandate)

### Top 50 economies reference set
The denominator is held constant at 50, representing the world's 50 largest economies by GDP. The composition shifts over time (e.g., South Korea enters the top 50 in the 1960s-70s, China rises dramatically), but the denominator stays at 50 for consistency. Countries are assessed against the approximate top-50 of their era.

### Computing the penetration score
For each year: count the number of top-50 economies with at least one active form of the legislation described above. Divide by 50. Multiply by 100.

### The 2018 discontinuity
The EU AVMSD represents a qualitative shift — from cinema-specific screen quotas to streaming content quotas. This is the modern equivalent: when Netflix replaced cinemas as the primary delivery mechanism for American cultural content, the protective legislation followed. The jump in 2018-2020 is real, not a methodological artifact. The EU explicitly framed the AVMSD as protecting European cultural diversity against American streaming dominance.

## Notes

### Key inflection points
1. **1927-1930 (First wave):** Hollywood captured 60-90% of screen time across Europe after WWI. The UK's Cinematograph Films Act (1927) was the first formal response, setting quota at 7.5% rising to 20%. France, Germany, Italy, and others followed within months. This was a panic response to a genuine cultural emergency.

2. **1946 (Post-war bargain):** The Blum-Byrnes agreement exemplifies the dynamic — France needed American money for reconstruction and had to accept American films as part of the deal. French screens were required to show American films for 9 out of 13 weeks per quarter. French filmmakers protested, and quotas were adjusted upward within two years.

3. **1985 (UK capitulates):** Britain's abolition of screen quotas under Thatcher marked the high point of trade liberalization. The quotas had been declining for years (from 45% in 1948 to 15% in 1982 to suspended in 1983).

4. **1993 (GATT cultural exception):** The EU's refusal to include audiovisual services in the Uruguay Round free trade agreement was a landmark moment. The US pushed hard; France led the resistance. The compromise was that neither side committed — audiovisual services remain one of the least-committed sectors in WTO.

5. **2006 (Korea bows to US pressure):** South Korea's halving of screen quotas from 146 to 73 days was a direct precondition for the US-Korea FTA. The domestic film industry protested fiercely. Interestingly, Korean cinema's global dominance (Parasite, etc.) came *after* the quota reduction, suggesting the industry had matured beyond needing protection.

6. **2018-2020 (Streaming expansion):** The EU AVMSD's 30% European content quota for streaming platforms represents the largest expansion of audiovisual protectionism in history. It brought ~10+ additional top-50 economies into the "active quota" column overnight. France and Italy went further with 20-25% investment obligations.

### The paradox of this metric
This is a *defensive* metric — high scores mean more countries felt threatened enough to legislate. But the score has been rising even as Hollywood's global market share in cinema has been declining from its 1990s peak. The explanation: protection shifted from cinema (where Hollywood already won) to streaming (where Netflix, Disney+, and Amazon represent the new front). Countries learned from the cinema era and acted faster on streaming.

### Caveats
- The top-50 economies set shifts over time. A country counted in 1925 (e.g., Austria) might not be in the top 50 today, and vice versa.
- Some quotas existed on paper but were poorly enforced (Italy's 1927 quota was unenforced due to insufficient domestic production).
- Subsidy systems (France's CNC, Germany's DFFF, Korea's KOFIC) serve a similar protective purpose but are counted separately unless they include explicit quota requirements.
- The 2018 EU AVMSD jump may overcount — some member states had minimal enforcement or treated the 30% requirement as already met.
- China's system is unique: it's an import quota (capping foreign films) rather than a screen quota (mandating domestic days). Both achieve the same goal of protecting the domestic market.
