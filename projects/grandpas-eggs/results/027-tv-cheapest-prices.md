# Television — Cheapest Prices

## Summary

Television is the poster child for "progress wins" in consumer electronics. The cheapest mainstream TV has dropped in nominal price from ~$200 in 1950 to ~$88-$148 in 2025 — while the product transformed from a 10-12" black-and-white tube with 3 channels into a 32-43" 4K smart TV with streaming apps. This is one of the few consumer goods where the cheapest version today is both nominally cheaper AND dramatically superior to what existed 75 years ago. The price trajectory shows a plateau in the 1960s-1980s (as color replaced B&W at a higher price point), followed by steep deflation from the mid-2000s onward as LCD/LED manufacturing scaled globally. The BLS CPI for televisions shows an average annual deflation of -6.6% since 1950 — one of the most dramatic sustained deflations in any consumer product category.

## Methodology

**What "cheapest mainstream retail" means for TVs:** In each era, this tracks the lowest-priced television set available at mainstream US retailers (Sears, Kmart, Walmart, Best Buy, Amazon depending on era) that was a functional, new television suitable for primary household use. This excludes novelty items (wristwatch TVs, 2" pocket screens) and floor models/refurbished units.

**Technology transitions:** The "cheapest TV" underwent several technology transitions:
- **1950-1975:** Black & white sets were the cheapest option. B&W portables kept declining in price even as color TVs entered the market.
- **1975-2005:** Color CRT became the standard. By ~1975, B&W was niche/irrelevant. The cheapest TV was a 13" color CRT for most of this period.
- **2005-2010:** CRT-to-flat-panel transition. Brief price increase as the cheapest flat-panel LCD cost more than the departing CRT.
- **2010-2025:** LCD/LED era. Rapid deflation as Chinese and Taiwanese panel manufacturing scaled.

**The 2005-2010 bump:** There's a visible price increase from ~$80-$100 (cheapest CRT in 2000-2005) to ~$200 (cheapest LCD in 2010). This reflects a genuine technology transition: CRTs were discontinued by retailers, and early flat panels cost more. By 2015 the price had fallen below the old CRT floor and kept dropping.

**Data source approach:** Pre-1990 data relies heavily on historical catalog references, TV history archives, and secondary sources summarizing retail advertising. Post-2010 data benefits from archived retail pricing and industry reporting. The 1990-2005 period has the weakest primary source coverage — prices are estimated from multiple indirect references.

**Nominal dollars throughout.** No inflation adjustment applied, per plan.md. The labor-minutes calculation handles purchasing power by dividing by concurrent median hourly earnings.

## Known gaps

- **1950-1965 specific catalog prices:** While tvhistory.tv and other archives provide ranges, pinning the exact cheapest model at Sears or Montgomery Ward in a given year requires accessing the actual catalog scans. The prices used are representative midpoints of documented ranges.
- **1985-2000 entry-level prices:** This period has surprisingly weak primary source data for the cheapest 13" TV. Sears catalogs ended ~1993. Prices are estimated from multiple secondary references and are likely accurate within ±15%.
- **2005 transition period:** Difficult to pin because CRT and LCD coexisted briefly. The "cheapest TV" depends on whether the researcher counts departing CRTs (cheaper but being discontinued) or arriving LCDs (more expensive but becoming standard). We tracked the cheapest available regardless of technology.
