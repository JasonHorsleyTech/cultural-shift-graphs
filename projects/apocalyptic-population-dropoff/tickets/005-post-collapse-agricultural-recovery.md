# Ticket 005: Post-Collapse Agricultural Recovery Timeline

## What to research

After a sudden collapse of modern agriculture, how quickly can food production ramp up? This is about the transition gap — the period between "stored food runs out" and "new agriculture feeds the survivors."

## Specific data needed

1. **Year 0-1: What can be grown immediately?**
   - Seed availability (where do survivors get seeds? Home garden seed stocks, feed stores, unharvested fields)
   - Growing season constraints (collapse in October vs. April matters enormously)
   - Calories producible by hand-labor gardening per person-hour
   - Wild foraging potential (acorns, wild plants, mushrooms) — caloric contribution

2. **Year 1-3: First real harvests**
   - Yield expectations for first-generation hand-farmed crops
   - Learning curve — how quickly can non-farmers learn subsistence farming?
   - Historical precedents: Victory Gardens (WWII), Cuban Special Period agriculture, Khmer Rouge forced agrarianism
   - What crops are viable without modern inputs in year 1? (Potatoes, beans, squash — the survival crops)

3. **Year 3-10: Scaling up**
   - Draft animal breeding timeline (horse gestation: 11 months; one mare produces one foal per year)
   - From ~300K draft animals to functional agricultural draft population: how many years?
   - Tool production — can survivors manufacture plows, scythes, hoes from scrap metal?
   - Irrigation without electric pumps — gravity-fed, hand-pump, windmill

4. **Year 10-20: Approaching pre-industrial levels**
   - When does food production reach 1900-equivalent levels?
   - Knowledge preservation: what agricultural knowledge survives in books, university libraries?
   - Soil recovery timeline after modern monoculture

## Where to look

1. **Cuban Special Period (1991-2000)** — Best modern analogue. Cuba lost 80% of its petrochemical imports overnight. Agricultural transition is well-documented.
2. **WWII Victory Gardens** — 40% of US vegetables came from home gardens by 1944. How fast did that ramp up?
3. **ALLFED (Alliance to Feed the Earth in Disasters)** — specifically studies post-catastrophe food production
4. **Jeavons, John — "How to Grow More Vegetables"** — biointensive agriculture caloric yield data
5. **Historical agricultural transition literature**
6. **Permaculture / small-scale farming yield data**

## Result format

Key Data Points table with time-varying estimates:

| parameter | value | range_low | range_high | unit | timeframe | source | confidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Key timeframes: year 1, year 2, year 3, year 5, year 10, year 20.
Key parameter: `pct_of_current_food_production` — what fraction of today's food output can be produced at each time point.

## What good data looks like

- Uses the Cuban Special Period as primary calibration (real data, not models)
- Accounts for seasonal timing of collapse
- Distinguishes between "calories produced" and "calories produced per capita" (as population drops, per-capita production improves even if total production is low)
- Year 1 production should be very low (1-5% of current). If higher, explain what's being assumed about organization and seed access
