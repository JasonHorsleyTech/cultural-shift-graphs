# Extended History: Automotive Fuel Economy and Consumption 1950–1975

## What to Research

Pre-CAFE era data on vehicle fuel economy and gasoline consumption, 1950–1975. This provides context for the primary window — showing what happened *before* efficiency regulation (basically: efficiency stagnated while consumption soared).

## Where to Look

1. **Primary:** FHWA Highway Statistics (published since 1945). Historical tables for VMT, fuel consumed, registered vehicles.
2. **Secondary:** EPA Automotive Trends Report — includes pre-CAFE fleet efficiency estimates (adjusted by EPA from manufacturer data).
3. **Tertiary:** EIA historical petroleum data — motor gasoline supplied.
4. **Academic:** Daniel Sperling, various LBNL publications on historical vehicle efficiency.

## Scope

- 1950–1975, annual.
- Same metrics as tickets 002-003: fleet MPG, total gasoline consumption, VMT.
- This era shows: efficiency was FLAT (or slightly declining — cars got bigger/heavier/more powerful through the 1960s). Then the 1973 oil crisis and 1975 CAFE act reversed the trend.

## Data Gaps

- Fleet MPG estimates for the 1950s-60s are rougher than post-CAFE data (no standardized testing existed).
- VMT estimation methodology was less sophisticated pre-1970.
- Accept ±10% uncertainty in early years.

## What "Good Data" Looks Like

- Fleet MPG: roughly flat or slightly declining from ~14-15 MPG (1950) to ~12-13 MPG (1973). Cars got heavier, more powerful, and gained automatic transmissions and AC — all bad for fuel economy. No regulation incentivized efficiency.
- Gasoline consumption: dramatic growth (~3x from 1950 to 1975) driven by population growth + suburbanization + rising VMT per capita + declining efficiency.
- VMT: rapid growth from ~500 billion (1950) to ~1,300 billion (1975) — interstate highway system, suburbanization, two-car households.
- The punchline: this is the "no Jevons because no efficiency gains" era. Efficiency was flat and consumption soared anyway (pure demand growth). Contrasts with the CAFE era where efficiency improved but consumption growth slowed.
