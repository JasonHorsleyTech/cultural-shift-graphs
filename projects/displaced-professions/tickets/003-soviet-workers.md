# Ticket 003: Soviet Defense & Industrial Workers

## Event
The collapse of Soviet defense, heavy manufacturing, and state R&D employment following the dissolution of the USSR, 1991–1998. The canonical example of a discrete shock — near-total systemic collapse.

## Mechanism
**Discrete shock** (regime)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track employment collapse across key Soviet/Russian sectors from ~1985 to ~2005:
- Military-industrial complex (VPK): ~5 million at peak
- Heavy manufacturing / state enterprises
- Collective agriculture
- State R&D / scientific workers
- Total formal employment vs. total labor force

Key dates: Perestroika reforms (1986–1991), dissolution (Dec 1991), shock therapy (1992), 1998 financial crisis, Putin-era recovery (1999+).

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Rosstat (Russian Federal State Statistics Service) — labor force surveys, employment by sector
2. World Bank World Development Indicators (WDI) — GDP, employment, life expectancy
3. OECD Economic Surveys: Russian Federation (various years)
4. Brainerd, "Economic Reform and Mortality in the Former Soviet Union" (2010)
5. Gaddy, "The Price of the Past: Russia's Struggle with the Legacy of a Militarized Economy" (1996)
6. Ashwin & Clarke, "Russian Trade Unions and Industrial Relations in Transition" (2003)

### Income Data
Real wage trajectory is the story here. Nominal wages are meaningless during hyperinflation.

- Real wages fell to 34–40% of 1991 levels by 1998 trough (Brainerd)
- Did not recover to 1991 levels until ~2007
- Track real wages annually 1990–2007

**Known caveats:** Hyperinflation (1992–1995), barter economy, currency redenomination (1998). Use purchasing power reconstructions. Report methodology transparently.

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Brainerd (2010) — reconstructed real wages
2. Rosstat wage statistics deflated by CPI
3. World Bank WDI — GNI per capita (Atlas method)
4. Milanovic, "Income, Inequality, and Poverty during the Transition from Planned to Market Economy" (1998)

### Where They Went
- Shadow economy: grew to 40–50% of GDP by mid-1990s (Schneider & Enste 2000)
- Shuttle traders (*chelnoki*): estimated 10–30 million at peak
- Private security: ~800K+ as a new profession
- Petty trade / kiosk economy
- Emigration (brain drain — scientists, engineers to US, Israel, Germany)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Schneider & Enste, "Shadow Economies: Size, Causes, and Consequences" (2000)
2. Rosstat labor force surveys by sector
3. Gerber & Hout, "More Shock than Therapy" (1998)
4. Academic studies on post-Soviet labor market transitions

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Early oligarchs who acquired state assets (Khodorkovsky, Berezovsky). Scientists who emigrated pre-collapse. Engineers who pivoted to IT sector in late 1990s.
- **False movers:** Workers who stayed at state enterprises expecting back-pay that never came. Cooperative movement of late 1980s that was wiped out by hyperinflation.
- **Deniers:** Military-industrial workers who believed the state would always need them. Specific quotes from trade union leaders or factory directors.
- **Survival pattern:** What distinguished workers who recovered from those who didn't? Age, education, geography, connections?

### Unique Texture
- Male life expectancy drop: 64.2 years (1989) → 57.6 years (1994) — 6.6 years in 5 years
- "Deaths of despair" — alcohol-related mortality spike
- The speed: from superpower to economic collapse in ~2 years
- Comparison of defense cities (closed cities / naukograd) vs. diversified cities
- The *chelnoki* phenomenon — millions of former engineers doing cross-border petty trading

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Employment by sector annually from 1990–2005 (at least 8 data points)
- Real wage trajectory with at least 6 data points spanning 1990–2007
- Shadow economy size estimates with sourcing
- Life expectancy data (the "unique texture" that makes this event viscerally striking)
- At least 2 specific adapter/denier stories with names and dates
