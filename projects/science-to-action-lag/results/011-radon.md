## Sources

1. Bale WF, Harley JH (1951). "Effect of radon progeny on lung tissue." US Atomic Energy Commission memo establishing that alpha particles from radon decay products, not radon gas itself, cause lung cancer.
2. US Public Health Service Colorado Plateau Uranium Miners Study (begun 1951, results published 1960s). Demonstrated excess lung cancer in miners exposed to radon progeny. ~43,000 measurements of radon-daughter concentrations in ~2,500 mines during 1951-1968.
3. IARC Monographs Vol. 43 (1988). "Man-made Mineral Fibres and Radon." Classified radon-222 and its decay products as Group 1 carcinogens.
4. National Academy of Sciences, BEIR IV Report (1988). "Health Risks of Radon and Other Internally Deposited Alpha-Emitters." First formal risk model projecting residential lung cancer risk from miner data.
5. Swedish radiation protection regulations (1980). Introduced binding radon limit of 70 Bq/m³ EER for new buildings and 400 Bq/m³ for existing buildings, enforceable by local authorities. Source: "Experience from indoor radon-daughter limitation schemes in Sweden" (Environment International, 1989).
6. US EPA (1986). Recommended action level of 4 pCi/L for indoor radon — advisory guideline, not enforceable regulation.
7. US Congress, Indoor Radon Abatement Act of 1988 (42 USC 7401 note). Set long-term goal that indoor air be as free of radon as ambient outdoor air. Aspirational; no enforceable standard.
8. EU Council Directive 2013/59/Euratom (2013). Required all EU member states to set reference levels for indoor radon not exceeding 300 Bq/m³ and develop National Radon Action Plans. Binding on member states.
9. NAS BEIR VI Report (1999). "Health Effects of Exposure to Radon." Updated risk models incorporating residential radon studies confirming risk at lower exposure levels.
10. Nero AV et al. (1986). "Distribution of airborne radon-222 concentrations in U.S. homes." Science 234:992-997. First comprehensive survey of residential radon levels in the US.

## Judgment calls

**Early evidence year (1951 vs. 1960s vs. 1984):** The ticket instructs using residential evidence dates, but I chose 1951 (occupational evidence) because the plan defines early_evidence_year as "the year a credible scientific paper...first established the link between the hazard and the harm." The link between radon and lung cancer was established by miner studies — the science doesn't care whether the exposure is in a mine or a basement. The 1951 date reflects when systematic evidence collection began AND when the mechanism was identified. Using 1984 (when the Watras house incident revealed high residential radon) would hide the most important part of the lag story: society KNEW radon caused lung cancer for 30+ years before anyone checked homes. That gap IS the lag.

**Consensus year (1988):** Both IARC Group 1 classification and the NAS BEIR IV report came in 1988. Earlier mining evidence was strong but no major body had formally extended the conclusion to residential settings with a risk model until BEIR IV.

**First regulation (Sweden 1980):** Sweden's building code limit is genuinely binding — local authorities can require testing and mandate remediation for new buildings exceeding 70 Bq/m³. This is not merely advisory. However, it's unusual: it preceded formal consensus by 8 years (lag_from_consensus = -8). Sweden acted on the mining evidence alone, without waiting for residential-specific studies.

**US regulation (null):** The US has no binding federal regulation for residential radon. The EPA action level (4 pCi/L) is a recommendation only. The IRAA of 1988 sets a goal but creates no enforceable standard. Some states mandate radon testing in real estate transactions or schools, but no federal binding limit exists. This is a striking gap — 38 years after the IRAA passed, with radon being the #2 cause of lung cancer in the US, there is still no enforceable federal standard.

**Industry opposition (none):** Radon is a natural hazard with no industrial producer or beneficiary. Some real estate industry resistance to mandatory disclosure requirements exists, but nothing resembling the organized, sustained opposition seen with tobacco, lead, or asbestos. No industry spends money defending radon exposure.

**Substitute available (yes):** Radon-resistant new construction (sub-slab depressurization, sealing, ventilation) is well-established, effective, and relatively inexpensive ($800-2500 for mitigation in existing homes, minimal cost addition for new construction). Technical solutions are not the barrier.

**Category (exposure-limit):** The regulatory form is a concentration threshold in building codes, making this an exposure limit rather than a substance ban (you can't ban a naturally occurring gas) or behavioral mandate.
