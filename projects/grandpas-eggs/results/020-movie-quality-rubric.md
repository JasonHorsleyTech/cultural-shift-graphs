# Movie Ticket — Quality Rubric

## The 1955 baseline product

The standard 1955 moviegoing experience was a single-admission program at a neighborhood single-screen theater, seating 300–1,000 people. The program typically included:

- **Pre-feature content:** A newsreel (5–10 minutes of current affairs footage), one or two cartoon shorts (e.g., Looney Tunes, Tom and Jerry), and coming-attractions trailers. This was *entertainment*, not advertising — audiences looked forward to it. By 1955 the newsreel was in decline due to TV news competition, but still common.
- **The feature film:** Projected on 35mm film, typically in Academy ratio (1.37:1). By 1955, many downtown and first-run theaters had upgraded to CinemaScope (2.55:1 widescreen) with four-track stereophonic magnetic sound (left, center, right, surround). However, the typical neighborhood theater still showed most films in standard Academy ratio with mono optical sound. CinemaScope prints required special anamorphic lenses and magnetic sound heads that many smaller theaters hadn't installed.
- **Double features** were still common in 1955 but declining — you might get two films for one admission at second-run or neighborhood houses. By the late 1950s, single-feature programs with widescreen became the norm.
- **Air conditioning** was widespread in theaters by 1955. Movie palaces had been early adopters of air conditioning since 1925 (the Rivoli Theater in NYC was Willis Carrier's first installation). By the 1950s, air conditioning was a major draw — "Come in, it's COOL inside!" — and more reliable in theaters than in most homes. The theater was one of the few reliably cool public spaces in summer.
- **Seating:** Fixed upholstered seats, no cup holders, no stadium raking. Decent sightlines in most single-screen theaters because the single auditorium was purpose-built, with a raked floor and a large screen relative to the room. Screens in typical neighborhood theaters were roughly 20–30 feet wide (vs. 45–65 feet in modern large-format auditoriums, or 15–25 feet in small multiplex rooms).
- **Showtime flexibility:** One or two showtimes per day. You showed up when it started or you missed it. No reserved seating — first come, first served.
- **Concessions:** Popcorn, candy, and soft drinks. Simple and modestly priced, but already a theater tradition.

The experience was a communal social event — a night out. Dressing up for the movies was still common in the mid-1950s, especially for first-run downtown theaters.

## Quality-equivalent product definition

**The 1955 baseline maps to the modern standard ticket in most dimensions.** The typical 1955 experience was a neighborhood theater with mono sound, a modestly-sized screen, fixed seats, and a single-feature program. The modern standard multiplex ticket delivers clearly better picture quality, dramatically better sound, stadium seating with unobstructed views, and far more showtime flexibility. On pure technical attributes, the 2025 standard ticket is *superior* to the 1955 baseline.

**However, there are two arguments for mapping to premium:**

1. **Screen dominance.** The 1955 single-screen theater had a screen that filled the room. In a modern multiplex, you might get assigned to a small side auditorium with a 20-foot screen. IMAX and Dolby Cinema auditoriums are the closest modern analog to the "the screen is the whole wall" experience of a dedicated single-screen theater.
2. **Pre-show experience.** The 1955 program included free entertainment content (newsreels, cartoons). The modern pre-show is 25–30 minutes of paid advertising. No modern format fixes this.

**Verdict: The quality-equivalent is the standard ticket.** The balance of evidence — picture, sound, seating, convenience — strongly favors the modern standard experience as an *improvement* over 1955, not a degradation. The pre-show is worse, and the screen-size lottery is a real downside, but these don't outweigh the gains. The premium formats (IMAX, Dolby Cinema at $18–25) represent a *new tier* that far exceeds anything available in 1955, not a restoration of a lost baseline.

For the price data ticket (021), Line A and Line B should be the **same line** — the standard movie ticket — because the quality-equivalent hasn't degraded. The premium format price should be noted as annotation showing a new *upper* tier that didn't exist before, not as the quality-equivalent.

## Divergence timeline

**There is no meaningful quality divergence — the standard product improved.** But a timeline of format tiers:

- **Pre-1963:** Single-tier market. One theater, one screen, one price. (Drive-ins existed as a format variant but were a comparable price point.)
- **1963–1990s:** Multiplex era begins (AMC, 1963). Screens multiply but the experience is roughly uniform per theater — you don't choose a "premium" auditorium.
- **1970–2000s:** IMAX exists as a niche format (mostly museums, documentaries). Not a mainstream moviegoing option until IMAX Digital arrives in 2008.
- **2008–2014:** IMAX Digital expands into mainstream multiplexes. First clear premium tier with a $3–5 surcharge. 3D films (Avatar, 2009) create another premium tier.
- **2014–present:** Dolby Cinema launches (2014). Luxury recliner conversions accelerate (~2014–2016 at AMC, Regal, Cinemark). The market now has 3–4 price tiers: standard matinee ($10–13), standard evening ($14–17), premium format ($18–22), luxury/dine-in ($20–30+).

Unlike food or clothing, the tiering in movies moved *upward* — the floor rose (standard got better) while a new ceiling was added (premium formats). This is the opposite of the eggs/suits story where the floor dropped and you pay extra to get back to baseline.

## Overall assessment

**Progress wins.** The modern standard moviegoing experience is better than 1955 on a majority of measurable attributes: picture quality, sound, seating comfort, sightlines (stadium seating), showtime flexibility, and convenience (online ticketing, reserved seats). The two dimensions where it's worse — pre-show advertising replacing entertainment content, and screen-size inconsistency in multiplexes — are real but don't outweigh the improvements.

This makes movie tickets a useful contrast case in the commodity grid. While eggs, suits, and bread tell a "quality tax" story (the cheap version got worse, pay more to get what grandpa had), the movie ticket tells a "progress wins" story alongside TV and bicycles. The cheap version genuinely improved.

**One caveat:** This rubric covers the *theater experience itself*, not the cultural role of moviegoing. In 1955, movies were the dominant mass entertainment medium — there was no home video, no streaming, limited TV. Going to the movies was *the thing to do*. In 2025, the theatrical experience competes with home streaming on 65" 4K TVs with surround sound. The *relative* premium of the theater over home viewing has narrowed dramatically, even as the absolute quality improved. This is real but out of scope — the rubric measures the product itself, not its competitive position.
