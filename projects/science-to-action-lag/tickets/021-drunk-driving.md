# Ticket 021: Drunk Driving → Fatalities

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that alcohol impairment causes driving fatalities to binding regulation setting enforceable BAC limits. This is a tricky case — early regulation preceded formal science.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — New Jersey banned drunk driving in 1906, before formal studies. The AMA formed a committee to study alcohol and driving in 1938. Borkenstein's breathalyzer was developed in 1954 and enabled quantitative measurement. What's the earliest FORMAL SCIENTIFIC STUDY of alcohol's effect on driving ability?

2. **Consensus date** — When did a major scientific body formally establish a BAC threshold for impaired driving? The Grand Rapids Study (Borkenstein et al., 1964) was landmark — established that crash risk increases significantly above 0.05% BAC. AMA resolutions? NHTSA reports?

3. **First OECD regulation** — Norway enacted a 0.05% BAC limit in 1936 (one of the earliest in the world). UK Road Safety Act 1967 set 0.08% BAC. What was the first OECD country to enact a binding per se BAC limit?

4. **US regulation** — The US history is primarily state-level. Federal action: National Minimum Drinking Age Act (1984), federal 0.08% BAC standard (2000 — states required to adopt by 2004 or lose highway funding). When is the appropriate US regulation date?

5. **Special dynamics** — Drunk driving regulation was driven partly by grassroots activism (MADD, founded 1980) rather than scientific pressure. The MADD movement is a unique factor in the science-to-action timeline.

6. **50% exposure reduction** — Alcohol-related traffic fatalities in the US have dropped from ~25,000/year (1982) to ~10,000-13,000/year. NHTSA FARS data tracks this.

## Where to look

1. Borkenstein et al. (1964) — Grand Rapids Study
2. NHTSA historical documents on impaired driving
3. MADD history and legislative impact
4. Norwegian traffic safety legislation history
5. UK Road Safety Act 1967
6. NHTSA FARS data on alcohol-impaired driving fatalities

## What "good data" looks like

- The pre-science regulation (NJ 1906, Norway 1936) should be noted in judgment calls but not used as the regulation date
- Start the evidence clock from the first formal scientific study, not common-sense knowledge
- The MADD movement (1980s) as a non-scientific driver of regulatory action — document in notes
- Fatality reduction data for the secondary metric

## Known pitfalls

- This is one of the only cases where REGULATION PRECEDED FORMAL SCIENCE. Norway had a BAC limit in 1936, the Grand Rapids study wasn't until 1964. If you use the formal study date (1938 or 1964) and the earliest regulation (Norway 1936), you get a NEGATIVE lag. Handle this: use the earliest BAC limit set AFTER formal scientific evidence established an impairment threshold.

- Industry opposition: moderate. The alcohol industry didn't fight BAC limits as hard as tobacco fought smoking restrictions, but they lobbied on specific thresholds (0.10 vs. 0.08) and drinking age.
