# Ticket 018: Vioxx (Rofecoxib) → Cardiovascular Events

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that Vioxx (rofecoxib) increases cardiovascular risk to its withdrawal from market. This is a modern pharmaceutical case — fast withdrawal, but with a sub-lag of how long the manufacturer knew before acting.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Vioxx was approved by FDA in 1999. The VIGOR trial (published 2000) showed a significantly elevated risk of heart attacks compared to naproxen. Merck attributed this to naproxen being cardioprotective rather than Vioxx being cardiotoxic. When did independent evidence confirm the cardiovascular risk?

2. **Consensus date** — The APPROVe trial (2004) was designed to study colon polyps but showed clear cardiovascular risk, leading Merck to voluntarily withdraw the drug on September 30, 2004.

3. **First regulation** — Merck voluntarily withdrew Vioxx worldwide on September 30, 2004, before any regulatory agency mandated withdrawal. Was there any country that formally banned it before Merck's withdrawal?

4. **US regulation** — FDA did not force withdrawal — Merck acted voluntarily. FDA's role was criticized: an FDA safety officer (David Graham) testified that FDA had failed to act on safety signals. The withdrawal led to reforms in FDA's drug safety monitoring (FDA Amendments Act 2007).

5. **What Merck knew and when** — Internal documents (revealed in litigation) suggested Merck was aware of cardiovascular signals earlier than publicly acknowledged. The gap between internal knowledge and public action is part of the story.

6. **Scale of harm** — Estimated 88,000-140,000 excess cardiovascular events in the US during the period Vioxx was on the market. Merck paid $4.85 billion in settlements.

## Where to look

1. VIGOR trial (NEJM, 2000)
2. APPROVe trial data and FDA documents
3. David Graham's congressional testimony (2004)
4. Merck litigation documents (publicly available through litigation archives)
5. Eric Topol's analysis and commentary
6. FDA Amendments Act of 2007 (FDAAA) — legislative response

## What "good data" looks like

- Precise timeline: VIGOR data (2000), APPROVe data (2004), voluntary withdrawal (September 30, 2004)
- Documentation of the gap between VIGOR signal (2000) and withdrawal (2004) — 4 years where a cardiovascular signal was public but the drug remained on market
- The voluntary withdrawal is unusual — company acted before regulators. Note this dynamic.
- Litigation revelations about what Merck knew internally

## Known pitfalls

- This was a VOLUNTARY withdrawal, not a regulatory ban. The first_regulation_year should be the withdrawal date (2004), but note the regulatory context — FDA didn't force it.
- The VIGOR trial (2000) is ambiguous as evidence — Merck plausibly argued the naproxen-protective interpretation for years. The APPROVe trial (2004) was definitive. Choose the early_evidence_year carefully.
- Category: product-withdrawal.
- Industry opposition: moderate — Merck disputed the cardiovascular risk interpretation from 2000-2004 but ultimately withdrew the product.
