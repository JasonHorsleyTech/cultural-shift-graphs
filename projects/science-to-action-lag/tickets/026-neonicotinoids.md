# Ticket 026: Neonicotinoids → Pollinator Collapse

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that neonicotinoid pesticides harm pollinators (particularly honeybees and wild bees) to binding regulation restricting neonicotinoid use.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Neonicotinoids (imidacloprid, clothianidin, thiamethoxam) were introduced commercially in the 1990s. French beekeepers reported problems in the late 1990s, and France partially suspended imidacloprid on sunflowers in 1999. Peer-reviewed evidence: Whitehorn et al. (2012), Henry et al. (2012) in Science showed sub-lethal effects on bee colonies. When did credible evidence establish the harm?

2. **Consensus date** — EFSA risk assessments (2013, updated 2018) concluded neonicotinoids pose unacceptable risks to bees. Worldwide Integrated Assessment (van der Sluijs et al., 2015) was a comprehensive review. When did a major body formally confirm the risk?

3. **First OECD regulation** — France partially suspended imidacloprid in 1999 (based on field reports, before peer-reviewed studies). EU imposed a partial ban on three neonicotinoids (2013, restrictions on flowering crops) and a full outdoor ban (2018). Find the first BINDING restriction.

4. **US regulation** — No federal ban as of 2026. EPA completed risk assessments. Some states restrict neonicotinoids. This is an active regulatory divergence case (EU banned, US hasn't).

5. **Industry opposition** — Bayer and Syngenta (major neonicotinoid manufacturers) funded counter-studies and lobbied against bans. Bayer sued the European Commission over the 2013 restrictions (case dismissed 2018). Moderate to strong opposition.

6. **Substitute availability** — Alternative pest control methods exist but with varying effectiveness. Integrated pest management. Older pesticides (organophosphates, pyrethroids) are themselves environmentally problematic.

## What "good data" looks like

- The 1999 French suspension as possibly the earliest regulatory action (though based on field reports, not peer-reviewed studies)
- The EU 2013 partial ban as the first formal science-based restriction
- US inaction as a contrast point — document the regulatory divergence
- Manufacturer litigation against the EU ban as a form of industry opposition

## Known pitfalls

- The "Colony Collapse Disorder" phenomenon (named 2006) attracted public attention but its causes are multifactorial. Don't conflate CCD with neonicotinoid-specific harm — the scientific case for neonicotinoid harm is about sub-lethal effects and population decline, which is related but distinct from CCD.
- France's 1999 action was precautionary, before strong peer-reviewed evidence. This is a case where regulation preceded consensus. Note this dynamic.
