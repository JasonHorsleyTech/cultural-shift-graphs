# Result: Labor Force Participation Rate by Sex, 1948–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** Bureau of Labor Statistics, Current Population Survey (Household Survey); FRED series LNS11300001 (men) and LNS11300002 (women)
- **Secondary source:** BLS Women in the Labor Force: A Databook (2023 edition); BLS Monthly Labor Review articles on labor force trends
- **Date accessed:** 2026-05-01

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Civilian labor force participation rate = (civilian labor force / civilian noninstitutional population) × 100. The labor force includes all persons 16 years and over who are either employed or unemployed (actively seeking work).
- **Universe:** Civilian noninstitutional population aged 16 years and over, by sex. Excludes active-duty military and institutional populations (prisons, nursing homes, etc.).
- **Data frequency:** Monthly, seasonally adjusted. Values below are annual averages of the 12 monthly observations.
- **Known methodology changes:** CPS redesign in 1994 (introduced computer-assisted interviewing, revised questions); population controls updated with each decennial census. These changes produce minor level shifts but do not significantly affect long-run trends.

## Gaps and Caveats
- **Pre-1948 data:** BLS CPS begins in 1948. No comparable data exists for 1947.
- **1994 CPS redesign:** The introduction of computer-assisted interviewing and revised survey questions created a minor discontinuity. BLS estimates the redesign raised the measured female LFPR by about 0.5 pp. The effect was small enough that BLS does not publish separate pre/post series.
- **Part-time workers:** The participation rate counts anyone working or seeking work, regardless of hours. A person working 5 hours/week counts the same as 40 hours/week. This is a known limitation for interpreting economic well-being.
- **2020 anomaly:** The COVID-19 pandemic caused a sharp drop in both series (men fell 1.5 pp, women fell 1.2 pp from 2019). Recovery was uneven — men regained most lost ground by 2022, women slightly lagged.
- **2024 estimate:** The 2024 annual average is based on the full year of monthly data. Men's rate of 68.0% and women's rate of 57.5% are consistent with the monthly trajectory observed through December 2024.
- **Confidence assessment:**
  - 1948–2023: **High confidence.** Benchmark values (decade markers) independently verified against multiple BLS publications. Year-to-year values may differ from official annual averages by ±0.1–0.2 pp in some cases due to rounding, but the trends are accurate.
  - 2024: **Medium-high confidence.** Based on monthly data through December 2024 and consistent with reported figures, but the official BLS annual average publication may show values ±0.1 pp different.

## Key Narrative Points
- Men's LFPR declined 18.6 pp over 76 years (86.6% → 68.0%), driven by earlier retirement, rising disability, and longer education.
- Women's LFPR rose 24.8 pp (32.7% → 57.5%), with the bulk of gains occurring 1965–1999 (+20.7 pp in 34 years).
- Women's participation peaked at 60.0% in 1999 and has since declined modestly, influenced by aging demographics and the same forces depressing men's rates.
- The gender gap narrowed from 53.9 pp (1948) to 10.5 pp (2024).
- For the "share of the pie" graph: household income would have declined *much more* without women's massive entry into paid work. The fact that the median household's share of GDP fell *despite* adding a second earner makes the story more compelling.
