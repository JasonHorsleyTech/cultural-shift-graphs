# Result: BLS Labor Share of Nonfarm Business Output, 1947–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, Major Sector Productivity and Costs program. FRED series PRS85006173 (Nonfarm Business Sector: Labor Share for All Workers, Index 2017=100). Quarterly data for all four quarters retrieved from BLS data retrieval system.
- **Calibration source:** BLS Productivity and Costs news release (USDL-26-0580, March 24, 2026), which states Q4 2025 nonfarm business labor share = 54.4%. Used to convert index to actual percentage: factor = 54.4 / 96.224 = 0.565348.
- **Cross-reference:** BLS Multifactor Productivity series MPU4910141 (Private Nonfarm Business Sector: Labor Share), annual level data 1987–2024, used for validation (note: different sector definition — private nonfarm business excludes government enterprises).
- **Date accessed:** 2026-05-01

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Labor share = total labor compensation / current-dollar output of the nonfarm business sector × 100. Labor compensation includes wages and salaries plus supplements (employer contributions to employee benefit plans, employer contributions for social insurance).
- **Universe:** Nonfarm business sector. This covers approximately 75% of GDP. It excludes general government, nonprofit institutions, paid employees of private households, and the farm sector.
- **Computation:** Quarterly index values (2017=100) were retrieved for Q1–Q4 of each year. Annual averages were computed as the simple mean of the four quarters. The index was converted to actual labor share percentage using the calibration point from the BLS Q4 2025 news release (54.4% = index 96.224), yielding a 2017 base-year labor share of 56.53%.
- **Known methodology notes:** The BLS revised the productivity and costs program data in 2018 (comprehensive revision) and periodically thereafter. Current-vintage data may differ from values cited in older publications. For example, the BLS 2017 article cited Q1 1947 labor share as 65.8%, while current-vintage data yields approximately 65.2% for the same quarter — a reflection of benchmark revisions, not error.

## Gaps and Caveats

- **No missing data.** Complete annual series from 1947 to 2024 (78 data points).
- **Index-to-percentage conversion:** The BLS publishes this series as an index (2017=100), not as a direct percentage. The conversion uses a single calibration point from the Q4 2025 BLS press release (54.4%). If that press release value is itself revised, all percentage values would shift proportionally by the same factor. The relative movements and trends are unaffected by the calibration choice.
- **Data revisions:** Current-vintage values differ modestly from older publications due to BLS comprehensive revisions (most recently 2018, with ongoing annual revisions). Values from pre-2018 articles (e.g., "65.8% in Q1 1947") no longer match the current data vintage.
- **Sector coverage:** The nonfarm business sector covers ~75% of GDP. It excludes government, nonprofits, and farms. This means the labor share here is not directly comparable to whole-economy labor shares computed from NIPA data, which are typically several percentage points higher.
- **Mean, not median:** This is a mean measure (total compensation / total output). It captures the aggregate split between labor and capital but does not reflect the distribution within labor. Median workers may have experienced a larger decline than this series shows, since high earners pull the mean up.
- **Includes benefits:** Labor compensation includes employer-paid benefits (health insurance, retirement contributions, payroll taxes). The decline in labor share would appear even steeper if measured in wages alone, since benefits have grown as a share of compensation.
- **2020 anomaly:** The spike to 57.8% in 2020 reflects the pandemic dynamic: output fell sharply while compensation (including PPP-supported payrolls) was more sticky, mechanically raising the ratio. This reversed by 2021.
- **Post-2000 structural decline:** The roughly 6–7 percentage point decline from 2000 (63.1%) to 2024 (54.7%) is one of the most-studied macroeconomic shifts of the 21st century. Contributing factors include globalization, automation, rising market concentration, the shift toward capital-intensive tech firms, and declining worker bargaining power. The decline was particularly steep from 2000–2012.
- **Cyclical pattern:** Labor share tends to rise during recessions (wages are sticky while output drops) and fall during expansions — visible in the data as temporary spikes in 1954, 1958, 1970, 1974–75, 1980, 1982, 2001, 2008–09, and 2020.
- **Confidence: High** for the full 1947–2024 period. This is a single consistent series from the BLS with no methodology breaks or source switches. The only uncertainty is in the index-to-percentage calibration (±0.5 percentage points at most).

## Validation Against Private Nonfarm Business Series

Cross-checking against the BLS MFP series MPU4910141 (Private Nonfarm Business Sector: Labor Share, expressed directly as a percentage level):

| Year | Nonfarm Business (this ticket) | Private Nonfarm Business (MPU) | Difference |
|------|-------------------------------|-------------------------------|------------|
| 1987 | 62.9% | 67.8% | +4.9 pp |
| 2000 | 63.1% | 67.7% | +4.6 pp |
| 2010 | 56.3% | 61.2% | +4.9 pp |
| 2017 | 56.5% | 61.5% | +5.0 pp |
| 2024 | 54.7% | 58.3% | +3.6 pp |

The private nonfarm business labor share (MPU) is consistently ~4–5 percentage points higher because: (1) different sector definition (excludes government enterprises), and (2) different denominator (total costs vs. current-dollar output — the MFP program measures labor's share of total factor costs, whereas the productivity program measures labor's share of gross output including operating surplus). Both series show the same declining trend pattern, confirming the structural story.
