# Ticket 016: British Domestic Servants

## Event
The long decline of domestic service in Britain, ~1911–1960s. A gradual erosion driven by multiple reinforcing causes: WWI disrupted the servant class, cultural attitudes shifted, household technology reduced need, and women found better employment alternatives.

## Mechanism
**Gradual erosion** (cultural, war, tech_tool)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track UK domestic servant employment from ~1851 to ~1971:

- Domestic service was the single largest employment category for women in Britain until WWI
- Peak: ~1.4 million (1891 Census) or possibly higher. ~1.3 million in 1911.
- Post-WWI: sharp drop. Men who survived didn't return to service; women found factory/office work.
- Inter-war period: partial recovery but with changed terms (daily help replacing live-in servants)
- Post-WWII: continued steep decline
- By 1971: ~200,000 (estimate) — mostly daily cleaners, not traditional live-in servants

**Source hierarchy:**
1. UK Census returns 1851–1971 (occupational data)
2. Horn, "The Rise and Fall of the Victorian Servant" (1975)
3. Todd, "Young Women, Work, and Family in England 1918–1950" (2005)
4. Delap, "Knowing Their Place: Domestic Service in Twentieth-Century Britain" (2011)
5. Schwarz, "English Servants and Their Employers During the Eighteenth and Nineteenth Centuries" (various)

### Income Data
- Servant wages by role (cook, housemaid, parlour maid, valet, butler) over time
- The "all-in" compensation issue: live-in servants received room, board, and often clothing in addition to cash wages. Cash wages alone understate total compensation.
- Compare to factory wages for women (the key alternative)
- Track from 1900–1960

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Horn (1975) — wage tables
2. Burnett, "Useful Toil: Autobiographies of Working People from the 1820s to the 1920s" (1974)
3. Census/Ministry of Labour wage data for domestic servants vs. factory workers
4. Bank of England historical CPI

### Where They Went
- Factory work (especially during and after WWI)
- Office/clerical work (growing rapidly after 1900)
- Shop work (retail)
- Nursing and healthcare
- Marriage (many left service upon marriage — track marriage rates among former servants)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. UK Census occupational data showing women's employment shifts
2. Todd (2005) — women's employment transitions
3. Braybon & Summerfield, "Out of the Cage: Women's Experiences in Two World Wars" (1987)

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Servants who trained as nurses, typists, or telephone operators during WWI and never returned. Upper servants (cooks, butlers) who moved to hotel/restaurant work.
- **False movers:** The "servant shortage" campaigns of the 1920s–30s — attempts to attract workers back into service with better conditions that never materialized.
- **Deniers:** The British upper class's persistent belief that the servant shortage was temporary. "Servant problem" discourse in newspapers and magazines.
- **Survival pattern:** Transition to the service *industry* (hotels, restaurants, institutional catering) rather than service in private homes.

### Unique Texture
- WWI as the rupture point: men went to war, women went to factories, neither came back to service
- The Downton Abbey phenomenon — cultural memory of the servant class
- The class dimension: servant-keeping was a marker of middle-class status. The decline of service was also the decline of a class signifier.
- Household technology (vacuum cleaners, washing machines, gas stoves) reduced the *need* for servants — this is tech displacement but of the household, not the workplace
- The daily help vs. live-in servant transition — same work, completely different social relationship

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Census employment data for domestic service at each census from 1891–1971 (at least 6 data points)
- Women's employment by sector over the same period (showing what replaced service)
- Wage comparisons (service vs. factory/office)
- The WWI disruption quantified (pre-1914 vs. post-1918 employment)
- The household technology timeline
