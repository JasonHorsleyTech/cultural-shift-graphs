# Childhood Risk DALY Comparison

## The Question

Which childhood exposures actually cause more expected misery: the ones we've made illegal (no car seat, secondhand smoke) or the ones we've normalized (unrestricted screen time, unlimited sugar, social media at age 8)?

## The Impetus

Jason drives his 4-year-old in a '93 pickup with a lap belt — technically child endangerment. But those truck rides produced some of their best conversations. Meanwhile, handing a kid a tablet with YouTube for 8 hours a day is perfectly legal, CPS-approved, and arguably far more destructive long-term. There's a disconnect: we aggressively regulate gruesome/acute/visible risks and completely ignore slow/invisible/chronic ones.

## The Metric: DALYs (Disability-Adjusted Life Years)

WHO's universal unit for health burden. 1 DALY = 1 year of healthy life lost. Combines premature death + years lived with disability. Already used in public health to compare wildly different conditions on the same scale.

**Formula per exposure:**
Expected DALYs = P(bad outcome | exposure) × DALY burden of that outcome

This is standard actuarial/epidemiological math. Same framework insurance companies and courts use.

## Categories to Compare

### Illegal / Socially Unacceptable
- No car seat (lap belt only)
- Secondhand smoke exposure
- No bicycle helmet
- Riding in truck bed
- Leaving kid in car
- Kid riding ATV / dirt bike unsupervised

### Legal / Socially Acceptable (but potentially more harmful)
- Unrestricted screen time (>4hrs/day)
- Unrestricted social media access (pre-teen)
- Sedentary lifestyle / no physical activity mandate
- High-sugar diet / unlimited junk food
- Early onset caffeine consumption
- No sleep hygiene enforcement
- Early exposure to pornography
- Social isolation (digital substitution for human interaction)

## Data Sources to Look For

- **Car accidents:** NHTSA data on unrestrained child injuries/fatalities per trip-mile. DALY burden of various injury severities.
- **Secondhand smoke:** EPA/WHO relative risk data for childhood SHS exposure → lung disease, asthma, cancer. DALY burden per condition.
- **Screen time:** AAP studies, longitudinal cohort studies linking screen time to depression, anxiety, ADHD-like symptoms. WHO Global Burden of Disease for DALYs per condition.
- **Social media:** Surgeon General's advisory (2023) quantified risks. Studies on adolescent depression/suicide correlation with social media hours.
- **Sugar/diet:** Pediatric diabetes and obesity rates. Relative risk of metabolic syndrome from high-sugar diets. DALY burden of type 2 diabetes.
- **Sedentary lifestyle:** WHO physical activity guidelines, studies on childhood inactivity → adult cardiovascular disease.
- **Autism/ADHD rates:** Current prevalence (autism ~5% boys, ADHD ~16%). Time series data. Correlation with screen time in early childhood — some studies exist, controversial.

## Key Stats Jason Found

- Autism: ~5% of boys
- ADHD: ~16% (1 in 5ish)
- Screen time ages 2-4: avg 3 hrs/day
- Screen time ages 5-8: avg 4 hrs/day
- Teens: 4-8 hrs/day
- Childhood diabetes: 2%, projected 700% increase at current trends

## The Causation Problem (and why it doesn't matter for the graph)

Can't prove Oreos → diabetes in isolation. Can't ethically run the experiment. But:
1. Correlational data exists and is robust for most of these exposures
2. The car seat math is ALSO correlational — not every unbelted kid gets hurt
3. We're comparing risk profiles on equal methodological footing
4. The graph should be transparent about this: "expected DALYs based on observed relative risk ratios, not proven causal chains"

The point isn't to prove screen time causes autism. The point is to show that the expected harm from normalized parenting choices dwarfs the expected harm from the things we made illegal — even using conservative estimates.

## Graph Format Ideas

- **Bar chart:** One bar per exposure, height = expected DALYs per childhood (0-18). Color-coded: red = illegal/regulated, blue = legal/normalized.
- **The punchline:** The blue bars are taller than the red ones. Way taller.
- Could add a "combined" bar showing total expected DALYs from "the typical negligent-but-legal parenting package" vs. "the typical illegal stuff."

## Bias Disclosure

Jason is asking this question from a contrarian position. The graph needs to be honest — if the data shows car seats matter more than screen time, show that. But the hypothesis is strong enough to test.

## Workshopping Notes

- Need to nail down the exposure assumptions. "Driving without a car seat" — how often? Per-trip risk or cumulative childhood risk? Same for screen time — are we comparing "one truck ride" to "one hour of YouTube" or "a childhood of each"?
- Probably best to normalize to "expected DALYs across an entire childhood (0-18)" assuming the exposure is the norm for that household.
- The autism/ADHD angle is the most controversial and least provable. Might want to focus on things with stronger epi data: depression, anxiety, diabetes, obesity, cardiovascular disease.
- Or: include the ADHD/autism angle but with massive error bars and a note about diagnostic criteria changes.
