# Ticket 007: Post-Collapse Violence and Social Dynamics

## What to research

What role does organized and disorganized violence play in post-collapse mortality? The caloric math says one thing; human behavior says another. This ticket quantifies the violence amplifier.

## Specific data needed

1. **Violence as cause of death in historical collapses:**
   - What fraction of excess deaths were from violence vs. starvation vs. disease in each major collapse?
   - Rwanda: almost entirely violence. Leningrad: almost entirely starvation. Where do other collapses fall?

2. **The arms factor:**
   - US civilian firearms: ~400M guns, ~150M gun owners
   - Historical comparison: no collapsed society has ever had this level of civilian armament
   - Does widespread armament increase or decrease total violence mortality? (Arguments both ways)

3. **Resource competition dynamics:**
   - How quickly do armed groups form to control food/water?
   - Historical precedent: warlordism in Somalia, Yugoslavia, Syria
   - Expected timeline: days to organized gangs, weeks to territorial control?

4. **Cooperation dynamics (the counterweight):**
   - How quickly do communities self-organize for mutual defense?
   - Historical precedent: Katrina mutual aid, Puerto Rico community response, Occupy Sandy
   - Dunbar number and community formation — what size groups cooperate effectively?
   - Military/National Guard: how do organized armed forces behave when the chain of command breaks?

5. **Violence mortality estimate:**
   - In a US grid-down scenario, what additional percentage of deaths come from violence on top of starvation/disease?
   - Time profile: when does violence peak? (Probably weeks 2-8 as stored food runs out, then declining as populations thin)

## Where to look

1. **Pinker, Steven — "The Better Angels of Our Nature"** — historical violence rates
2. **Keeley, Lawrence — "War Before Civilization"** — pre-state violence mortality
3. **Collier, Paul — conflict economics research**
4. **Kalyvas, Stathis — "The Logic of Violence in Civil War"**
5. **ACLED (Armed Conflict Location & Event Data)** — conflict mortality databases
6. **Disaster sociology literature** — Quarantelli, Solnit ("A Paradise Built in Hell")
7. **Military planning documents** — FEMA, DoD continuity of government studies

## Result format

Key Data Points table. Key parameters:
- `violence_mortality_amplifier` — multiplier on top of starvation/disease deaths (e.g., 1.3x means 30% more deaths from violence)
- `peak_violence_period` — timeframe when violence deaths are highest
- `historical_violence_pct_of_collapse_deaths` — for each documented collapse

## What good data looks like

- Acknowledges the deep uncertainty (this is inherently speculative for a modern US scenario)
- Uses historical collapses as calibration but notes the unprecedented firearms variable
- Provides a range, not a point estimate. The range might be very wide (violence adds 10-50% to mortality) — that's honest
- Distinguishes between interpersonal violence and organized group violence
