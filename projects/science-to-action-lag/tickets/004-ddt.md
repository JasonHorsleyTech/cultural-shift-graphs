# Ticket 004: DDT → Ecosystem Damage

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that DDT causes ecological harm (eggshell thinning, bioaccumulation, wildlife population decline) to binding regulation banning or restricting DDT use.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Rachel Carson's "Silent Spring" (1962) is the iconic publication, but scientific concerns predated it. Look for the earliest peer-reviewed evidence of DDT's ecological harm. Key: studies on bioaccumulation, eggshell thinning (Ratcliffe 1967), and effects on bird populations.

2. **Consensus date** — When did a major scientific body formally conclude DDT's ecological harm? The EPA hearing process (1971-1972) is one endpoint. NAS or other scientific body reports?

3. **First OECD regulation** — Hungary and Norway banned DDT in 1968. Sweden banned it in 1970. US banned agricultural use in 1972. Find the earliest OECD country ban.

4. **US regulation** — EPA Administrator William Ruckelshaus banned DDT for agricultural use in 1972 after administrative hearings. Confirm scope and date.

5. **Stockholm Convention** — DDT was listed under the Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants (2001/2004), restricting use globally but allowing exceptions for malaria control.

6. **Industry opposition** — Chemical manufacturers (primarily Montrose Chemical Corporation) fought the ban. The opposition was real but less organized/prolonged than tobacco or lead.

7. **50% exposure reduction** — DDT levels in wildlife and humans dropped rapidly after the US ban. Peregrine falcon and bald eagle recovery timelines provide vivid exposure reduction data.

## Where to look

1. Rachel Carson, "Silent Spring" (1962)
2. EPA historical documents on the DDT ban (1972 decision document)
3. Stockholm Convention text and ratification history
4. PubMed — "DDT eggshell thinning" and "DDT environmental persistence"
5. US Fish & Wildlife Service — bald eagle and peregrine falcon recovery data

## What "good data" looks like

- Evidence dates that focus on ECOLOGICAL harm (the metric for this hazard), not human health effects
- Clear regulatory dates for the agricultural ban (not just restrictions on specific uses)
- Context on the speed of response relative to the era — 10 years from Silent Spring to US ban was considered fast for the 1960s-70s
- Note that DDT is still used for malaria control in some countries under Stockholm Convention exceptions

## Known pitfalls

- "Silent Spring" (1962) is a popular book, not a peer-reviewed study. The peer-reviewed ecological evidence accumulated through the 1960s. Decide whether 1962 is the evidence date or a slightly later study.
- DDT's HUMAN health effects are debated to this day. The regulatory action was based on ECOLOGICAL harm. Keep the focus on ecosystem damage, not cancer risk.
- Category classification: this is a "substance-ban" (agricultural pesticide banned), not an international treaty, even though the Stockholm Convention later addressed it globally.
