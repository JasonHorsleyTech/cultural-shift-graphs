## Sources

1. Tseng, W.P. et al. (1968). "Prevalence of Skin Cancer in an Endemic Area of Chronic Arsenicism in Taiwan." Journal of the National Cancer Institute, 40(3), 453-463. — First major epidemiological study linking arsenic in drinking water to cancer.
2. IARC (1980). Monographs on the Evaluation of Carcinogenic Risks to Humans, Volume 23: Some Metals and Metallic Compounds. — First Group 1 classification of arsenic.
3. IARC (2004). Monographs Volume 84: Some Drinking-Water Disinfectants and Contaminants, including Arsenic. — Updated evaluation specifically addressing drinking water arsenic.
4. WHO (1993). Guidelines for Drinking-Water Quality, 2nd edition. — Revised provisional guideline from 50 μg/L to 10 μg/L.
5. Council Directive 98/83/EC of 3 November 1998 on the quality of water intended for human consumption. Official Journal of the European Communities. — First binding OECD regulation at 10 ppb.
6. EPA (2001). "National Primary Drinking Water Regulations; Arsenic and Clarifications to Compliance and New Source Contaminants Monitoring." Federal Register, 66 FR 6976, January 22, 2001.
7. National Research Council (1999). Arsenic in Drinking Water. National Academy Press. — Concluded 50 ppb MCL did not achieve EPA's goal for public health protection.
8. National Research Council (2001). Arsenic in Drinking Water: 2001 Update. National Academy Press. — Confirmed cancer risks at concentrations below 50 ppb.
9. EPA Drinking Water Arsenic Rule History (https://www.epa.gov/dwreginfo/drinking-water-arsenic-rule-history). — Regulatory timeline including Bush administration suspension and reinstatement.
10. Tseng, W.P. (1989). "Blackfoot Disease in Taiwan: A 30-Year Follow-up Study." Angiology, 40(6). — Long-term follow-up confirming the arsenic-disease relationship.

## Judgment calls

**Early evidence year (1968 vs. earlier candidates):** Arsenic toxicity has been known since antiquity, and blackfoot disease in Taiwan was documented from the early 1900s. Chen and Wu published on blackfoot disease in 1962, but that study focused on the vascular disease itself, not cancer. Tseng et al. (1968) is the first large-scale epidemiological study specifically demonstrating a dose-response relationship between arsenic in drinking water and cancer (skin cancer). Earlier clinical observations exist but lack the systematic epidemiological design required to establish causation. I chose 1968 over 1962 because the cancer link (not just vascular disease) is what drove regulation.

**Consensus year (1980 vs. 1999):** IARC Volume 23 (1980) classified arsenic as Group 1 based on sufficient evidence in humans, including the Taiwanese drinking water studies. This is the first formal international scientific body consensus statement. The NAS 1999 report was specifically about drinking water standards and concluded 50 ppb was inadequate, but the underlying scientific consensus on carcinogenicity was already established by 1980. I used 1980 as it represents formal consensus on the hazard; 1999 represents consensus on the specific regulatory threshold.

**First regulation year (1998 vs. 2003):** The EU Directive was adopted November 1998 and entered into force December 1998, creating a binding legal obligation on member states. However, member states had until December 2003 to achieve compliance for chemical parameters. I used 1998 as the regulation year because the binding obligation was created then, consistent with how the plan defines "first binding law or regulation." If compliance date is preferred, use 2003.

**Why not count the 50 ppb standard (1942/1975)?** The US Public Health Service set 50 ppb in 1942, carried into the Safe Drinking Water Act in 1975. This preceded the key epidemiological evidence. However, 50 ppb is now known to be unsafe (cancer risk ~1 in 100 at lifetime exposure). The science-informed revision to 10 ppb is the meaningful regulatory response to the cancer evidence. Using 1942 would create a negative lag, which misrepresents the story: we regulated before we had the science, but at an inadequate level.

**Industry opposition (moderate vs. strong):** Opposition came primarily from mining interests and small water systems facing high compliance costs, not from a coordinated multi-decade campaign of science denial. The Bush administration's March 2001 suspension of the rule was politically significant, but the standard was reinstated within 8 months. This contrasts with tobacco or leaded gasoline where industry funded decades of doubt-manufacturing. "Moderate" reflects real political opposition without the sustained organized denial characteristic of "strong."

**Substitute available (partial):** Treatment technologies exist (coagulation/filtration, adsorption media, reverse osmosis, ion exchange) and are proven effective. However, costs are prohibitive for many small community water systems — EPA estimated ~3,000 systems serving 11 million people would be out of compliance. Alternative water sources exist in some areas but not all. "Partial" reflects that solutions exist but with significant cost barriers.

**Harm visibility (low):** Chronic low-level arsenic exposure causes cancers that develop over 20-40 years. Individual cancer cases cannot be attributed to arsenic (no biomarker distinguishing arsenic-caused cancer from other causes). The relationship is purely statistical/epidemiological. Acute arsenic poisoning is highly visible, but that's a different exposure scenario from chronic drinking water contamination at ppb levels. At the concentrations relevant to regulation (10-50 ppb), effects are invisible without epidemiological analysis.
