# Pre-1950 Water Use Context

## What to research

Gather **anecdotal and fragmentary data points** about US water usage before 1950 (when USGS systematic reporting began). These won't form a continuous series — they're isolated dots that provide left-side context for the historical graph.

The goal: show that per-capita water usage was dramatically lower before modern infrastructure, then exploded with industrialization and municipal water systems, then peaked and declined. The pre-1950 data tells the "explosion" part of the story.

## What to find

### Municipal water system history
- When did major US cities get running water? (Philadelphia 1801, NYC 1842, Chicago 1840s, etc.)
- What was per-capita municipal water consumption in early systems?
- **Key data point:** Chicago reportedly went from 33 gal/person/day in 1856 to 144 gal/person/day in 1882 as indoor plumbing spread. Find the primary source for this and any similar city-level data.

### Indoor plumbing adoption
- What percentage of US homes had indoor plumbing by decade? (1890: ~X%, 1920: ~Y%, 1940: ~Z%)
- When did flush toilets become standard? (Major water use driver.)
- When did bathtubs/showers become common?

### Agricultural irrigation pre-1950
- Mormon settlers began organized irrigation in Utah in 1847.
- Reclamation Act of 1902 federalized western irrigation.
- Total irrigated acreage by decade: 1900, 1910, 1920, 1930, 1940.
- Any rough estimates of total irrigation water use pre-1950.

### Industrial water use in the early industrial era
- When did water-intensive industries emerge? (Steel mills, textile mills, paper mills.)
- Any estimates of industrial water consumption in the late 1800s / early 1900s.

### Total estimated water use
- Has anyone attempted to back-extrapolate total US water withdrawal before 1950?
- Even rough order-of-magnitude: was the US using 10 Bgal/day in 1900? 50? 100?

## Data source hierarchy

1. **National Academies** — "Drinking Water and Health" series; history of US water infrastructure.
2. **USGS historical publications** — Early water resource surveys (pre-USGS compilations).
3. **American Water Works Association (AWWA)** — Historical municipal water data.
4. **Census Bureau** — Historical housing data (indoor plumbing rates).
5. **Bureau of Reclamation** — History of federal irrigation projects.
6. **Historical Statistical Abstracts of the United States** — May have pre-1950 water data.
7. **Academic papers on the history of water infrastructure** — Blake (1956) "Water for the Cities"; Melosi (2000) "The Sanitary City."

## Output format

### City-level data points
```
| City | Year | Per Capita Use (gal/day) | Population Served | Context | Source |
```

### National milestones
```
| Year | Milestone | Estimated Impact on Water Use | Source |
```

### Infrastructure adoption
```
| Year | % US Homes with Indoor Plumbing | Source |
```

### Estimated national withdrawal (if available)
```
| Year | Estimated Total (Bgal/day) | Confidence | Basis for Estimate | Source |
```

## What "good data" looks like

- 5-10 specific city-level data points from the 1800s-1940s with primary sources
- A clear narrative of how per-capita water use scaled with infrastructure (10 gal/day with a well → 50 gal/day with piped water → 100+ gal/day with flush toilets and baths)
- Indoor plumbing adoption curve (this is the single biggest driver of residential water use growth)
- Some estimate of irrigation water use growth pre-1950
- Enough to anchor the left side of the graph even if the data points are isolated dots rather than a continuous line
