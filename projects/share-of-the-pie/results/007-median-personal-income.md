# Result: Median Personal Income (Individual), 1947–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** FRED series MEPAINUSA646N — Median Personal Income in the United States, Current Dollars (sourced from U.S. Census Bureau, CPS ASEC). URL: https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/MEPAINUSA646N
- **Secondary source:** U.S. Census Bureau, Historical Income Tables: People, Table P-8 — "Age--People, All Races, by Median Income and Sex: 1947 to 2018." Downloaded from https://www2.census.gov/programs-surveys/cps/tables/time-series/historical-income-people/p08ar.xls
- **Cross-reference:** FRED series MEPAINUSA672N — Real Median Personal Income in the United States (2024 CPI-U-RS adjusted dollars). URL: https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/MEPAINUSA672N
- **Date accessed:** 2026-04-29

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Median total money income of persons with income, both sexes combined. "Money income" is total pre-tax cash income received on a regular basis, excluding capital gains, lump-sum payments, and non-cash benefits (e.g., employer health insurance, food stamps).
- **Universe:** Persons 15 years old and over with income, beginning with March 1980 CPS. Persons 14 years old and over for earlier years. Excludes persons with zero income (non-working dependents, children, non-working adults).
- **Survey:** Current Population Survey Annual Social and Economic Supplement (CPS ASEC), conducted each March. Income data refers to the preceding calendar year.
- **Known methodology changes:**
  - **1980:** Age threshold changed from 14+ to 15+.
  - **2013:** CPS ASEC income questionnaire redesigned (new retirement income questions, income range option, eliminated screeners). Census estimated a ~3.2% upward break in median household income; a similar magnitude likely affected personal income. Census published dual estimates for 2013 under both old and new methodology.
  - **2017/2019:** Processing system overhauled (improved imputation, removed caps on means-tested benefits, increased top codes). Census published dual estimates for 2017 under both old and new processing.
  - Various years have updated population controls from decennial Census benchmarks (1980, 1990, 2000, 2010, 2020). These introduce small discontinuities.
- **"Both sexes" combined median:** The FRED series MEPAINUSA646N provides a single median across all persons with income regardless of sex. This series begins in 1974. For 1947–1973, only sex-specific medians are available from Census Table P-8 — the combined "both sexes" median cannot be computed from male and female medians alone (you'd need the full distribution).

## Gaps and Caveats

### Pre-1974 combined median unavailable
- The "both sexes" combined median personal income series (FRED MEPAINUSA646N) begins in 1974. For 1947–1973, only sex-specific medians are available from Census Table P-8.
- The combined median **cannot** be computed by averaging male and female medians. Computing it requires the full income distribution (or at minimum, knowing which sex contains the overall 50th percentile and its distribution shape around that point).
- However, the sex-specific data from Table 2 is still useful: the male median serves as a rough upper bound and the female median as a lower bound for the combined figure. The male median is closer to the combined because men were the majority of income recipients in earlier decades (e.g., in 1947, 46.8M men vs 21.5M women had income).

### Methodology breaks
- **2013:** Questionnaire redesign caused an upward break of ~3.2% in median income estimates. For 2013, Census published estimates under both old and new methodology. The FRED series uses the new-methodology value for 2013 onward. Table 2 uses the new-methodology (footnote 39) values for 2013.
- **2017:** Processing system overhaul. Census published dual estimates for 2017. The FRED series uses the updated-processing value. Table 2 uses the updated-processing (footnote 40) values for 2017.
- **1980:** Age threshold changed from 14+ to 15+. Minor impact on the median.

### Universe notes
- This series counts **persons with income only**. Persons with zero income are excluded. This means the "median" is not the median of all adults — it's the median of the income-receiving population. As women's labor force participation increased, more low-earning women entered the denominator, which can depress the combined median even as individual incomes rise.
- The income definition excludes non-cash benefits (employer health insurance, food stamps/SNAP, housing subsidies) and capital gains. This understates total economic resources, especially in recent decades as non-cash compensation has grown.

### 2020 anomaly
- The 2020 nominal value ($35,850) is slightly below 2019 ($35,980), likely reflecting pandemic disruption. However, government stimulus payments and expanded unemployment benefits may have partially offset this. The CPS ASEC's March timing captures the preceding year's income, so the 2020 value reflects calendar year 2020 income.

### Confidence assessment
- **1974–2024, both sexes combined (FRED):** High confidence. Official Census Bureau series, consistent methodology within each sub-period.
- **1947–2018, by sex (Census P-8):** High confidence for individual values. The series is internally consistent within sub-periods, though methodology breaks in 2013 and 2017 mean levels are not perfectly comparable across those transitions.
- **Pre-1974 combined median:** Not available. Marked as missing.

### Cross-validation
- 2023 both-sexes nominal median: $42,220 — consistent with Census P-60 No. 282 report.
- 2024 both-sexes nominal median: $45,140 — consistent with Census P-60 No. 286 report.
- The both-sexes median ($33,710 in 2018) falls between male ($41,615) and female ($27,079) medians as expected.
- The both-sexes median is substantially lower than median household income (~$63,179 in 2018), confirming that households typically have multiple income earners.
