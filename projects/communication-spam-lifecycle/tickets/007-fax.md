# 007: Fax Machines

**System:** Facsimile (fax) machines
**Time range:** 1966–2000s (mainstream era: 1980s–2000s)
**Expected data quality:** Moderate — TCPA legislative record, industry reports, FCC data

## Research task

Trace the fax machine's rapid rise and fall. Fax is one of the clearest spam lifecycle examples: it went from essential business communication to junk-fax-choked in under a decade. The problem got so bad that Congress specifically addressed it in the TCPA (1991) and the Junk Fax Prevention Act (2005). Fax spam was uniquely offensive because it consumed the recipient's paper and ink — the spammer was literally making YOU pay for their ad.

## Key years to sample

- 1966 (Xerox introduces first commercial fax for businesses)
- 1980 (fax machines becoming affordable for small businesses)
- 1985 (fax boom — nearly every office has one)
- 1988 (junk faxes becoming a major complaint)
- 1991 (TCPA passes — includes junk fax provisions)
- 1995 (fax still essential, junk faxes continue despite TCPA)
- 2000 (email beginning to displace fax)
- 2005 (Junk Fax Prevention Act)
- 2010 (fax in sharp decline, mostly healthcare/legal holdouts)
- 2020 (fax essentially dead except in regulated industries)

## Data sources to check

- **Congressional records for TCPA (1991)** — testimony includes statistics on junk fax volume
- **FCC enforcement actions** on junk fax violations
- **Junk Fax Prevention Act (2005)** legislative history
- **Industry reports** on fax machine sales and usage
- **Academic papers** on unsolicited fax advertising
- **FTC complaint data**

## Dimensions to collect

1. **Spam percentage** — % of faxes received that were unsolicited junk. Congressional testimony from TCPA hearings should have estimates. Industry surveys from the late 1980s–1990s.
2. **Total message volume** — total fax pages transmitted per year in US
3. **Primary users** — % of US businesses relying on fax as primary document delivery
4. **Total users** — % of US businesses/households with a fax machine
5. **User cost per message** — cost per fax page (phone charges + paper + ink/toner). Unique: the RECIPIENT pays for paper and ink on junk faxes.
6. **User time per message** — time to prepare and feed a fax
7. **Infrastructure cost per message** — phone line cost per fax page
8. **Platform ARPU** — N/A (decentralized system, no platform)
9. **Barrier to reach** — phone-number (fax numbers were often published in directories)
10. **Reach per message** — one-to-one (but auto-dial fax spammers hit many numbers sequentially)
11. **Gatekeeping** — open (anyone can fax any fax number)
12. **Years to spam saturation** — roughly 1980 (mainstream adoption) to ~1988 (junk faxes a major problem) = ~8 years
13. **Regulatory response** — TCPA (1991), Junk Fax Prevention Act (2005)
14. **Defenses** — TCPA lawsuits (private right of action), caller ID on fax, email displacing fax entirely

## Special notes

Fax is the speed-run example. From mainstream adoption to spam saturation in under a decade. Also the only system where spam physically cost the recipient resources (paper and ink). This is why it provoked the fastest regulatory response. Important contrast with email: email spam costs the recipient nothing tangible, which is partly why email spam was tolerated longer.
