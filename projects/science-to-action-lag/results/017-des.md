# DES (Diethylstilbestrol) → Cancer / Birth Defects

## Data

- slug: des-pregnancy
- hazard: DES (diethylstilbestrol)
- harm: Vaginal clear-cell adenocarcinoma and reproductive abnormalities in exposed offspring
- category: product-withdrawal
- early_evidence_year: 1971
- early_evidence_desc: Herbst, Ulfelder & Poskanzer published in NEJM (April 22, 1971) linking maternal DES use during pregnancy to clear-cell adenocarcinoma of the vagina in daughters exposed in utero. Case-control study of 8 patients born 1946–1951 found 7 of 8 mothers had received DES in the first trimester (p < 0.00001).
- consensus_year: 1971
- consensus_desc: Scientific community accepted the Herbst findings almost immediately. FDA issued Drug Bulletin in November 1971 formally contraindicting DES in pregnancy and removing miscarriage prevention as an approved indication — effectively a regulatory consensus within months of publication.
- first_regulation_year: 1971
- first_regulation_country: United States
- first_regulation_desc: FDA Drug Bulletin (November 1971) added pregnancy as a contraindication to DES labeling, removed miscarriage prevention as an approved indication, and required all DES manufacturers to update labeling. This was a binding labeling change that effectively withdrew DES for pregnancy use, though the drug remained available for other indications (prostate cancer, menopausal symptoms).
- us_regulation_year: 1971
- us_regulation_desc: FDA Drug Bulletin November 1971 — contraindicated DES in pregnancy, removed miscarriage prevention indication. FDA ordered withdrawal of 25mg and 100mg tablets on February 5, 1975 (effective February 18, 1975). Full FDA withdrawal of all DES approval came in 2000.

## Judgment calls

**Early evidence year (1971 vs. 1953):** The ticket flags two candidate dates. The 1953 Dieckmann study proved DES was *ineffective* for preventing miscarriage — a finding about efficacy, not safety. The 1971 Herbst paper established the *harm* (cancer in DES daughters). Per the plan.md definition, early_evidence_year captures "the first credible scientific paper establishing the link between the hazard and the harm." The harm is cancer, not inefficacy. Used 1971. The 1953 inefficacy finding is documented in notes as critical context: DES was prescribed for 18 more years after being shown not to work.

**Nature of the 1971 FDA action:** The November 1971 FDA Drug Bulletin was not a market withdrawal or outright ban. It was a binding labeling change: pregnancy was added as a contraindication, and miscarriage prevention was removed as an approved indication. Under FDA regulatory authority, this is an enforceable labeling requirement — manufacturers could not legally market DES for pregnancy use after this change. While the drug remained available for other indications (prostate cancer, lactation suppression, menopausal symptoms), the specific pregnancy use was effectively withdrawn. This counts as a "product withdrawal" under the plan's category definitions because a specific use of a specific drug was removed.

**Speed of response:** The lag from evidence to regulation is 0 years (April 1971 → November 1971, approximately 7 months). This is one of the fastest regulatory responses in the dataset, comparable to thalidomide. The speed is likely explained by: (a) dramatic, visible harm (young women with rare cancers), (b) a viable substitute existed (no substitute needed — the drug didn't work for its intended purpose), and (c) the pharmaceutical industry did not mount a sustained opposition campaign once the cancer evidence emerged.

**Industry opposition rated "moderate":** Before 1971, the pharmaceutical industry actively promoted DES for pregnancy use despite the 1953 evidence of inefficacy. Carson Frailey, VP of the American Drug Manufacturers Association, was employed by DES manufacturers and lobbied the FDA for approval, persuading 54 doctors to write in support. However, once the cancer link was published in 1971, there was no organized, sustained campaign to delay the contraindication. This contrasts with "strong" opposition cases like tobacco or lead where industry fought for decades after harm was established. The opposition was front-loaded (marketing a drug that didn't work) rather than post-evidence (fighting regulation after harm was proven).

**Harm visibility rated "medium":** The harm was dramatically visible once it appeared (rare vaginal cancers in young women), but it was delayed by 15–25 years (daughters developed cancer in their teens and twenties from prenatal exposure in the 1940s–1960s). The delay between exposure and visible harm is what allowed DES to be prescribed for decades. Once the harm became visible, the causal link was clear and the regulatory response was fast.

**Substitute available rated "yes":** This is an unusual case. No pharmacological substitute was needed because the 1953 Dieckmann study had already proven DES was ineffective for preventing miscarriage. The "substitute" for DES in pregnancy was simply not prescribing it. There was no therapeutic gap to fill.

**European lag:** While the US acted in November 1971, European countries were significantly slower. The Netherlands added a warning label in 1972 and banned DES for pregnancy in 1975. France banned it in 1977. Some European countries continued prescribing DES to pregnant women into the early 1980s. This 6–12 year gap between US and European action is noteworthy, as it's the reverse of the usual pattern (US typically lags behind other OECD countries). Used the US as first_regulation_country since it acted first in this case.

**Scale of exposure:** An estimated 5–10 million Americans (mothers and children) were exposed between 1940 and 1971. Effects extended to DES daughters (vaginal/cervical cancer, breast cancer, fertility problems), DES sons (testicular abnormalities), and possibly DES granddaughters (transgenerational epigenetic effects). The total global exposure was larger, with significant use in France, the Netherlands, and the UK.

**The 18-year sub-lag (1953–1971):** Perhaps the most striking aspect of this case is the 18-year period during which DES continued to be prescribed after being proven ineffective. The drug was shown not to work in 1953, yet prescribing continued until a separate and more severe harm (cancer) was discovered in 1971. This sub-lag illustrates that evidence of inefficacy alone is insufficient to change medical practice — it took evidence of active harm to trigger action.
