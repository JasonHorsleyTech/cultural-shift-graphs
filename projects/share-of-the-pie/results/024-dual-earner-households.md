# Result: Dual-Earner Household Share, 1960–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** BLS, "Women in the Labor Force: A Databook," Table 19/23 — Married-couple families by number and relationship of earners, 1967–2020. Series from CPS Annual Social and Economic Supplement (ASEC). Definition: percentage of married-couple families where both husband and wife had earnings from work during the year.
- **Secondary source:** BLS, "Employment Characteristics of Families" (ECOF) annual news releases, Table 2. Definition: percentage of married-couple families where both husband and wife were employed during the CPS reference week. Available 2000–2025.
- **Tertiary source (pre-1967 estimates):** BLS historical labor force participation rates for married women (husband present), used as a proxy for pre-1967 dual-earner share estimates.
- **Date accessed:** 2026-05-01

## Methodology
- **Primary measure (earners):** A married-couple family is classified as "dual-earner" if both husband and wife had any earnings from work during the calendar year. This is from the CPS ASEC (March supplement), published in BLS "Women in the Labor Force" databooks (Table 19/23).
- **Universe:** All married-couple families, regardless of age, including those with no earners (retirees). The denominator is total married-couple families.
- **Secondary measure (employed):** Both spouses employed during the survey reference week. From monthly CPS, published in ECOF annual releases. This measure is consistently 5–8 percentage points below the "earners" measure because it requires employment at a specific point in time rather than any earnings during the year.
- **Pre-1967:** No direct BLS earner-status data. Estimates derived from married women's labor force participation rate (husband present), adjusted upward ~4–5 pp to account for women who worked part of the year but were not in the labor force at the survey date.
- **Known methodology changes:** CPS redesign in 2014 (minimal impact on this series); COVID-19 stimulus income in 2020 does not affect this measure since it tracks employment/earnings from work, not transfers.

## Gaps and Caveats

- **Pre-1967 (1960–1966): Low confidence.** No direct BLS dual-earner data. Estimates derived from married women's LFPR (husband present) with a ~4–5 pp upward adjustment to approximate the annual earner concept. These should be treated as illustrative of the trend, not precise values.
- **1967–2020 interpolated values: Medium confidence.** The trajectory shape is well-established (rapid rise 1967–1990, peak 1996, gradual decline post-2000), but specific annual values between confirmed benchmarks are interpolated. True values from Table 23 may differ by ±1–2 pp from interpolated estimates.
- **Confirmed benchmarks: High confidence.** The following are directly from BLS publications: 1967 (43.6%), 1978 (~50%), 1996 (60.4%), 1999 (~60%), 2000 (53.2% employed), 2007 (58%), 2008 (51.4% employed), 2011 (53%), 2020 (52.5%), and the Employment Characteristics of Families series 2008–2025.
- **Two definitions:** The primary "earners" series (Table 23/19) counts families where both spouses had ANY earnings during the year. The secondary "employed" series (ECOF) counts families where both were employed during the reference week. The earners measure runs ~4–8 pp higher. The TypeScript field `dualEarnerHouseholdShare` should use the earners series.
- **2020 anomaly:** The pandemic caused a sharp dip in the "employed" series (45.5%) due to temporary layoffs. The "earners" series showed a smaller dip (52.5%) because many workers had some earnings before being laid off.
- **Post-2020 (2021–2024): Medium-low confidence.** The BLS Women in the Labor Force Databook (earners series) has not been updated past 2020. Post-2020 earner values are estimated by adding the historical gap (~4 pp) to the confirmed ECOF "employed" series values.
- **Trend interpretation:** The rise from ~36% (1960) to 60.4% (1996) represents one of the most significant structural changes in the American economy. The post-1996 decline reflects both aging of the married-couple population (more retirees in the denominator) and slight declines in prime-age women's LFP in the 2000s.

## Notes for Graph Phase

The `dualEarnerHouseholdShare` field should use the primary "earners" series values from the first table. This metric is crucial context: household median income stayed closer to GDP per capita partly because families added a second earner. The gap between single-earner and dual-earner household income trajectories reveals how much of the "share of pie" was maintained through added labor supply rather than rising per-worker compensation.

Key narrative points:
- 1960–1990: Rapid rise from ~36% to ~58% — families "running to stand still"
- 1996 peak at 60.4% — the dual-earner strategy is maxed out
- Post-2000 plateau at 52–53% — no further gains possible from this margin
- This means post-2000 stagnation in median household income relative to GDP cannot be masked by adding workers; it's genuine per-worker share erosion
