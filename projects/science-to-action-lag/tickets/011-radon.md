# Ticket 011: Radon → Lung Cancer

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that indoor radon exposure causes lung cancer to binding regulation or enforceable guidelines for radon mitigation.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Lung cancer in uranium miners was observed as early as the 1500s (Schneeberg mines, "mountain sickness"). Modern radon-cancer link: studies of uranium miners in the 1950s-1960s established the causal relationship. The connection to RESIDENTIAL radon (not just mining) came later — National Academy of Sciences BEIR IV report (1988)?

2. **Consensus date** — When did a major body formally state residential radon causes lung cancer? NAS BEIR reports, WHO radon handbook, EPA assessments. IARC classified radon as a Group 1 carcinogen.

3. **First OECD regulation** — Radon regulation is unusual — most countries use "action levels" (recommended maximum concentrations) rather than binding laws. Some countries mandate radon testing in schools or workplaces. Sweden has building codes requiring radon-resistant construction. What's the earliest BINDING regulation (not just an advisory action level)?

4. **US regulation** — EPA set a recommended action level of 4 pCi/L in 1988 (advisory, not binding). The Indoor Radon Abatement Act (1988) set a long-term goal but isn't binding. Some states have mandatory testing requirements. Is there any binding FEDERAL regulation?

5. **Industry opposition** — Less organized than most hazards. No single industry defends radon (unlike tobacco or lead). Real estate industry has some interest in avoiding mandatory disclosure.

## Where to look

1. NAS BEIR IV (1988) and BEIR VI (1999) reports on radon
2. EPA radon program history
3. WHO Radon Handbook (2009)
4. IARC Monograph on radon
5. Indoor Radon Abatement Act of 1988 (42 USC 7401 note)
6. European radon regulations (EU Council Directive 2013/59/Euratom)

## What "good data" looks like

- Clear distinction between mining/occupational radon evidence (1950s-60s) and RESIDENTIAL radon evidence (1980s-2000s). Use residential evidence dates since that's the general population exposure.
- Honest characterization of whether any OECD country has truly binding radon regulation or whether it's all advisory/recommended action levels. If regulation is mostly advisory, regulation_status may be "in-progress" or the binding regulation is building codes.
- EU Directive 2013/59/Euratom requires member states to set reference levels — this may be the first binding international regulation.

## Known pitfalls

- Radon is a natural hazard, not an industrial product — there's no one to ban and no one profiting from radon exposure. The regulatory model (building codes, testing mandates) is fundamentally different from product bans.
- The distinction between "advisory action level" and "binding regulation" is blurry for radon. Document the reasoning for what counts.
- Category will likely be "exposure-limit" but the enforcement mechanism is unusual.
