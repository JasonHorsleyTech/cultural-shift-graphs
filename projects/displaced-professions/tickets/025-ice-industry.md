# Ticket 025: Ice Harvesters / Ice Deliverers

## Event
The displacement of the natural ice harvesting and ice delivery industry by mechanical refrigeration, ~1900–1940. A gradual erosion that took ~40 years as refrigerators slowly replaced iceboxes in American homes.

## Mechanism
**Gradual erosion** (tech_tool)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track US ice industry employment from ~1880 to ~1950:

- Ice harvesting: cutting and storing natural ice from lakes/rivers. Peaked in the late 19th century.
- Ice delivery: icemen delivering blocks to homes and businesses. Peaked ~1920s.
- Total industry employment at peak: estimate (ice harvesters + ice plant workers + delivery drivers + retail)
- Key dates: first commercial ice plant (1868), electric home refrigerator introduced (~1913), GE Monitor-Top (1927, first mass-market fridge), by 1944 ~85% of US homes had refrigerators
- By 1950: natural ice harvesting essentially extinct; manufactured ice delivery sharply reduced

**Source hierarchy:**
1. US Census occupational data via IPUMS (look for "ice dealer," "ice man," "ice harvester")
2. Historical Statistics of the United States — manufacturing data for ice
3. Rees, "Refrigeration Nation: A History of Ice, Appliances, and Enterprise in America" (2013)
4. Weightman, "The Frozen-Water Trade" (2003) — the natural ice industry
5. US Ice Manufacturers Association records (if accessible)

### Where They Went
- General delivery / trucking (the same physical skill, different product)
- Manufactured ice plant operation (some natural ice workers moved to ice plants)
- Other delivery services (milk, coal, laundry)
- General manual labor / construction

**Source hierarchy:**

3. Industry histories

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Ice companies that built or bought ice manufacturing plants, transitioning from harvesting to manufacturing. Companies that added cold storage and refrigeration services.
- **False movers:** Companies that invested in better natural ice harvesting equipment as mechanical ice was already taking over.
- **Deniers:** The belief that natural ice was superior to "artificial" ice. Resistance to electric refrigerators on grounds of unreliability and cost.
- **Survival pattern:** Moving from the product (ice) to the service (cold) — cold storage, refrigerated transport.

### Unique Texture
- Frederic Tudor, the "Ice King" — built a global empire shipping New England ice to India, the Caribbean, and the American South
- The 3-stage disruption: natural ice → manufactured ice → home refrigerators. The manufactured ice industry killed natural ice, then was itself killed by refrigerators.
- The "iceman" as an American archetype — Norman Rockwell, cultural memory of a vanished profession
- Pollution concerns: natural ice from increasingly polluted urban lakes drove the shift to manufactured ice *before* home refrigeration
- The icebox: a piece of furniture (still called "icebox" colloquially) that became obsolete

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Natural ice production volume over time (tons harvested)
- Refrigerator adoption rates in US homes (% of households with refrigerators 1920–1950)
- Employment estimates for ice industry at peak and during decline
- The 3-stage disruption documented with dates
- Price comparison: cost of ice delivery vs. cost of operating a refrigerator
