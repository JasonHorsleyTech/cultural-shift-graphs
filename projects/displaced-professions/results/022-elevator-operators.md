# Elevator Operators

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Elevator operator / elevator attendant
- **Country/Region:** United States (concentrated in NYC, Chicago, Philadelphia)
- **Peak Year:** 1950 (census enumeration)
- **Peak Workforce:** 114,473 (Atkinson & Wu, "False Alarmism," ITIF 2017, using US Census/IPUMS data)
- **Primary Mechanism:** gradual_erosion
- **Cause Tags:** tech_tool, regulatory
- **Killing Event:** Automatic push-button elevators (Otis Autotronic system, 1948–1950s) replaced human operators building-by-building over three decades, catalyzed by the 1945 NYC elevator strike which demonstrated buildings could function without operators and motivated investment in automation.

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1950 (automation of new installations began; existing buildings retained operators for years)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~8 (est. ~1958 — rapid adoption of Autotronic in new buildings)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** ~12 (est. ~1962 — building modernization wave)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** ~18 (est. ~1968 — most commercial buildings converted)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** ~25 (est. ~1975 — luxury/legacy holdouts)
- **Decline Shape:** s_curve (slow start as technology proved itself, rapid middle phase as building owners converted en masse for cost savings, long tail of holdouts in luxury/historic buildings)
- **Current Status:** extinct (a handful of novelty/prestige positions remain in luxury NYC residences and theme parks, but not tracked as an occupation)

## Surplus Capture

- **Capital owners:** 50% — Building owners captured massive savings. Otis projected $7,000/car/year in labor cost savings. A 10-elevator building saved ~$70,000/year (1950s dollars). In 1956 alone, Otis did $20 million in conversion work. Real estate operating costs dropped significantly.
- **Consumers:** 30% — Tenants and building users benefited from faster service (no waiting for operators), 24-hour elevator availability, and some pass-through of reduced operating costs in commercial rents. Retail/residential tenants gained self-service convenience.
- **Adjacent workers:** 10% — Elevator installers, mechanics, and repairers gained a growing market for automatic systems. The IUEC (elevator constructors union) grew as installation and maintenance demand increased.
- **Government/public:** 10% — Municipal building codes were simplified. Public buildings reduced staffing costs. Safety improved over time as automatic systems became more reliable than human-operated ones.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
1. **Building "starters" in NYC (1950s–1960s):** During the transition, many buildings retained former operators as lobby attendants who directed foot traffic to the correct bank of elevators, managed rush-hour flows, and provided building security. This "starter" role was a deliberate bridge — same workers, different function — pioneered by large NYC commercial landlords who needed to manage the human side of the automation transition.
2. **BSEIU/SEIU leadership pivot (1960s):** The union itself adapted. Recognizing that elevator operator positions were terminal, BSEIU leadership pivoted to organizing other building service workers — janitors, security guards, cleaners. In 1968, the union renamed itself the Service Employees International Union (SEIU), dropping "Building" from the name entirely to reflect its diversified membership base. Today SEIU has 2+ million members.

### False Movers
1. **The 1945 NYC elevator strike:** 15,000 elevator operators, doormen, and porters struck for better pay and against proposed cuts. The 6-day strike cost NYC an estimated $100 million, prevented 1.5 million office workers from reaching their desks, and halted mail delivery ($8M/day in lost federal tax revenue). The strikers won their immediate demands — but the strike's lasting effect was to demonstrate to building owners that buildings *could* function without human operators, and to motivate massive investment in automation. Within 5 years, Otis installed the first fully automated commercial elevator. The strike accelerated the very outcome it was meant to prevent.
2. **Safety-based building codes:** Many municipalities had building codes requiring human operators for passenger elevators. Operators and unions lobbied to preserve these codes as a safety measure. But as automatic systems proved safer than human-operated ones (fewer incidents per ride), the codes were systematically repealed through the 1950s–1960s, removing the last regulatory protection for the occupation.

### Deniers
- Building owners initially resisted automatic elevators on the grounds that "passengers can't be trusted to operate elevators." Otis had invented a working automatic elevator in 1900, but passengers would walk in, see no operator, and walk right back out. It took 50 years of incremental trust-building — including ad campaigns featuring children and grandmothers cheerfully pushing buttons — before the public fully accepted automatic elevators. (NPR, 2015)
- The Realty Advisory Board on Labor Relations initially tried to manage elevator operators' wages rather than eliminate the occupation, suggesting tiered pay scales based on the number of floors serviced. This attempt to optimize a dying model delayed but couldn't prevent automation.

### Survival Pattern
Workers who stayed within the building services ecosystem — pivoting to maintenance, security, or lobby/concierge roles within the same buildings and unions — fared best. Those who left the sector entirely faced downward mobility into general unskilled labor.

## Unique Texture

1. **The only occupation automation has ever fully eliminated.** Of 270 detailed occupations listed in the 1950 US Census, elevator operator is the sole one that no longer exists due to automation. Other disappeared occupations vanished due to demand loss (boardinghouse keepers) or technological obsolescence (telegraph operators), but elevator operators are the only case where the *task itself* was automated away. (James Bessen, Harvard; Quartz, 2017)

2. **The close-door button is a memorial to the elevator operator.** After the Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 mandated minimum door-open times (3–5 seconds), the "close door" button in most US elevators became a placebo — it does nothing for regular passengers. It exists purely to give riders the illusion of control in a windowless box that used to have a human in it. (New Yorker, 2008; Psychology Today, 2016)

3. **The 1945 strike was the most expensive backfire in labor history.** The NYC elevator strike cost $100 million in 6 days and stranded 1.5 million workers — but its lasting legacy was proving that buildings could function without operators, catalyzing the investment that destroyed the occupation within a generation. (Ancestry.com historical records; NPR, 2015)

4. **Automatic elevators existed for 50 years before anyone would use them.** Otis built a working automatic elevator in 1900. Passengers refused to ride without a human operator. It wasn't until the post-WWII labor crisis that manufacturers succeeded in normalizing the idea through safety features (emergency phones, alarms, stop buttons) and marketing campaigns. The gap between technical capability and social acceptance was half a century. (NPR, 2015; Inc.com)

5. **A handful of NYC luxury buildings still employ elevator operators as a prestige amenity.** In buildings like The Dakota and other prewar Upper East/West Side co-ops, white-gloved operators in manual elevators serve as a status signal — the human cost is the point. These positions pay $40,000–60,000/year and are among the last vestiges of a profession that once employed over 114,000 Americans. (Quora reports; NYC real estate coverage)

## Known Data Gaps

- **Decade-by-decade census figures between 1870 and 1950:** The ITIF report provides a chart (Figure 4) with data for every census year 1870–1990, but the actual numerical values for intermediate decades (1880, 1890, 1900, 1910, 1920, 1930) are read from a chart, not published as a table. The 1870 (497) and 1950 (114,473) endpoints are stated explicitly; intermediate values in the workforce timeline are estimates.
- **Post-1950 census counts:** The 1960, 1970, and 1980 figures are estimates based on automation adoption rates and contemporary accounts. The occupation was progressively absorbed into broader census categories, making precise counts difficult.
- **Individual-level income tracking:** No panel data tracks what specific former elevator operators earned in successor occupations. The "where they went" section relies on union membership transitions and concurrent growth in building service occupations.
- **Precise wage data 1945–1960:** BLS Occupational Wage Surveys from this era include elevator operators but the specific figures were not accessible through web search. The 1934 strike demand ($25/week) and 1966 Chicago rate ($2.50/hour) bracket the period, with intermediate values estimated.
- **Non-NYC geography:** Most historical accounts focus on New York City. Elevator operators in smaller cities, department stores, and government buildings are poorly documented.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium
- **Workforce numbers:** medium — Solid endpoints from ITIF/Census (1870 and 1950) and qualitative trajectory confirmed by multiple sources, but intermediate decades and post-1950 figures are estimates.
- **Income data:** low — Only two specific data points (1934 strike demands, 1966 Chicago union rate) with the rest estimated from occupational category averages. Income figures should be treated as order-of-magnitude.
- **Where-they-went:** medium — Inferred from union evolution (BSEIU → SEIU), concurrent growth in building service occupations, and the "starter" transition documented in NYC. No individual-level tracking available.
