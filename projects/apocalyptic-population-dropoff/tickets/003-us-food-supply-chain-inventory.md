# Ticket 003: US Food Supply Chain — Total Inventory and Depletion Timeline

## What to research

How much food exists in the US at any given moment, where it is, and how long it would last under various rationing/distribution assumptions.

## Specific data needed

1. **Retail grocery inventory** — Average store inventory in days of normal consumption. Number of grocery stores in US. Total retail food inventory.
2. **Warehouse/distribution center inventory** — Cold storage, dry goods warehouses, total capacity.
3. **Restaurant/institutional supply** — Restaurant food supply, school cafeterias, hospital kitchens, military mess.
4. **Strategic reserves** — Current state of the USDA commodity reserves. Bill Emerson Humanitarian Trust. Strategic grain reserves (or lack thereof).
5. **On-farm storage** — Grain elevators, silage, livestock feed stored on farms.
6. **Home pantry** — Average American household food supply in days. Mormon/prepper culture stockpiles as a separate category.
7. **Livestock as walking calories** — US cattle (94M), pigs (74M), chickens (518M) — caloric value if slaughtered, how long they last without feed.

**For each category, calculate:**
- Total calories available
- Days of feeding 335M people at 2000 cal/day
- Days at severe rationing (1200 cal/day)
- Accessibility problems (locked warehouses, refrigeration failure, distribution logistics)

## Where to look

1. **USDA ERS (Economic Research Service)** — food availability data, loss-adjusted food supply
2. **USDA NASS** — grain stocks reports, livestock inventory
3. **FMI (Food Marketing Institute)** — retail inventory data
4. **IFIC / industry trade publications** — supply chain logistics
5. **FEMA / DHS planning documents** — food security assessments
6. **Academic literature on food system resilience**

## Result format

Use the standard Key Data Points table. One row per food category with calories and days-of-supply as the units.

Include a summary section with total days of food supply under three assumptions:
- Perfect distribution, normal consumption
- Perfect distribution, severe rationing
- Realistic distribution (accounting for hoarding, spoilage, access)

## What good data looks like

- Total food supply estimate lands in the 30-120 day range (based on exploratory research). Anything outside this range needs justification
- Accounts for the 40% that requires refrigeration and will spoil within days of grid failure
- Distinguishes between "food exists" and "food is accessible to the average person"
- Livestock caloric value accounts for the fact that without feed, animals begin dying within weeks
