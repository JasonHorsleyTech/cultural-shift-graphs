# Result: Income Shares by Quintile and Top 5%, 1967–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** U.S. Census Bureau, Current Population Survey (CPS), Annual Social and Economic Supplement (ASEC), Historical Income Table H-2AR — "Share of Aggregate Income Received by Each Fifth and Top 5 Percent of Households (All Races)"
- **Direct download:** https://www2.census.gov/programs-surveys/cps/tables/time-series/historical-income-households/h02ar.xlsx
- **Date accessed:** 2026-04-30

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Share of aggregate pre-tax, pre-transfer money income received by each quintile of households and the top 5 percent.
- **Universe:** All households in the United States, all races. Households are ranked by total money income and divided into quintiles (each containing 20% of households).
- **Income concept:** Money income includes wages/salaries, self-employment income, interest, dividends, rents, Social Security, public assistance, and other cash income. It excludes capital gains, noncash benefits (employer health insurance, food stamps, housing subsidies), and tax payments.
- **Known methodology changes:**
  - **(12) 1967:** Implementation of a new CPS processing system.
  - **(13) 1971:** Introduction of 1970 Census-based population controls.
  - **(14) 1972:** Full implementation of 1970 Census weighting controls.
  - **(15) 1974:** Introduction of new money income questions.
  - **(16) 1974–1975:** Full implementation of revised income questions.
  - **(17) 1976:** Introduction of 1970 Census-based sample design.
  - **(18) 1979:** Introduction of 1980 Census-based population controls.
  - **(19) 1984:** Implementation of CPS computer-assisted interviewing.
  - **(20) 1985:** Introduction of revised income questions.
  - **(21) 1987:** Introduction of 1980 Census-based sample design.
  - **(22) 1992:** Introduction of 1990 Census-based population controls and revised income questions with higher topcode thresholds.
  - **(23) 1993:** Introduction of 1990 Census-based sample design with computer-assisted interviewing.
  - **(24) 1994:** Full implementation of 1990 Census-based sample design.
  - **(25) 1995:** Introduction of revised income questions with higher topcodes.
  - **(29) 1999:** Implementation of Census 2000-based population controls and sample design.
  - **(30) 2000:** Implementation of Census 2000-based processing.
  - **(35) 2004:** Introduction of Census 2000-based weighting.
  - **(36) 2009:** Introduction of 2010 Census-based population controls.
  - **(37) 2010:** Full implementation of 2010 Census population controls.
  - **(38) 2013 old:** Last year with pre-redesign ASEC processing.
  - **(39) 2013 new:** First year of redesigned CPS ASEC questions (income questions expanded and improved). This is a **major methodology break** — creates a discontinuity in the time series.
  - **(40) 2017 new:** Updated CPS ASEC processing system.
  - **(41) 2020:** COVID-19 pandemic impact on survey collection (mixed-mode data collection).

## Gaps and Caveats

- **No missing years.** Complete annual data from 1967 through 2024 (58 rows, plus dual entries for 2013 and 2017 methodology breaks).
- **Quintile shares sum to ~100%.** Verified: all rows sum to 99.9–100.1% (rounding). The Top 5% is a subset of the Top 20%, not additive.
- **Two major methodology breaks produce dual-year entries:**
  - **2013:** The CPS ASEC was redesigned with expanded income questions. The new method (39) shows slightly more inequality than the old method (38) for the same year. For a continuous series: use old-method values through 2012 and new-method (39) values from 2013 onward.
  - **2017:** Updated CPS processing system. The new processing (40) shows more inequality than old processing for the same year. For a continuous series: use the pre-2017 values through 2016, and new-processing (40) values from 2017 onward.
- **1992–1993 jump:** The sharp increase in top-quintile share from 46.9% (1992) to 48.9% (1993) is largely a measurement artifact from CPS sample redesign and revised income questions with higher topcode thresholds (footnotes 22→23). The real trend was upward, but the magnitude of this one-year jump overstates the actual change.
- **Top-coding:** Census caps reported income at certain thresholds, compressing measured top-quintile and top-5% shares. The true concentration at the top is higher than these figures suggest. This is why CBO data (tickets 011–013) is preferred for distributional analysis — CBO adjusts for topcoding using IRS tax data.
- **Pre-tax, pre-transfer:** These shares reflect market income plus cash transfers (Social Security, public assistance) but exclude noncash benefits, employer health insurance, and tax effects. The after-tax, after-transfer distribution is less unequal.
- **2020 anomaly:** COVID-19 stimulus payments (Economic Impact Payments) are included in money income, while the pandemic also caused job losses. The net effect on shares was modest — the distribution barely changed from 2019.
- **Confidence:** High for 1967–2024. This is a well-established, continuously published Census series with consistent methodology (within the noted break periods).

## Key trends

1. **Bottom 80% lost share; top 20% gained.** The top quintile's share rose from 43.6% (1967) to 52.2% (2024), an increase of 8.6 percentage points. Every other quintile lost share.
2. **Bottom quintile most squeezed:** From 4.0% to 3.1% — a 22.5% relative decline.
3. **Middle quintile compressed:** From 17.3% to 13.9% — a 19.7% relative decline.
4. **Most concentration happened 1980–1993:** The top share went from 44.1% (1980) to 48.9% (1993), accounting for about 56% of the total 1967–2024 shift. Much of this reflects both real economic changes (deregulation, globalization, skill premium) and measurement changes (higher topcodes capturing more top-end income).
5. **Post-2000 plateau with slight drift:** The top share has fluctuated between ~49% and ~52% since 2000, with methodology changes accounting for some of the apparent increase.
