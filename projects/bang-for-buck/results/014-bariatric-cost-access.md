# Result: Bariatric Surgery — Cost, Insurance, and Access for Bob

## Summary

Bariatric surgery is one of the most cost-effective interventions for severe obesity when measured per QALY, but the upfront cost creates an enormous barrier for Bob. Without insurance, sleeve gastrectomy runs $14,500–$22,000 and gastric bypass $18,000–$35,000 — roughly equal to Bob's entire annual household income. With Medicaid (available in 48 states for bariatric coverage), out-of-pocket costs drop to near zero for the surgery itself, leaving only lifelong vitamin supplementation (~$700–$840/year) as the ongoing expense. The realistic path for Bob is through Medicaid or a heavily subsidized ACA marketplace plan, but even then he faces 6–12 months of mandatory pre-surgical requirements, 2–4 weeks of unpaid recovery time he likely can't afford, and a 41.6% chance of losing Medicaid coverage post-surgery (which jeopardizes follow-up care). Only 1% of clinically eligible patients actually receive bariatric surgery — the gap between clinical eligibility and real-world access is massive, and it's widest for Bob's bracket.

## QALY Evidence

*Full QALY analysis in ticket 013. Summary referenced here for completeness.*

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.08
- **Range:** 0.05 – 0.12 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** Borisenko et al. 2017; Patel et al. 2022; SOS Study (Sjöström et al.)
- **Study type:** Multiple cost-effectiveness analyses and long-term cohort studies
- **Population studied:** Adults with BMI ≥35–40, mean age 46–55
- **Population match to Bob:** Good — obesity prevalence ~35% in Bob's income bracket, comorbidity profile matches study populations
- **Key caveats:** Benefits front-loaded (years 1–3); some weight regain by years 5–10; mental health risks (suicide, alcohol use disorder) partially offset gains

## Cost Analysis (for Bob at ~$30k household income)

### Surgical Costs (Without Insurance)

| Procedure | Low | Average | High | Source |
|-----------|-----|---------|------|--------|
| Sleeve Gastrectomy | $10,000 | $14,500 | $25,000 | Multiple surgery centers, GoodRx, CareCredit |
| Roux-en-Y Gastric Bypass (RYGB) | $18,000 | $23,000 | $35,000 | ASMBS, Bariatric Surgery Source |

Cost breakdown components: surgeon fees $10,000–$20,000, hospital/facility fees $3,000–$10,000, anesthesia $1,000–$2,500.

### Pre-Surgical Costs (Gatekeeping)

Most insurers and many self-pay programs require a 3–6 month supervised weight management program before surgery:

| Item | Cost (Uninsured) | Notes |
|------|-------------------|-------|
| Monthly physician/dietitian visits (6 months) | $900–$1,800 | $150–$300/visit × 6 |
| Psychological evaluation | $150–$400 | Range from $150 (self-pay specialists) to $900+ (NYC); $200–$400 typical |
| Pre-op testing (blood work, EKG, imaging) | $500–$1,000 | Hospital labs charge more; freestanding labs cheaper |
| Nutritional counseling | $200–$400 | Often bundled with supervised weight loss visits |
| **Total pre-surgical** | **$1,750–$3,600** | **Midpoint: ~$2,200** |

### Post-Surgical Ongoing Costs (Annual)

| Item | Annual Cost (Uninsured) | Notes |
|------|-------------------------|-------|
| Bariatric multivitamin + supplements | $600–$840 | $50–$70/month, lifelong; NOT covered by insurance |
| Follow-up physician visits | $300–$600 | 4/year in year 1, then 1–2/year; $200–$350/visit uninsured |
| Annual lab panels | $200–$500 | CBC, CMP, iron, B12, vitamin D, folate, PTH, calcium |
| **Total ongoing annual** | **$1,100–$1,940** | **Midpoint: ~$1,300** |

Note: Protein supplements and dietary changes (higher-quality food for restricted diet) add ~$30–$50/month but overlap with normal food budget and are not counted as medical costs.

### Total Cost Calculation

**Without Insurance (Primary Figure)**
- One-time: Surgery ($20,000 mid for sleeve) + Pre-surgical ($2,200) = **$22,200**
- Ongoing annual: **$1,300**
- Startup amortized over 3 years: $22,200 / 3 = **$7,400/year**
- **Total annual cost (best estimate): $8,700**
- **Cost range: $7,100 – $10,500**

**With Medicaid**
- Surgery: $0–$100 copay (covered in 48 states)
- Pre-surgical program: covered
- Vitamins/supplements: NOT covered (~$700–$840/year)
- Follow-up visits: covered or minimal copay ($0–$50/year)
- Lab work: covered
- **Total annual cost: ~$800–$1,000/year**

**With ACA Marketplace (Silver CSR plan at ~200% FPL)**
- Surgery: out-of-pocket max ~$3,000–$4,000 (hitting annual OOP limit)
- Pre-surgical: covered after deductible
- Ongoing: vitamins ($700) + visit copays ($100–$200) = ~$900/year
- Amortized over 3 years: ($3,500 / 3) + $900 = ~$2,067/year
- **Total annual cost: ~$2,000–$2,500/year**

### Insurance Pathways for Bob

| Scenario | Likelihood for Bob | Surgery Cost | Annual Ongoing |
|----------|-------------------|--------------|----------------|
| Medicaid (expansion state) | ~30–35% of bracket | $0–$100 | $800–$1,000 |
| ACA marketplace (subsidized) | ~30–35% of bracket | $3,000–$4,000 OOP | $900–$1,200 |
| Employer insurance | ~10–15% of bracket | $2,000–$6,000 OOP | $500–$1,000 |
| Uninsured | ~20–27% of bracket | $17,000–$25,000 | $1,100–$1,940 |

Critical finding: At $30k household income, Bob is likely at ~195% FPL (single) or ~117–147% FPL (family of 2–3). In Medicaid expansion states (40 states + DC), a family of 3 at $30k qualifies for Medicaid. A single individual at $30k does not (above 138% FPL threshold) but qualifies for heavily subsidized ACA marketplace coverage with Cost Sharing Reductions.

- **Annual direct cost:** $1,300/year (ongoing medical only)
- **One-time startup cost:** $22,200 (surgery + pre-surgical, without insurance)
- **Startup cost amortized over 3 years:** $7,400/year
- **Total annual cost (best estimate):** $8,700 (without insurance); $2,200 (with ACA); $900 (with Medicaid)
- **Cost range:** $900 – $10,500 (depending on insurance scenario)
- **With insurance (if applicable):** $900–$2,500/year
- **Without insurance:** $8,700/year (amortized)
- **Cost notes:** Without insurance, bariatric surgery costs roughly 73% of Bob's annual gross income as a one-time expense — effectively inaccessible without financing or insurance. The realistic path for most Bobs is Medicaid or ACA marketplace coverage. The mid estimate for the graph uses the uninsured amortized figure per plan.md conventions, but the with-insurance figures are more representative of actual access.

## Time Commitment

- **Hours per week:** 2.0 (averaged over first 2 years: meal prep for restricted diet ~1 hr/wk, vitamin regimen ~0.5 hr/wk, follow-up appointments amortized ~0.5 hr/wk; decreases to ~1 hr/wk after year 2)
- **Category:** Medium (1-3 hr/wk)
- **Pre-surgical phase:** Monthly appointments for 6 months (~2 hrs/month including travel)
- **Recovery:** 2–4 weeks off work (sleeve: 2 weeks typical; RYGB: 3–4 weeks; physical labor jobs: up to 6 weeks)

## Access and Barriers

- **Activation energy:** High
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - **Financial:** Surgery cost equals ~73% of annual income without insurance; even with Medicaid, 2–4 weeks unpaid recovery is $1,150–$2,300 in lost wages (at $15/hr)
  - **Insurance gatekeeping:** 6–12 month mandatory supervised weight loss program delays surgery by up to a year; 25% of patients face initial insurance denial
  - **Post-surgery insurance loss:** 41.6% of Medicaid bariatric patients lost coverage within 1 year of surgery (in non-expansion states); 49% of those had NO coverage for follow-up care
  - **Geographic:** Bariatric surgery centers concentrated in metro areas; rural Bobs face travel burden for monthly pre-op visits and post-op follow-ups
  - **BMI threshold:** Must have BMI ≥40 or ≥35 with comorbidities — not all obese Bobs qualify
  - **Recovery time:** 2–4 weeks off work; FMLA only applies to employers with 50+ employees; many low-income workers lack paid sick leave or short-term disability
  - **Utilization gap:** Only 1% of clinically eligible patients actually receive bariatric surgery (ASMBS)
  - **Lifetime commitment:** Lifelong vitamin supplementation, dietary restrictions, and follow-up monitoring required; non-compliance leads to nutritional deficiencies

## Side Effects and Risks

*Detailed in ticket 013. Cost-relevant risks summarized here:*

- **Revision surgery:** 1.5–2.9% at 5 years; cost $25,000–$33,000 without insurance
- **Nutritional deficiency complications:** Require additional specialist visits and treatment if supplements are skipped (common in uninsured patients)
- **Mental health:** Elevated suicide risk (19.4/100,000 patient-years) and alcohol use disorder (8–23%) may require additional psychiatric care costs
- **Dumping syndrome (RYGB):** 20–50% incidence; not costly but affects quality of life and dietary compliance

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate:** 0.62 (follow-up appointment attendance)
- **Source:** PMC11679841 (2024 systematic review); SOARD 2018 systematic review
- **Notes:** The anatomical change is permanent (100% "adherence" to surgery itself), but post-operative behavioral compliance — dietary guidelines (48% poor adherence), supplement regimen, follow-up visits — is where patients fail. Weight regain affects ~50% to some degree. Employment status correlates with follow-up adherence (65.6% employed vs 50% unemployed). The 41.6% post-op Medicaid coverage loss directly undermines follow-up adherence for Bob's bracket. Vitamin supplement compliance drops when patients bear the full cost out-of-pocket ($600–$840/year is ~2.8% of Bob's gross income).

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 1
- **Tier 1** = Multiple RCTs or high-quality meta-analyses, Bob-adjacent populations
- **Key studies:**
  - ASMBS Access to Care Fact Sheet — utilization and insurance denial data
  - FAIR Health surgical cost database — procedure-level cost data
  - Funk et al. 2017 "Loss of Medicaid Insurance After Successful Bariatric Surgery" (PMC5741532) — post-op insurance loss data
  - Wang et al. 2013 — cost per QALY: $6,600 (RYGB), $6,200 (LAGB)
  - ASMBS cost-effectiveness ratios: $5,000–$16,100/QALY (women), $10,000–$35,600/QALY (men)
- **Limitations:**
  - Cost data for uninsured patients is scattered across facility-specific pricing; no single authoritative source
  - Medicaid coverage rules vary widely by state — 48 states cover bariatric surgery but with different requirements
  - Pre-surgical program costs rarely reported in literature (patient-borne, not tracked by insurers)
  - Post-op Medicaid loss study (Funk 2017) was from non-expansion states pre-ACA expansion; situation has improved but the dynamic still exists

## Confidence Level

**High for cost data, moderate for Bob-specific access.** Surgical procedure costs are well-documented across multiple sources with consistent ranges. Insurance coverage rules are publicly available but state-dependent. The main uncertainty is in Bob's specific insurance scenario — at $30k household income, he straddles the Medicaid/ACA marketplace boundary depending on family size and state of residence, which creates a wide cost range ($900–$10,500/year). The post-surgery Medicaid coverage loss finding (Funk et al. 2017) is a critical insight for Bob's bracket but comes from pre-expansion data. Ongoing costs (vitamins, follow-up) are well-established. The 1% utilization rate (ASMBS) underscores that the theoretical cost-effectiveness of bariatric surgery is irrelevant for most Bobs — the access barriers are the story.
