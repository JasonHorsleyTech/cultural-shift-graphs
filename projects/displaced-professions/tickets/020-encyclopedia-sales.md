# Ticket 020: Encyclopedia Salespeople

## Event
The extinction of door-to-door encyclopedia sales, driven first by CD-ROMs (Encarta, 1993) and then by Wikipedia (2001). A platform shift that didn't just displace workers — it eliminated the entire business model of selling expensive reference sets to households.

## Mechanism
**Tipping point** (tech_platform)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track encyclopedia sales employment from ~1970 to ~2012:

- Peak: difficult to pin down. Britannica alone had ~2,300 US sales staff at peak (verify). World Book, Funk & Wagnalls, Compton's, Americana — each had sales forces. Total industry may have been 10,000–20,000+ (many part-time or commission-only).
- Key dates: Encarta launched (1993, $99 CD-ROM vs. $1,500+ print Britannica), Britannica drops door-to-door sales (1996), Wikipedia launched (2001), Encarta discontinued (2009), Britannica stops print edition (2012)
- The decline was fast: from dominant information product to irrelevant within ~15 years

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Britannica corporate records / press releases (sales force size)
2. Direct Selling Association reports (encyclopedia segment)
3. Shane, "Encyclopedia Britannica" Harvard Business School case study (various editions)
4. Newspaper/magazine reporting on encyclopedia industry collapse
5. BLS data for SOC 41-9091 (Door-to-Door Sales Workers) — partial overlap

### Income Data
- Encyclopedia sales were often commission-only or low-base-plus-commission
- Top salespeople earned well; median was likely modest
- Britannica sets sold for $1,500–2,200; World Book for $500–1,000. Commissions of 30–50% on sales.
- As sales volumes dropped, even good salespeople couldn't make a living

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Direct Selling Association compensation data
2. Britannica company data (if available via case studies)
3. BLS wage data for door-to-door sales workers

### Where They Went
- Other direct sales (insurance, home improvement, telecom)
- General retail sales
- Many were part-time or supplemental income — may have simply stopped
- Some pivoted to selling Britannica online subscriptions (much lower revenue per sale)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS employment data for direct sales occupations
2. Direct Selling Association industry reports

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** World Book's pivot to school/library institutional sales. Britannica's (eventual) online subscription model.
- **False movers:** Britannica's initial refusal to bundle their content with PCs or go digital — they feared cannibbalizing $500M+ in print revenue. By the time they went online, Wikipedia had made encyclopedias free.
- **Deniers:** Britannica CEO: "The Britannica has no competitor" (early 1990s). The belief that a free, user-edited encyclopedia could never match expert-written content.
- **Survival pattern:** Institutional sales (schools/libraries) survived longer than consumer sales. Brand value mattered for the transition.

### Unique Texture
- The price comparison: Britannica $1,500 vs. Encarta $99 vs. Wikipedia $0. The most extreme price disruption in the dataset.
- Encarta killed the old model, then Wikipedia killed Encarta. Two disruptions in 15 years.
- Door-to-door encyclopedia sales as a cultural institution — a rite of passage for many Americans, now completely gone
- The "Britannica has no competitor" quote as a canonical example of disruption blindness
- Encyclopedia Britannica's survival (it's still a company!) through radical reinvention — from 32-volume set to online service
