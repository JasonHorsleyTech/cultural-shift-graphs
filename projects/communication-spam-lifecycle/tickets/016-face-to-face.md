# 016: Face-to-Face Communication

**System:** In-person verbal communication
**Time range:** All of recorded history
**Expected data quality:** Speculative — sociological studies, historical analysis, survey data

## Research task

This is the dark kicker — the punchline of the entire graph. Every other communication system follows the same arc: clean → spam → dead. The question is whether face-to-face communication, the one system we assume is immune, is following the same trajectory.

Define "face-to-face spam" as: conversations where one party is acting as an unwitting (or witting) mouthpiece for an external message — religious proselytizing, political movement talking points, MLM sales pitches, personal brand performance, virtue signaling, humble bragging, or otherwise delivering content that serves an agenda rather than genuine human connection.

## Historical "spam spikes" to research

- **Religious movements:** Great Awakenings (1730s, 1800s, 1870s, 1960s-70s), door-to-door proselytizing (Jehovah's Witnesses, Mormon missionaries), Spanish Inquisition, Salem witch trials. What % of social interaction in these communities was dominated by religious messaging?
- **Political movements:** French Revolution, McCarthyism, Cultural Revolution, modern political polarization. Periods where political ideology dominated daily conversation.
- **Commercial:** Traveling salesmen era (1800s-1950s), Tupperware parties (1950s), MLM boom (Amway 1959, Herbalife 1980, LuLaRoe 2010s). When did commercial pitches in social settings become culturally notable?
- **Modern personal brand era:** Social media behavior bleeding into meatspace — people performing their Instagram/LinkedIn persona in real life, treating conversations as content opportunities.

## Key years/eras to sample

- Ancient/medieval (baseline — what % of conversation was "spam"?)
- 1730s (First Great Awakening)
- 1800s (Second Great Awakening, traveling salesmen)
- 1870s (Third Great Awakening)
- 1920s (door-to-door sales golden age)
- 1950s (Tupperware parties, McCarthyism)
- 1960s (Fourth Great Awakening, counterculture, political activism)
- 1980s (MLM boom — Amway, Mary Kay)
- 1990s (self-help movement, political talk radio influence on conversation)
- 2000s (early social media, people starting to "perform" in person)
- 2010s (Instagram/influencer culture, political polarization, MLM resurgence via social media)
- 2020s (peak personal branding, political tribalism, AI-generated talking points)

## Data sources to check

- **Sociological surveys** on conversation quality and content (if they exist)
- **MLM participation statistics** — Direct Selling Association, FTC data. How many Americans are active MLM participants at any given time?
- **Religious affiliation and proselytizing data** — Pew Research, Gallup
- **Political polarization surveys** — Pew, ANES (American National Election Studies) — measuring how much political identity dominates social interaction
- **Time-use surveys** — American Time Use Survey (BLS) may have data on how people spend social time
- **Academic research** on conversational narcissism, self-promotion behavior
- **Social media behavior studies** that document online behavior bleeding into offline
- **Historical accounts** of social dynamics during Great Awakenings, McCarthyism, etc.

## Dimensions to collect

1. **Spam percentage** — This is the hardest to quantify. Estimate: what % of an average person's daily face-to-face conversations involve someone delivering an "agenda" (religious, political, commercial, self-promotional) rather than genuine personal communication? Use survey data, participation rates, and historical analysis as proxies.
2. **Total message volume** — Average face-to-face conversations per day per person (relatively stable across history, maybe declining with digital displacement)
3. **Primary users** — 100% of population (until recent decades when digital started replacing face-to-face)
4. **Total users** — 100% (always)
5. **User cost per message** — $0 (always free, just proximity)
6. **User time per message** — varies, but face-to-face has the highest time investment per message of any system
7. **Infrastructure cost per message** — $0 (no infrastructure needed)
8. **Platform ARPU** — N/A (no platform)
9. **Barrier to reach** — physical proximity (the ultimate barrier — you have to be in the same room)
10. **Reach per message** — one-to-one or one-to-few
11. **Gatekeeping** — gated (social norms, physical proximity, you can walk away)
12. **Years to spam saturation** — has it ever crossed 50%? That's the question. Probably not on average, but possibly during specific historical moments in specific communities.
13. **Regulatory response** — harassment laws, restraining orders, "no soliciting" signs, anti-proselytizing ordinances
14. **Defenses** — walking away, social norms against being "that guy," changing the subject, avoiding certain people/events

## Special notes

This ticket is inherently more speculative than the others, and that's okay. The point isn't to produce data as rigorous as the Cisco email spam reports. The point is to:

1. Show that the same phenomenon (communication channel → spam) applies even to the most fundamental human communication system
2. Identify historical spikes where face-to-face "spam" was notably high
3. Ask whether the current moment — personal branding, political polarization, MLM — represents a new high or just another historical spike

The graph should show face-to-face as a mostly flat line near the bottom (0-10% for most of history) with periodic spikes during religious/political movements, and an open question mark at the right edge. It's the contrast between this uncertain, low line and the dramatic mountains of the other systems that makes the visualization powerful.

Be transparent about what's measured vs. estimated. Use ranges rather than point estimates. Cite sociological research where it exists and be honest about where you're extrapolating.
