# Ticket 014: Arsenic in Drinking Water → Cancer

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that arsenic in drinking water causes cancer and other health effects to binding regulation setting enforceable drinking water standards for arsenic.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Arsenic toxicity has been known since antiquity, but the specific link between CHRONIC LOW-LEVEL arsenic exposure in drinking water and cancer is more recent. Key studies: Taiwanese studies from the 1960s-1970s (blackfoot disease, skin cancer), Bangladeshi/West Bengal groundwater contamination studies from the 1990s. What's the earliest credible epidemiological evidence for cancer from drinking water arsenic?

2. **Consensus date** — When did a major body formally state that arsenic in drinking water causes cancer at commonly-encountered levels? IARC classified arsenic as Group 1 carcinogen. WHO guideline of 10 ppb. NAS report? EPA review?

3. **First OECD regulation** — WHO guideline was 50 ppb for decades, reduced to 10 ppb in 1993. When did the first OECD country adopt a binding 10 ppb standard? The EU Drinking Water Directive (1998) set 10 ppb. The US adopted 10 ppb in 2001 (effective 2006).

4. **US regulation** — The original US arsenic MCL was 50 ppb (set in 1942, carried over into the Safe Drinking Water Act 1974). EPA revised to 10 ppb in January 2001 under Clinton, briefly suspended under Bush, reinstated October 2001. Effective 2006.

5. **Industry opposition** — Mining and smelting industries, plus small water systems that would bear compliance costs. The political fight in 2001 (Bush administration's attempted delay) is well-documented.

6. **Known data on arsenic exposure reduction** — US water system compliance data before and after the 10 ppb standard.

## Where to look

3. IARC Monograph on arsenic

## What "good data" looks like

- Focus on cancer risk from DRINKING WATER arsenic specifically, not occupational arsenic exposure
- The 50 ppb → 10 ppb transition is the key regulatory event — the question is whether 50 ppb counts as "meaningful regulation" or whether only 10 ppb does
- Political context of the 2001 US standard (Clinton → Bush → reinstatement) shows how regulatory action can be contested

## Known pitfalls

- Arsenic toxicity has been known for millennia (it's a famous poison). But chronic low-level exposure from drinking water causing cancer is a DIFFERENT claim from acute poisoning. Use the drinking water epidemiology evidence, not ancient knowledge of arsenic as a poison.
- The 50 ppb standard (1942/1974) WAS a binding regulation — but at a level now considered unsafe. If you use 1942 as the regulation date, the lag from evidence is negative (regulation preceded evidence). Use the science-based revision (10 ppb) instead.
- Category: exposure-limit (drinking water maximum contaminant level).
