## Judgment calls

**Early evidence year (1974):** Molina & Rowland's Nature paper is universally cited as the foundational scientific evidence. There was no significant prior work specifically linking CFCs to ozone destruction. Paul Crutzen's 1970 work on nitrogen oxides and ozone was related but did not address CFCs specifically. 1974 is unambiguous.

**Consensus year (1976 vs. 1985):** Used 1976 (NAS report) rather than 1985 (ozone hole discovery). The NAS report represents formal scientific consensus — it concluded the evidence was credible and recommended action within two years. The 1985 ozone hole discovery by Farman et al. was dramatic empirical confirmation that galvanized public/political will, but scientific consensus had already been established in 1976.

**First regulation (1978 national ban vs. 1987 Montreal Protocol):** Per ticket guidance, used the 1978 US aerosol ban as "first binding regulation" since it meaningfully restricted population exposure. The Montreal Protocol (1987) is the defining international treaty that comprehensively phased out all CFC uses globally, but the 1978 ban was the first binding action. This makes the CFC case appear very fast (4-year lag), which is accurate for the initial national response. The comprehensive international solution took 13 years from evidence.

**Industry opposition (moderate):** DuPont, the world's largest CFC manufacturer, initially fought the science aggressively — the chair of DuPont's board called the theory "a science fiction tale...a load of rubbish...utter nonsense." However, DuPont reversed position relatively quickly once (a) the science was confirmed by NAS in 1976, and (b) the company developed HFC/HCFC substitute patents. By the mid-1980s, DuPont supported regulation. This contrasts sharply with tobacco or fossil fuel industry opposition which persisted for decades. "Moderate" reflects the short-lived nature of organized opposition.

**Substitute available (yes):** HFCs and HCFCs were commercially viable drop-in replacements for CFCs. DuPont held key patents on substitutes, which actually incentivized the company to support the phase-out. These substitutes later turned out to be potent greenhouse gases (a "regrettable substitution" addressed by the 2016 Kigali Amendment), but at the time they enabled the political deal.

**Harm visibility (medium):** Before 1985, ozone depletion was entirely theoretical and invisible — you cannot see UV radiation or observe a gradual ozone thinning. However, the 1985 discovery of the Antarctic ozone hole provided a dramatic, visible signal that made the threat tangible. The ultimate harms (skin cancer, cataracts, crop damage) are delayed and statistical. "Medium" reflects the combination of invisible direct mechanism but eventually dramatic confirmation via the ozone hole.

**50% exposure reduction (1993):** Estimated from available data. Global CFC production peaked at ~1.1 million metric tons in 1987-1988. By 1995, production was 77% below peak (~253,000 tonnes). Assuming gradual decline through 1989-1990 (Montreal Protocol entered force January 1989) accelerating after the 1990 London Amendment (which mandated full phase-out by 2000) and the 1992 Copenhagen Amendment (which accelerated to phase-out by 1996), 50% of peak production was likely reached around 1992-1993. Used 1993 as a conservative estimate.

**Why this case matters for the graph:** CFCs/ozone is widely cited as the premier success story of science-to-regulation. The 4-year lag from evidence to first binding national regulation is among the fastest in the dataset. Key enabling factors: (1) small number of manufacturers, (2) viable substitutes available, (3) dramatic visible confirmation (ozone hole), (4) industry's economic incentive to switch. It provides a positive benchmark against which slower cases (tobacco, climate, PFAS) can be measured.
