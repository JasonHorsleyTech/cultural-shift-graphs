# Grandpa's Eggs

## Core question

For a basket of commodities that have existed continuously since the mid-20th century, how much does the *quality-equivalent* version cost in productivity-adjusted labor-hours compared to the cheapest available version — and how has that gap changed over time?

## Why it matters

When CPI says "eggs cost about the same," it's comparing a 2025 battery-cage, antibiotic-fed, conveyor-belt egg to a 1955 free-range, bug-fed, brown egg from a normal chicken and calling them the same product. They're not. Every commodity has been relentlessly optimized to reduce production cost — at the expense of quality, durability, animal welfare, worker conditions, and hidden downstream costs (data harvesting, planned obsolescence, subscription models). If you want the egg your grandfather actually bought, it's now the most expensive option at the fanciest store. That price difference is invisible to every inflation metric.

## The unit

Productivity-adjusted labor-hours. BLS publishes output-per-hour from 1947. One productivity unit = one hour of median labor weighted by the productivity index. This corrects for the fact that a 2025 worker produces roughly 3x more value per hour than a 1955 worker, so raw labor-hours would overstate how "cheap" things have gotten.

## Per commodity, two lines

- **Line A:** Cost of the cheapest available version (approximately what CPI tracks)
- **Line B:** Cost of the quality-equivalent version (the product grandpa actually got when he bought the default option)

The gap between the lines is the hidden inflation — the quality tax you pay to maintain a standard that no price index captures.

## Commodity candidates (~12 categories)

- **Food:** eggs, milk, bread, a cut of beef
- **Durable goods:** a bicycle, a television, a men's suit
- **Services:** medical stitches, a movie ticket, a semester of state college, a haircut
- **Shelter:** median rent or mortgage payment

## Key research challenge

Defining "quality-equivalent" objectively. For each commodity, the explore phase needs to establish:
1. What was the standard/default product in ~1950-1960?
2. What is the closest modern equivalent? (Pasture-raised heritage eggs, steel-frame single-speed bike, a dumb monitor, etc.)
3. Is there a meaningful quality difference, or did the product genuinely improve? (Cars probably improved. Eggs probably degraded. Both are valid findings.)

## Don't assume the thesis is right

Some commodities genuinely got better AND cheaper. Cars are safer, more reliable, more comfortable. Some clothing is better. Let the data go where it goes. The graph is only interesting if it's honest — the ones that break the pattern are what make the whole thing credible.

## Sidebar idea

"Things that used to be normal and are now effectively unavailable" — backyard chickens, a pet horse, single-income homeownership, a doctor who makes house calls. These are items whose cost went from near-zero to effectively infinite. They can't be graphed as a line, but they're a powerful annotation.

## Demographic note

Median data inherently captures racial/demographic convergence. Grandfather's white middle-class experience was above-median. Using median is honest. Consider percentile bands if the data supports it.
