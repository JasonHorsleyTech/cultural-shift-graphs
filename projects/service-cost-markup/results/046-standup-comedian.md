# Comedy — Stand-Up Comedian (Feature Act)

## Summary

Stand-up comedy at the club level is one of the most extractive industries in entertainment. Feature act pay has been frozen at roughly $75–100 per show since the mid-1980s — a rate that was literally won through a labor strike in 1979 and has barely moved since. Meanwhile, ticket prices have quintupled and drink prices have quadrupled. The comedian's share of total customer spend has collapsed from ~3% in the 1980s to ~1% today. The two-drink minimum, originally designed to keep the lights on, now generates more revenue per show than the entire talent budget.

## Methodology

**Worker definition:** The feature act — the mid-card comedian who performs 15–30 minutes per show. This is the "fundamental worker" of stand-up comedy: the road comic who does it for a living, distinguishable from the opener (often an unpaid or barely-paid amateur getting stage time) and the headliner (a draw-dependent star operating in a different economic tier). Feature act pay is the clearest signal of what the comedy industry values its working-class labor at.

**Worker compensation:** Feature act pay per show at a typical comedy club. Sources are remarkably consistent: $50–100 per show is the standard range at road clubs nationwide, and has been since the late 1980s. Multiple industry sources (Quartz, Mic, Comedy Hints, working comedians' accounts) confirm this rate has been essentially frozen for 35–40 years. Pre-1979, showcase clubs like The Comedy Store paid comedians nothing at all — the 1979 strike won a $25/set rate. During the 1980s comedy boom, feature act pay rose to $75–100 as clubs competed for talent. It has not meaningfully increased since.

**Customer cost:** Ticket price plus the two-drink minimum, which is functionally part of the admission price at most comedy clubs. Ticket prices estimated from industry sources and inflation-adjusted comparisons to other entertainment (movie tickets, concert tickets) for earlier decades. Modern ticket prices ($25–35 for a standard club show) are well-documented. Drink prices at comedy clubs run $8–12 per drink in 2025; the mandatory 2-drink minimum adds $16–24 to the customer's spend. Total per-person spend at a modern comedy club is approximately $50–55.

**Customers/Worker:** Full audience size, since the feature act performs for every person in the room. Typical comedy club capacity is 100–200 seats. During the 1980s comedy boom, clubs were larger and packed; during the early-1990s bust, many clubs closed and audiences shrank. Modern clubs average 150–200 seats at ~80% occupancy. These figures represent the audience at a single show — most clubs run 2 shows per night on weekends, but each show is a separate performance with a separate audience.

**Why this proxy and not others:** The alternative would be to use total talent cost for the entire lineup (opener + feature + headliner) divided by total audience spend. That approach yields ~3–4× the ratios shown here, since 3 comics share the stage. I chose the feature act alone because: (1) it isolates the "fundamental worker" — the person actually doing this as a full-time job, (2) it makes the ratio comparable to other single-worker industries in this dataset, and (3) the headliner's pay is highly variable (from $200 at a small club to $1M+ at an arena) and would distort the signal. The feature act's frozen pay is the real story.

**Pre-1979 note:** Before the 1979 Comedy Store strike, comedians at showcase clubs in LA and NYC were paid nothing. Club owners (notably Mitzi Shore at The Comedy Store) considered stage time to be compensation enough — "like going to college." The 0% ratio for 1975 is literal, not estimated.

## Notes

- **The $25-that-never-moved:** The Comedy Store's smaller room was reportedly still paying $25/set as of 2022 — the exact rate won in the 1979 strike. Adjusted for inflation, that $25 in 1979 dollars would be ~$110 in 2025 dollars. The feature act rate at road clubs ($75–100) has essentially been in the same nominal range since the mid-1980s.
- **The two-drink minimum is the real ticket price.** At many clubs, the drink minimum generates more revenue than the door. A 175-person audience at $22/person in drinks = $3,850 in beverage revenue vs. $5,250 in ticket revenue ($30/ticket). The club keeps nearly all of the drink revenue at 75%+ margins. This is why clubs can afford to pay comedians almost nothing — the comedian is a loss-leader for bar sales.
- **Touring headliners live in a different universe.** The Pollstar top 25 comedy tours averaged $82.49/ticket and $374K gross per show in 2024. Kevin Hart earns $1.5M+ per arena show. These numbers are completely disconnected from the club economy. A feature act earning $100/show would need to perform 15,000 shows to match one Kevin Hart arena date.
- **The 1980s comedy boom briefly helped.** The explosion of comedy clubs in the 1980s (from ~50 clubs in 1979 to ~300+ by 1988) created competition for talent and pushed feature pay up from $25 to $75–100. The subsequent bust in the early 1990s killed many clubs but didn't reduce pay below $75 — that floor has held ever since, even as everything else got more expensive.
- **Podcasting and self-produced shows are the escape hatch.** Comedians who build audiences through podcasts or social media can self-produce shows, rent venues, and keep 80–90% of ticket revenue. This inverts the club ratio entirely — but it requires an existing audience, making it inaccessible to the average feature act.
- **The 1975 zero is not an exaggeration.** Major LA and NYC showcase clubs literally did not pay performers before the 1979 strike. Mitzi Shore at The Comedy Store considered the stage time itself to be the compensation. Comedians like Jay Leno, David Letterman, and Tom Dreesen walked the picket line to win the right to be paid at all.
