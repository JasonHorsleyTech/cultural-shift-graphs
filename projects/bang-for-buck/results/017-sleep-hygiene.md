# Result: Sleep Hygiene / CBT-I

## Summary

Sleep hygiene improvements are best studied through the lens of CBT for Insomnia (CBT-I), which incorporates sleep hygiene education as one component alongside sleep restriction, stimulus control, and cognitive restructuring. Basic sleep hygiene alone lacks QALY data, but CBT-I has a growing cost-effectiveness literature showing modest but consistent QALY gains. For Bob, digital or self-guided CBT-I is the most accessible and cost-effective delivery method, costing under $50/year amortized — though environmental barriers (noise, shift work, overcrowding) may limit effectiveness. The intervention is notable for having no pharmacological side effects and producing durable improvements that persist after the initial 6-8 week program.

## QALY Evidence

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.03
- **Range:** 0.02 – 0.05
- **Primary source:** Darden et al. (2021), "Cost-effectiveness of digital cognitive behavioral therapy (Sleepio) for insomnia: a Markov simulation model in the United States," *SLEEP* 44(4); Leerssen et al. (2021), guided iCBT-I RCT, *Journal of Medical Internet Research*
- **Study type:** Markov simulation model (Darden) informed by RCT data; RCT with economic evaluation alongside (Leerssen)
- **Population studied:** General adult insomnia populations in the US and Netherlands
- **Population match to Bob:** Fair
- **Key caveats:**
  - QALY estimates derived by annualizing 6-month trial data: Darden model showed 0.013 QALY gain over 6 months (~0.026/year); Leerssen RCT showed 0.0183 QALYs over 6 months (~0.037/year). Midpoint ~0.03/year.
  - Most studies use general adult populations, not low-income specifically. Bob's higher baseline insomnia prevalence could mean larger absolute gains, but environmental barriers could offset this.
  - EQ-5D and SF-6D may underestimate sleep-related quality of life gains because neither instrument has a dedicated sleep dimension. ISI-to-EQ-5D mapping studies suggest the utility gain from resolving moderate insomnia (ISI ~18 → ISI ~8) could be ~0.09-0.13 based on the 0.022 utility per ISI point coefficient (Dietch et al. 2012), but direct trial-measured QALY gains are substantially smaller.
  - Long-term durability of QALY gains beyond 6-12 months is assumed but not firmly established in economic evaluations. Clinical evidence suggests CBT-I effects persist 1-3 years post-treatment.

## Cost Analysis (for Bob at ~$30k household income)

- **Annual direct cost:** $0-50 depending on delivery method
- **One-time startup cost:** $20-200
  - Free tier: CBT-I Coach app (VA, free to all) + blackout curtains ($15) + white noise fan ($20) = ~$35
  - Self-help tier: Workbook ($20) + supplies ($35) = ~$55
  - Digital app tier: Sleepio or equivalent ($100-449) + supplies ($35) = ~$135-485
- **Startup cost amortized over 3 years:** $12-162/year
- **Total annual cost (best estimate):** $50
- **Cost range:** $15 – $200 (low = free app + minimal supplies amortized; high = digital CBT-I app + sleep environment upgrades)
- **With insurance (if applicable):** Some insurers and employers cover Sleepio at no cost; VA covers CBT-I for veterans
- **Without insurance:** $15-200/year for self-guided; $300-700/year if therapist-delivered (6 sessions at $150-350/session, amortized over 3 years)
- **Cost notes:** CBT-I is a time-limited intervention (6-8 weeks), not ongoing. Costs are almost entirely upfront. The cheapest effective route (free CBT-I Coach app + basic environmental modifications) costs under $40 total. Therapist-delivered CBT-I without insurance runs $900-2,800 for a full course but is not the cost-optimal delivery for Bob. Prescription sleep aids (for comparison) cost $30-100+/month ongoing, making CBT-I more cost-effective over any horizon beyond a few months.

## Time Commitment

- **Hours per week:** 0.8 (annualized: ~2.5 hrs/wk for 8 weeks of active treatment + ~0.3 hrs/wk maintenance for the remaining 44 weeks)
- **Category:** Low (<1 hr/wk)

## Access and Barriers

- **Activation energy:** Medium
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - **Environment:** Low-income housing may mean shared bedrooms, street noise, inconsistent temperature — factors that undermine core sleep hygiene recommendations (dark, quiet, cool room). Blackout curtains and a fan help but don't solve noise from neighbors or housemates.
  - **Shift work:** ~16% of low-income workers do shift work. Conventional sleep hygiene (consistent bedtime, dark room at night) is partially or fully incompatible with rotating shifts. Shift-adapted CBT-I protocols exist but are less well-studied.
  - **Screen reduction:** Reducing screen time before bed competes with screens as primary (free) entertainment. This is a lifestyle cost not captured in dollar figures.
  - **Sleep restriction component:** The sleep restriction technique in CBT-I requires temporary sleep curtailment, which causes daytime sleepiness for 1-2 weeks. This is challenging for anyone doing physical labor or driving for work.
  - **Digital literacy:** App-based delivery assumes smartphone access and comfort with digital tools. Most but not all in Bob's bracket have smartphones.
  - **Mental health comorbidity:** Bob's higher depression prevalence (~12-14%) complicates insomnia treatment. Depression and insomnia are bidirectional — CBT-I can improve both, but depressed individuals are more likely to drop out.

## Side Effects and Risks

- Temporary increased daytime sleepiness during sleep restriction phase (1-2 weeks) — mild severity, resolves as sleep consolidates
- Possible transient worsening of mood during early sleep restriction — more pronounced in those with depression
- No pharmacological side effects, no dependency risk, no long-term adverse effects
- Sleep restriction should be used cautiously in people operating heavy machinery or driving (relevant to Bob's likely occupation profile)

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate:** 47%
- **Source:** Systematic review of fully automated digital CBT-I (npj Digital Medicine, 2025); Adherence to CBT-I systematic review (Matthews et al., 2013, Sleep Medicine Reviews)
- **Notes:** In-person/group CBT-I shows 60-86% completion in RCTs, but real-world digital CBT-I completion is ~47%. Sleep restriction is the hardest component — only ~44% of participants fully adhere to it even in structured programs. Higher depression severity, psychiatric comorbidities, and higher anxiety all predict worse adherence. Bob's elevated depression rate (12-14%) is a risk factor for dropout. The counterintuitive nature of deliberately restricting sleep to improve sleep is a major friction point.

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 2
- **Tier justification:** CBT-I itself has strong RCT evidence (multiple meta-analyses, Cochrane reviews, NICE first-line recommendation), which would suggest Tier 1. However, the QALY-specific evidence is more limited — most CBT-I studies measure sleep outcomes (ISI, PSQI) rather than QALYs directly. Only a handful of economic evaluations report incremental QALYs, and those that do show modest gains that may underestimate true benefit due to EQ-5D/SF-6D insensitivity to sleep. Population match to Bob is Fair, not Good — studies rarely target low-income populations specifically, and Bob's environmental and occupational barriers are underrepresented in the literature.
- **Key studies:**
  - Darden et al. (2021). Cost-effectiveness of digital CBT (Sleepio) for insomnia: a Markov simulation model in the United States. *SLEEP*, 44(4). — US-focused model showing digital CBT-I most cost-effective vs pharmacotherapy, individual CBT, group CBT.
  - Leerssen et al. (2021). Guided internet-based CBT for insomnia: health-economic evaluation alongside an RCT. *JMIR*, 23(10). — 0.0183 QALYs gained over 6 months, ICER €11,285/QALY.
  - Dietch et al. (2012). Mapping ISI to EQ-5D utilities. *Health and Quality of Life Outcomes*, 9:119. — Established 0.022 utility per ISI point mapping.
  - Matthews et al. (2013). Adherence to CBT-I: systematic review. *Sleep Medicine Reviews*, 17(6). — 14-40% dropout across modalities.
  - Watanabe et al. (2015). Cost-effectiveness of CBT-I comorbid with depression. *Psychiatry and Clinical Neurosciences*. — ICER ~$14,000/QALY.
- **Limitations:**
  - Few studies directly measure QALYs; most rely on mapping from ISI or other sleep instruments to EQ-5D.
  - 6-month time horizons dominate — long-term QALY trajectories are modeled, not measured.
  - Self-guided and digital delivery (Bob's most likely access route) has less economic evaluation evidence than therapist-delivered CBT-I.
  - No cost-effectiveness studies specifically target low-income US adults.
