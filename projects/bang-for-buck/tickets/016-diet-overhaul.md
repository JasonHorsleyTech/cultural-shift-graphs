# Ticket 016: Diet Overhaul (Home Cooking vs. Fast Food) — QALY Evidence and Cost

## Objective

Research the cost-effectiveness of shifting from a typical lower-income diet (high in fast food, processed food, sugar-sweetened beverages) to a healthier home-cooked diet for someone in Bob's profile.

## What to research

### QALY Evidence
1. **Dietary intervention cost-effectiveness analyses** — are there published $/QALY estimates for dietary changes?
2. **Mortality reduction** from improved diet quality (Mediterranean diet trials, DASH diet evidence)
3. **QALY gains** — this is likely one of the harder interventions to find clean QALY data for. Look for:
   - CEA studies of dietary counseling programs
   - Modeling studies that convert dietary risk factor reduction to QALYs
   - Sugar-sweetened beverage reduction studies
4. **What counts as "diet overhaul"?** — need to define a specific, measurable intervention, not just "eat better." Possible anchors:
   - 5+ servings fruits/vegetables per day
   - Cooking at home 5+ nights/week (vs. Bob's likely 1-2)
   - Eliminating sugar-sweetened beverages

### Cost Analysis
1. **Food cost comparison**
   - Fast food / convenience meals: typical weekly spending for Bob's bracket (BLS CEX data)
   - Home-cooked equivalent meals: weekly grocery cost
   - Net cost difference (could be positive OR negative — home cooking can be cheaper)
   - The "time cost of cooking" is real but goes in the time category, not financial cost
2. **Startup costs** — basic cookware if Bob doesn't have it ($50-100), grocery staples
3. **Annual net financial cost** — may be negative (savings) or small positive (healthier ingredients cost more)

### Access and Barriers
1. **Food deserts** — % of low-income areas without full-service grocery within 1 mile (urban) or 10 miles (rural)
2. **Time to cook** — Bob may work long/irregular hours. Cooking adds 30-60 min/day.
3. **Knowledge/skill barrier** — cooking skills, nutrition literacy
4. **Food assistance** — SNAP eligibility at $30k, impact on food budget

### Adherence
- Dietary change adherence rates are notoriously low — find data on sustained diet improvement

## Where to look

1. **Tufts CEA Registry** — "dietary intervention" or "nutrition" cost-effectiveness
2. **USDA Economic Research Service** — food spending by income, food desert data
3. **BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey** — food at home vs. food away from home by income quintile
4. **Key studies:**
   - PREDIMED trial (Mediterranean diet) — cardiovascular outcomes
   - DASH diet trials
   - Afshin et al. (2017) — dietary risks and mortality modeling
5. **PubMed** — "dietary intervention QALY" or "healthy diet cost-effectiveness"

## Output format

Use the full intervention result format from plan.md. This intervention may be harder to pin down than medical interventions — be honest about evidence quality.

## What "good data" looks like

- QALY estimates tied to a specific, defined dietary change (not just "eat healthy")
- Cost comparison using Bob's actual food spending patterns, not middle-class assumptions
- Acknowledgment that this is one of the less well-defined interventions — Tier 2-3 evidence is expected
