# Bicycle — Cheapest and Quality-Equivalent Prices

## Summary

Bicycle prices tell a "progress wins with caveats" story. In nominal dollars, the cheapest adult bicycle has risen modestly from ~$37 in 1955 to ~$128 in 2025 — a roughly 3.5x increase over 70 years, well below general inflation (~10x). The quality-equivalent bike (the standard good bike a normal person buys from a bike shop) has risen from $49.95 to ~$550 — an ~11x increase, roughly tracking inflation. The gap between the two lines opened dramatically after ~1975 when the 10-speed boom and mass imports created a distinct "department store" tier. A key structural break occurred in the late 1980s–1990s when Chinese manufacturing flooded the cheapest tier with sub-$100 mountain-bike-styled machines, actually *lowering* the floor price in real terms while bike-shop prices continued climbing. Post-2020 supply chain disruptions and inflation pushed both tiers up noticeably.

## Methodology

### Cheapest tier (Line A) operationalization

"Cheapest new adult bicycle at a mainstream retailer" means:
- **1950–1993:** The cheapest adult-sized (26" wheel) bicycle listed in the Sears catalog (J.C. Higgins brand in 1950s, Spaceliner/Free Spirit in 1960s–1980s). These were typically made by Murray or Huffy under contract. When Sears catalog data was unavailable, prices were estimated from forum discussions of period catalogs, cross-referenced with Schwinn budget-line pricing (always higher than store brands).
- **1995–2025:** The cheapest adult bicycle at Walmart or Target (brands include Huffy, Roadmaster, Next, Kent, Mongoose, or Schwinn-branded imports). These are what the cycling community calls BSOs (Bike-Shaped Objects) — technically functional bicycles but with components designed to a price point, not a performance standard.

### Quality-equivalent tier (Line B) operationalization

Per the quality rubric (029), the quality-equivalent is "a $350–$500 hybrid/commuter from a bike shop" in current terms. Working backward:
- **1950–1970:** The Schwinn mid-range product (American, Tiger, then Varsity as lightweights emerged). Schwinn catalog prices are well-documented through bikehistory.org and the Schwinn Lightweight Data Book (SLDB).
- **1975–1985:** The Schwinn Varsity 10-speed, which was the quintessential "standard good bike" of the era — the Honda Civic of bicycles. Prices from SLDB and Schwinn catalogs.
- **1990–2000:** Entry-level bike-shop hybrids (Trek MultiTrack 700-series, Giant Cypress, Specialized Crossroads). Hybrids replaced the 10-speed road bike as the default "normal person" bike in the late 1980s.
- **2005–2025:** Trek FX series (7100/7200/7.1/7.2/FX 1), Giant Escape 3, or equivalent. MSRP data from Bicycle Blue Book, Trek catalogs, and current retailer listings.

### Divergence handling

Per the quality rubric, **divergence date is ~1975**. Before 1975, the cheapest and quality-equivalent are separate products (budget store brand vs. Schwinn) but from the same single-tier market. After 1975, they represent genuinely different product categories: department store BSO vs. bike-shop quality bicycle.

In practice, there was already a price spread pre-1975 (Murray at $37 vs. Schwinn at $50 in 1955), but the quality difference was modest — both were heavy steel cruisers built to last decades. Post-1975, the quality gap became categorical: the cheapest tier shifted to disposable import machines while the quality tier maintained functional longevity.

### Data source notes

- **Schwinn catalog prices** are the most reliable data in this set. The Schwinn Lightweight Data Book (SLDB) at schwinnbikeforum.com and bikehistory.org catalog transcriptions provide confirmed retail prices for most years 1955–1986.
- **Sears catalog prices** for the cheapest tier are harder to pin down. Forum discussions on Rat Rod Bikes and The CABE provide anecdotal data ($29.95–$40 for Murray/Huffy in the 1950s; $84 for a 1977 10-speed). Prices between confirmed data points are interpolated based on the BLS CPI for "Sports vehicles including bicycles."
- **Modern pricing (2000–2025)** relies on forum discussions, Bicycle Blue Book MSRP data, and current retailer listings. The cheapest tier is well-documented through Walmart's consistent pricing; quality-equivalent pricing comes from Trek and Giant official MSRP data and retailer listings.
- **1985–2000 is the weakest period** for both tiers. The Sears catalog ended in 1993, Schwinn went bankrupt in 1992, and the hybrid bicycle category was still emerging. Prices for this period are estimated from fragmentary sources.

## Known gaps

- **1950 and 1960 cheapest prices** are estimated from 1955 forum data and general inflation trends. No specific catalog pages confirmed.
- **1985 quality-equivalent** is the least confident data point. The bike-shop market was fragmenting as Schwinn declined, Trek/Giant/Specialized were rising, and the product category was shifting from road bikes to hybrids. The $225 figure is a rough estimate.
- **1990–2000 cheapest prices** are based on anecdotal reports and general Walmart pricing knowledge. Specific Walmart catalog pages from this era were not located.
- **2025 Trek FX 1 Gen 4 pricing** shows significant range ($550–$714) across sources, suggesting either regional variation, model variant differences (disc vs. rim brake), or mid-year price changes.
