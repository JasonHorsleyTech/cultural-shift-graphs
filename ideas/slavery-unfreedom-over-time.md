# Slavery / Unfreedom Over Time

Status: very messy, not a question yet. Jason needs to learn more before he can ask something sharp.

## The kernel

What percentage of humanity has been living in some form of slavery/unfreedom over time? Most people would draw a spike around the Atlantic slave trade and then a drop to zero. The real shape is probably very different — high for most of history, with the *composition* shifting rather than the total.

## Possible tiers of unfreedom

- **Chattel slavery** — owned as property (legally a movable asset, like cattle — same Latin root). Can be bought, sold, inherited. Children are also property.
- **Bonded/debt labor** — tied to a creditor, debt is structurally unpayable by design, can't leave until debt is "paid."
- **Serfdom/forced labor** — tied to land or state, legally restricted movement.
- **Functionally trapped** — no legal bondage but no practical exit. Example: undocumented immigrants in the US who can't report wage theft without risking deportation, tied to employers willing to hire them.

The interesting insight might be: the *label* tells you almost nothing about the *experience*. A Roman household slave with a path to citizenship and a Brazilian sugar mill slave are both "chattel" but their daily reality has nothing in common.

## Things Jason found interesting while workshopping

### Chattel slavery wasn't monolithic
- Roman slavery had the widest spectrum: mine slaves (dead in months) to educated household slaves (doctors, tutors, accountants who lived better than free poor). Manumission was so common Augustus had to cap it. Freedmen became citizens. Some became wealthy.
- Islamic slavery: Sharia required humane treatment, manumission was religiously encouraged, children of slave women and masters were born free. Mamluk military slaves became the ruling dynasty of Egypt. But the trans-Saharan trade itself had 30-50% mortality.
- The Atlantic trade was recent, well-documented, and unusually racialized — dominates the curriculum but is a narrow slice of slavery's full history.

### Brazil vs. American South economics
Brazil sugar plantations: 300-500% markup on sugar, cheap slave supply from Atlantic trade, so the math worked out to "buy, destroy in 6-18 months, replace." American South: cotton needed skill and consistency, Atlantic trade closing post-1808, domestic slave prices skyrocketed, so preservation became the rational economic strategy. Both were profit-maximizing with different input costs.

### The "are modern Americans slaves?" test
Jason tested whether modern American conditions (student debt, US taxation abroad, wage labor) count as unfreedom. Answer: mostly no. The honest test is "what happens if you try to leave?" If your credit score tanks, that's not slavery. If you get beaten and dragged back, that is. The line is violence as enforcement mechanism, not "my options suck."

**Exception:** undocumented immigrants in the US have genuine overlap with forced labor definitions. Can't report abuse, tied to specific employers, exploited with impunity. ILO counts hundreds of thousands in forced labor conditions in the US.

### Military as voluntary unfreedom
Jason noted the US military meets several unfreedom criteria (can't leave, separate legal system, labor/location/appearance controlled). Doesn't belong in slavery tiers because: voluntary entry, defined end date, compensation, retained legal personhood. But worth a footnote.

### Ethnicity breakdown — handle with care
Jason suspects the average slave across all of history was probably not African — possibly Chinese or European given the scale and duration of slavery in those civilizations. The Roman Empire alone ran for centuries with massive slave populations. This is interesting but the framing has to be careful — "who had it worse" is not the question. Need to figure out how to ask this without producing a graph that reads as "black people shouldn't complain." That framing problem is part of why this idea isn't ready yet.

## Rough data availability
- Atlantic slave trade: good data
- Roman-era estimates: decent
- Medieval European serfdom rates: decent
- Russian serfdom: good (didn't free serfs until 1861, ~50% of population)
- Modern forced/bonded labor: good (ILO Global Slavery Index, ~50 million / 8 billion = ~0.6%)
- Pre-1500 Asia and Africa: rough estimates, big gaps

## Adjacent ideas (not for this graph)
- **Happiness vs. freedom:** Are unfree people necessarily unhappy? Jason's happiest period was the US Army (shared adversity, physical exertion, community, acute stress with real recovery). Slave communities built rich internal cultures (music, family, religion). Easterlin Paradox says wealth doesn't buy happiness past a threshold. This is a separate graph — overlaying happiness on a slavery chart would read as apologism regardless of intent. Maybe a future idea.
- **Intensity of slavery:** Even within a single tier, the lived experience varies enormously. Could there be a secondary axis for "harshness"?
