# Ticket 007: Mercury → Neurological Damage

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that mercury (particularly methylmercury) causes neurological damage to binding regulation restricting mercury exposure in the general population.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Minamata disease was identified in 1956 (methylmercury poisoning from industrial discharge into Minamata Bay, Japan). Earlier occupational evidence exists (hatters' disease, thermometer workers). For population-level harm, Minamata is the landmark case. Also: Iraq methylmercury poisoning (1971-1972, contaminated grain).

2. **Consensus date** — When did a major scientific body formally state that mercury is a neurological hazard requiring regulation? WHO reports, FDA advisories on fish consumption, UNEP mercury assessments.

3. **First OECD regulation** — Distinguish between: (a) restrictions on industrial mercury discharge (environmental regulation), (b) limits on mercury in consumer products (thermometers, batteries), (c) restrictions on mercury in food (fish advisories). Focus on the first binding restriction that reduced GENERAL POPULATION exposure.

4. **US regulation** — FDA fish consumption advisories, EPA mercury emission standards (Mercury and Air Toxics Standards, 2011), state-level thermometer bans. What was the first binding FEDERAL action?

5. **Minamata Convention** — Global treaty on mercury (2013, entered into force 2017). Named after the Minamata disaster. Key international milestone.

6. **Industry opposition** — Chisso Corporation (Minamata) concealed evidence. Broader mercury-using industries? Level of organized opposition?

7. **50% exposure reduction** — Mercury levels in fish, blood mercury levels in populations? NHANES data?

## Where to look

1. WHO mercury fact sheet and historical reports
2. UNEP Global Mercury Assessment
3. Minamata Convention text and background
4. EPA mercury regulation history (MATS, Clean Air Act)
5. FDA fish consumption advisory history
6. PubMed — "Minamata disease" and "mercury regulation history"

## What "good data" looks like

- Focus on METHYLMERCURY and general population exposure, not just occupational elemental mercury exposure
- Clear distinction between advisories/guidelines (not binding) and actual regulations (binding)
- Minamata disease dates verified — discovery 1956, cause identified 1959, Chisso acknowledged responsibility much later

## Known pitfalls

- Mercury has multiple exposure routes (industrial discharge, consumer products, dental amalgam, fish consumption, coal combustion) with DIFFERENT regulatory timelines. Pick the primary general-population route (methylmercury in food/environment) and be consistent.
- Fish consumption advisories are ADVISORIES, not binding regulation. The first binding regulation may be emission standards, not food limits.
- Category will likely be "international-treaty" (Minamata Convention 2013) or "exposure-limit" depending on which regulatory action you identify as first. Document the choice.
