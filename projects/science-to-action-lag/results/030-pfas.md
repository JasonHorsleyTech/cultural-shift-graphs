## Sources

1. Giesy, J.P. & Kannan, K. (2001). "Global Distribution of Perfluorooctane Sulfonate in Wildlife." Environmental Science & Technology, 35(7), 1339–1342. First peer-reviewed study documenting global PFOS contamination in wildlife.
2. C8 Science Panel (2012). Probable Link Evaluations. Independent epidemiological panel (Fletcher, Savitz, Steenland) found probable links between PFOA and six diseases: kidney cancer, testicular cancer, thyroid disease, ulcerative colitis, high cholesterol, pregnancy-induced hypertension. Based on study of ~69,000 people near DuPont's Parkersburg, WV plant.
3. EFSA (2020). "Risk to human health related to the presence of perfluoroalkyl substances in food." EFSA Journal, 18(9):6223. Set tolerable weekly intake of 4.4 ng/kg body weight per week for sum of PFOA, PFNA, PFHxS, and PFOS.
4. National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (2022). "Guidance on PFAS Exposure, Testing, and Clinical Follow-Up." Established exposure thresholds: <2 ng/mL (low concern), 2–20 ng/mL (potential effects in sensitive populations), >20 ng/mL (increased risk).
5. IARC (2023). Monographs Volume 135. Classified PFOA as Group 1 (carcinogenic to humans) and PFOS as Group 2B (possibly carcinogenic). Published in The Lancet Oncology, November 30, 2023.
6. Stockholm Convention (2009). COP-4 listed PFOS in Annex B. COP-9 (2019) listed PFOA in Annex A. COP-10 (2022) listed PFHxS in Annex A. COP-11 (2025) listed long-chain PFCAs in Annex A.
7. US EPA (2024). "PFAS National Primary Drinking Water Regulation." Final rule set MCLs for PFOA and PFOS at 4 ppt, PFNA, PFHxS, and HFPO-DA at 10 ppt, plus a hazard index for mixtures.
8. Denmark Ministry of Environment (2020). Ban on PFAS in food contact paper and board — first country to ban PFAS class-wide in food packaging.
9. Richter, L. et al. (2023). "The Devil They Knew: Chemical Documents Analysis of Industry Influence on PFAS Science." Annals of Global Health, 89(1):37. Documented decades of corporate concealment by 3M and DuPont.
10. EWG (2019). "For 50 Years, Polluters Knew PFAS Chemicals Were Dangerous but Hid Risks from Public." Timeline of corporate concealment from 1950s onward.
11. EPA press release (2024). "Biden-Harris Administration Finalizes First-Ever National Drinking Water Standard to Protect 100M People from PFAS Pollution." April 10, 2024.
12. Earthjustice / EWG / NRDC (2025–2026). Reporting on Trump administration partial rollback of PFAS drinking water standards: EPA proposed extending PFOA/PFOS compliance deadline to 2031 and rescinding standards for four other PFAS compounds. D.C. Circuit denied EPA request to summarily vacate the rules (January 2026).

## Judgment calls

**Early evidence year (2001):** Chose Giesy & Kannan's 2001 publication as the first credible PUBLIC scientific evidence. Internal 3M and DuPont studies showed PFAS toxicity in animals as early as the 1960s, and 3M disclosed PFOS presence in human blood to the EPA in 1998. However, the plan specifies "credible scientific paper" establishing a link, and the 1998 disclosure was a regulatory notification about bioaccumulation, not a peer-reviewed study of harm. The 2001 paper established that PFOS was a global environmental contaminant found in wildlife on every continent, which implied universal human exposure. Earlier occupational health studies existed within companies but were not publicly available. Note: the ~40-year gap between internal industry knowledge (1960s) and public scientific evidence (2001) is itself a significant finding about corporate concealment comparable to the tobacco industry.

**Consensus year (2023):** Used the IARC Group 1 classification of PFOA as the consensus marker because IARC classifications are the most authoritative and comparable standard across the dataset. Alternative candidates considered: C8 Science Panel findings (2012) — an independent scientific panel but court-ordered and focused on one compound/community; EFSA tolerable intake opinion (2020) — a major regulatory science body but focused on dietary exposure; NASEM report (2022) — comprehensive but primarily clinical guidance. The IARC 2023 classification represents the broadest, most authoritative scientific consensus statement.

**First regulation year (2009) and category:** The Stockholm Convention PFOS listing in 2009 was the first binding international action restricting any PFAS compound. Categorized as international-treaty because this was a multilateral treaty action, not a single country's legislation. Note: the EU had restricted PFOS marketing via Directive 2006/122/EC in 2006, which could be considered the first OECD-country regulation, but the Stockholm Convention listing is a cleaner regulatory endpoint for the treaty category. Denmark's 2020 ban on PFAS in food packaging was the first domestic regulation targeting the broader PFAS class. The regulatory landscape is fragmented: different countries are regulating different PFAS compounds through different mechanisms (bans, exposure limits, treaty obligations).

**Negative lag from consensus (-14 years):** The Stockholm Convention listed PFOS in 2009 based on environmental persistence, bioaccumulation, and ecotoxicity — NOT primarily on human health evidence. Formal health consensus (IARC 2023) came 14 years later. This is unusual in the dataset and reflects the precautionary principle applied through environmental treaties. The environmental and health regulatory tracks operated on different timelines.

**US regulation (2024) and current status:** The Biden administration finalized the first federal PFAS drinking water standards in April 2024. As of May 2026, the Trump administration has proposed extending compliance deadlines to 2031 and rescinding standards for four of six regulated PFAS compounds (retaining only PFOA and PFOS limits). The D.C. Circuit denied EPA's request to summarily vacate the rules. The regulatory situation remains fluid and contested.

**Industry opposition (strong):** 3M and DuPont concealed evidence of PFAS health and environmental risks for approximately four decades (1960s–2000s). Strategies included non-publication of internal studies, failure to notify EPA as required, and commissioning of industry-favorable research. This pattern is comparable to the tobacco industry's campaign, though the primary mechanism was suppression rather than manufactured doubt. 3M announced PFOS phase-out in 2000 only after EPA pressure following the Giesy & Kannan findings. DuPont's concealment of PFOA contamination near its Parkersburg, WV plant led to the landmark Tennant/Bilott litigation and the C8 Science Panel.

**Substitute availability (partial):** PFAS are used in thousands of applications (nonstick coatings, waterproofing, food packaging, firefighting foam, semiconductors). For some uses, viable non-PFAS alternatives exist (food packaging, some textiles, firefighting foam). For others, particularly industrial applications like semiconductor manufacturing, substitutes are limited or nonexistent. Many "substitutes" have been shorter-chain PFAS (e.g., GenX replacing PFOA), which themselves are now being scrutinized. The EU's proposed universal PFAS restriction (2023) would force substitution across all applications.

**Scope:** "PFAS" encompasses ~12,000+ individual compounds. Regulation to date has targeted specific legacy compounds (PFOS, PFOA, PFHxS) rather than the class as a whole. The EU's 2023 proposal to restrict all PFAS as a class would be unprecedented. This ticket treats PFAS as a single hazard for graphing purposes, but the regulatory reality is compound-by-compound action with slow expansion to broader class-based approaches.
