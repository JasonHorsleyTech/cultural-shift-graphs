# Result: CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy) — QALY Evidence

## Summary

A standard 12–16 session course of CBT for depression produces an estimated net QALY gain of approximately 0.06 per year during the benefit period, anchored primarily on the CoBalT trial's incremental QALY gain of 0.057 and Ross et al.'s decision-analytic model showing CBT slightly outperforming SSRIs. CBT's distinctive advantage is durability: unlike SSRIs (which produce benefit only while taken), CBT teaches skills that persist after treatment ends, reducing relapse rates and improving cost-effectiveness over longer time horizons. The CoBalT trial found a 92% probability of cost-effectiveness at 46 months (ICER £5,374/QALY), and Ross et al. found CBT had a 73–77% likelihood of being cost-effective at 5 years. However, applicability to Bob's profile (low-income, potential access barriers) is uncertain — evidence shows lower SES is associated with less improvement in psychotherapy, and real-world completion rates for depression (~65%) reduce the effective population-level benefit.

## QALY Evidence

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.06
- **Range:** 0.03 – 0.10 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** CoBalT trial (Wiles et al. 2013, 2016); Ross et al. (2019) Annals of Internal Medicine; Cuijpers et al. (2023) World Psychiatry meta-analysis
- **Study type:** RCT (CoBalT), decision-analytic cost-effectiveness model (Ross), comprehensive meta-analysis (Cuijpers)
- **Population studied:** Primary care adults with treatment-resistant depression (CoBalT, n=469); simulated cohort 62.2% female, mean age 40.7 based on STAR*D distributions (Ross); 52,702 patients across 409 trials (Cuijpers)
- **Population match to Bob:** Fair — CoBalT used UK primary care patients (accessible populations, not specifically low-income). Evidence shows lower SES, lower education, and unemployment are associated with slower and less improvement across depression treatments (Falconnier 2009; Socioeconomic Predictors study 2022). Bob's profile predicts outcomes at the lower end of the range.
- **Key caveats:**
  - CBT is only indicated for clinical depression/anxiety — benefit is zero for non-symptomatic individuals
  - Bob has ~19% probability of current depression (ticket 002), so the population-weighted QALY for a random Bob is ~0.01
  - The 0.06 estimate is for a Bob who completes treatment; real-world completion (~65%) reduces effective gains
  - Most QALY evidence comes from CBT + usual care vs. usual care (adjunct design), not CBT monotherapy vs. nothing
  - EQ-5D instruments used in trials may not fully capture all dimensions of CBT benefit (e.g., improved coping, self-efficacy)

### Evidence Stream 1: CoBalT Trial (Wiles et al. 2013, 2016)

The CoBalT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy as an Adjunct to Pharmacotherapy for Treatment-Resistant Depression in Primary Care) trial is the strongest single source of CBT QALY data.

**12-month results:**
- CBT + usual care vs. usual care alone for treatment-resistant depression
- **Incremental QALY gain: 0.057** (equivalent to 21 quality-adjusted life-days per year)
- Mean cost of CBT per participant: £910
- Incremental health and social care cost: £850
- **ICER: £14,911/QALY**
- 74% probability of cost-effectiveness at NICE threshold of £20,000/QALY
- Clinical response at 6 months: 46.1% (CBT) vs. 21.6% (usual care), OR 3.26 (95% CI: 2.10–5.06)

**Long-term follow-up (average 46 months):**
- Benefits sustained over nearly 4 years
- **ICER improved to £5,374/QALY**
- **92% probability of cost-effectiveness at £20,000/QALY**
- Demonstrates CBT's lasting benefit — improved cost-effectiveness over time as upfront treatment costs are spread across continued QALY gains

**Caveat:** CoBalT studied CBT as an adjunct to ongoing pharmacotherapy in treatment-resistant patients, not CBT monotherapy. The incremental QALY gain is from adding CBT on top of medication that had already failed to produce adequate response. This is a conservative test — CBT for treatment-naïve depression may produce larger gains.

### Evidence Stream 2: Ross et al. (2019) — CBT vs. SGAs Decision Model

Published in Annals of Internal Medicine. US-based decision-analytic cost-effectiveness model comparing CBT to second-generation antidepressants (SGAs) for initial treatment of major depressive disorder.

**1-year horizon:**
- CBT produced **0.008 additional QALYs** vs. SGAs (95% CI: −0.013 to 0.025) — approximately 3 quality-adjusted life-days
- CBT cost $900 more (health care sector) or $1,500 more (societal perspective)
- ICER: $119,000/QALY (health care sector), $186,000/QALY (societal)
- SGAs had 64–77% likelihood of being cost-effective at $100,000/QALY at 1 year

**5-year horizon:**
- CBT produced **0.055 additional QALYs** vs. SGAs (95% CI: −0.044 to 0.160) — approximately 20 quality-adjusted life-days
- CBT **saved** $1,800 (health care sector) or $2,500 (societal)
- CBT was **dominant** (more effective AND cheaper) at 5 years
- CBT had **73–77% likelihood** of being cost-effective at $100,000/QALY

**Key implication:** Since SSRIs produce ~0.05 QALY/year (ticket 009), and CBT produces 0.008 more than SSRIs at 1 year, CBT for depression yields approximately 0.058 QALY in year 1 vs. no treatment. The widening gap at 5 years (0.055 cumulative incremental QALYs) reflects CBT's relapse prevention benefit — patients maintain gains after treatment ends while SSRI patients experience higher relapse rates upon discontinuation.

### Evidence Stream 3: Cuijpers et al. (2023) — Comprehensive Meta-Analysis

The largest meta-analysis of CBT for depression: 409 trials, 52,702 patients, published in World Psychiatry.

- **CBT vs. control conditions** (care as usual, waitlist): Hedges' g = 0.79 (95% CI: 0.70–0.89)
- **CBT vs. other psychotherapies:** g = 0.06 (95% CI: 0–0.12) — small, mostly non-significant
- **CBT vs. pharmacotherapy:** Comparable short-term; CBT **more effective long-term**
- **NNT:** Earlier Cuijpers meta-analysis (2013) found NNT = 2.6 from 94 comparisons

**Converting effect size to QALY:** The g = 0.79 cannot be directly converted to QALYs without utility-weight mapping, but it indicates a large clinical effect. For reference, a g of 0.79 on the PHQ-9 (range 0–27) corresponds to roughly a 4–5 point reduction, which maps to approximately 0.05–0.10 utility gain depending on baseline severity (using Peasgood et al. 2014 EQ-5D mapping from ticket 009).

### Evidence Stream 4: Systematic Review of CBT Cost-Effectiveness (2022)

Systematic review of 38 economic evaluations (26 high methodological quality).

- **Face-to-face CBT monotherapy/combination vs. antidepressants and usual care:** ICER range −$241,212 to $33,032/QALY over 12–60 months
- **Internet-based CBT (guided or unguided):** ICER range −$37,717 to $73,841/QALY over 3–36 months
- **CBT for depression prevention:** ICER range −$23,932 to $26,092/QALY over 9–60 months

Negative ICERs indicate CBT is dominant (cheaper and more effective than the comparator).

### CBT-I (for Insomnia) — Separate QALY Evidence

CBT for insomnia (CBT-I) is a distinct protocol from CBT for depression but relevant given Bob's likely sleep issues.

- **12-month QALY gain:** 0.025 (95% CI: 0.016–0.035) from meta-analysis of 7 studies (n=4,811)
- **6-month QALY gain:** 0.013 (95% CI: 0.008–0.018)
- **ICUR range:** £6,260 to £22,882/QALY (healthcare perspective)
- Cost-neutral at 12 months (upfront costs offset by reduced healthcare utilization)
- CBT-I is the gold standard for chronic insomnia treatment (preferred over pharmacotherapy by AASM guidelines)

**Note:** CBT-I and CBT for depression are separate interventions. This graph entry covers CBT for depression/anxiety. CBT-I data is included for context but not used in the primary QALY estimate.

## Short-Term vs. Long-Term QALY Profile

CBT's defining characteristic is **skill persistence** — unlike pharmacotherapy, the benefits continue after treatment ends because patients learn cognitive restructuring techniques they continue to apply.

**Relapse prevention evidence:**
- Meta-analysis (2022): CBT relapse/recurrence rate 31.6% vs. control 41.3% at ≤12 months and >12 months
- 10-year follow-up in older adults (2024): CBT group showed 88% remission of depressive diagnoses vs. 54% control; relapse rates 25–31% (CBT) vs. 50–78% (control)
- Patients relapse less often after discontinuing acute-phase CBT than after discontinuing pharmacotherapy
- CBT had "the longest but not continuous effect" in preventing relapse

**QALY trajectory model:**

| Year | Estimated QALY Gain | Basis |
|------|---------------------|-------|
| Year 1 (during/post treatment) | 0.057 | CoBalT direct measurement |
| Year 2 (post-treatment) | 0.04 | Partial persistence, relapse prevention data |
| Year 3 (post-treatment) | 0.03 | Further diminishing, still above baseline |
| Year 4+ | 0.01–0.02 | Residual benefit, some relapse |

**Cumulative QALY gain over 3 years:** ~0.13
**Annualized over 3-year benefit period:** ~0.043

**Cumulative QALY gain over 5 years:** ~0.18
**Annualized over 5-year benefit period:** ~0.036

The midpoint estimate of **0.06** weights toward the stronger year-1 evidence because: (1) the graph compares annual benefit rates, and the year of active benefit is the most defensible estimate, (2) for a time-limited intervention, the per-year-of-benefit figure is more meaningful than the per-year-of-life figure, and (3) the lasting benefits are real but methodologically harder to quantify precisely.

## Side Effects and Risks

- **Temporary emotional distress:** Engaging with painful thoughts and memories during treatment can increase short-term distress. This is therapeutically intentional but may feel worse before it feels better. No formal disutility weight exists; clinical consensus is that this is transient (sessions 1–4).
- **Misapplication risk:** If CBT is poorly delivered (undertrained therapist, cultural mismatch), it can reinforce unhelpful patterns. This is a quality-of-care issue, not intrinsic to CBT.
- **No pharmacological side effects:** Zero risk of sexual dysfunction, weight gain, emotional blunting, or withdrawal syndrome — a significant advantage over SSRIs for Bob.
- **Net side-effect disutility: ~0** — CBT has no meaningful side-effect QALY penalty, unlike SSRIs (~0.015 disutility from ticket 009).

## Adherence

- **Real-world completion rate (full course ≥12 sessions):** ~65%
- **Pretreatment dropout:** 15.9% (meta-analysis of CBT dropout, Fernandez et al. 2015)
- **During-treatment dropout:** 26.2% (same meta-analysis)
- **Depression-specific dropout:** Highest among all diagnoses in CBT dropout literature
- **Dropout timing:** Front-loaded in sessions 1–4; patients who reach session 5 are likely to complete
- **Source:** Fernandez et al. (2015) meta-analysis of CBT dropout; BJPsych Advances review
- **Moderators of dropout:**
  - Treatment format: e-therapy has highest dropout
  - Setting: inpatient lower than outpatient
  - More sessions = lower dropout (counterintuitively — likely reflects engaged patients)
- **Bob-specific factors:**
  - Shift work and limited scheduling flexibility → higher dropout risk
  - Transportation barriers → missed sessions → dropout
  - Lower health literacy may reduce engagement with homework assignments
  - No copay cost barrier if using community mental health center (ticket 012)
- **Notes:** Unlike SSRIs (which require indefinite daily adherence), CBT requires time-limited commitment. The relevant adherence metric is completion of the course, not ongoing compliance. A completed course of CBT produces lasting benefit regardless of subsequent "adherence."

## Population Match: CBT Effectiveness by Socioeconomic Status

**Evidence for reduced effectiveness in low-SES populations:**
- Falconnier (2009): Lower SES (Hollingshead Index) associated with less improvement across all depression treatments including psychotherapy
- Psychiatric Services (2022): Not having a college education, unemployment, or being non-White each predicted slower and less improvement
- Low-SES patients are less likely to be diagnosed, seek treatment, or receive treatment

**Mitigating factors:**
- Interventions with culturally specific provider training show improved outcomes
- Booster sessions improve long-term effectiveness in low-SES populations
- CBT's structured, skill-based approach may be more accessible than insight-oriented therapies
- Community health center delivery models can improve access

**Assessment:** Population match is **Fair**. CBT works for low-income populations but with attenuated effects. The QALY range (0.03–0.10) accounts for this: Bob is more likely at the lower end.

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 1
- **Rationale:** CBT has the strongest evidence base of any psychotherapy. The Cuijpers et al. (2023) meta-analysis alone covers 409 RCTs with 52,702 patients. The CoBalT trial provides direct QALY measurement in a pragmatic primary care setting. Ross et al. (2019) provides US-specific cost-effectiveness with QALY outcomes. Population match is Fair (not specifically low-income), but the sheer volume and quality of evidence justifies Tier 1. The CoBalT trial's primary care setting is reasonably Bob-adjacent.
- **Key studies:**
  - Wiles et al. (2013) — CoBalT trial, CBT for treatment-resistant depression (n=469), direct QALY outcomes
  - Wiles et al. (2016) — CoBalT long-term follow-up, 46-month cost-effectiveness
  - Ross et al. (2019) — CBT vs. SGAs decision model, Annals of Internal Medicine
  - Cuijpers et al. (2023) — 409-trial meta-analysis of CBT for depression, World Psychiatry
  - Systematic review (2022) — 38 economic evaluations of CBT for depression (PubMed 35422392)
  - Fernandez et al. (2015) — Meta-analysis of CBT dropout rates
- **Limitations:**
  - CoBalT studied CBT as adjunct (not monotherapy) in treatment-resistant patients — may overestimate or underestimate monotherapy benefit
  - Ross et al. model results are sensitive to relapse rate assumptions
  - Most QALY data comes from UK and US general populations, not low-income specifically
  - QALY instruments (EQ-5D) may undervalue CBT's non-clinical benefits (improved coping, self-efficacy, resilience)
  - Wide confidence intervals in all QALY estimates (e.g., Ross 5-year: −0.044 to 0.160)
