# Encyclopedia Salespeople

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Door-to-door encyclopedia sales representatives
- **Country/Region:** United States (and to a lesser extent UK, Canada, Australia)
- **Peak Year:** ~1978 (workforce peak); 1990 (revenue peak)
- **Peak Workforce:** ~65,000–70,000 US sales representatives across all publishers (World Book ~60,000, Britannica ~2,300, others ~5,000–8,000) — most were part-time or commission-only independent contractors (Greenstein & Devereux, HBS; Wikipedia/World Book Encyclopedia)
- **Primary Mechanism:** tipping_point
- **Cause Tags:** tech_platform, cultural
- **Killing Event:** CD-ROM encyclopedias (Encarta, 1993) destroyed the price basis of print sets ($1,500 → $99), then Wikipedia (2001) made encyclopedias free, eliminating the entire business model within 15 years.

## Workforce Timeline

The encyclopedia sales force is difficult to track with a single consistent source because the industry comprised multiple private companies (Britannica, World Book, Compton's, Collier's, Americana, Funk & Wagnalls) with different reporting practices. World Book dominated in headcount with a mass part-time/independent contractor model; Britannica had fewer but more professional full-time salespeople.

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1980 (slow workforce erosion began as women entered workforce/cultural shift); 1993 (tipping point — Encarta launch triggered nonlinear collapse)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~10 (1978 → ~1988, gradual pre-digital erosion)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** ~15 (1978 → ~1993, coinciding with Encarta launch)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** ~17 (1978 → ~1995)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** ~18 (1978 → ~1996, Britannica/Collier's mass layoffs)
- **Decline Shape:** s_curve — slow decline through the 1980s (cultural factors), then sharp cliff from 1993–1996 triggered by CD-ROM disruption, then flatline near zero
- **Current Status:** extinct (door-to-door consumer sales); remnant (~95 institutional salespeople at World Book)

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: estimated

Encyclopedia sales were overwhelmingly commission-based, making median income extremely difficult to pin down. Most salespeople were independent contractors, not W-2 employees, so they don't appear in BLS wage surveys. The range was enormous: top performers earned six figures while the median was likely modest. The BLS category "Door-to-Door Sales Workers" (SOC 41-9091) captures this occupation but includes non-encyclopedia direct sellers.

- **Income at Peak:** Highly variable. Commission of 30–50% on sets priced $800–$2,200. Top earners: $100,000+/year. Estimated median: ~$25,000–$35,000/year in 1980s dollars. Weekly range: $300–$4,000 in commissions. (StratoServe; Saturday Evening Post; Quora accounts)
- **Income at Trough:** $0 — profession ceased to exist for consumer sales by 1996–2000
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** N/A — the profession was eliminated, not reduced. Individual recovery depended entirely on pivot destination.
- **Inflation Adjustment Method:** CPI-U where applicable

## Surplus Capture

- **Capital owners:** ~15% — Encyclopedia publishers themselves were devastated; Britannica sold for $135M (from $650M peak revenue). The main capital beneficiary was Microsoft, which bundled Encarta as a loss leader to sell PCs and operating systems.
- **Consumers:** ~70% — The most dramatic consumer surplus capture in the dataset. Families went from paying $1,500–$2,200 for a reference set to $99 (Encarta) to $0 (Wikipedia). The entire $1.2B+ annual spend on encyclopedias was effectively returned to consumers.
- **Adjacent workers:** ~10% — Wikipedia's volunteer model created no new paid jobs. Some value flowed to digital content creators, web developers, and educational technology workers.
- **Government/public:** ~5% — Schools saved enormously on reference materials. Public libraries shifted budgets from print reference sets to digital subscriptions.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
1. **World Book's institutional pivot (2000s–present):** Recognizing that consumer door-to-door sales were dead, World Book transformed from a 40,000-person direct sales operation into an institutional publisher with 95 salespeople serving schools and libraries. By 2018, 30 million students had access to World Book's digital products. They survived by abandoning the consumer market entirely and selling to institutions that still valued curated, age-appropriate content. (Publishers Weekly; World Book Wikipedia article)
2. **Tom Corddry at Microsoft (1993):** The Encarta team knew they were a transitional product. Corddry later said: "We knew from the beginning that we were a temporary business, eventually to be overtaken by the internet." This rare honesty about their own impermanence let Microsoft extract maximum value from Encarta as a PC-bundling tool without overinvesting. (Tedium)

### False Movers
1. **Britannica's $995 CD-ROM (1994):** When Britannica finally released a CD-ROM version, they priced it at $995 — ten times the price of Encarta ($99). They were trying to protect their print revenue and their sales force's commissions simultaneously. By the time they dropped the price, Encarta had captured the market. The sales force they were trying to protect was eliminated two years later anyway. (Encyclopedia.com; FundingUniverse)
2. **Britannica.com free launch (1999):** After years of resistance, Britannica went fully free online. The site crashed from traffic on day one — proving demand existed — but the free model couldn't sustain the business. They subsequently laid off 20% of remaining staff and retreated to a subscription model. The pivot came too late and overcorrected. (FundingUniverse; Britannica.com)

### Deniers
1. **Joseph Esposito, Britannica CEO (mid-1990s):** Dismissed CD-ROM encyclopedias as mere marketing tools: "Reference materials on CD-ROM are being 'bundled' in order to advance the claims of technology companies (principally Microsoft)..." — framing the existential threat as a temporary marketing gimmick. (Tedium)
2. **Britannica's institutional culture of denial:** The company had been around for over 200 years. Internal resistance was summarized by observers as: "This can't be happening to us, we have been around for 200 years, we have world authorities writing on every topic." The powerful sales force actively resisted digital transition because it would eliminate their jobs — and they influenced marketing and advertising decisions to keep nurturing door-to-door leads through the early 1990s. (StratoServe)

### Survival Pattern
Survivors abandoned the consumer door-to-door model entirely and pivoted to institutional sales (schools/libraries) or digital subscriptions — those who tried to preserve any part of the old model failed.

## Unique Texture

1. **The most extreme price disruption in the dataset:** Britannica $1,500–$2,200 → Encarta $99 → Wikipedia $0. A 100% price reduction in 15 years, with an intermediate step that was itself killed by the final disruptor. No other profession in this study faced a product that went from premium to literally free. (Multiple sources)

2. **Encarta killed the old model, then Wikipedia killed Encarta.** Microsoft discontinued Encarta in 2009, just 16 years after launch. The disruptor was itself disrupted. Tom Corddry's quote — "We knew from the beginning that we were a temporary business" — makes this the only case in the dataset where the killer openly acknowledged its own mortality. (Tedium)

3. **World Book went from 60,000 sales reps to 95.** A 99.84% reduction in sales force, yet the company still exists. This is the most dramatic workforce reduction of any surviving entity in the dataset. (Wikipedia/World Book; Publishers Weekly)

4. **The 2005 Nature study demolished Britannica's quality argument.** Jim Giles's study found Wikipedia averaged 3.9 errors per article vs. Britannica's 2.9 — close enough to destroy the claim that expert-written content was worth $1,500 more than free crowdsourced content. Britannica furiously disputed the study, but the damage was done. (Nature, 2005)

5. **Britannica's $650M-to-$135M fire sale happened in just 6 years (1990–1996)** — a 79% destruction of company value in less time than it takes to get a PhD. Jacob Safra bought the company for reportedly less than half its estimated book value. (FundingUniverse; Encyclopedia.com)

## Known Data Gaps

- **Total industry workforce:** No single source tracks the combined sales force across all US encyclopedia publishers. The ~65,000–70,000 peak estimate is constructed from individual company data (World Book 60,000 + Britannica 2,300 + estimated others). The real number could be higher if smaller publishers' forces are undercounted.
- **Median income:** Encyclopedia salespeople were overwhelmingly commission-only independent contractors, making them invisible to BLS wage surveys. Income estimates are based on commission structures and anecdotal accounts, not systematic data.
- **Post-displacement tracking:** No longitudinal data exists on where encyclopedia salespeople went after layoffs. The "where they went" section relies on one named example (insurance) and inference from growth in adjacent direct-selling industries.
- **World Book revenue:** As a subsidiary of Berkshire Hathaway (via Scott Fetzer), World Book's revenue was not broken out after the early 1990s when sales plunged. Warren Buffett stopped reporting it separately.
- **Non-US markets:** UK, Australian, and other international encyclopedia sales forces are even less documented than US ones. The ~65,000–70,000 figure is US-only.
- **Smaller publishers:** Compton's, Collier's, Americana, and Funk & Wagnalls sales force sizes are not individually documented in available sources.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium
- **Workforce numbers:** medium — World Book and Britannica have documented sales force sizes, but industry total requires estimation; the part-time/contractor nature of most reps makes headcounts unreliable
- **Income data:** low — almost entirely commission-based independent contractors with no systematic wage data; estimates based on commission structures and anecdotal accounts
- **Where-they-went:** low — one named example (insurance) plus inference; no systematic tracking of displaced encyclopedia salespeople exists
