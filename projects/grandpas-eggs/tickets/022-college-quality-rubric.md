# State College — Quality Rubric

## What to research

Define what "a year at a public four-year university" meant in ~1960 and build an honest multi-attribute quality rubric. This is the most important rubric ticket in the project because angle-02 flagged the college quality-equivalent comparison as the most susceptible to bias.

**The explore phase explicitly warned:** The first pass cherry-picked faculty attention as the ONE dimension where 1960 wins and priced the equivalent at $58K. That's not honest. This rubric MUST score ALL dimensions — including the many where 2025 is genuinely better.

## Research tasks

1. **1960 baseline:** What was the experience at a state flagship university? Class sizes, faculty credentials, course diversity, lab equipment, library resources, career services, campus facilities, student body diversity, completion rates.

2. **Quality attributes to score (minimum 6 — be thorough):**
   - Class size / student-faculty ratio
   - Percentage of courses taught by tenure-track faculty (vs. adjuncts/TAs)
   - Course/major diversity (how many programs offered)
   - Lab and research facilities
   - Library and information resources (physical 1960 vs. physical + digital 2025)
   - Career services and internship infrastructure
   - Campus facilities (dorms, dining, recreation, health services)
   - Student body diversity (racial, gender, socioeconomic)
   - Completion/graduation rates
   - Administrative overhead (ratio of administrators to faculty)

3. **Honest assessment:** On how many attributes is 2025 genuinely better? The answer is probably "most of them." The quality-equivalent framing for college may not cleanly apply — 2025 state college is better on most dimensions, just dramatically more expensive. That IS an interesting finding (pure cost inflation, not quality degradation), and the rubric should say so plainly.

4. **If quality degradation IS found**, it's likely concentrated in:
   - Faculty attention (larger classes, more adjuncts)
   - Administrative bloat (more of each tuition dollar goes to non-instructional purposes)
   These are real, but they don't dominate the overall quality picture.

## Data source hierarchy

1. NCES Digest of Education Statistics — historical and current data on class sizes, faculty composition, expenditure per student
2. AAUP (American Association of University Professors) — faculty salary and composition data
3. College Board Trends in College Pricing and Student Aid
4. Academic studies on higher education quality metrics over time
5. Historical university catalogs and accreditation reports

## Output format

Follow the **Type 2: Quality rubric** format from plan.md.

The overall assessment here might be "Mixed" with a note that most measurable attributes improved while cost exploded — making this primarily a cost story, not a quality-degradation story.

## What "good data" looks like

- At least 8 scored attributes
- Honest acknowledgment that 2025 state college is better on most dimensions
- Clear identification of the 1-2 dimensions where degradation occurred (faculty attention, instructional spending share)
- If the rubric concludes "progress wins on quality but cost exploded," that's a valid and interesting finding
