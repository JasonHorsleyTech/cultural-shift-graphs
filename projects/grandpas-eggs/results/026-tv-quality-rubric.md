# Television — Quality Rubric

## The 1955 baseline product

The standard television in 1955 was a **black-and-white CRT set with a 17–21" screen**, powered by vacuum tubes, receiving VHF channels 2–13 via a rooftop antenna. The 21-inch screen was becoming the new standard by mid-decade, replacing the earlier 12" and 17" models.

**Typical product:** An RCA "Medalist 21" or Zenith tabletop/console set. B&W, 21" diagonal screen, ~480-line interlaced resolution (equivalent to roughly 480i), built-in mono speaker, rotary channel dial and manual adjustment knobs (fine-tune, brightness, contrast, vertical/horizontal hold). Major brands included RCA, Zenith, Admiral, Philco, and DuMont.

**Price range:** $175–$300 for a standard tabletop B&W set; $300–$500 for a console (furniture-grade cabinet) model. The average TV price had fallen to roughly $200 by 1953–55 from ~$500 in the late 1940s. Color sets existed (RCA CT-100, later CTC-4) but cost $800–$1,000+ and were a tiny fraction of sales — the baseline product was unambiguously B&W.

**Content access:** In a major city, a viewer typically received 3–7 VHF stations — the three networks (ABC, CBS, NBC) plus sometimes a DuMont affiliate and/or independent stations. Rural areas might get 1–2 stations. All content was free over-the-air. By 1955 there were ~432 operating stations across 282 U.S. cities, and approximately 36 million TV households.

**Maintenance:** Sets contained 15–25 vacuum tubes that degraded over time (typical tube life 2,000–10,000 hours). Viewers regularly visited drug stores and hardware stores with tube testers, or called a TV repairman. Annual maintenance cost was meaningful but the set was fully serviceable — a thriving repair ecosystem existed.

## Quality attributes

| Attribute | 1955 Baseline | 2025 Cheapest | 2025 Quality-Equiv | Direction |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|--------------------|-----------| 
| Screen size | 17–21" diagonal | 55" (budget 4K LED, ~$230–280) | 55–65" (mid-range 4K, ~$300–500) | **Improved** — 3× larger for lower real cost |
| Picture quality (resolution, color, contrast) | ~480 lines, B&W only, low contrast, ghosting/snow common | 3840×2160 (4K), full color, HDR support, 60Hz | 3840×2160 (4K), full color, HDR10/Dolby Vision, 120Hz, local dimming | **Improved** — incomparably better on every dimension |
| Sound quality | Built-in mono speaker, 3–5W, adequate for the era; console models had larger speakers with richer bass | Thin-bezel design forces tiny downward-firing speakers; often tinny and bass-deficient; most buyers add a soundbar | Same thin speakers; mid-range sets may include slightly better processing but still thin | **Degraded** — 1955 console speakers were acoustically superior to most modern built-in TV audio; form factor trade-off |
| Reliability / maintenance burden | Required periodic tube replacement (1–3 tubes/year typical); warm-up time 15–30 seconds; occasional repairman visits | Solid-state — no maintenance, instant-on, no warm-up; but failures are catastrophic (board-level, not component-level) | Same as cheapest | **Improved** — vastly more reliable day-to-day; no scheduled maintenance |
| Content access | 3–7 free OTA channels via antenna; all content included with purchase | Built-in smart TV OS with streaming apps; but useful content requires internet ($50–85/mo) + streaming subscriptions ($50–75/mo average); free OTA still available via antenna (~30–100 channels in cities) | Same as cheapest | **Mixed** — enormously more content available, but access model shifted from free to subscription; total cost of viewing is dramatically higher |
| Energy consumption | 200–400W (vacuum tube + CRT) | 50–80W for a 55" LED (4K) | 60–100W for a 55–65" LED/QLED | **Improved** — 75–80% reduction despite 3× larger screen |
| Lifespan (years of useful service) | 10–20 years with maintenance (tube replacement, occasional CRT rejuvenation); many 1950s sets survived into the 1970s | 5–10 years; LED backlight degradation or board failure typically ends life; software updates may cease after 3–5 years | 7–12 years for premium brands; same fundamental limitation | **Degraded** — shorter usable life, and software obsolescence is a new failure mode |
| Repairability | Fully repairable — modular vacuum tubes, discrete components, widely available schematics; TV repair shops in every neighborhood | Essentially unrepairable — surface-mount components, proprietary boards, glued panels, no parts availability; economically irrational to repair a $250 TV | Slightly better parts availability for premium brands, but still mostly unrepairable | **Degraded** — from fully serviceable to disposable |

## Quality-equivalent product definition

The quality-equivalent framing **barely applies to television** because the 1955 product was the only tier — there was no premium vs. budget distinction. Every household that owned a TV had roughly the same product.

The closest modern equivalent to the 1955 experience would be: **any budget 55" 4K LED smart TV ($230–300)**, such as a TCL S4, Hisense A6, or Walmart's onn. brand. Even the absolute cheapest modern TV exceeds the 1955 baseline on screen size, resolution, color, contrast, reliability, and energy efficiency by enormous margins.

If we interpret "quality-equivalent" as "what matches the 1955 *experience* rather than specs" — a TV that provides a complete entertainment experience without ongoing subscription costs — then the equivalent would be the same budget TV **plus a digital antenna** (which delivers 30–100 free HD channels in most metro areas), at a total cost of ~$250–320. This is the only configuration that matches the 1955 content access model.

For price research purposes: **Line A (cheapest) and Line B (quality-equivalent) should be the same product** for television, since even the cheapest modern TV vastly exceeds the 1955 baseline. The interesting annotation is the **total cost of viewing** (TV purchase + content access), which has diverged dramatically.

## Divergence timeline

**There is no meaningful quality divergence for television.** Unlike food or clothing, TVs did not split into a "degraded cheap tier" and a "quality-equivalent expensive tier." Instead, the entire product category improved relentlessly:

- **1950s–1960s:** Single tier. B&W → color transition (color sets reached price parity by ~1970).
- **1970s–1980s:** Solid-state transistor sets replaced vacuum tubes. Incremental improvements in screen size (up to 25–27"), reliability, and color quality. Still essentially one tier — you bought whatever size/brand fit your budget, but the underlying technology was uniform.
- **1990s–2000s:** The flat-panel transition created temporary price tiers (CRT vs. plasma vs. LCD), but these converged quickly. By 2010, a $400 LCD was better than a $3,000 plasma from 2005.
- **2010s–2020s:** OLED created a genuine premium tier ($1,500+), but the budget tier (LED/QLED at $200–400) continued to improve year over year. A 2025 budget TV is objectively better than a 2015 flagship.

The split that matters for this project is not in the hardware but in the **content access model**: the shift from free OTA → cable ($30–100/mo, 1980s–2010s) → streaming ($50–150/mo, 2015–present). This is the real "quality tax" story for television, even though it's not embedded in the TV purchase price.

## Total cost of viewing annotation

This data is critical for the graph phase, even though it's not the primary metric:

| Era | TV hardware (amortized/year) | Content access (annual) | Total annual cost of viewing |
|-----|------------------------------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| 1955 | ~$25–40/yr (set cost / 10-yr life) | $0 (free OTA) | ~$25–40/yr |
| 1975 | ~$30–50/yr | $0 (free OTA, more channels) | ~$30–50/yr |
| 1990 | ~$40–60/yr | $300–600/yr (basic cable) | ~$350–660/yr |
| 2005 | ~$50–100/yr | $600–1,000/yr (expanded cable + internet) | ~$650–1,100/yr |
| 2025 | ~$30–60/yr (cheap TV / 7-yr life) | $1,200–2,400/yr (internet $700–1,000 + streaming $500–900 or cable $1,200–1,500) | ~$1,230–2,460/yr |

Note: These are rough estimates for annotation purposes. The hardware cost has fallen in real terms while the content access cost has exploded. A 2025 household pays 30–60× more annually to "watch TV" than a 1955 household, even though the TV itself is cheaper.

## Overall assessment

**Progress wins.** The modern cheap TV is superior on 5 of 8 measured attributes (screen size, picture quality, reliability, energy consumption, and content access volume). The 1955 product wins on 3 attributes (sound quality, lifespan, repairability), but these are modest compared to the scale of improvement on the winning dimensions. A $250 TV in 2025 would be indistinguishable from magic to a 1955 viewer.

The important caveat: **the TV purchase price tells only a fraction of the story.** The shift from free broadcast to paid subscription content means the total cost of television viewing has increased dramatically, even as the hardware has gotten cheaper and better. This is an annotation for the graph, not a change to the "progress wins" classification — the rubric evaluates the product (the television set), not the ecosystem.
