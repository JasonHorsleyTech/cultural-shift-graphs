# US Dollar as Global Reserve Currency

The dollar's dominance as the world's reserve currency is both a cause and effect of American cultural-economic power. When every country's central bank holds dollars, when oil is priced in dollars, when international trade is denominated in dollars — that's structural American dominance embedded in the global financial system.

**Metric:** US dollar's share of global foreign exchange reserves, by decade (1900-present).

**Data proxy:** IMF COFER data (1999-present), historical central bank reserve composition studies, Barry Eichengreen's academic work on reserve currencies, BIS data.

**Interesting angles:**
- British pound was the dominant reserve currency until WW1. Dollar overtook it in the 1920s (some argue as early as 1925).
- Bretton Woods (1944): formalized dollar dominance by pegging all currencies to the dollar (and dollar to gold).
- Nixon Shock (1971): ended gold convertibility. Dollar's share briefly dipped — but then recovered because there was no alternative.
- Petrodollar system (1974): Saudi Arabia agreed to price oil in dollars. Every oil-importing nation now needed dollar reserves.
- Euro's emergence (1999): was supposed to challenge dollar dominance. Track its reserve share — has it actually dented the dollar?
- China's renminbi internationalization push (2010s): IMF added it to SDR basket (2016). Has it moved the needle?
- Current dollar share (~58%) is lowest in decades but still overwhelmingly dominant. Track the slow decline narrative vs. actual data.
