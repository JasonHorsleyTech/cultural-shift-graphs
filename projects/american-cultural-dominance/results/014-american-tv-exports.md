# American TV Show Exports and Broadcast Hours Abroad

## Summary
American television programming dominated global TV exports from the 1960s through the 2000s, peaking around 2007 when US companies accounted for an estimated 76% of all finished programs exported worldwide. The trajectory follows a clear arc: negligible presence in the 1950s, rapid expansion through cheap syndication in the 1960s-70s, peak cultural penetration in the 1980s-90s (the "Dallas"/"Baywatch" era), sustained dominance through the 2000s, and then a measurable decline in the streaming era as platforms like Netflix shifted to commissioning local content. By 2025, US-made content's share of viewing on major streaming platforms outside the US had fallen to ~45%, and non-English titles comprised the majority of Netflix original TV releases for the first time.

## Penetration Score Definition
**Denominator:** US-produced content's share of all internationally exported/distributed television programming (finished programs and format licenses), weighted by the number of countries reached.
**0% anchor:** No American TV content is broadcast or streamed outside the United States. All countries produce or import exclusively non-US programming.
**100% anchor:** Every television program watched outside the US is American-produced. Foreign networks and streaming platforms carry nothing but US content.

## Methodology
The penetration score tracks American TV content's share of globally exported/distributed television programming across the full timeline. The metric combines:

1. **Share of finished program exports** (the primary measure where available — e.g., the documented 76% in 2007)
2. **Share of foreign broadcast schedules** (UNESCO studies from the 1970s, European Audiovisual Observatory data)
3. **Share of streaming platform catalogs and viewing** (post-2015, using Parrot Analytics and platform data)

For earlier decades (1955-1975), scores are estimated from the Nordenstreng & Varis UNESCO studies, academic literature on "one-way flow," and the number of countries with television service vs. countries receiving US content.

The methodology intentionally includes both direct exports (finished programs sold to foreign broadcasters) and format licenses (American formats adapted locally, e.g., The Office, Idol, Survivor), as both represent American cultural penetration of foreign TV schedules.

The scoring accounts for the EU's Television Without Frontiers Directive (1989) and its successors, which mandated 50%+ European content on European broadcasters. Compliance data (European works averaging 63-67% of broadcast hours in the early 2000s) helps calibrate the scores for that period.

Consistency note: The shift from broadcast-era metrics to streaming-era metrics around 2015 introduces a methodological discontinuity. The broadcast era measured "share of exports sold" while the streaming era measures "share of content viewed/cataloged." Both are valid proxies for cultural penetration but are not perfectly comparable. The transition is smoothed by using overlapping data where available.

## Notes
- **Format exports are an undercount problem.** When "Who Wants to Be a Millionaire" airs in India with an Indian host in Hindi, it's technically a British format adapted locally — but the format originated from the Anglo-American TV industry ecosystem. Similarly, The Office has 15 international adaptations. These format exports represent deep cultural penetration that isn't captured by "finished program" export statistics alone.
- **Telenovela counter-flow.** Latin American telenovelas (especially from Mexico's Televisa and Brazil's TV Globo) represent the most significant counter-flow to US TV dominance, exported to 100+ countries including reverse flow into the US via Spanish-language networks. This prevented US penetration from being even higher in Latin America.
- **Japan exception.** Japan was heavily dependent on US TV imports from 1945-1955 during post-war reconstruction, but by the 1960s was producing nearly all domestic content. Japan is one of the earliest examples of a country "graduating" from US TV dependence.
- **The quota paradox.** The EU's Television Without Frontiers Directive (1989) required 50%+ European content — but its very existence is evidence of how much American content was flooding European airwaves. Compliance hovered around 63-67% European content in the early 2000s, meaning American content still claimed a significant share of the remaining 33-37%.
- **Streaming disrupts the measurement.** In the broadcast era, you could count "hours of American programming on Channel X." In the streaming era, a single platform like Netflix carries thousands of titles from dozens of countries, and what matters is viewing share, not catalog share. US titles are 48% of European VOD catalog presences but viewing patterns may differ.
- **The 2020s structural shift is real.** Netflix is spending more on international content than US content for the first time. Non-English originals are now the majority of Netflix releases. Turkey and South Korea have emerged as major exporters. The US remains the single largest source country, but its share is declining from a historically unprecedented peak.
