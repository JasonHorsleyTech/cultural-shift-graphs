# American Cultural Dominance — The Americanization of Everything

## Core question

How thoroughly has American culture consumed the world's national cultures, and how far back does it actually go?

## The hypothesis

People living in Uruguay, Zimbabwe, Japan — everywhere — have become increasingly Americanized as American media, language, brands, and institutions spread globally. Most people date this to the internet age or maybe the 90s (kids in Zimbabwe hearing about Nickelodeon), but the real arc starts much earlier — English loanwords flooding Japanese since the Meiji era, Hollywood crushing European cinema in the 1920s, the dollar replacing the pound after WW1.

## What we're measuring

Multiple proxy metrics of American cultural influence, each with different historical reach, layered on the same timeline:

- **Language:** English loanword adoption in other languages (1800s+), English as second language rates, English replacing French as lingua franca
- **Film:** Hollywood's global box office share (1920s+), screen quotas passed defensively
- **Music:** English-language share of national music charts (1960s+), American genre adoption globally (jazz → rock → hip-hop)
- **Television/Streaming:** American TV exports, US content share on Netflix by country
- **Consumer brands:** Coca-Cola and McDonald's expansion timelines, American fashion adoption (jeans, sneakers)
- **Economic:** US dollar as reserve currency, American tech platform market share
- **Institutional:** Foreign students at US universities, American missionary/educational presence abroad
- **Military:** US bases abroad as cultural footprint
- **Media:** AP/UPI wire service dominance
- **Defensive responses:** Countries passing laws to resist Americanization (France's Toubon Law, screen quotas, tech bans)

## Why it works as a graph

No single metric is definitive — this is inherently somewhat subjective. But the convergence of 25+ independent measurements all pointing the same direction, each entering the chart when its data begins, makes the argument visually overwhelming. Loanwords from the 1800s, Hollywood from the 1920s, military bases from the 1940s, music charts from the 1960s, tech platforms from the 2000s — all telling the same story.

## What makes it interesting

Everyone knows America is culturally dominant. Nobody has seen it graphed across this many dimensions simultaneously, and almost nobody realizes how far back it goes.
