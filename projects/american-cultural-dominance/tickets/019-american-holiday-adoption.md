# American Holiday Adoption Abroad

Halloween, Black Friday, Valentine's Day (in its American commercial form), and even Thanksgiving-adjacent shopping events have spread to countries with no historical connection to them. When Japanese department stores put up Halloween decorations, that's American cultural export in its purest commercial form.

**Metric:** Number of non-US countries with significant commercial observance of American holidays (Halloween, Black Friday, Valentine's Day American-style), by decade. Secondary: retail sales data associated with these holidays in non-US markets.

**Data proxy:** Retail industry reports, national retail association data, news coverage of holiday adoption, consumer survey data on holiday participation by country.

**Interesting angles:**
- Halloween: virtually unknown outside North America before the 1990s. Now commercially observed in Japan, UK, Germany, France, Australia, and more. Track when each market started.
- Black Friday: purely American (day after Thanksgiving). Now a major retail event in UK, Germany, Brazil, India, and dozens of other countries that don't celebrate Thanksgiving. Amazon was a key vector.
- Valentine's Day: existed in some form in Europe, but the American commercial version (cards, chocolates, roses, dinner reservations) has been exported globally. Japan adopted it in the 1950s-60s via chocolate companies.
- "Singles' Day" (China, 11/11): a counter-example of a local commercial holiday, but one that was partly inspired by the American model of manufactured shopping events.
- Track retail spending associated with these holidays in non-US markets over time — the dollar amounts show depth of adoption.
