# US Freshwater Inventory & Renewable Supply

## Summary

The United States holds vast freshwater reserves — roughly 70,000–100,000+ km3 of fresh groundwater, 22,671 km3 in the Great Lakes alone, and hundreds of km3 in reservoirs. Its renewable supply is approximately 3,069 km3/year (total, including cross-border inflows). At the national level, total withdrawal peaked at ~20% of renewable supply in 1980 and has since declined to ~14.5% by 2015 — well below the 40% threshold that hydrologists consider "high stress." But national averages mask severe regional crises: the Colorado River is overallocated by 30%+, the Ogallala Aquifer is being drained 3–50x faster than it recharges, and California's Central Valley groundwater depletion is accelerating.

**Consumption as % of supply (limited data):** USGS reported total consumptive use only through 1995. The 1995 estimate was approximately 100 Bgal/d (138 km3/yr), or ~4.5% of renewable supply. Irrigation dominates consumption: in 2015, irrigation consumptive use alone was 73.2 Bgal/d. Total national consumption is likely 90–110 Bgal/d (~3.5–5% of renewable supply), far below the withdrawal percentage because most thermoelectric and public supply water is returned to waterways.

### Regional Stress Points

**Colorado River Basin**
- Legal allocation: 18.5 km3/year (15 million acre-feet between Upper/Lower Basin + Mexico treaty)
- Actual average natural flow: ~18.5 km3/year historically, declining to ~15 km3/year in recent decades due to aridification
- Overallocated by at least 30% during 2004–2013 drought period
- Groundwater depletion of 5.6 km3/year observed via GRACE satellite data (2004–2013)
- Consumptive use + reservoir evaporation routinely exceeds inflows

**Ogallala (High Plains) Aquifer**
- Spans 8 states; largest aquifer in North America
- Withdrawals exceed recharge by 3–50x depending on location; average ~8x
- Only ~1 inch of annual precipitation reaches the aquifer for recharge
- 9% of saturated volume depleted since 1950 (with much higher depletion in Kansas/Texas portions)
- Natural replenishment timeline: 6,000+ years
- Depletion rate observed: 1.24 feet/year vs recharge of 0.76 feet/year in studied areas

**California Central Valley**
- Annual groundwater overdraft: ~2 million acre-feet (~2.5 km3/year)
- Long-term depletion rate: 1.86 km3/year (1961–2021 average)
- Accelerating: 2.41 km3/year (2003–2021), 8.58 km3/year (2019–2021)
- Drought depletion: 19 km3 lost (2007–2009 drought), 28 km3 lost (2012–2016 drought)
- Post-drought recovery only 19–34% of overdraft volume
- Supplies ~25% of US food production; overdraft has national food security implications

## Key Findings

- **The US is not running out of water nationally.** At 14.5% of renewable supply (2015), total withdrawal is well below hydrological stress thresholds. Peak stress was ~20% in 1980, and the ratio has been declining since despite population doubling.
- **The withdrawal-to-supply ratio peaked in 1980 and is back to 1960s levels.** This happened because of thermoelectric efficiency gains (closed-loop cooling), industrial decline, and agricultural conservation — not reduced demand.
- **Consumption (water that doesn't come back) is only ~4–5% of renewable supply.** Most withdrawn water is returned. This is the strongest "no, we're not running out" data point.
- **Regional crises are real and severe.** The Colorado River, Ogallala Aquifer, and Central Valley are mining fossil water or overallocating renewable flows. These are not national problems — they're geographic mismatches between water supply and agricultural demand, particularly in the arid West.
- **Groundwater is the US's largest freshwater store but also its most vulnerable.** Unlike lakes and rivers (visible, monitored, regulated), aquifer depletion is invisible and largely unregulated in many states. The Ogallala will take millennia to recover.
