# State College — Quality Metrics

## Summary

The story is mixed but tilts toward improvement on measurable dimensions. Student-faculty ratios improved modestly, graduation rates rose steadily (52% → 64%), and total instructional spending per student increased in real terms. However, the *composition* of who teaches and where the money goes shifted dramatically: tenure-track faculty collapsed from ~53% to ~32% of all positions (replaced by adjuncts and contingent workers), and instruction's share of total spending fell from ~32% to ~26% as administrative and student-services spending grew faster. The university of 2025 spends more per student on instruction, but a smaller fraction of its budget — and a much larger share of that instruction is delivered by part-timers without institutional investment in their careers. Quality improved on the metrics students see (graduation rates, facilities, services) while degrading on the metrics they don't (who's actually teaching them and how secure those teachers' jobs are).

**Note on public 4-year specifically:** NCES COE reports that instruction was 34% of *core* expenses (a narrower category excluding hospitals, auxiliary enterprises, etc.) at public 4-year institutions in FY 2020-21, compared to 26-27% of *total* expenditures. The percentage of total is lower because public universities' total spending includes hospitals, auxiliary enterprises, and independent operations. The declining trend is consistent across both measures.

**Trend:** Clear decline. Instruction's share of total spending fell from roughly 38% in the late 1980s to 26% by 2022. This doesn't mean less money for instruction — absolute instructional spending per student rose — but it means other categories (student services, academic support, institutional support, research infrastructure) grew faster. At public 4-year institutions, academic support + student services + institutional support was 28% of core expenses in 2020-21, nearly matching instruction's 34%.

## Methodology

**Student-faculty ratio:** The best longitudinal data is for all degree-granting institutions combined (NCES Digest Table 301.20). Public 4-year breakouts only become available as standalone tables from ~2011 onward (Table 314.50). Pre-2011 estimates are derived from the all-institution series adjusted by the known relationship between public 4-year and all-institution ratios in years where both are available. The ratio counts all faculty (full-time + part-time), not FTE-adjusted, in the Table 301.20 series; the Table 314.50 series uses FTE, which produces slightly lower ratios.

**Faculty composition:** AAUP Data Snapshots (2018, 2023, 2025) are the definitive source, drawing on NCES NSOPF (1987-2003) and IPEDS (2003+). Pre-1987 data comes from NCES historical estimates and is less precise. The data covers all degree-granting institutions; public 4-year institutions have somewhat higher tenure-track shares than the national average but follow the same trend.

**Instructional spending:** NCES Digest Table 334.10 provides direct expenditure data for public institutions from FY 2009-10 onward. The Delta Cost Project database extends coverage back to 1987 but with some methodological differences. The percentage-of-total figure includes all institutional spending (hospitals, auxiliary enterprises, etc.); the percentage-of-core-expenses figure (used by NCES COE) excludes these and shows a higher instruction share (~34% vs. ~27%). Both show the same declining trend.

**Administrative staff:** Data comes primarily from the Goldwater Institute's 2010 analysis of IPEDS data (covering 1975-2005) and the American Council of Trustees and Alumni (ACTA) analysis covering 1993-2007. Post-2007 estimates are extrapolated from these trends and partial CUPA-HR data. The distinction between "administrators" (senior management) and "professional staff" (the broader category) matters enormously — the most dramatic growth is in the latter.

**Graduation rates:** NCES Table 326.10 is the definitive source, covering first-time, full-time bachelor's-seeking students at 4-year institutions from the 1996 cohort onward. This is the highest-confidence data series in this ticket.

**Class size:** No national longitudinal dataset exists for postsecondary class sizes. The Common Data Set collects this from individual institutions but is not aggregated nationally in a consistent way.

## Notes

**Which metrics show improvement:**
- **Graduation rates** — unambiguously improved, from ~52% to ~64% over two decades. This is the strongest quality signal.
- **Student-faculty ratio** — modestly improved, from ~17-18:1 to ~14:1. But this is partly illusory because much of the faculty growth was in part-time/adjunct positions.
- **Absolute instructional spending per student** — increased in real terms, even as its share of total spending declined.

**Which metrics show degradation:**
- **Faculty composition** — dramatic shift from tenure-track to contingent/adjunct. The professor your grandfather had was almost certainly full-time, tenure-track, and invested in the institution. The professor today may be a part-timer teaching at three schools, earning $3,000-5,000 per course with no benefits.
- **Instructional spending share** — declined from ~38% to ~26% of total expenditures. The university spends more on administration, student services, compliance, athletics, and facilities relative to instruction.
- **Administrative bloat** — administrators and professional staff grew far faster than faculty or enrollment. Some of this is defensible (IT, disability services, Title IX compliance), but the cumulative effect is that a smaller fraction of each tuition dollar reaches the classroom.

**Overall picture:** The college of 2025 produces better measurable *outcomes* (higher graduation rates, more student services, better facilities) but delivers instruction through a fundamentally degraded *labor model* (contingent workers instead of career faculty). Whether this constitutes quality improvement or degradation depends entirely on whether you measure the product (the degree, the graduation rate) or the process (who teaches, how they're employed, what share of the budget reaches the classroom). For the purposes of the grandpa's-eggs quality rubric — which asks "what did the 1955 baseline product look like vs. today?" — the honest answer is: *the measurable outputs improved, but the teaching workforce was hollowed out*.

**Confidence:** High for graduation rates and faculty composition. Medium for student-faculty ratios and instructional spending trends. Low for administrative staffing (depends heavily on definitions and data source) and class size (insufficient longitudinal data).
