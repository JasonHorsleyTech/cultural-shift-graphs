# Men's Suit — Quality-Equivalent Prices

## Summary

The quality-equivalent men's suit — defined as 100% wool worsted, half-canvas or full-canvas construction, made in a developed country — traces a distinctive arc. Before ~1985, this was simply the standard suit: every mainstream retailer sold wool/canvas/domestic products, so the "quality-equivalent" was the mid-range offering at Sears, JCPenney, or a department store. From the mid-1950s through the 1970s, the standard wool suit rose modestly from ~$43 to ~$85 as inflation and the polyester revolution reshaped the market. After the divergence point (~1985), when fused construction, synthetic fabrics, and offshore manufacturing created a separate "cheap" tier, the quality-equivalent migrated upmarket — first to Brooks Brothers and Hart Schaffner Marx at department stores ($200–$350), then to specialist retailers like Suitsupply ($500–$700 by 2025). In nominal dollars, the quality-equivalent suit price has risen roughly 14× since 1955 ($43 → $600), while the cheapest suit barely doubled ($30 → $55). The gap between these two lines IS the quality tax.

## Methodology

**Quality-equivalent operationalized as:** The lowest-priced new two-piece suit (jacket + matching trousers) available at a mainstream retailer that matches the 1955 baseline construction: 100% wool worsted fabric, half-canvas or full-canvas interlining, made in a developed country or to developed-country quality standards. This is the product that best approximates what EVERY suit was in 1955, now available only at a premium tier.

**Pre-divergence era (1950–1980):** Before ~1985, essentially all mainstream suits used wool, canvas construction, and domestic manufacture. The "quality-equivalent" WAS the standard product. I used the mid-range department store price (not the absolute cheapest) because the 1955 baseline is described as the typical middle-class purchase, not the rock-bottom option. The mid-range ran roughly 30–50% above the cheapest tier. Pre-1975, Line A and Line B are close. After 1975, Sears dropped wool entirely, so even finding a wool suit required trading up to a better retailer — the gap was opening.

**Post-divergence era (1985–2025):** After ~1985, the quality-equivalent became a distinct product segment. I anchored pricing to:
- **1982–2000:** Brooks Brothers mainline (Own Make → 1818 line) as upper anchor, Hart Schaffner Marx at department stores as mid-market anchor. Used mid-point of the quality-equiv range.
- **2000–2010:** Brooks Brothers 1818 and Hart Schaffner Marx as dual anchors, with the quality-equiv price reflecting what a consumer had to pay for wool + canvas + developed-country construction.
- **2010–2025:** Suitsupply enters as the primary anchor (~$500–$700 half-canvas S110s wool) alongside Spier & Mackay ($400–$500 full canvas). Brooks Brothers 1818 moved upmarket post-bankruptcy ($900–$1,200). Used Suitsupply's mid-range as the representative quality-equiv, since it's the closest modern analog to "Sears in 1955" — mass-market, accessible, consistent quality.

**Key source transitions:**
- **1950–1975:** Sears/JCPenney catalog data via ThePeopleHistory, RetroWow, and Cafe Hayek (Don Boudreaux analysis of 1975 Sears catalog). Supplemented with MentalScoop average price data.
- **1980–2000:** Brooks Brothers retail prices from Ask Andy About Clothes forum (specific 1982 price list) and Encyclopedia.com company history. BLS CPI for men's suits (1978–2025) used for trend direction. HSM pricing from Styleforum and Ask Andy forum discussions.
- **2000–2025:** Suitsupply official site pricing, Brooks Brothers retail site, Spier & Mackay, Gentleman's Gazette reviews, Men's Journal 2025 pricing guide, Gear Patrol suit recommendations.

**No CPI adjustment:** All prices are nominal dollars, per plan.md methodology.

## Known gaps

- **1950–1960:** No direct catalog prices found for mid-range wool suits. The $37 and $42 estimates are triangulated from cheapest-tier data plus average ranges. Could be off by ±$5–10.
- **1965–1975:** Very sparse data. The transition from universal wool to polyester makes this the murkiest period. The 1975 figure ($85) is extrapolated from the fact that Sears carried zero wool suits — consumers wanting wool had to pay department store or Brooks Brothers prices.
- **1980–2000:** Most estimates rely on extrapolation from a few Brooks Brothers anchor points (1982 price list) and BLS CPI trends. Direct retail price data for mid-tier quality-equiv suits (HSM at department stores) in this era is very hard to find via web sources. Vintage catalog data would improve this range significantly.
- **2010–present:** Much stronger data. Suitsupply and Brooks Brothers pricing is directly verifiable from current and archived retail sites. Confidence improves markedly from 2010 onward.
- **Brooks Brothers post-2020 discontinuity:** BB filed for bankruptcy in July 2020 and was acquired by SPARC (Authentic Brands + Simon Property Group) for $325M. Post-acquisition, 1818 line prices inflated significantly ($900–$1,200 vs. pre-bankruptcy $600–$800). BB is no longer the best quality-equiv anchor; Suitsupply fills that role.

**Overall confidence level:** Medium-low. The pre-1985 era relies on sparse catalog data and interpolation — individual data points could be off by ±15–25%. The 1985–2005 period is the weakest, estimated primarily from a handful of Brooks Brothers anchor prices and BLS CPI trends. From 2010 onward, confidence is medium-high thanks to directly verifiable Suitsupply and Brooks Brothers retail pricing. The overall trajectory — relatively flat pre-1975, steep climb 1975–2000 as quality-equivalent migrated upmarket, plateau at $500–$600 from 2010 onward — is well-supported even if individual data points carry significant error bars.
