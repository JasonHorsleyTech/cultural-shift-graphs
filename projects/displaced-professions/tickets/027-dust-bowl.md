# Ticket 027: Dust Bowl Farmers

## Event
The displacement of Great Plains farmers during the Dust Bowl, ~1930–1940. An environmental catastrophe amplified by agricultural practices (sod-busting, deep plowing) that turned drought into a disaster, driving ~2.5 million people to leave the Plains states.

## Mechanism
**Discrete shock** (environmental)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track population and agricultural employment in the most affected states (Kansas, Oklahoma, Texas panhandle, Colorado, New Mexico) from ~1925 to ~1945:

- Pre-Dust Bowl: Great Plains farming expanded dramatically in the 1910s–20s (WWI wheat demand)
- 1930–31: Drought begins
- 1934–35: Worst dust storms. "Black Sunday" (April 14, 1935)
- 1935: Soil Conservation Service established
- By 1940: ~2.5 million people had left the Great Plains (estimated). Not all were farmers, but the agricultural collapse drove the migration.
- Track farm population in affected counties specifically

**Source hierarchy:**
1. US Census of Agriculture, 1930 and 1940 (farm counts, farm population by state/county)
2. US Census of Population, 1930 and 1940 (county-level population changes)
3. Worster, "Dust Bowl: The Southern Plains in the 1930s" (1979) — the definitive history
4. Gregory, "American Exodus: The Dust Bowl Migration and Okie Culture in California" (1989)
5. Egan, "The Worst Hard Time" (2006) — narrative history of those who stayed
6. Historical Statistics of the United States — agricultural data

### Income Data
- Farm income in Great Plains states 1929–1940
- Wheat prices: collapsed from ~$1.18/bushel (1929) to ~$0.38 (1932) — both drought AND Depression
- Per capita income in affected counties vs. national average
- Migrant worker wages in California (the destination for "Okies")

**Known caveats:** The Dust Bowl overlapped with the Great Depression. Separating environmental displacement from economic displacement is impossible — they reinforced each other. Be explicit about this.

**Source hierarchy:**
1. USDA agricultural statistics (farm income, crop prices, farm counts)
2. Census farm population data
3. Worster (1979) — economic data
4. Gregory (1989) — migrant income data
5. California state labor reports on migrant workers

### Where They Went
- California (the "Okie" migration): estimated 300,000–400,000 from Oklahoma alone
- Other western states (Oregon, Washington, Arizona)
- Urban centers within Plains states
- Some stayed and adapted (changed farming practices, Soil Conservation Service programs)
- FSA (Farm Security Administration) camps — government migrant camps in California

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Gregory (1989) — migration data and destinations
2. US Census 1940 — state of birth vs. state of residence
3. California Department of Agriculture — migrant labor data
4. Steinbeck, "The Grapes of Wrath" (1939) — fictional but based on extensive reporting

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Farmers who adopted contour plowing, crop rotation, and conservation practices. Those who left early (1931–33) before prices collapsed further.
- **False movers:** Farmers who doubled down on wheat during the 1910s–20s boom, leveraging to buy more land. When prices and rain both failed, they were wiped out.
- **Deniers:** "The rain follows the plow" — the pseudoscientific belief that farming would increase rainfall. Promotional materials encouraging settlement of semi-arid land.
- **Survival pattern:** Diversified farming (not all-wheat), lower debt, willingness to adopt conservation practices or migrate.

### Unique Texture
- "Black Sunday" (April 14, 1935): a dust storm that turned day to night across the Great Plains. Robert Geiger coined the term "Dust Bowl" the next day.
- The human health toll: "dust pneumonia" killed an unknown number — hundreds at minimum
- Steinbeck's "The Grapes of Wrath" — the most famous literary depiction of labor displacement in American history
- The "Okie" stigma in California — treated as unwanted refugees in their own country
- Dorothea Lange's photographs (FSA) — second most powerful visual documentation in this dataset after Lewis Hine's child labor photos
- The irony: the Southern Plains are farmed again today, but with the Ogallala Aquifer declining — a slow-motion replay?

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Population change in affected counties (1930 vs. 1940)
- Farm counts and farm acreage in Great Plains states
- Wheat prices 1925–1940
- California migration numbers from Plains states
- At least one county-level case study (Baca County CO, Cimarron County OK, or similar)
- Separation of Dust Bowl displacement from general Depression effects (at least attempted)
