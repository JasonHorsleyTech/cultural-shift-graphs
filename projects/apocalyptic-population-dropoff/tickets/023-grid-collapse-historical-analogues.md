# Ticket 023: Grid Collapse — Historical Analogues and Partial Precedents

## What to research

No country has experienced a permanent, nationwide grid collapse. But several partial precedents exist. What can they tell us about the mortality curve?

## Specific events to cover

1. **Puerto Rico post-Hurricane Maria (2017):**
   - Grid was ~95% destroyed
   - Official death toll: 2,975 (revised from initial 64)
   - Harvard/GWU study estimated ~4,645 excess deaths
   - Recovery timeline: months to years, with external aid
   - Key lesson: mortality was mainly from medical care loss, not direct storm damage
   - Population: 3.4M → significant outmigration (how many left?)

2. **Venezuela economic collapse (2016-present):**
   - Rolling blackouts, partial grid failure for years
   - Healthcare system collapse — mortality increase
   - Population exodus (~7M have left)
   - Key lesson: gradual grid degradation shows what fails first

3. **Iraq post-2003 invasion:**
   - Deliberate infrastructure destruction
   - Grid operated at ~30-50% capacity for years
   - Excess mortality estimates: Lancet studies (controversial)
   - Key lesson: partial grid with international support still produces significant excess mortality

4. **Lebanon (2020-present):**
   - Grid operating at ~2-4 hours/day
   - Generator culture as adaptation
   - Hospital and healthcare impacts
   - Key lesson: society adapts but at enormous cost

5. **Northeast US blackout (2003):**
   - 55 million people, 1-2 days
   - ~11 deaths attributed directly
   - Key lesson: short-duration blackout ≠ long-duration collapse. The curve changes character after ~72 hours.

6. **Texas winter storm Uri (2021):**
   - Partial grid failure for 3-5 days in winter
   - Estimated 246-702 excess deaths
   - Key lesson: even brief winter grid failure kills via hypothermia and medical care loss

## Where to look

1. **GWU / Harvard — Puerto Rico mortality study (2018)**
2. **WHO / PAHO** — Venezuela health crisis reports
3. **Lancet — Iraq mortality studies (2004, 2006)** — note the methodological controversy
4. **World Bank / IMF** — Lebanon infrastructure reports
5. **NERC — 2003 blackout after-action report**
6. **Texas DSHS** — Winter Storm Uri excess mortality analysis

## Result format

Key Data Points table. For each event:
- `population_affected`
- `grid_loss_pct`
- `duration_days`
- `excess_deaths` (with range)
- `excess_death_rate_per_month` (deaths per 1000 population per month of crisis)
- `external_aid_available` (yes/no — critical variable)
- `primary_cause_of_death`

## What good data looks like

- Puerto Rico is the most valuable analogue — most complete data, modern infrastructure
- Extrapolation to a nationwide event WITHOUT external aid is the key analytical step
- Notes that all analogues had external aid arriving eventually. A full US grid collapse has no external aid at US scale
- The excess death rate per month is the key number to extract — it can be scaled (cautiously) to the US population
