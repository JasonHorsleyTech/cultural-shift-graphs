# English Loanwords in French

France is the world's most aggressive defender against English loanword adoption, making the data doubly interesting — both the adoption rate AND the resistance are measurable. The Académie française has been fighting anglicisms since the 1800s. The Toubon Law (1994) literally made it illegal to use English in advertising, government, and broadcasting without a French equivalent.

**Metric:** Number of new English-origin words entering standard French dictionaries per decade (Le Petit Robert, Le Petit Larousse).

**Data proxy:** French lexicographic studies on anglicismes. Academic tracking of words added to major dictionaries. The Académie française publishes periodic lists of rejected anglicisms with their prescribed French alternatives.

**Interesting angles:**
- 19th century: English words entered French mainly through commerce and industry (railway, tunnel, sport).
- Post-WW1: American military presence brought a wave.
- Post-WW2: another wave, plus Marshall Plan economic influence.
- 1960s-70s: enough concern to coin "franglais" as a pejorative.
- Toubon Law (1994): government legislates against English. Enforcement data exists — fines issued, cases brought.
- Internet era: French youth increasingly code-switch to English despite official resistance.
- Compare adoption rate vs. Académie rejection rate — the gap shows how much pressure exists.
