# English as Second Language — Global Rates

## Summary
The percentage of the non-Anglophone world population studying or speaking English as a second/foreign language grew from roughly 3% in 1900 to ~33% by 2020 — a tenfold increase driven by three waves: post-WWII American superpower status and Cold War cultural diplomacy (1945–1970), globalization and the internet (1990–2005), and Asian mandatory-education mandates (1997–2010). The curve shows accelerating growth from 1950 to 2010, with signs of plateau around 2020 as proficiency actually begins declining despite high enrollment — consistent with David Graddol's 2006 prediction of a peak near 2 billion learners.

## Penetration Score Definition
**Denominator:** Percentage of the world's non-native-English population that is actively studying or has achieved functional conversational proficiency in English as a second or foreign language.
**0% anchor:** No one outside native-English-speaking countries studies or speaks English. English has zero international reach as a learned language.
**100% anchor:** Every person in every non-Anglophone country is studying or has achieved functional English proficiency. Universal global bilingualism with English.

## Methodology
The penetration score represents the estimated percentage of the world's non-Anglophone population that is either actively studying English or has achieved functional conversational proficiency as a second/foreign language.

**Numerator construction:** Combines three populations: (1) people in non-Anglophone countries who speak English as a statutory second language (e.g., India, Nigeria, Philippines), (2) people actively enrolled in English-as-foreign-language education (school systems, private tutoring, online courses), and (3) adults who completed English education and retain functional proficiency. These overlap somewhat but together represent "people for whom English is a real presence in their life."

**Denominator:** Total world population minus the population of countries where English is the primary native language (US, UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, Ireland — roughly 400-500M depending on year).

**Pre-1960 estimates** are extrapolated from colonial education records, British Council historical reports, and David Crystal's reconstruction in "English as a Global Language" (2nd ed., 2003). These are order-of-magnitude estimates. The 1960 figure (~300M) is anchored to British Council estimates from that era.

**Post-2000 estimates** are better sourced: British Council's own surveys, EF Education First's proficiency testing of millions, Eurostat's comprehensive education statistics, and national education ministry data from China, South Korea, Japan, and others.

**Consistency:** The same conceptual measure (% of non-Anglophone population with English exposure) is used throughout. The proxy shifts from "colonial/elite education" in early decades to "mandatory school enrollment + adult proficiency" in later decades, reflecting the genuine change in how English is learned — but the underlying question ("what fraction of non-English-speakers are learning English?") stays constant.

## Notes
- **British vs. American attribution:** English's global spread is partly a British colonial legacy (India, Nigeria, Kenya, etc. inherited English from the British Empire, not America). However, the post-1945 explosion in *voluntary* English adoption — countries choosing to mandate English in schools — is overwhelmingly driven by American economic, technological, and cultural dominance. The metric captures both, which slightly inflates the "American" attribution for the pre-1945 period.
- **The 2020s plateau is real:** Despite near-universal English mandates in school curricula worldwide, actual proficiency is declining. The EF EPI shows 60% of countries with lower scores in 2024 vs. 2023. This suggests the low-hanging fruit (mandating English) is exhausted, and the next phase of penetration requires deeper cultural integration — or may not happen at all.
- **China is the swing factor:** With ~400M learners (more than the populations of the US and UK combined), China's English education policy has outsized impact on the global number. China's 2001 mandate was triggered by WTO accession — a direct response to American-led globalization.
- **"English fever" as evidence of cultural pressure:** South Korea spends ~$15.8B annually on English education — roughly 1% of GDP. This extraordinary expenditure is itself evidence of perceived American cultural/economic dominance. Parents invest because English proficiency is seen as essential for economic mobility in an American-dominated global economy.
- **Former French colonies switching:** The trend of former French colonies in Africa and North Africa (Tunisia, Morocco, Algeria, Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger) rejecting French in favor of English is one of the most striking recent developments — countries choosing to align with Anglo-American rather than Francophone cultural influence.
- **Data gap:** Reliable global statistics before 1960 are sparse. The pre-1960 estimates are constructed from fragmentary colonial education records and should be treated as order-of-magnitude.
