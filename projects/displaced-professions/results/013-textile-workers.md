# US Textile Mill Workers

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Textile mill production workers (weaving, spinning, dyeing, finishing)
- **Country/Region:** United States, concentrated in the Carolinas, Georgia, and New England
- **Peak Year:** 1973
- **Peak Workforce:** 1,300,000 (textile mills SIC 22); 2,380,400 combined textile + apparel (SIC 22+23) (BLS CES, American Affairs Journal)
- **Primary Mechanism:** gradual_erosion
- **Cause Tags:** globalization, tech_tool, regulatory
- **Killing Event:** Decades-long offshoring of production to low-wage countries, accelerated by NAFTA (1994), China's WTO accession (2001), and the expiration of the Multi-Fibre Arrangement (2005)

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1973 (sustained decline from peak; some cyclical recoveries in late 1970s)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~7 years (by ~1980, textile mills fell from 1.3M to ~848K = 35% decline)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** ~17 years (by ~1990, fell to ~600K)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** ~30 years (by ~2003, textile mills ~325K)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** ~40 years (by ~2013, textile mills ~130K)
- **Decline Shape:** s_curve — slow erosion through the 1970s–1980s, steepening sharply after NAFTA (1994) and China WTO (2001), then flattening as the remnant stabilized around technical/industrial textiles
- **Current Status:** remnant (<10% peak) — approximately 90,000 textile mill workers remain (NAICS 313), ~7% of peak

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: estimated

Textile workers were consistently among the lowest-paid manufacturing workers in the US. Specific textile mill wage series are fragmented across SIC/NAICS transitions.

- **Income at Peak:** ~$3.50/hr (textile mills, 1973, nominal) — approximately $2.75–$3.50/hr for production workers vs. manufacturing average of ~$4.09/hr (BLS CES)
- **Income at Trough:** $15.32/hr (textile product mills, 2018, nominal) (BLS MLR 2019)
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** N/A — decline was gradual, not a discrete shock. Workers remaining in the industry saw steady nominal wage increases that roughly tracked inflation but fell further behind manufacturing averages.
- **Inflation Adjustment Method:** CPI-U

## Surplus Capture

- **Capital owners:** 30% — Textile firms that offshored captured massive margin improvements; retailers and brands (Gap, J.C. Penney, Walmart) gained enormously from sourcing in Bangladesh, Vietnam, China. Fast fashion business model became viable.
- **Consumers:** 50% — Clothing prices increased only 8.9% in nominal terms from 1990–2026, while overall CPI rose ~130%. Apparel became one of the least inflationary consumer categories. Europeans increased clothing purchases by 40% in weight between 1996–2012 as prices fell.
- **Adjacent workers:** 5% — Workers in overseas textile industries gained employment but at very low wages; fewer than 2% of global fashion workers earn a living wage. US logistics/import workers saw some gains.
- **Government/public:** 15% — Import tariff revenue (before reduction), plus reduced consumer expenditure on clothing freed up household spending for other taxable consumption. TAA and retraining programs represented a small public cost.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
1. **Milliken & Company (Spartanburg, SC):** Founded in 1865, peaked at 25,000 employees in the 1980s. When free trade made commodity textiles uncompetitive, Milliken diversified into specialty chemicals, healthcare, and technical textiles. Won the Malcolm Baldrige National Quality Award in 1989. Shrank to ~7,000 employees but survived as a diversified materials company. During COVID-19, pivoted to manufacturing medical PPE barrier fabrics.
2. **Mount Vernon Mills:** Once a mass-market denim producer for big-box retailers, pivoted to specialty stretch denims and fire-resistant fabrics for electricians and oil-field workers — markets where price sensitivity is lower than performance requirements.

### False Movers
1. **Automation-as-salvation bet:** Multiple US mills invested heavily in automated looms and robotics in the 1980s–1990s, expecting to compete on productivity with Asian labor. While this improved output per worker, labor costs were only part of the equation — overseas mills also automated while retaining their wage advantage. The investment delayed closures but didn't prevent them.
2. **Workers who followed relocated mills:** Some workers relocated from New England to the Carolinas in the 1950s–1970s chasing mills that moved south for lower wages, only to see those same mills close or offshore within a generation.

### Deniers
1. **ATMI (American Textile Manufacturers Institute):** Lobbied for decades for import quotas and tariff protection. Got three major protectionist bills through Congress — all vetoed by the President. Senior executives launched a 2003 grassroots campaign declaring "job-destroying Chinese imports" must be stopped. Allen E. Gant Jr. (CEO, Glen Raven) noted 300,000 jobs lost since January 2001 alone. The organization eventually disbanded, its members acknowledging that "joining the free trade movement" was preferable to fighting it.
2. **Multi-decade tariff faith:** The Multi-Fibre Arrangement (1974–2004) provided 30 years of quota protection that slowed but never reversed the decline. Industry leaders repeatedly told Congress that quotas would "save the industry" — yet employment fell continuously throughout the MFA's entire existence.

### Survival Pattern
Survivors either moved upmarket into technical/performance textiles where competition is on innovation rather than cost, or diversified entirely out of textiles (like Milliken into chemicals).

## Unique Texture

1. **The racial integration irony:** Southern textile mills were almost entirely white until the Civil Rights Act of 1964 — Black employment rose from 3.3% in 1960 to 25% by 1978. By the time Black workers finally had equal access to textile jobs, the industry was entering terminal decline. (Minchin, "Hiring the Black Worker," 1999)

2. **Cannon Mills/Pillowtex — largest single-day layoff in NC history:** On July 30, 2003, 7,650 workers were laid off overnight across multiple plants (4,340 in Kannapolis alone). The 6-million-square-foot mill building — larger than the Pentagon — sat empty. Mayor Darrell Hinnant: workers had "tears dripping off their chins. Some were collapsing on the sidewalk." (EdNC, 2023)

3. **Cotton production survived; manufacturing didn't:** The US remains the world's third-largest cotton producer and #1 cotton exporter (providing ~35% of global cotton trade). But the raw cotton is shipped overseas, processed into clothing abroad, then imported back. American farms grow the fiber; Bangladeshi and Vietnamese workers make the shirts. (USDA ERS)

4. **Apparel prices barely moved in 35 years:** A $20 garment in 1990 cost about $21.78 in 2026 — an 8.9% nominal increase while overall CPI rose ~130%. Clothing became one of the least inflationary consumer categories in American history, subsidized by the displacement of US textile workers. (BLS CPI-U Apparel Index; OfficialData.org)

5. **800+ mills closed in a single decade (1975–1985):** And that was before NAFTA or China's WTO entry. The acceleration after 2001 turned a slow bleed into a hemorrhage — the combined textile+apparel workforce dropped from 1.57 million (1993) to 752,800 (2003), losing more than half in just 10 years. (NCpedia; USITC)

## Known Data Gaps

- **Pre-1990 textile-only employment:** SIC 22 annual data exists in BLS archives but was not consistently available via web-accessible FRED/BLS interfaces. Numbers for 1973–1989 are derived from BLS publications and academic sources rather than continuous time series.
- **Textile mills vs. textile products vs. apparel:** The three subsectors (NAICS 313, 314, 315) track differently. Many published figures combine them. The ticket scope is "textile mills" but much available data covers the combined sector.
- **Individual-level wage tracking:** No panel data following specific displaced textile workers over time. Income trajectory for the occupation is tracked; individual recovery is inferred from aggregate data and case studies (Pillowtex, TAA outcomes).
- **Precise "where they went" data:** No comprehensive national survey of displaced textile workers. Estimates are based on regional case studies (Pillowtex response, NC community college retraining data) and BLS Displaced Worker Survey aggregates.
- **SIC-to-NAICS transition:** Data comparability across the 1997 classification change is imperfect. Employment figures from 1990–1997 use SIC; after 1997 use NAICS. Minor definitional differences exist.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium
- **Workforce numbers:** medium-high — Peak and recent data are well-documented via BLS; mid-period estimates have gaps due to SIC/NAICS transition
- **Income data:** medium — Textile workers' relative position (lowest-paid manufacturing subsector) is well-established; specific hourly wage figures for individual years are fragmented across sources
- **Where-they-went:** medium-low — No comprehensive national tracking; best data comes from Pillowtex case study and TAA program outcomes, which may not be representative of the broader population
