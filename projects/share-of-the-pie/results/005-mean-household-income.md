# Result: Mean Household Income (Nominal), 1967–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** U.S. Census Bureau, Current Population Survey (CPS) Annual Social and Economic Supplement (ASEC), Historical Income Table H-5: "Race and Hispanic Origin of Householder — Households by Median and Mean Income" (All Races, Current Dollars column). Downloaded from https://www2.census.gov/programs-surveys/cps/tables/time-series/historical-income-households/h05.xlsx
- **Date accessed:** 2026-04-29

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Mean (average) total money income of all households in the United States, in current (nominal) dollars. Computed by dividing aggregate household income by the number of households. "Money income" includes wages, salaries, self-employment income, interest, dividends, rents, royalties, Social Security, public assistance, pensions, and other regular payments. Excludes capital gains, noncash benefits (employer health insurance, food stamps, housing subsidies), and imputed income.
- **Universe:** All households (one or more persons sharing a housing unit), householder age 15+. Includes single-person households, unrelated individuals, and families.
- **Survey:** CPS ASEC, conducted each March. Income refers to the prior calendar year (e.g., the March 2025 survey collects 2024 income).
- **Key difference from median:** The mean is pulled upward by high earners, making it systematically higher than the median. The mean/median ratio is a direct measure of income skew — it was ~1.12 in 1967 and ~1.45 by 2024, reflecting increasing concentration at the top.
- **Known methodology changes:**
  - **2013 CPS redesign (footnote 38→39):** Redesigned income questions captured previously unmeasured income. The new-methodology mean ($75,200) is ~3.5% higher than the old-methodology value ($72,640). Both values are included in the table; the new-methodology value is consistent with 2014+.
  - **2017 processing system update (footnote 40):** Census implemented an updated data processing system. The updated-processing mean ($87,640) is ~1.6% higher than the legacy value ($86,220). The updated value is consistent with 2018+.
  - **2020 pandemic (footnote 41):** Lower CPS ASEC response rates due to COVID-19 may introduce nonresponse bias. Income estimates were potentially biased upward by 2–4% due to lower-income households being harder to reach. Stimulus payments (Economic Impact Payments) are included in money income.
  - **Minor adjustments:** Various footnotes in earlier years (1967, 1971, 1972, 1974–1976, 1979, 1984–1985, 1987, 1992–1995, 1999–2000, 2004, 2009–2010) indicate weighting changes, population control updates, and sample redesigns. These are small enough that the Census Bureau treats the series as continuous.

## Gaps and Caveats

- **No data before 1967.** The CPS ASEC did not track household income before 1967. The mean household income series begins in 1967, coinciding with the start of the median series (ticket 003). There is no reliable way to reconstruct pre-1967 mean household income (unlike median family income, which has some decennial Census anchors — see ticket 004).
- **2013 methodology break (~3.5% jump).** The CPS redesign raised measured mean income more than it raised the median (~3.5% vs. ~3.2%), consistent with the redesign capturing previously unmeasured income that skews toward higher earners. For graphing, use the new-methodology 2013 value ($75,200), which is consistent with 2014+. The old-methodology value ($72,640) is consistent with 2012 and earlier.
- **2017 processing system change (~1.6% difference).** The two 2017 estimates differ by $1,420. For continuity with 2018+, use the updated processing system value ($87,640).
- **2019 jump is notably large.** Mean income jumped from $90,020 (2018) to $98,090 (2019), a 9.0% increase in one year. This is partly a real increase (strong labor market, stock market gains flowing to top earners who pull the mean) and partly reflects the cumulative effect of the 2017 processing update being fully phased in. The median jumped similarly (~8.7%). Tax Cuts and Jobs Act effects on reported income may also play a role.
- **2020 anomaly.** Mean household income actually *fell* slightly ($98,090 → $97,470) despite stimulus payments, because job losses and reduced earnings among higher earners pulled the mean down. This contrasts with the median, which also fell. The mean/median ratio stayed roughly constant at ~1.43.
- **Mean is more sensitive to top-coding.** The Census Bureau top-codes very high incomes to protect confidentiality. This compresses the right tail and makes the reported mean lower than the true mean. The degree of top-coding has changed over time, generally becoming less restrictive, which means some of the apparent increase in mean income is an artifact of measurement rather than real income growth. This effect is modest but real.
- **"Money income" definition is the same as ticket 003.** Excludes employer health insurance, noncash transfers, and capital gains. The CBO data (tickets 011–013) uses a broader income definition that better captures total resources.
- **Mean/median ratio as inequality measure:**
  - 1967: $7,989 / $7,143 = 1.118
  - 1980: $21,060 / $17,710 = 1.189
  - 2000: $57,140 / $41,990 = 1.361
  - 2024: $121,000 / $83,730 = 1.445
  - The steady increase confirms that income growth has been concentrated at the top.
- **Confidence:**
  - 1967–2012: **High.** Census CPS is the gold standard for income measurement.
  - 2013: **Medium.** Methodology break creates ambiguity about the exact level.
  - 2014–2019: **High.** Consistent post-redesign, post-processing-update methodology.
  - 2020: **Medium.** Pandemic-related response rate issues and potential nonresponse bias.
  - 2021–2024: **High.** Normal data collection resumed.
