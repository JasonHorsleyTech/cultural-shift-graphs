# Result: Pre-Columbian US Population — Baseline for Land Carrying Capacity

## Summary

Pre-Columbian population estimates for the area of the modern United States range from ~1.9M to ~18M, with current scholarly consensus settling around 5–8M as the most defensible central estimate. This population was supported by a mix of intensive agriculture (Eastern Woodlands, Southwest) and hunter-gatherer/fishing economies (Great Plains, Pacific Northwest), demonstrating what the land can sustain without fossil fuels, industrial chemistry, or mechanized farming. The post-contact population collapse of ~90–95% over 150 years — driven primarily by epidemic disease — provides a real-world analogue for civilizational disruption, though the mechanism (virgin-soil epidemics) differs fundamentally from infrastructure collapse.

## Why This Is a Lower Bound (Not a Ceiling) for Post-Collapse Carrying Capacity

Pre-Columbian populations operated without several advantages a post-collapse modern population would retain:

1. **No Old World domesticated animals.** No horses, cattle, pigs, or chickens until European introduction. Draft animals alone multiply agricultural output 3–5x.
2. **No steel tools.** Stone and bone tools are effective but slower. Even crude post-collapse metalworking exceeds pre-Columbian tooling.
3. **No Old World crop varieties.** Wheat, rice, barley, oats were unavailable. Post-collapse populations would have seed stock for high-yield modern varieties.
4. **No formal crop rotation.** European/Asian agricultural knowledge (nitrogen fixation via legumes, fallow systems) increases per-acre yield significantly.
5. **Retained knowledge.** Books, trained farmers, agricultural extension knowledge would persist even without electricity. The knowledge base is fundamentally different from building it from scratch.

However, pre-Columbian populations had advantages a post-collapse population would lack:

1. **Centuries of accumulated ecological knowledge.** Which local plants are edible, seasonal patterns, soil characteristics — knowledge built over millennia.
2. **Population-environment equilibrium.** Pre-Columbian populations had millennia to reach equilibrium with their environment. A post-collapse population of 335M would face a catastrophic overshoot problem.
3. **No degraded ecosystems.** Modern agriculture has depleted topsoil, drawn down aquifers (Ogallala), eliminated old-growth forests, and reduced biodiversity. The land pre-contact was far more productive per acre for low-input agriculture.
4. **No chemical dependency.** Modern crop varieties are bred for high-input farming (fertilizer, pesticide). Without these inputs, yields for modern seeds may be lower than pre-Columbian landraces adapted to local conditions.

**Net assessment for the graph:** Pre-Columbian population of ~5–8M represents a plausible lower bound for long-term US carrying capacity without industrial agriculture. With retained knowledge and Old World crops/animals, a post-collapse equilibrium population could be significantly higher — perhaps 50–130M (the range for pre-industrial European-style agriculture applied to US arable land). But the transition period (years 1–10) is the killer: 335M people cannot be fed by pre-industrial methods, and the die-off during transition to lower-input farming would be severe regardless of eventual carrying capacity.

## Regional Population Distribution — Relevance to Post-Collapse

| region | pre_columbian_pop_pct | agricultural_basis | modern_pop_pct | post_collapse_relevance |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Eastern Woodlands / Southeast | ~40% | Three Sisters agriculture, rich river valleys | ~45% | High. Best farmland in US. Would remain primary population center. |
| California | ~15% | Acorn gathering, fishing, diverse foraging | ~12% | Medium. Mediterranean climate is favorable but water infrastructure is critical. |
| Pacific Northwest | ~10% | Salmon fisheries, cedar plank culture | ~4% | Medium. Salmon runs could support modest population without agriculture. |
| Southwest | ~10% | Irrigation agriculture (Hohokam, Puebloan) | ~8% | Low-Medium. Water-limited. Irrigation systems require maintenance. |
| Great Plains | ~10% | Bison hunting, some marginal agriculture | ~10% | Low. Without modern irrigation (Ogallala aquifer), carrying capacity drops dramatically. |
| Great Basin / Plateau | ~8% | Foraging, seasonal movement | ~3% | Very low. Arid, low carrying capacity. |
| Arctic / Subarctic | ~7% | Hunting, fishing | ~2% | Very low. Extreme climate limits population. |

The pre-Columbian population distribution roughly mirrors where post-collapse populations would concentrate: the Eastern Woodlands and Southeast, where rainfall agriculture is most productive. The Great Plains — now home to ~10% of the US population thanks to the Ogallala aquifer and mechanized farming — supported very low pre-Columbian populations and would revert toward that baseline without industrial inputs.

## Data Gaps

- **Specific regional population estimates remain highly uncertain.** The breakdown by culture area in the table above is informed inference, not precise data. Scholarly estimates for individual regions vary by 3–5x.
- **Carrying capacity modeling is sparse.** No rigorous study maps pre-Columbian population to carrying capacity in terms useful for post-collapse projection. The connection requires inference across disciplines (archaeology, agronomy, ecology).
- **The Dobyns high-end estimates (12–18M) remain contested.** If correct, they significantly raise the implied carrying capacity floor. The methodological debate (depopulation ratios applied to early colonial censuses) is unresolved.
- **The 2025 Kelly et al. PNAS study** using 61,000 radiocarbon dates shows pre-contact population peaked around 1150 CE and declined ~30% by 1450 CE — meaning European contact hit an already-declining population. This complicates the "disease did everything" narrative and suggests environmental/climatic factors also constrained population.
- **Post-contact decline timeline is approximate.** Year-by-year population figures for 1492–1900 don't exist. The table above interpolates between well-established anchor points (pre-contact estimate, 1900 census nadir).
