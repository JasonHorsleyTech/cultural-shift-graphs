# English Loanwords in German

German has absorbed English heavily, especially post-WW2. The phenomenon is prominent enough to have its own name: "Denglisch" (Deutsch + English). Unlike French, Germany has no language police — the Verein Deutsche Sprache campaigns against anglicisms but has no legal authority, making German a useful "undefended" comparison to French.

**Metric:** Number of new English-origin loanwords entering standard German dictionaries per decade (Duden is the standard reference).

**Data proxy:** Duden dictionary editions track new additions. Academic studies on anglicisms in German (Anglizismen-Index) catalog English loanwords with dates of first attestation.

**Interesting angles:**
- Pre-WW2: English influence existed but was modest (Sport, Trainer, fair).
- Post-WW2 American occupation of West Germany created massive cultural influence — American GIs, Armed Forces Network radio, Hollywood films.
- East Germany (DDR) actively suppressed English loanwords in favor of Russian — compare adoption rates East vs. West as a natural experiment.
- Reunification (1990) suddenly exposed 16 million East Germans to the anglicized West German vocabulary.
- Business German is now heavily anglicized (Meeting, Feedback, Deadline, Team, Marketing).
- Compare the "undefended" German adoption rate to "defended" French — how much difference does legislation actually make?
