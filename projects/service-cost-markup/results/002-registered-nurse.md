# Healthcare — Registered Nurse

## Summary

The registered nurse's share of healthcare spending has been remarkably stable compared to physicians — declining only about 17% over 64 years (from ~7.6% to ~6.3%), versus the PCP's 83% collapse. The ratio peaked around 1980 (~8.6%) during a massive nursing workforce expansion, held steady at 7-7.5% from 1990-2010, then dropped sharply in the 2010s as NHE growth outpaced wage growth. The post-COVID travel nursing explosion partially reversed the decline, pushing the 2024 ratio back up to ~6.3%.

## Methodology

**Ratio formula:** RN annual compensation / (NHE per capita x population per RN)

This measures: of all healthcare dollars spent by one RN's notional patient panel, what fraction reaches the RN as compensation? The denominator is identical to ticket 001 (PCP) — total National Health Expenditure per capita from CMS — so the two lines are directly comparable on the same graph.

**Worker compensation:** Average (mean) annual wages for full-time registered nurses. Sources vary by era:

- **1960:** Estimated at ~$4,000 based on ANA and WSNA records showing staff nurses earned approximately $300-$400/month. WSNA reported $400/month, but Washington state paid above the national average. National average was likely closer to $330-$350/month. Low confidence — could plausibly be $3,500-$4,800.
- **1970:** Estimated at ~$7,500 based on BLS occupational wage surveys showing general duty nurses earning $3.40-$3.80/hr ($7,072-$7,904 annualized at 2,080 hours). Low confidence.
- **1980:** Estimated at ~$17,000 based on BLS PATC surveys showing average RN hourly earnings of ~$8.13/hr (AJN "Salary Figures for Staff Nurses Updated to 1980"), giving $16,910 annualized. Cross-referenced with NSSRN 1980 survey data. Medium confidence.
- **1990:** Estimated at ~$30,000 by interpolation from 1980 data ($17,000) at ~5.8% compound annual growth, cross-referenced against NSSRN 1992 median of $36,073 for full-time staff RNs. Medium confidence.
- **2000:** ~$44,000 based on NSSRN 2000 data showing median full-time staff RN salary of $42,983, with mean of $43,968 (1999 NSSRN data) and BLS OES 2002 mean of $49,858. High confidence.
- **2010:** $67,720 — BLS OES mean annual wage, May 2010. High confidence.
- **2020:** $80,010 — BLS OES mean annual wage, May 2020 (employment: 2,986,500). High confidence.
- **2024:** ~$99,000 estimated from BLS OES May 2024 median of $93,600 and the historical mean/median ratio of ~1.06. BLS OES May 2023 mean was $94,480 (Statista/BLS). Medium confidence on the 2024 mean specifically; median of $93,600 is high confidence.

**Customer cost proxy:** Total National Health Expenditure (NHE) per capita from CMS National Health Expenditure Accounts — identical to the physician ticket (001). Same rationale: NHE per capita captures all healthcare spending (insurance, Medicare, Medicaid, out-of-pocket, employer contributions) and is the most honest denominator for "what the customer pays for healthcare."

**Customers per worker:** U.S. resident population divided by the number of employed registered nurses.

RN workforce estimates by source:
- **1960:** ~500,000 employed RNs. Based on ANA workforce reports and interpolation between 375,000 active graduate nurses in 1940 and ~700,000 in 1970.
- **1970:** ~700,000 employed RNs. Multiple sources cite this figure (Penn Nursing, WSNA, PMC historical census data).
- **1980:** 1,238,710 employed RNs. NSSRN November 1980 survey (rural 180,590 + urban 1,058,120).
- **1990:** ~1,730,000 employed RNs. Interpolated from NSSRN 1988 (1,615,986) and NSSRN 1992 (1,845,882).
- **2000:** 2,178,775 employed RNs. NSSRN March 2000 survey (rural 396,441 + urban 1,782,334).
- **2010:** ~2,737,000 employed RNs. BLS OES estimate.
- **2020:** 2,986,500 employed RNs. BLS OES May 2020.
- **2024:** 3,282,010 employed RNs. BLS OES May 2024.

**Why use the same denominator as the physician ticket:** The ticket specifically requests consistency with ticket 001 so that the nurse and physician lines are directly comparable. Using NHE per capita as the denominator for both means the two ratios show each worker type's share of the same healthcare spending pie. Individual ratios will be small — that's expected. The comparison between how each line moves is the story.

## Notes

- **The nurse ratio is dramatically more stable than the physician ratio.** PCPs went from 7.8% (1960) to 1.3% (2024) — an 83% collapse. RNs went from ~7.6% to ~6.3% — only a 17% decline over the same period. Nurses have been far more effective at maintaining their share of healthcare spending. This is the headline finding.

- **The 1970-1980 workforce explosion is the most dramatic structural change.** The number of employed RNs nearly doubled in a decade (700K → 1.24M), dropping the population-per-RN ratio from 293 to 183. This massive expansion was driven by the nursing shortage crisis of the late 1960s, federal investment in nursing education, and the growing complexity of hospital care post-Medicare. Despite adding ~540,000 nurses, the ratio actually increased slightly (7.2% → 8.4%) because nurse salaries grew enough to keep pace with the expanding workforce.

- **The 2010-2020 decade saw the sharpest decline.** The ratio dropped from 7.1% to 5.7% — a 20% decline in a single decade. NHE per capita grew 50.5% ($8,394 → $12,637) while RN mean wages grew only 18.1% ($67,720 → $80,010). The workforce barely expanded relative to population (pop/RN dropped from 113 to 111). This decade represents the healthcare cost explosion outrunning nursing wages.

- **Post-COVID wage recovery is real but incomplete.** From 2020 to 2024, RN wages grew ~24% (to an estimated $99,000 mean), partly recovering the 2010s decline. The travel nursing explosion of 2021-2022, where crisis rates hit $100-$150/hr, forced permanent wage increases across the industry. But BLS OES data captures staff positions, not travel contracts — the true peak compensation in 2021-2022 was likely even higher than captured here.

- **Why nurses held their share better than doctors:** Three factors: (1) Nurse workforce grew much faster than physician workforce, meaning more total nurse compensation as a share of spending; (2) Hospital reimbursement models embed nursing costs as a line item (unlike physician services which are billed separately and subject to fee schedule compression); (3) Nursing shortages create immediate, visible crises (understaffed wards) that force wage responses, while physician supply constraints are managed through specialist referrals and wait times.

- **The 1960 and 1970 data points are the weakest.** Pre-NSSRN nursing workforce data relies on ANA reports and Census approximations. Salary data for these years is reconstructed from BLS occupational wage surveys, WSNA records, and the Handbook on Women Workers — all of which have geographic and methodological limitations. The ratio could plausibly be anywhere from 6-10% for 1960 and 6-9% for 1970.

- **BLS OES vs. NSSRN salary data:** BLS OES reports mean annual wages from employer surveys (available consistently from ~1997). NSSRN reports average annual earnings from nurse surveys (available every 4 years from 1977-2008, then 2018 and 2022). The two measures differ slightly — NSSRN tends to capture total nursing income (including overtime, shift differentials), while OES captures base occupational wages. I used OES where available (2010, 2020, 2024) and NSSRN/other sources for earlier years. This introduces some measurement inconsistency, but both sources track the same underlying trend.
