# Ticket 017: DES (Diethylstilbestrol) → Cancer / Birth Defects

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that DES causes vaginal clear-cell adenocarcinoma in daughters of women who took it during pregnancy to regulatory action restricting its use.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — DES was prescribed to pregnant women from the 1940s to prevent miscarriage. A 1953 study (Dieckmann et al.) showed DES was INEFFECTIVE for preventing miscarriage, but use continued. The cancer link came in 1971: Herbst, Ulfelder & Poskanzer published in NEJM linking DES exposure in utero to clear-cell adenocarcinoma of the vagina in young women.

2. **Consensus date** — The Herbst 1971 paper was rapidly accepted. FDA issued a Drug Bulletin in November 1971. How fast was the scientific consensus compared to the regulatory action?

3. **First OECD regulation** — FDA issued a warning against DES use in pregnancy in 1971. Did other countries act simultaneously or earlier? European timelines?

4. **US regulation** — FDA Drug Bulletin (November 1971) warning against DES in pregnancy. Not a formal ban initially — but the warning effectively ended prescribing. DES was not withdrawn from market entirely (it had other uses). When was it formally contraindicated/banned for pregnancy use?

5. **The earlier failure** — DES was shown to be INEFFECTIVE for its intended use (preventing miscarriage) in 1953. It continued to be prescribed for 18 more years. This 18-year gap between "doesn't work" and "causes cancer, stop using it" is a sub-lag worth documenting.

6. **Scale of harm** — Estimated 5-10 million women were exposed in the US alone. DES daughters, DES sons (reproductive abnormalities), and even DES granddaughters (transgenerational effects) were affected.

## Where to look

1. Herbst, Ulfelder & Poskanzer (1971) — NEJM paper on DES and vaginal cancer
2. Dieckmann et al. (1953) — study showing DES ineffective for miscarriage prevention
3. FDA regulatory history for DES
4. CDC DES Update information
5. PubMed — "DES diethylstilbestrol history" or "DES regulatory response"

## What "good data" looks like

- The 1953 inefficacy study as early context — prescribing continued for 18 years after the drug was shown not to work
- Fast regulatory response once CANCER evidence emerged (1971) — compare speed to other cases
- Accurate characterization of the FDA 1971 action — was it a binding ban, a warning, or something in between?

## Known pitfalls

- DES was not "banned" outright in 1971 — it had other approved uses (prostate cancer, menopausal symptoms). The 1971 action specifically warned against use in PREGNANCY. Be precise about what was restricted.
- The "early evidence" could be 1953 (drug doesn't work) or 1971 (drug causes cancer). These are different claims. Use 1971 for the cancer/harm evidence, but note the 1953 inefficacy finding.
- Category: product-withdrawal (specific drug use contraindicated/withdrawn for pregnancy).
- This is a case where the harm emerged DECADES after exposure — DES daughters developed cancer in their teens/twenties. The visibility of harm was delayed but dramatic once it appeared.
