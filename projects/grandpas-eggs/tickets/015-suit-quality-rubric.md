# Men's Suit — Quality Rubric

## What to research

Define what a "men's suit" meant in ~1955 and what the quality-equivalent product is today. Build a multi-attribute quality rubric.

## Research tasks

1. **1955 baseline:** What did a typical middle-class man buy when he needed a suit? What were the materials (wool weight, lining), construction method (full canvas, half canvas, or fused?), country of manufacture, and expected lifespan? Use Sears catalog entries from the mid-1950s as the benchmark — this was the median purchase, not a luxury product.

2. **Quality attributes to score (minimum 5):**
   - Fabric composition (100% wool vs. poly blend vs. synthetic)
   - Fabric weight and origin (domestic mill vs. imported)
   - Construction method (full canvas vs. half canvas vs. fused interlining)
   - Country of manufacture (US/UK vs. developing world)
   - Expected lifespan (years of regular wear before replacement)
   - Fit and finishing (hand-sewn details, buttonholes, lining quality)

3. **Modern quality-equivalent:** What specific modern product matches a 1955 Sears suit? This is probably NOT a $200 department store suit (fused, poly-blend, imported) and NOT a $5,000 Savile Row bespoke. It's likely in the $800-1,500 range: half-canvas or full-canvas construction, 100% wool, possibly made in Italy or Portugal. Name brands or tiers as examples (Suitsupply, Brooks Brothers mainline, J.Crew Ludlow, etc.)

4. **Divergence timeline:** When did suit manufacturing move offshore? When did fused construction become the norm for sub-$500 suits? The story of the US garment industry decline (1970s-2000s).

## Data source hierarchy

1. **Sears catalog archives** (digitized, available through various libraries and online archives) — The gold standard for 1950s-1990s durable goods pricing with product descriptions.
2. Academic studies on US garment industry history
3. Menswear industry publications
4. BLS CPI apparel indices (for trends, not absolute prices)

## Output format

Follow the **Type 2: Quality rubric** format from plan.md.

## What "good data" looks like

- Specific Sears catalog entries cited (year, page, product, price, description)
- Honest about dimensions where modern cheap suits are BETTER (wrinkle resistance, variety of styles, ease of care)
- Lifespan comparison is critical — if a 1955 suit lasted 10 years and a 2025 suit lasts 3, the cost-per-year story is different from cost-per-purchase
