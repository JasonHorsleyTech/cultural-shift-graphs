# Water Usage of Everything

## The question

How much water does America actually "use," where does it go, and is AI water usage the crisis people claim — or a rounding error?

## The hypothesis

Water usage statistics are wildly misleading. The same gallon gets counted multiple times as it cycles through the system. Per-capita usage has likely been *falling* for decades due to efficiency gains. And AI data centers, despite the panic, are a tiny fraction of total usage — dwarfed by agriculture, power generation, and even residential lawn watering.

## Why this is interesting

Everyone has a take on AI water usage. Nobody knows how much water a golf course uses. The numbers tell a story that contradicts the vibes: the US uses less water per person than it did 40 years ago, and the newest "water hog" (AI) is invisible next to the ones nobody talks about.

## What we're measuring

**Blue water withdrawal and consumption** — actual freshwater pulled from rivers, lakes, and aquifers. Not "water footprint" (which counts rain), not "grey water" (a pollution metric disguised as volume). The metric that honestly represents competition for scarce freshwater.

## The graphs (one page, multiple charts)

1. **Per capita withdrawal over time (1950-2020)** — Stacked area by sector. Punchline: peaked in 1980, been falling since.
2. **Where does your water go? (current snapshot)** — Per capita daily breakdown including fun sub-categories (your share of golf courses, lawn watering, pool evaporation, AI data centers).
3. **Are we running out? (usage vs supply)** — Total withdrawal as % of renewable freshwater, with reference lines for total Earth water. Answer: no, nationally. Ask the Colorado River.
4. **The new kid on the block (AI in context)** — AI/data center water use plotted against other sectors to show relative scale. Maybe a zoomed inset to even find it.
