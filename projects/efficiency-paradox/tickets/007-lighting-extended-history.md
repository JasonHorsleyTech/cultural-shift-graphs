# Lighting: Extended History 1950–1990

## What to Research

Historical data on lighting efficiency and consumption before the modern era (pre-LED, pre-CFL). This provides the longer-arc context: how much did total lighting energy grow during the era of relatively constant efficiency (incandescent dominance)?

## Where to Look

1. **Primary:** Nordhaus (1996) "Do Real Output and Real Wage Measures Capture Reality? The History of Lighting Suggests Not." This paper has the definitive long-run series on lumens per dollar and lighting technology efficacy.
2. **Secondary:** Fouquet & Pearson (2006) "Seven Centuries of Energy Services: The Price and Use of Light in the United Kingdom" — useful for cross-validation even though it's UK data.
3. **Tertiary:** EIA historical data — total electricity generation by end use goes back to 1949.
4. **Fallback:** US Census of Manufactures (historical) has data on lamp production volumes.

## Scope

- **Time range:** 1950–1990 (bridging from post-war to the start of the primary window).
- **Efficiency:** Average efficacy of predominant lighting technology (incandescent: ~12-15 lm/W, with fluorescent tubes in commercial: ~60-70 lm/W). Stock average was probably ~20-25 lm/W through most of this period.
- **Consumption:** Total electricity for lighting (or best proxy: residential electricity per household as a rough bound, since lighting was the dominant residential use in the 1950s-60s).

## Data Gaps

- Lighting-specific electricity data pre-1980 is almost certainly estimated/modeled, not measured.
- Total lumen production estimates are very rough for the US specifically (most academic work is global or UK).
- Accept decadal estimates (1950, 1960, 1970, 1980, 1990) rather than annual.

## What "Good Data" Looks Like

- Shows that lighting efficiency was essentially flat (~15-20 lm/W stock average) from 1950–1990.
- Shows that total lighting electricity grew enormously (perhaps 5-8x) over the same period.
- This is the "pure Jevons" era for lighting: zero efficiency improvement, massive consumption growth. (Not actually Jevons since efficiency didn't improve — it's just growth.) The contrast with the 1990-2020 era (where efficiency finally improved and consumption dropped) is the storytelling hook.
- Decadal data points are fine. Don't force annual precision that doesn't exist.
