## Source
- **Primary source:** Congressional Budget Office, "The Distribution of Household Income in 2021" (Publication 60341), September 2024. Supplemental data Table 3: "Average Household Income, by Income Source and Income Group, 1979 to 2021."
- **Companion report:** CBO, "Trends in the Distribution of Household Income From 1979 to 2021" (Publication 60342), September 2024.
- **Data file:** CBO supplemental data spreadsheet (60341-supplemental-data.xlsx), accessed via Internet Archive mirror.
- **Date accessed:** 2026-04-30

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Income after transfers and taxes = Market income + Social insurance benefits + Means-tested transfers − Federal taxes. This is the most comprehensive CBO income measure — it represents what households actually have available to spend. Market income includes labor income, business income, capital income (including capital gains), and other income. Social insurance benefits include Social Security, Medicare Part A, unemployment insurance, and workers' compensation. Means-tested transfers include Medicaid, CHIP, SNAP, SSI, TANF, housing assistance, EITC, child tax credits, and other means-tested programs. Federal taxes include individual income taxes, payroll taxes, corporate income taxes (allocated to capital owners), and excise taxes.
- **Universe:** All U.S. households. CBO ranks individuals by their household's size-adjusted income (equivalized by dividing by the square root of household size), then divides the population into quintiles of equal numbers of people (not households). The dollar values reported are total household income, not per-person or equivalized.
- **Unit:** Average (mean) household income after transfers and taxes per income group, in constant 2021 dollars. Inflation adjustment uses the BEA price index for personal consumption expenditures (PCE).
- **What's included that Census misses:** Employer-sponsored health insurance premiums are included in CBO's "market income" (and thus flow through to after-tax income). Non-cash government benefits (Medicaid, Medicare, SNAP, housing subsidies) are counted. Refundable tax credits (EITC, child tax credit) reduce taxes and can make effective tax rates negative.
- **What's excluded:** State and local taxes are NOT subtracted. After-tax income here is after *federal* taxes only. This means the figures overstate true disposable income, especially for middle and upper quintiles who pay significant state/local taxes.

## Gaps and Caveats
- **No missing years.** Complete annual series from 1979–2021 (43 data points per quintile).
- **Constant 2021 dollars.** All values are inflation-adjusted using the PCE price index, NOT CPI. PCE typically shows lower inflation than CPI, so growth rates will differ from CPI-adjusted series. The plan.md specifies "nominal dollars unless stated otherwise" but CBO only publishes this data in constant dollars (their primary presentation). The graph phase should note the base year and avoid double-deflating.
- **These are means, not medians.** CBO reports average (mean) income per quintile. Within each quintile the mean can be pulled by outliers, especially at the top. The top 1% figures are extremely volatile year-to-year due to capital gains realization timing.
- **CBO rebasing.** The September 2024 report uses 2021 as the base year. Earlier CBO reports used different base years. Values here supersede earlier-base-year figures.
- **Federal taxes only.** After-tax income subtracts only federal taxes (individual income tax, payroll tax, corporate income tax allocated to capital owners, and excise taxes). State and local taxes (averaging ~10% of income) are NOT subtracted. This means these figures overstate true disposable income, more so for higher quintiles.
- **Non-cash benefits included.** CBO's "means-tested transfers" include the insurance value of Medicaid/CHIP and housing subsidies, which are not cash in hand. This inflates bottom-quintile after-tax income relative to what households actually receive as spendable dollars.
- **Pandemic distortion in 2020–2021:** The bottom three quintiles saw massive spikes in 2020–2021 due to Recovery Rebate Credits (stimulus checks), expanded unemployment compensation, and expanded child tax credits. The bottom quintile's effective federal tax rate went deeply negative (−16.8% in 2020, −22.9% in 2021) meaning the federal government was paying these households substantially more in refundable credits than they owed in taxes. These are real values, not measurement errors, but they are not representative of long-run trends and were largely temporary policies.
- **Comparison with market income (ticket 011):** The gap between after-tax income and market income reveals the net effect of the tax-and-transfer system. For the bottom quintile in 2021: market income was $15,400 but after-tax income was $48,700 — transfers and tax credits added $33,300 per household. For the top quintile: market income was $403,400 but after-tax income was $316,800 — federal taxes removed $86,600. This gap has grown dramatically over time, especially at the bottom (in 1979 the gap was only $10,000 for the bottom quintile).
- **Equivalized ranking, unequalized dollars.** Same as ticket 011: CBO ranks people by household income divided by the square root of household size, but dollar values are total household income (unequalized).
- **Confidence: HIGH.** This is CBO's definitive data product, sourced from merged IRS tax return data and Census survey data. The methodology is transparent and refined over decades.

## Key patterns for the graph phase
- Middle quintile after-tax income grew ~68% over 42 years (1979–2021): $51,500 → $86,300. That's about 1.2% real annual growth — substantially more than the ~21% growth in middle-quintile market income over the same period. The difference is entirely due to expanding means-tested transfers and declining effective tax rates.
- Bottom quintile after-tax income grew ~131%: $21,100 → $48,700. Market income for this group grew only ~39% ($11,100 → $15,400). The safety net accounts for nearly all of the bottom quintile's income growth.
- Top quintile after-tax income grew ~167%: $118,600 → $316,800. Top 1% grew ~442%: $405,600 → $2,196,900.
- The ratio of top quintile to bottom quintile after-tax income actually NARROWED slightly from 5.6:1 (1979) to 6.5:1 (2021) — much less than the market income ratio which widened from 14.3:1 to 26.2:1. The tax-and-transfer system dramatically compresses inequality, and has done so increasingly over time.
- 2020–2021 are outliers for the bottom three quintiles due to pandemic-era fiscal policy. Without temporary policies, growth would be substantially lower (CBO estimates bottom quintile growth would be ~96% instead of 132% through 2021).
