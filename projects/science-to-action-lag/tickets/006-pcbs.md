# Ticket 006: PCBs → Cancer / Developmental Effects

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs) cause cancer and developmental harm to binding regulation banning PCB manufacture and use.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — When did scientific studies first link PCBs to health harm? Yusho disease incident in Japan (1968, cooking oil contaminated with PCBs) caused mass poisoning. Earlier evidence from occupational exposure studies? Look for the first credible evidence of chronic health effects (cancer, developmental harm), not just acute toxicity.

2. **Consensus date** — When did a major scientific body formally classify PCBs as a health hazard? IARC classification? EPA determination? NAS report?

3. **First OECD regulation** — Japan restricted PCB production after the Yusho incident. Sweden and other Nordic countries acted early. The US Toxic Substances Control Act (TSCA, 1976) banned new PCB manufacturing. Find the earliest OECD ban.

4. **US regulation** — TSCA (1976) banned PCB manufacture and severely restricted use. EPA cleanup standards for PCB contamination came later.

5. **Stockholm Convention** — PCBs listed as one of the initial "dirty dozen" persistent organic pollutants (2001/2004). Global phase-out target 2025.

6. **Industry opposition** — Monsanto was the sole US manufacturer. Their internal documents showed awareness of health risks. Level of organized opposition?

7. **Substitute availability** — Alternatives existed for most PCB applications (transformer oil, etc.) but were more expensive or less effective.

## Where to look

1. EPA historical documents on PCB regulation under TSCA
2. IARC Monograph on PCBs
3. Yusho disease literature (Japanese medical journals, WHO reports)
4. Stockholm Convention — PCB profile and global elimination status
5. PubMed — "PCB health effects history" or "polychlorinated biphenyls regulation"

## What "good data" looks like

- Evidence dates that distinguish between acute poisoning incidents (Yusho 1968) and chronic exposure evidence (occupational cancer studies)
- Clear regulatory dates — PCBs were banned for NEW manufacture but existing installations were allowed to continue for years
- The Yusho incident as a catalyst for action (visible mass poisoning event, similar to thalidomide in mechanism)

## Known pitfalls

- PCB regulation is about manufacturing/new use, not cleanup of existing contamination. The ban date is when new production stopped, not when contaminated sites were cleaned.
- Category: substance-ban (banned from manufacture/sale), though the Stockholm Convention adds an international dimension.
- Environmental persistence means PCBs are still present decades after the ban — don't conflate "ban date" with "exposure eliminated."
