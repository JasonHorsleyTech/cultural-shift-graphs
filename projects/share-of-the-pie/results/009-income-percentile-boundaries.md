# Result: Household Income Percentile Boundaries (P20, P80, P95), 1967–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** U.S. Census Bureau, Current Population Survey, 1968–2025 Annual Social and Economic Supplements (CPS ASEC). Historical Income Table H-1: "Income Limits for Each Fifth and Top 5 Percent of All Households: 1967 to 2024." File: `h01ar.xlsx`
- **URL:** https://www2.census.gov/programs-surveys/cps/tables/time-series/historical-income-households/h01ar.xlsx
- **Date accessed:** 2026-04-29

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Table H-1 reports the upper income limit (in current dollars) for each fifth (quintile) of all households, plus the lower income limit of the top 5 percent. These are household total money income thresholds.
- **Universe:** All households in the United States, as of March of the following year. Includes all household types (families, unrelated individuals, etc.).
- **P20** = upper income limit of the lowest quintile (20th percentile boundary)
- **P40** = upper income limit of the second quintile (40th percentile boundary)
- **P60** = upper income limit of the third quintile (60th percentile boundary)
- **P80** = upper income limit of the fourth quintile (80th percentile boundary)
- **P95** = lower income limit of the top 5 percent (95th percentile boundary)
- **Important: P50 (median) is NOT directly available from this table.** The third quintile upper limit is P60, not P50. The median household income is covered by ticket 003 (Census Table H-6). The P60 column is included here as additional context.
- **Known methodology changes:**
  - **2013 CPS ASEC redesign:** The Census Bureau introduced redesigned income questions. Dual estimates were published for 2013 — one using the old questionnaire and one using the new. This table uses the new-questionnaire estimate (footnote 39) for consistency with 2014+ data. The old-questionnaire estimate (footnote 38) is noted below.
  - **2017 updated processing:** The Census updated its processing system. Dual estimates were published for 2017. This table uses the updated-processing estimate (footnote 40) for consistency with 2018+ data.
  - **2020 pandemic:** Pandemic-year data collection was affected. Response rates dropped. Values may be less reliable than other years.

## Gaps and Caveats

- **No missing years.** Complete annual coverage from 1967 through 2024 (58 rows).
- **P50 (median) is not in this table.** Table H-1 provides quintile boundaries at P20, P40, P60, and P80, plus the P95 threshold. The median (50th percentile) falls between P40 and P60 and must be sourced from ticket 003 (Census Table H-6). The ticket description asked for P50 — this is noted as a clarification, not a gap.
- **2013 methodology break.** The CPS ASEC redesigned its income questions starting with the 2014 survey (collecting 2013 income). The new questionnaire produced modestly higher estimates across all percentiles. The main table above uses the new-questionnaire estimates from 2013 onward for consistency with subsequent years. Pre-2013 data uses the old questionnaire. This creates a small upward discontinuity at 2013 (~1–5% depending on the percentile).
- **2017 processing update.** Updated processing methods were introduced. Differences are small (generally <1% for P20–P80, but ~3% for P95). Main table uses updated processing for 2017 onward.
- **2020 pandemic effects.** Lower response rates and unusual income patterns (stimulus payments, unemployment insurance supplements) affect 2020 data. P20 dropped slightly (some low-income households temporarily boosted above P20 by stimulus), while upper percentiles held relatively steady.
- **Rounding.** Census rounds these thresholds to varying precision. Values below $10,000 are often rounded to nearest dollar; higher values to nearest $10 or $100. Do not over-interpret small year-to-year movements at the bottom quintile in early years.
- **P80/P20 ratio trend (inequality check):**
  - 1967: 3.95
  - 1980: 4.21
  - 2000: 4.56
  - 2024: 5.09
  - Confirms steadily rising inequality as expected.
- **Confidence:** High for all years 1967–2024. This is the Census Bureau's primary published series. The only caveats are the 2013 methodology break and 2020 pandemic-year data quality.
