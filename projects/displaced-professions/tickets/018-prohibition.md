# Ticket 018: Prohibition-Era Alcohol Workers

## Event
The elimination of legal alcohol production and distribution in the United States with the 18th Amendment (1920–1933). A regulatory shock that displaced brewers, distillers, bartenders, saloon keepers, and related workers — then reversed 13 years later.

## Mechanism
**Discrete shock** (regulatory)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track employment in US alcohol-related industries from ~1910 to ~1940:

- Pre-Prohibition: ~1,300 breweries (1914), ~500+ distilleries, ~177,000 saloons (estimated). Total employment in production + distribution + retail: estimate (probably 500K–1M+)
- January 17, 1920: Prohibition takes effect
- Immediate displacement: brewers, distillers, barrel-makers (coopers), bottlers, bartenders, saloon keepers, wine makers
- December 5, 1933: Repeal (21st Amendment)
- Post-repeal recovery: how many jobs came back? Which segments recovered and which didn't?

**Source hierarchy:**
1. US Census data 1910, 1920, 1930, 1940 (IPUMS for occupational codes)
2. Okrent, "Last Call: The Rise and Fall of Prohibition" (2010)
3. Historical Statistics of the United States — manufacturing data for brewing/distilling
4. Brewery and distillery production statistics (TTB historical data)
5. Blocker, Fahey & Tyrrell, "Alcohol and Temperance in Modern History" (2003)

### Income Data
- Brewer/distiller wages pre-Prohibition (union wages — these were often well-paid skilled jobs)
- Income alternatives during Prohibition (near beer, soft drinks, ice cream, etc.)
- Post-repeal wages — did the industry return to pre-Prohibition compensation levels?

**Known caveats:** Bootlegging employed significant numbers of people at high (but illegal and risky) incomes. This shadow economy component is large and unmeasurable. Acknowledge it but don't try to quantify precisely.

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Census earnings data for brewing/distilling occupations
2. US Bureau of Labor Statistics historical wage data
3. Brewery Workers Union records (if accessible)
4. Okrent (2010) for economic analysis

### Where They Went
- Near beer / soft drink production (Anheuser-Busch survived by making near beer and soda)
- Ice cream manufacturing (some breweries converted)
- Bootlegging / speakeasies (the shadow economy — huge but illegal)
- General manufacturing
- Some brewers went into yeast, malt extract, and bread-making
- Coopers (barrel-makers) had no alternative — barrels without booze

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Okrent (2010) — company adaptation stories
2. Smith, "Beer in America: The Early Years, 1587–1840" and related brewing histories
3. Census occupational data

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Anheuser-Busch (diversified into soft drinks, ice cream, yeast). Yuengling (produced ice cream and near beer). Wineries that got "sacramental wine" permits.
- **False movers:** Breweries that invested heavily in near beer (0.5% ABV) expecting it to be commercially viable — it wasn't profitable enough to sustain operations.
- **Deniers:** Much of the industry assumed Prohibition would never pass, then assumed it would be quickly repealed. They were right about repeal but wrong about the timeline (13 years).
- **Survival pattern:** Diversification and patience. The companies that survived 13 years of Prohibition had the strongest positions post-repeal.

### Unique Texture
- This is the only displacement in the dataset that was *reversed* — the industry came back after 13 years. What did the labor market look like on the other side?
- The number of breweries went from ~1,300 (1914) to ~160 (1933, at repeal) to ~750 (1940). Not all came back.
- Prohibition created new professions: bootlegger, rum-runner, speakeasy operator. Organized crime as a "successor industry."
- The constitutional dimension: it took an amendment to kill the industry AND an amendment to bring it back
- Saloons never recovered in their pre-Prohibition form — replaced by bars, cocktail lounges, and restaurants with liquor licenses. The *job* came back but the *institution* didn't.

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Number of breweries/distilleries before, during, and after Prohibition
- Employment estimates for alcohol-related occupations pre-Prohibition
- Post-repeal recovery data (how many jobs returned, how quickly)
- At least 3 company adaptation stories
- The shadow economy (bootlegging) acknowledged even if unquantifiable
