# Haircut — Quality Rubric

## What to research

Define what a "men's haircut" meant in ~1955 and assess whether the product has meaningfully changed. Build a quality rubric. The explore phase expects this to be Baumol's cost disease poster child — a product with essentially no quality change, where the price increase is pure labor cost. Verify whether that's actually true.

## Research tasks

1. **1955 baseline:** A standard men's haircut at a barbershop. What did this include? (Cut, possibly a hot towel shave, conversation.) How long did it take? Who performed it (licensed barber, apprentice)?

2. **Quality attributes to score (minimum 4):**
   - Service scope (cut only vs. cut + extras like hot towel, razor neck cleanup)
   - Time spent (minutes per haircut)
   - Provider training/licensing requirements
   - Ambiance and amenities (basic barbershop vs. modern salon)
   - Product usage (pomade/tonic in 1955 vs. styling products today)

3. **Is there actually a quality gap?** Possible arguments:
   - **Yes:** Modern barbershops often spend less time per cut (15 min vs. 25 min in 1955). The $12 Great Clips haircut is a faster, more assembly-line experience than a 1955 barbershop.
   - **No:** A basic haircut is a basic haircut. Scissors, clippers, mirror, done. The core product is unchanged.
   - **Inverted:** Modern techniques, better tools, more styling options. The cheap haircut may actually be better.

4. **Modern tiers (if they exist):**
   - Budget chain (Great Clips, Supercuts): $15-20
   - Standard barbershop: $25-40
   - Premium barbershop (hot towel, straight razor, craft beer): $40-65
   - Salon/stylist: $50-100+

## Data source hierarchy

1. Historical barber trade publications
2. BLS CPI "haircut" or "barber services" component
3. Academic studies on Baumol's cost disease using barber services as an example
4. Newspaper archives for historical haircut prices

## Output format

Follow the **Type 2: Quality rubric** format from plan.md.

## What "good data" looks like

- Honest assessment of whether there IS a quality gap (the hypothesis is "no" — verify it)
- If the quality gap is negligible, the overall assessment should be "No change" and the story is pure Baumol's cost disease
- If there IS a gap (e.g., time-per-cut decreased), the quality-equivalent is a $30-40 neighborhood barbershop
