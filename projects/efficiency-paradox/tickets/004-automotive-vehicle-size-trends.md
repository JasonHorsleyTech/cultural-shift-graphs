# Automotive: Vehicle Size and Weight Trends 1975–2022

## What to Research

How the average US light-duty vehicle changed in size, weight, and power over time. This is the **quality substitution** context for the automotive domain — it shows where efficiency gains went besides fuel savings.

## Where to Look

1. **Primary:** EPA Automotive Trends Report (annual since ~2004, with historical data back to 1975). Contains:
   - Average curb weight by model year
   - Average horsepower by model year
   - Average footprint (sq ft) by model year
   - Market share of cars vs. light trucks/SUVs
2. **Secondary:** FHWA Highway Statistics for fleet composition (cars vs. trucks registered).
3. **Fallback:** Ward's Automotive Yearbook data (may be behind paywall — use EPA first).

## Data Gaps

- Pre-1975 data unlikely to be in EPA Trends report. Don't spend time hunting for it.
- "New vehicle" stats vs. "fleet average" may diverge (fleet turns over slowly). Note which you're reporting.

## Methodology Notes

This is model-year data (characteristics of vehicles sold in that year), not fleet-in-use data. The fleet lags by ~10 years due to vehicle lifetimes.

## What "Good Data" Looks Like

- Shows weight dip in late 1970s-early 1980s (response to oil crisis), then steady climb.
- Shows SUV/light truck share rising from ~20% (1975) to ~55%+ (2022).
- Shows horsepower rising from ~100-130 average (1975-1985) to ~250+ (2022).
- The punchline: if vehicles had stayed at 1980 weight/size, the same engine efficiency would deliver ~35-40 MPG fleet average, not 25.
