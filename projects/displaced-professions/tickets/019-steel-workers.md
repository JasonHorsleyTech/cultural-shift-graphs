# Ticket 019: US Steel Workers

## Event
The decline of US steel industry employment from its peak in the 1950s–70s through the "Steel Crisis" of the 1980s and continuing globalization. A gradual erosion driven by foreign competition, mini-mills, and automation — concentrated in the Rust Belt.

## Mechanism
**Gradual erosion** (globalization, tech_tool, consolidation)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track US steel industry employment from ~1950 to present:

- Peak: ~650,000 (some estimates higher) in primary steel (SIC 331 / NAICS 3311–3312), probably early-to-mid 1950s
- Key dates: Japanese/European steel recovery (1960s), 1973 oil crisis, 1982 recession (devastating for steel), Bethlehem Steel bankruptcy (2001), US Steel restructuring
- Current: ~80,000–90,000 in iron and steel mills — verify
- Geographic concentration: Pittsburgh, Gary IN, Youngstown OH, Birmingham AL, Bethlehem PA

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS CES data, NAICS 3311 (Iron and Steel Mills) and 3312 (Steel Product Manufacturing)
2. American Iron and Steel Institute (AISI) annual statistical reports
3. Census Bureau Annual Survey of Manufactures
4. Hoerr, "And the Wolf Finally Came: The Decline of the American Steel Industry" (1988)
5. Reutter, "Sparrows Point: Making Steel" (2004) — Bethlehem Steel case study

### Income Data
Steelworkers were among the best-paid blue-collar workers in America — union wages far exceeded manufacturing average:

- United Steelworkers of America (USWA) contract wages over time
- Compare to manufacturing average wage
- Track real wages and total compensation (healthcare, pensions) from 1960–2010
- The pension/retiree healthcare crisis post-bankruptcy

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS OES and CES wage data for iron and steel mills
2. USWA contract data (summary terms published in trade press)
3. CPI-U for inflation adjustment
4. AISI labor cost data

### Where They Went
- Other manufacturing (auto parts, general fabrication)
- Service sector (often at dramatically lower wages)
- Unemployment / disability / early retirement
- Many never recovered — the "deaths of despair" in Rust Belt towns
- Mini-mill employment (Nucor, Steel Dynamics) — same industry, different companies, different locations

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS Local Area Unemployment Statistics for steel cities
2. Census data for Pittsburgh, Youngstown, Gary, Bethlehem
3. Academic studies on Rust Belt deindustrialization
4. Linkon & Russo, "Steeltown U.S.A.: Work and Memory in Youngstown" (2002)

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Nucor (mini-mill model, non-union, electric arc furnaces). Workers who retrained early into healthcare or skilled trades.
- **False movers:** Voluntary Restraint Agreements (VRAs) and import quotas — protected the industry temporarily but delayed adjustment. Workers who relied on protectionism to save their jobs.
- **Deniers:** Belief that American integrated mills could compete with Japanese quality. US Steel CEO David Roderick's statements in the early 1980s.
- **Survival pattern:** Geographic mobility (leaving steel towns) and/or retraining. Mini-mill workers survived; integrated mill workers didn't.

### Unique Texture
- Youngstown's Black Monday (September 19, 1977) — Youngstown Sheet and Tube announced closure, 5,000 workers displaced. The event that named the Rust Belt.
- Bethlehem Steel: from building the Golden Gate Bridge and the Empire State Building to bankruptcy. 300,000 employees at peak → 0.
- The pension crisis: retirees who depended on company pensions lost them in bankruptcy
- Pittsburgh's reinvention — from steel city to eds-and-meds economy. One of the few success stories.
- The mini-mill revolution: same product, 1/10 the workforce, non-union, located in the South

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Annual employment in primary steel from 1950–2020
- Wage data showing the premium steelworkers commanded
- Import penetration rates over time
- At least 2 city-level case studies (Youngstown, Pittsburgh, Gary, or Bethlehem)
- Steel production volume vs. employment (productivity divergence — same output, fewer workers)
