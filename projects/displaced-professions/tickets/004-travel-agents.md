# Ticket 004: Travel Agents vs. the Internet

## Event
The displacement of US travel agents by online booking platforms, ~2000–present. A slow erosion where the bottom of the market was eliminated while the top thrived — producing the counterintuitive result that surviving agents earn more than peak-era agents.

## Mechanism
**Gradual erosion** (tech_tool)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track US travel agent employment from ~1990 to present.

- Peak: ~124,000 (BLS, ~2000). Some sources cite higher numbers in the mid-1990s — verify.
- Current: ~56,400 (BLS, May 2023). Down ~55%.
- Annual or biennial data points from BLS OES.
- Key dates: Expedia/Travelocity launch (1996), airline commission cuts (1999–2002), Delta eliminates base commissions (2002).

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OES), SOC 41-3041 (Travel Agents)
2. BLS Current Population Survey (CPS) for earlier years
3. American Society of Travel Advisors (ASTA) — industry reports, membership numbers
4. Airlines Reporting Corporation (ARC) — agency counts and ticket volumes

### Income Data
This is a post-1950 US event — clean BLS data exists. The income story is fascinating: median went UP because the bottom of the market exited.

- Median annual wage ~$26,000 (2000) → ~$47,000 (2023) per BLS
- Track annually from 1998–2023
- Distinguish between median for all agents vs. luxury/corporate segment if possible

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS OES median annual wage for SOC 41-3041, annually
2. CPI-U for inflation adjustment
3. ASTA compensation surveys (if available)

### Where They Went
- Customer service / call center work
- Event planning
- Corporate travel management (TMCs survived)
- General office/admin work
- Retirement (age demographics of displaced agents)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS industry employment data for related sectors
2. CPS occupation-switching data
3. ASTA surveys on former agent career paths
4. PhoCusWright / Skift travel industry reports

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Agents who pivoted to luxury/experiential travel ($10K–25K+ trips). Named agencies that made the transition.
- **False movers:** Agencies that tried to compete on price with online platforms. Agents who invested in their own booking websites.
- **Deniers:** "People will always want a human to book their travel." Industry association statements from late 1990s.
- **Survival pattern:** High-touch, high-value relationships. Expertise in complex itineraries (multi-country, cruises, destination weddings).

### Unique Texture
- The survivor earnings paradox: headcount down 55%, median income up 80%. Pure composition effect.
- Airlines deliberately killed their own distribution channel (agent commissions) — an industry shooting its sales force.
- The "revenge travel" post-COVID bump — did it help surviving agents?
- Average trip value for surviving agents ($10K–25K+) vs. pre-internet agents (mostly domestic, $500–2000)
- The rebranding from "travel agent" to "travel advisor" — professional identity shift

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Annual BLS employment counts from 1998–2023 (25+ data points)
- Annual median wage data from 1998–2023
- Commission structure changes with dates
- At least 3 successor occupations with rough percentage estimates
- Clear narrative of the bottom-up erosion pattern
