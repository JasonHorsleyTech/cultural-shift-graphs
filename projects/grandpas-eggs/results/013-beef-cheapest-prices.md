# Ground Beef — Cheapest Retail Prices

## Summary

Ground beef is the cheapest mainstream beef product and has strong historical price data. From 1950 to 2025, the cheapest regular ground beef rose from ~$0.47/lb to ~$6.09/lb in nominal terms. The trajectory shows three distinct eras: stable low prices through the mid-1960s ($0.33-0.50/lb), a volatile inflationary surge in the 1970s that tripled the price, and then a long post-1984 period of gradual increases punctuated by cattle-cycle peaks (notably 2014-2015 and 2020-2025). The most dramatic modern increase is the 2020-2025 period, where prices rose ~50% due to COVID disruptions, drought-driven herd liquidation, and persistent inflation.

## Methodology

### Two-era data construction

**1984-2025 (high confidence):** Direct BLS Average Price series APU0000703112 — "Ground beef, 100% beef, per lb." This series tracks regular (non-lean, non-extra-lean) ground beef, which is the cheapest mainstream retail ground beef product. Annual averages computed from monthly BLS price surveys conducted in 75 urban areas. For 2016-2025, annual averages were computed directly from monthly BLS data. For 1984-2015, annual averages were sourced from moneynotmoney.com's compilation of the same BLS series.

**1950-1983 (medium confidence):** Reconstructed from BLS CPI item index for "Uncooked ground beef" (base 1982-84=100), anchored to the known 1984 APU price of $1.29/lb. The formula: `Price_year = $1.29 × (CPI_year / 98.442)`. This assumes the relationship between the CPI index and the APU retail price is approximately constant over time, which is reasonable for a commodity like ground beef but introduces ~10-15% uncertainty for individual years. Cross-checks against independent historical sources confirm the trajectory:
- 1966: CPI-derived $0.50 vs. independent source $0.49 (Mashed.com / NYT)
- 1970: CPI-derived $0.62 vs. newspaper ad price $0.59 (ads tend to be below average)
- 1980: CPI-derived $1.37 vs. KMOV news citation $1.38

### BLS methodology note

BLS average price data underwent a methodology change in 1978-1980. Pre-1978 average prices were estimated from January benchmark prices adjusted by CPI monthly changes. Post-1980, prices are from direct BLS price collector surveys. No average prices were published July 1978 through December 1979. For the pre-1984 period, this reconstruction uses the CPI index (which is continuous) rather than the discontinued pre-1978 average price series, so this gap does not affect the data.

### Which series is "cheapest"

BLS publishes multiple ground beef price series:
- **APU0000703112** — "Ground beef, 100% beef, per lb." = regular ground beef (typically 73/27 or 80/20). **This is the series used here.** It is the cheapest widely tracked tier.
- **APU0000703113** — "Ground beef, lean and extra lean" = 85/15 to 93/7. Significantly more expensive.
- **APU0000FC1101** — "All uncooked ground beef" = weighted average across all types. Higher than APU0000703112 because it includes lean/extra lean in the mix. For example, in 2020: regular = $4.12 vs. all-types = $4.63.

### Fat ratio evolution

The cheapest ground beef has shifted in lean/fat composition over time:
- **1950s-1970s:** "Hamburger" or "ground beef" was typically ~70/30 (70% lean, 30% fat). USDA allowed up to 30% fat.
- **1980s-1990s:** 80/20 became the standard "regular" designation as consumer preferences shifted and USDA labeling tightened.
- **2000s-present:** 80/20 remains the cheapest widely available tier. Some stores offer 73/27 at a lower price point, but 80/20 is the standard baseline tracked by BLS.

This shift from 70/30 to 80/20 represents a modest quality improvement in the cheapest tier — slightly leaner, slightly more expensive to produce. The price series captures this transition naturally.

### LFTB ("pink slime") impact

Lean finely textured beef (LFTB) — beef trimmings processed via centrifugal separation and treated with ammonia — was widely used in ground beef from the 1990s through 2012. It reduced production costs by recovering lean meat from otherwise low-value trimmings. In March 2012, an ABC News investigation triggered a massive consumer backlash. Major retailers (Walmart, Kroger, Safeway, and others) dropped LFTB from their ground beef. This removal of a cheap input likely contributed to the 2012-2014 price acceleration, though it coincided with drought-driven supply contraction. Some producers quietly reintroduced LFTB after the controversy faded, but usage never returned to pre-2012 levels.

## Known gaps

- **1950-1983 are estimates**, not direct measurements. Individual years may be off by 10-15%, but the trend line is reliable.
- **1953-1956 cattle bust period** shows a dramatic ~40% price drop. This is historically real (post-Korean War oversupply) but the magnitude of the reconstructed swing may be slightly exaggerated by the CPI-to-price conversion.
- **October 2025** data is missing due to a federal appropriations lapse that temporarily shut down BLS data collection. The 2025 average uses 11 months.
- **No pre-1950 data** was attempted. The CPI series exists back to 1943, but the wartime period (price controls, rationing) makes those years non-comparable to peacetime retail prices.

## Structural breaks to annotate on graph

1. **1951-1955:** Korean War boom → post-war cattle bust. Prices halved.
2. **1973:** Nixon price controls lifted + OPEC oil crisis. Prices spiked ~25% in one year.
3. **1978-1980:** Great Inflation. Ground beef nearly doubled in two years.
4. **2003:** BSE (mad cow disease) scare. Canadian beef imports banned; prices jumped.
5. **2012:** "Pink slime" controversy. LFTB removal increased production costs.
6. **2014-2015:** Drought-induced herd contraction. All-time highs (at the time).
7. **2020 (Apr-Jun):** COVID meatpacking plant shutdowns. Sharp temporary spike.
8. **2023-2025:** Sustained surge to new records. Smallest cattle herd in 60+ years.
