# Ticket 024: CO2 / Greenhouse Gases → Climate Change

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that CO2 emissions cause global warming to binding international regulation limiting greenhouse gas emissions. This is the most politically complex case in the dataset — the longest ongoing lag.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Svante Arrhenius calculated the greenhouse effect of CO2 in 1896. Guy Callendar presented evidence of warming from CO2 in 1938. Charles Keeling began continuous CO2 measurements in 1958 (the Keeling Curve). The President's Science Advisory Committee warned about CO2 in 1965. Roger Revelle's work. What's the earliest credible scientific evidence that anthropogenic CO2 will cause significant warming?

2. **Consensus date** — IPCC First Assessment Report (1990) stated that human activities are increasing greenhouse gas concentrations. IPCC Second Assessment Report (1995) stated "the balance of evidence suggests a discernible human influence on global climate." Which is the formal consensus?

3. **First OECD regulation** — UNFCCC signed at Rio (1992). Kyoto Protocol (1997, entered into force 2005). Paris Agreement (2015). Which counts as "first binding regulation"? Kyoto had binding targets for developed nations. Paris has binding procedural requirements but nationally determined contributions.

4. **US regulation** — The US signed but never ratified Kyoto. The US joined Paris (2015), withdrew (2017), rejoined (2021). EPA's Endangerment Finding (2009) and Clean Power Plan (2015, later replaced). What's the first binding US federal regulation specifically targeting CO2?

5. **Industry opposition** — The fossil fuel industry's organized campaign to delay climate action is the largest and longest industry opposition campaign in history. Exxon's internal research (1970s-80s) confirmed climate science while funding external denial. The Global Climate Coalition (1989-2002). API, Koch Industries. Strong, sustained, multi-decade, multi-billion-dollar opposition.

6. **50% exposure reduction** — Global CO2 emissions have NOT declined 50% — they're still rising. This is the only hazard in the dataset where regulation has not yet achieved meaningful exposure reduction.

## Where to look

1. IPCC Assessment Reports (1990, 1995, 2001, 2007, 2013/14, 2021/22)
2. UNFCCC/Kyoto Protocol/Paris Agreement texts
3. Oreskes & Conway, "Merchants of Doubt" — climate denial chapter
4. Naomi Oreskes, "The Scientific Consensus on Climate Change" (Science, 2004)
5. Inside Climate News, "Exxon: The Road Not Taken" series
6. EPA Endangerment Finding (2009) and Clean Power Plan history

## What "good data" looks like

- The VERY long evidence history (1896-present) requires a clear choice of early_evidence_year — use when evidence became specific and actionable, not when the greenhouse effect was first theorized
- The distinction between UNFCCC (framework), Kyoto (binding targets for some), and Paris (universal but nationally determined) matters for what counts as "binding regulation"
- Industry opposition must be characterized as "strong" — this is the gold standard of organized delay
- Note that exposure reduction has NOT occurred — emissions are still rising

## Known pitfalls

- Climate change is categorically different from other hazards in this dataset. The "regulation" required is global economic restructuring, not banning a single chemical. The lag reflects political and economic complexity, not just scientific communication failure. This must be noted prominently.
- The early_evidence_year is debatable across nearly a century. Don't use 1896 (theoretical physics, not empirical observation of warming). Consider 1965 (President's Science Advisory Committee warning) or 1979 (Charney Report, first NAS assessment of CO2 warming).
- Category: international-treaty. The longest lag in this category.
- substitute_available: partial (renewables exist but cannot yet fully replace fossil fuels at current scale).
