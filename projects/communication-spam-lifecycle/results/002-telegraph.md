# Result: Telegraph

## Summary

The electric telegraph (1844-2006) represents the clearest control case for the cost-vs-spam hypothesis. With per-message costs ranging from $100+ in 2026 dollars at launch down to ~$10-20 even in its cheapest era, the telegraph was simply too expensive for spam to dominate. Unsolicited commercial telegrams did exist — the first recorded spam was an 1864 dentist advertisement sent to British politicians, and by the 1920s wealthy Americans were "deluged with unsolicited telegrams with investment offers." Western Union actively facilitated mass commercial telegrams, with the largest recorded batch exceeding 200,000 messages from a single sender. A 1928 handbook by Nelson E. Ross even taught businesses how to efficiently distribute bulk telegrams. Yet the per-message cost ceiling kept unsolicited commercial traffic to an estimated 5-15% of volume at its peak — never approaching the 50% crossover that kills other communication systems.

The telegraph peaked at 236 million messages in 1945 (driven by WWII military and personal use), then declined rapidly as long-distance telephone costs fell. Message volume halved between 1945 and 1960. Western Union sent its last telegram on January 27, 2006. Crucially, spam did not kill the telegraph — the telephone did, by offering a superior product (real-time voice, no intermediary, eventually cheaper for long-distance). The telegraph died from competitive displacement, not from spam saturation. This makes it the anchor data point: high per-message cost provides natural spam immunity, but doesn't save a system from being outcompeted.

The telegraph was overwhelmingly a business tool. Commercial traffic (stock quotes, commodity prices, news dispatches, business orders) constituted the majority of messages throughout its history. Personal use spiked during wartime (WWII "we regret to inform you" telegrams) but the cost barrier meant ordinary Americans sent only 1-2 telegrams per year even at peak usage. The system never achieved mass adoption as a personal communication medium — at its height, perhaps 25-30% of Americans sent at least one telegram per year.

## Key Data

**System profile:** Launched 1844 | Spam never reached crossover | Died 2006 | **The control case**

The telegraph is the clearest evidence that per-message cost is a natural spam inhibitor. At $25--120 per message (2026 dollars), mass unsolicited messaging was simply uneconomical. Spam peaked at roughly 10% of traffic in the 1920s and never went higher. The system died from competitive displacement by the telephone, not spam saturation.

### By the Numbers

- **$120** cost of a telegram at launch (2026 dollars) — the ultimate spam barrier
- **236 million** messages at peak volume (1945, driven by WWII)
- **~10%** peak spam share (1920s investment telegram era) — never approached crossover
- **79,434** telegraph operators employed nationwide (1920 census)
- **162 years** of operation (1844--2006) before Western Union pulled the plug
- **0** the number of communication systems killed by high per-message cost. The phone killed the telegraph by being better, not cheaper to spam.

### Regulations

| Year | Name | What it did |

| 1934 | Communications Act of 1934 | Brought telegraph under FCC regulation alongside telephone and radio. Regulated rates, fees, and terms of access. |

### Natural Defenses (Why Spam Never Won)

- **High per-message cost** (from day one) — At $25--120+ per message in 2026 dollars, mass unsolicited messaging was economically prohibitive. This was the fundamental anti-spam mechanism.
- **Skilled operator intermediary** (from day one) — Every message required a trained telegraph operator to encode and transmit. Human gatekeeping made fully automated spam impossible.
- **Night rate pricing** (1867) — Western Union's tiered pricing segmented traffic and kept prime-time lines clearer, though it did enable cheaper commercial messaging via Night Letters.

### Timeline Highlights

- **1844** — Morse sends "What hath God wrought" from Washington to Baltimore. Cost per message: ~$120 in today's dollars. No commercial traffic yet.
- **1864** — The first recorded spam in history: a British dentist sends unsolicited telegrams to politicians advertising his services.
- **1920** — Peak spam era. 155.9 million messages/year, 79,434 operators. Wealthy Americans "deluged with unsolicited telegrams with investment offers." Night Letter service (50 words for the price of 10) enables cheaper commercial messaging. Even so, spam is only ~10% of traffic.
- **1928** — Nelson E. Ross publishes a handbook teaching businesses how to distribute bulk telegrams efficiently. The largest recorded batch: 200,000+ messages from a single NYC sender. Even at scale, the economics cap spam share.
- **1945** — All-time peak: 236.2 million messages, driven by WWII military telegrams ("we regret to inform you"). Spam drops to ~3% as wartime traffic overwhelms everything else. Average message cost: $13.86 (2026 dollars).
- **1960** — Volume halved from peak. A 3-minute phone call now costs the same as a 15-word telegram. The telephone wins on every dimension. Per capita usage: 0.69 telegrams/year and falling.
- **2006** — Western Union sends its last telegram on January 27. The company had long since pivoted to money transfers. The system was already functionally dead.

## Confidence Assessment

**High confidence:**
- Message volumes (from EH.net table citing census/FCC data): ±5%
- Cost per message nominal rates: ±10% (rates varied by distance; I used NY-Chicago as representative)
- Timeline (launch, peak, death): exact dates well-documented
- Cause of death (telephone displacement, not spam): well-established historical consensus

**Medium confidence:**
- Inflation-adjusted costs: ±15% (CPI methodology varies for pre-1913 data)
- User share percentages: ±30% (derived from per-capita message volumes and assumptions about average sender frequency)
- Infrastructure cost per message: ±40% (derived from profit margin estimates applied to revenue per message)

**Low confidence:**
- Spam percentage estimates: ±50% (no systematic data exists on the commercial/unsolicited share of telegraph traffic; estimates are inferred from qualitative historical accounts of investment telegram spam, commercial telegram services, and the 1928 handbook)
- User time per message: ±30% (varied enormously by location — urban office visit vs. rural delivery rider)
- Platform ARPU: null (insufficient data to calculate meaningful per-user revenue)

**Key analytical finding:** The telegraph is the strongest evidence that per-message cost is a natural spam inhibitor. Spam never exceeded ~10-15% of traffic because the economic barrier made mass unsolicited messaging prohibitively expensive. The system died from competitive displacement (telephone), not spam saturation — making it the control case that anchors the cost-vs-spam-death correlation this project is investigating.
