# Result: Cycling / Swimming (Alternative Cardio)

## Summary

Cycling has moderate-to-strong evidence for all-cause mortality reduction comparable to walking on a per-MET-hour basis (10% at 11.25 MET-h/week, Kelly et al. 2014), with a more recent meta-analysis (Zhao et al. 2021, n=478,847) showing 23% reduction at highest vs lowest cycling levels and a 9% dose-response per 5 MET-h/week increase. Because cycling is higher intensity than walking (~6 MET vs ~3.5 MET), the same time commitment yields more MET-hours, making cycling slightly more time-efficient. However, cycling carries real injury/fatality risk (~1,100 deaths/year in the US) and requires equipment investment. Swimming evidence is thinner — one major cohort (Chase et al. 2008, men only, high SES) showed 53% lower mortality vs sedentary, and a more recent meta-analysis found HR 0.76 (24% reduction), but no swimming-specific QALY or cost-effectiveness analyses exist. Swimming also has higher cost and access barriers. **Recommendation: Include cycling as a graph data point; exclude swimming due to insufficient QALY evidence and population mismatch.**

## Cycling — QALY Evidence

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.07
- **Range:** 0.03 – 0.13 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** Kelly et al. (2014), Zhao et al. (2021)
- **Study type:** Meta-analyses of prospective cohort studies
- **Population studied:** General adult populations, predominantly European commuter cyclists (Kelly: 187,000 individuals, 2.1M person-years across 7 studies; Zhao: 478,847 participants, 27,860 deaths across 17 studies)
- **Population match to Bob:** Fair — evidence is heavily European, from commuter cycling populations. US recreational cycling patterns differ. Low-income populations underrepresented in cycling studies.
- **Key caveats:**
  - Kelly 2014 cycling data showed 10% mortality reduction at 11.25 MET-h/wk (95% CI: 6–13%), compared to 11% for walking — functionally equivalent per MET-hour
  - Zhao 2021 found 9% reduction per 5 MET-h/week increase (non-linear dose-response)
  - Cycling injury risk partially offsets health benefit (though net benefit far exceeds risk by 9:1 to 20:1 ratio in economic analyses)
  - Most studies cannot fully separate cycling from other physical activity
  - Healthy cyclist bias: people who cycle may be healthier at baseline

### QALY Derivation

Using walking (ticket 004) as the methodological anchor:
- Walking at 150 min/week (~8.75 MET-h/wk) → 11% mortality reduction → 0.08 QALY/year
- Cycling at 150 min/week (~15 MET-h/wk at 6 MET) → higher MET dose → ~12-15% mortality reduction based on dose-response
- Cycling-specific data at matched MET-hours (11.25 MET-h/wk) shows 10% reduction → slightly below walking
- Net: cycling at 150 min/week likely yields 0.07-0.09 QALY/year gross, minus ~0.005 for injury risk offset
- **Best estimate: 0.07 QALY/year** (conservative due to European population bias, injury offset, and slightly weaker cycling-specific evidence vs walking)

### Injury Risk Offset

- ~1,100 cyclist fatalities/year among ~67 million US cyclists
- Fatality rate: ~0.0016% per year for an average cyclist
- Non-fatal injuries requiring ER: ~580,000/year → ~0.87% per year
- Economic analyses consistently show health benefits outweigh injury costs by factor of 9–20×
- QALY subtraction for injury risk: ~0.003–0.008 (modest offset, already incorporated in best estimate)

## Swimming — QALY Evidence (Insufficient for Graph Inclusion)

- **Mortality reduction:** HR 0.76 (24% reduction, 95% CI: 0.63–0.92) from meta-analysis
- **Chase et al. (2008):** 40,547 men, swimmers had 53% lower all-cause mortality vs sedentary, 50% lower vs walkers, 49% lower vs runners
- **Critical limitations:**
  - Chase study: all white, well-educated, middle-to-upper-class men — extreme population mismatch with Bob
  - No swimming-specific QALY or cost-effectiveness analyses found in literature
  - Self-selection bias: swimming requires existing fitness, pool access, and skills — confounding is severe
  - No dose-response data for swimming specifically
- **Verdict:** Insufficient evidence quality for a standalone data point. The 53% figure is almost certainly inflated by selection bias (people who swim regularly are a health-conscious, affluent subpopulation).

## Cost Analysis (for Bob at ~$30k household income)

### Cycling

- **Annual direct cost:** $120
- **One-time startup cost:** $100–170 (used bike $75–130 + helmet $25–40)
- **Startup cost amortized over 3 years:** $33–57/year
- **Annual maintenance:** $50–100 (tubes, chain, brake pads, basic tune-up)
- **Total annual cost (best estimate):** $120
- **Cost range:** $75 – $250 (low – high)
  - Low: used bike from Craigslist ($50), basic helmet ($20), minimal maintenance ($50/yr) = $23 amortized + $50 = $73
  - Mid: decent used bike ($100), helmet ($30), regular maintenance ($75/yr) = $43 amortized + $75 = $118
  - High: new entry-level bike ($300), helmet ($40), maintenance ($100/yr) = $113 amortized + $100 = $213
- **With insurance (if applicable):** N/A
- **Without insurance:** Same as above
- **Cost notes:** Walmart sells new bikes from $100–200 that are functional for recreational riding. Used market (Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist) is robust in most areas. Replacement tube ($5–10) is most common maintenance item. Major repairs (new chain, brake pads) ~$30–50/year. Lock ($15–30, amortized) is necessary to prevent theft.

### Swimming (for reference, not included in graph)

- **Annual cost estimate:** $360–720
  - Public pool drop-in: $3–5/session × 3 sessions/week × 50 weeks = $450–750
  - Monthly membership (where available): $25–50/month = $300–600
  - Swimwear: $30–40/year
  - Note: Some cities (Chicago, Portland) offer free or heavily subsidized pool access for low-income residents
- **Why it's more expensive than cycling:** Requires ongoing facility access; no free equivalent to "just riding outside"

## Time Commitment

- **Hours per week:** 2.5 (30 min × 5 days, or 50 min × 3 days)
- **Category:** Medium (1-3 hr/wk)
- **Note:** Cycling achieves equivalent MET-hours to 3.5 hr/wk of walking in 2.5 hr/wk due to higher intensity (~6 MET cycling vs ~3.5 MET walking). This is the time-efficiency advantage.

## Access and Barriers

- **Activation energy:** Medium
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - **Safety:** Low-income neighborhoods have fewer bike lanes; cycling fatality rate is disproportionately higher in areas without cycling infrastructure. Cyclists face 2× fatality share relative to trip share.
  - **Theft:** Bicycle theft is prevalent in low-income urban areas. A stolen bike means loss of the entire investment.
  - **Weather/season:** Unlike walking (which can be done in most conditions), cycling is impractical in rain, snow, ice, or extreme heat
  - **Storage:** Apartment dwellers may lack secure bike storage
  - **Skill/fitness barrier:** Cycling requires baseline balance and comfort on roads; re-entry barrier for those who haven't ridden since childhood
  - **Geography:** Rural areas may lack safe roads for cycling; urban areas have traffic danger
  - **Maintenance knowledge:** Basic repairs (fixing a flat) require tools and know-how that Bob may not have

## Side Effects and Risks

- **Traffic injury/fatality:** ~1,100 cyclist deaths/year in US (NHTSA 2022). Risk is real but health benefit far exceeds it.
- **Musculoskeletal:** Knee pain (cycling is generally joint-friendly but improper fit causes issues), neck/back strain from position
- **Saddle discomfort:** Common deterrent for beginners; resolves with proper saddle and shorts
- **Overuse injuries:** Less common than running; cycling is low-impact
- **Net risk:** Moderate. Higher than walking but substantially lower than running or contact sports. Economic analyses show 9:1 to 20:1 benefit-to-risk ratio.

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate:** 0.40
- **Source:** Composite estimate. General exercise dropout: ~50% within 6 months (Dishman 1988). Cycling has additional barriers (weather, equipment, safety concerns) that likely reduce adherence below general exercise rates. However, cycling-based exercise programs show somewhat lower dropout than running/walking programs in HIIT studies (STRRIDE trials).
- **Notes:** Adherence is highly seasonal in most US climates. Theft, mechanical failure, and injury create hard stops that don't apply to walking. Social cycling groups improve adherence but aren't consistently available. Stationary cycling (gym) removes weather/safety barriers but adds cost (gym membership).

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 2
- **Tier 2** = Some RCTs, or strong observational data, or good data but population mismatch
- **Rationale:** Strong meta-analytic data exists (Kelly 2014, Zhao 2021) with large sample sizes, but:
  - Predominantly European commuter populations (less applicable to US recreational cycling)
  - No cycling-specific QALY studies found (estimates derived from mortality data)
  - Cannot fully isolate cycling from other physical activity
  - Injury risk adds uncertainty to net benefit
- **Key studies:**
  - Kelly P, Kahlmeier S, et al. (2014). Systematic review and meta-analysis of reduction in all-cause mortality from walking and cycling. *Int J Behav Nutr Phys Act*, 11:132.
  - Zhao Y, et al. (2021). Association of Cycling with Risk of All-Cause and CVD Mortality: Dose-Response Meta-analysis. *Sports Medicine*, 51:1755–1765.
  - Oja P, Titze S, et al. (2011). Health benefits of cycling: a systematic review. *Scand J Med Sci Sports*, 21:496–509.
  - Chase NL, Sui X, et al. (2008). Swimming and All-Cause Mortality Risk Compared with Running, Walking, and Sedentary Habits in Men. *Int J Aquatic Research & Education*, 2(3):3.
- **Limitations:**
  - Cycling-specific mortality data limited to 7–17 studies vs walking's 14+ studies
  - European commuter cycling (infrastructure-heavy, culturally normalized) may not transfer to Bob cycling on US roads without bike lanes
  - Swimming evidence insufficient for independent data point (one major cohort, extreme population mismatch)
  - Adherence data for cycling specifically in low-income populations is essentially nonexistent
