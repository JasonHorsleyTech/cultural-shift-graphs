## Judgment calls

**Early evidence year (1950 vs. 1939):** Franz Müller published the world's first case-control epidemiological study linking tobacco to lung cancer in 1939 in Nazi Germany. However, I chose 1950 because: (1) Müller's study had significant methodological limitations (small sample of 96 cases, unexplained assumptions about 20 cases, unclear sampling of controls); (2) it was published in German in a journal not widely read internationally; (3) it was effectively buried after WWII due to association with Nazi-era science; (4) neither the international scientific community nor policymakers engaged with it until it was rediscovered decades later. The 1950 Doll & Hill and Wynder & Graham studies were independently conducted, larger, methodologically stronger, published in top English-language journals, and directly sparked the modern scientific and policy debate. They represent the effective starting point of the science-to-action clock.

**Consensus year (1962 vs. 1964):** The UK Royal College of Physicians report (March 1962) predates the US Surgeon General's report (January 1964) by nearly two years. The RCP report was the first formal statement by a major government-affiliated medical body explicitly declaring cigarettes a cause of lung cancer. It sold 50,000 copies in six weeks and triggered immediate public and policy response. Using 1962 rather than 1964 is correct per the plan's definition of "first major scientific body consensus statement."

**First regulation (Italy 1962):** Italy's Law 165 (April 10, 1962) is the first binding tobacco regulation in an OECD country. It comprehensively banned all tobacco advertising — not merely a labeling requirement or voluntary guideline. While an advertising ban is an indirect mechanism (it reduces new uptake rather than directly restricting the act of smoking), it is binding legislation that meaningfully restricts the tobacco industry's ability to drive population exposure. It predates the UK's voluntary advertising restrictions (1965 Television Act), the US labeling requirement (1965), and all other OECD binding tobacco measures.

**Category (behavioral-mandate):** Tobacco remains a legal product. Regulation took the form of advertising bans, health warnings, age restrictions, and indoor smoking prohibitions — all mandating or restricting behavior rather than banning the substance itself. This distinguishes it from substance-ban cases like DDT or lead paint.

**50% exposure reduction (2005):** CDC data shows US adult smoking prevalence peaked at 42.4% in 1965. According to CDC and National Academies reporting, prevalence declined to approximately half the 1965 rate by 2005 (~21%). This is a US-specific figure; other countries reached 50% reduction at different times.

**Industry opposition:** The tobacco case is the most extensively documented instance of organized industry opposition to science in history. Beginning with the December 1953 Plaza Hotel meeting of cigarette manufacturer executives and the subsequent creation of the Tobacco Industry Research Committee (with PR firm Hill & Knowlton), the industry sustained a 50+ year campaign to manufacture doubt, fund misleading research, suppress internal findings, and delay regulation. The 1969 internal memo stating "doubt is our product" became the emblematic statement of the strategy. A 2006 federal court found this constituted a RICO violation.

**Lag interpretation:** The 12-year lag from evidence to first regulation (1950→1962) is relatively short, but this captures only the first regulatory action. The full regulatory ratchet took decades: advertising bans (1962-1971), health warnings (1965+), broadcast ad bans (1971), workplace bans (1990s-2000s), indoor public smoking bans (2004-2010s), plain packaging (2012+). The gap between first evidence and comprehensive regulation is closer to 50-60 years. The graph will show the first binding action (1962) as the endpoint, but this understates the true lag to meaningful exposure reduction.
