# Music and Radio Content Quotas

Like film screen quotas, radio content quotas are defensive legislation against American cultural dominance. Countries that mandate minimum domestic-language or domestic-origin music on radio are admitting that without protection, American/English music would dominate.

**Metric:** Number of countries with active radio/music content quotas mandating domestic-language or domestic-origin music, by decade. Timeline of enactment for each.

**Data proxy:** National broadcasting authority regulations, UNESCO cultural diversity reports, WTO cultural exception documentation, European Audiovisual Observatory.

**Interesting angles:**
- France: Toubon Law (1994) requires 40% French-language music on radio. The most famous case.
- Canada: CRTC "Canadian content" rules (1971) require 35% Canadian music on radio. Predates France.
- Australia: voluntary "Australian music" codes and various quota proposals.
- South Korea: no formal quotas but government subsidies for K-pop development — a different defensive strategy.
- Nigeria: broadcasting code requires 60% Nigerian content.
- Compare quota countries' outcomes: does mandating domestic content actually sustain local music industries, or does it just irritate listeners?
- Digital platforms: quotas written for radio don't apply to Spotify/YouTube. Track whether streaming has undermined quota effectiveness.
- EU Audiovisual Media Services Directive (2018): requires streaming platforms to carry 30% European content. The fight has moved to streaming.
