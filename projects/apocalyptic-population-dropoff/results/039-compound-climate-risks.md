# Result: Compound Climate Risks — Multiple Tipping Points and Cascading Failure

## Summary

The compound climate scenario at 4°C+ warming involves multiple interacting tipping points (AMOC collapse, permafrost thaw, ice sheet loss, ecosystem dieback) that cascade and amplify each other, potentially locking Earth into a "Hothouse" trajectory. Unlike all other scenarios in this project, mortality unfolds over decades through economic decline, agricultural failure, water scarcity, displacement, and institutional erosion rather than a single acute event. The uncertainty range is enormous — plausibly 10–80% US population loss over a century — making this the widest-banded and most speculative curve in the set.

## Tipping Point Cascade Mechanics

### Which tipping points trigger which (Lenton et al. 2019, Armstrong McKay et al. 2022)

The cascade pathway at 4°C+:

1. **Already active (current ~1.2°C):** Greenland ice sheet loss, West Antarctic ice sheet loss, tropical coral reef die-off, boreal permafrost thaw (gradual), Labrador Sea circulation weakening
2. **Triggered at 1.5–2°C:** Mountain glacier loss (likely), additional permafrost acceleration, possible AMOC weakening, Amazon rainforest dieback begins
3. **Triggered at 2–4°C:** AMOC collapse becomes likely, boreal forest shift, West Antarctic ice sheet collapse accelerates, East Antarctic subglacial basins destabilize
4. **Above 4°C:** Potential "Hothouse Earth" lock-in — self-reinforcing feedbacks (permafrost carbon release, forest dieback CO₂, ice-albedo loss) drive further warming independent of human emissions

**Key interaction:** Tipping point interactions can lower individual thresholds. For example, AMOC collapse redistributes heat, accelerating Arctic ice loss, which reduces albedo, which warms permafrost, which releases methane, which accelerates everything. This cascade effect means the compound scenario is worse than the sum of individual risks.

### Regional US Compound Picture at 4°C+

- **Southwest:** Megadrought (already in worst drought in 1,200 years) becomes permanent aridification. Colorado River flows decline 30–40%. Ogallala Aquifer effectively nonrenewable. 40M+ people lose reliable water supply. Agricultural collapse in California's Central Valley.
- **Southeast:** Extreme heat (wet-bulb danger days multiply), intensified hurricanes, 1–2m sea level rise floods coastal cities. Gulf Stream effects compound with AMOC disruption.
- **Midwest:** Paradoxically may gain precipitation but faces extreme weather volatility (floods, heat waves, derecho events). Could become US agricultural core — but transition is chaotic.
- **Coasts:** 1–2m sea level rise displaces 13–20M people. Miami, New Orleans, Houston, parts of NYC face permanent inundation or managed retreat.
- **West:** Wildfire seasons become year-round. Ecological regime shifts (forests → scrubland). Water scarcity drives depopulation of major metros (Phoenix, Las Vegas, parts of LA).

## The Economic Collapse Pathway

Unlike other scenarios, climate cascades kill primarily through economic and institutional decline:

1. **GDP erosion:** Hsiang et al. (2017) estimate ~10% US GDP loss at 4°C (quadratic damage function: 0.283×ΔT + 0.146×ΔT²). Burke et al. (2015) show productivity peaks at 13°C mean annual temperature, declining sharply above — many US regions pushed well above optimal.
2. **Inequality amplification:** Poorest US counties face 2–20% income loss (Hsiang et al. 2017), while northern counties may see modest gains. This internal inequality destabilizes politics and institutions.
3. **Institutional stress threshold:** Historical analogues (Venezuela's 86% GDP contraction → institutional collapse; Soviet dissolution at ~40% GDP decline) suggest sustained GDP losses above 20–30% trigger institutional failure. Climate-driven decline would be slower but more sustained than these analogues.
4. **The critical difference from Venezuela:** Climate decline is not policy-reversible. Venezuela's collapse was self-inflicted and theoretically recoverable. Climate cascade locks in continued degradation regardless of policy changes once tipping points are crossed.

## Why This Curve Is Unique

1. **Timescale:** Decades to a century, not hours to years. The x-axis treatment must differ from other scenarios.
2. **Adaptation:** Unlike sudden disasters, this allows adaptation — but adaptation has diminishing returns as compound stresses multiply. The curve reflects the gap between adaptation potential and compound stress accumulation.
3. **No clear floor:** Other scenarios eventually stabilize at some carrying capacity. Climate cascades may not stabilize within a century if "Hothouse Earth" is triggered — warming continues, and carrying capacity continues to decline.
4. **Recommendation for graph:** This curve should be visually differentiated — a dashed line, separate panel, or different x-axis scale. Placing it on the same log-scale x-axis as nuclear war will make it look deceptively mild in the early years and incomparably catastrophic at long timescales.

## Data Gaps

- **Compound tipping interaction quantification:** Lenton et al. 2019 and Armstrong McKay et al. 2022 identify interactions qualitatively, but no model quantifies the compound mortality impact of multiple simultaneous tipping points. Each is modeled independently; the interaction effect is expert judgment, not simulation.
- **US-specific mortality at 4°C+:** Kemp et al. (2022) discuss 10% to extinction globally but provide no US-specific mortality curve. The US is better positioned than most nations (temperate latitude, agricultural capacity, institutional strength) but how much better is unquantified.
- **Adaptation ceiling:** No rigorous estimate exists for how much adaptation can offset compound climate stress. Models assume some adaptation but don't model adaptation failure or the cost of perpetual adaptation.
- **Institutional resilience under chronic stress:** Historical analogues for institutional failure (Venezuela, Soviet Union) involve acute political/economic shocks, not multi-decade environmental degradation. Whether US institutions erode gradually or hold until a breaking point is unknown.
- **Post-AMOC agricultural reorganization:** If AMOC collapses, the US Midwest may become the global agricultural core — but the transition period (decades) and its mortality cost are completely unmodeled.
- **Net temperature mortality crossover:** Below ~3°C warming, reduced cold deaths may offset increased heat deaths in the US. Above 3°C, heat mortality overwhelms. The exact crossover point and its sensitivity to adaptation are uncertain.
- **Migration-driven mortality:** Internal US climate migration at scale (tens of millions) would strain receiving communities. The mortality impact of mass internal displacement in a wealthy country has no historical precedent.
