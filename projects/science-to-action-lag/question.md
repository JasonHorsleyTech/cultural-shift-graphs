# How long does it take society to act on what science already knows?

## The question

For major public health and environmental hazards, what is the lag between scientific consensus forming and meaningful policy/behavioral change — and is that lag getting shorter or longer over time?

## Concrete example & napkin math

**Smoking causes cancer:**
- Scientific consensus: ~1953-1954 (Doll & Hill study, Wynder & Graham)
- Surgeon General report: 1964 (10 years)
- TV/radio ad ban: 1971 (17 years)
- Indoor smoking bans widespread: ~2005 (50 years)
- US smoking rate drops below 15%: ~2015 (60 years)

**Lead causes brain damage:**
- Scientific evidence strong: 1920s-1930s
- Lead paint banned (US): 1978 (~50 years)
- Leaded gas phase-out begins: 1975 (~45 years)
- Blood lead levels drop to "safe": ~1990s (60+ years)

**CFCs destroy the ozone layer:**
- Molina & Rowland paper: 1974
- Montreal Protocol signed: 1987 (13 years)
- Full phase-out in developed nations: 1996 (22 years)

The pattern seems to be roughly 15-50 years from consensus to action, depending on how you measure "action." But is there actually a pattern? Is it getting faster (we learned from smoking) or slower (corporate resistance is more sophisticated now)?

## What the graph would look like

X-axis: decade of scientific consensus. Y-axis: years to meaningful policy action.

**Expected shape:** A roughly flat line around 20-30 years, maybe with slight improvement in recent decades due to faster information flow.

**What would be surprising:** If the lag is actually getting LONGER despite better communication technology. This would suggest corporate/political resistance has scaled faster than scientific communication. Or if there's a clear bimodal distribution — some hazards get acted on in 5 years, others take 50, with nothing in between.

## The metric

**Primary metric:** Years from "scientific consensus" to "first binding national regulation in a major economy."

Defining "consensus": A date is assigned when either (a) a major scientific body issued a consensus statement, or (b) meta-analyses/reviews in top journals reached clear agreement. This will require judgment calls, but we can document the reasoning.

Defining "action": First binding national legislation or regulation (not voluntary guidelines) in the US, EU, or equivalent major economy that meaningfully restricts exposure.

**Secondary metric:** Years from consensus to 50% reduction in population exposure (where measurable).

## Where the data lives

- Medical/scientific journals for consensus dates (PubMed, Lancet, NEJM archives)
- Legislative databases for regulation dates (US Congress, EU directives, WHO recommendations)
- CDC/EPA/WHO reports for exposure reduction timelines
- Industry-specific regulatory histories (FDA, OSHA, EPA)
- Historical accounts for pre-1950 hazards

## What would make this NOT interesting

- If every hazard is completely idiosyncratic and there's no pattern whatsoever (just random scatter)
- If the answer is already well-known and widely published in this exact format (I've seen individual case studies but never a systematic comparison plotted together)
- If "scientific consensus" is too subjective to pin down consistently — if experts disagree wildly on when consensus formed for each item, the dates become meaningless
- If the metric reduces to "it depends on how much money the industry has" and nothing else — that's one sentence, not a graph
