# Ticket 013: Formaldehyde → Cancer

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that formaldehyde causes cancer (particularly nasopharyngeal cancer and leukemia) to binding regulation restricting formaldehyde exposure.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Animal studies showed formaldehyde caused nasal cancer in rats (CIIT study, 1979-1980). Epidemiological evidence in humans accumulated through the 1980s-2000s. NCI studies of funeral industry workers and formaldehyde plant workers showed elevated cancer risk. When did credible evidence of human carcinogenicity emerge?

2. **Consensus date** — IARC classified formaldehyde as "probably carcinogenic" (Group 2A) in 1995, upgraded to "carcinogenic to humans" (Group 1) in 2004 (reaffirmed 2012). NTP listed formaldehyde as "known to be a human carcinogen" in 2011. These are key consensus milestones.

3. **First OECD regulation** — Look for the earliest binding regulation specifically targeting formaldehyde in consumer products (not just occupational limits). EU restrictions on formaldehyde in cosmetics, building materials? Japan's building code restrictions? Some countries limit formaldehyde emissions from composite wood products (plywood, particleboard).

4. **US regulation** — OSHA workplace PEL (0.75 ppm, lowered from 3 ppm in 1992). EPA formaldehyde emission standards for composite wood products (Formaldehyde Standards for Composite Wood Products Act, 2010, regulations finalized 2016-2017). California CARB regulations preceded federal action (2007).

5. **Industry opposition** — The Formaldehyde Council and chemical manufacturers challenged OSHA's standard and lobbied against stricter limits. The Georgia-Pacific/Koch Industries connection to formaldehyde industry lobbying is documented.

6. **Substitute availability** — Formaldehyde-free resins exist for some applications but are more expensive. Partial substitutability.

## Where to look

1. IARC Monograph on formaldehyde (Volume 88, 2006; Volume 100F, 2012)
2. NTP Report on Carcinogens — formaldehyde profile
3. OSHA formaldehyde standard history (29 CFR 1910.1048)
4. EPA Formaldehyde Emission Standards for Composite Wood Products
5. PubMed — "formaldehyde carcinogenicity history" or "formaldehyde regulation"

## What "good data" looks like

- Distinguish between occupational exposure limits (OSHA PEL) and consumer product emission standards (composite wood) — both are regulatory actions, use the first binding restriction
- The IARC upgrade from Group 2A (1995) to Group 1 (2004) is itself a data point about how long it takes to formalize consensus
- US regulation history should note both the OSHA standard (occupational) and the composite wood product standard (consumer)

## Known pitfalls

- Formaldehyde is ubiquitous (building materials, cosmetics, embalming, lab preservative) — the regulatory story is fragmented across multiple product categories. Pick the primary general-population exposure route (building materials / indoor air) and be consistent.
- The IARC Group 1 classification (2004) and NTP "known carcinogen" listing (2011) are formal consensus, but regulation predates both (OSHA lowered PEL in 1992). This is a case where regulation moved before formal consensus — note this.
- Category: exposure-limit (though it borders on substance regulation for composite wood products).
