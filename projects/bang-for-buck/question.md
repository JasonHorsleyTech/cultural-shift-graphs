# Bang for Buck: Life Improvements for the Bottom Quartile

## The question
For an American at the 25th percentile of household income — with the correlated health, mental health, and social profile that income bracket carries — which common life interventions deliver the most quality-of-life improvement per unit of cost and effort?

## Why it's interesting
Everyone intuitively knows that exercise is good for you, but nobody frames it against the full competitive set of interventions (medication, surgery, therapy, lifestyle changes) using a unified cost-effectiveness metric. The hypothesis is that walking — free, zero-barrier, immediate-payoff walking — dominates every other intervention by a comical margin. If the data backs that up, the graph should make it visually obvious.

## The persona: "Bob"
Don't construct a synthetic worst-case composite. Anchor on the **25th percentile of US household income** (~$30k) and pull the actual correlated statistics for that bracket. CDC, BLS, Gallup, and BRFSS all slice by income quartile. That person already has the higher BMI, higher depression rates, fewer close relationships, less job autonomy, etc. That's Bob.

Quality-of-life dimensions to profile:
1. **Physical health** — BMI, chronic conditions, mobility, pain levels
2. **Mental health** — depression prevalence, anxiety, cognitive function
3. **Financial security** — debt, savings, emergency buffer, insurance status
4. **Social connection** — relationship status, close friendships, loneliness measures
5. **Autonomy / agency** — job flexibility, sense of control over own life

## Interventions to evaluate (not exhaustive)
- Walking (30 min/day)
- Gym membership / structured exercise
- Antidepressants (SSRIs)
- Therapy (CBT)
- Getting a dog
- Bariatric surgery
- Diet overhaul (cooking at home vs. fast food)
- Meditation / mindfulness
- Quitting or reducing social media / screen time
- Joining a club or community group
- Sleep hygiene improvements
- Strength training
- Yoga
- Volunteering
- Religious community participation
- Reading regularly
- Learning a new skill / continuing education

## Cost metric (composite, not just dollars)
Each intervention should be scored across:
- Financial cost (annual, relative to Bob's ~$30k income)
- Activation energy (how hard to start — insurance needed? referral? equipment? car?)
- Ongoing effort (daily/weekly burden to sustain)
- Time to first noticeable benefit
- Access barriers specific to Bob's bracket (geography, stigma, availability)
- Risks and downsides (side effects, injury risk, long-term health costs)

## Benefit metric
QALYs (Quality-Adjusted Life Years) or a QALY-adjacent measure. Published literature exists for QALY gains of most major interventions. This provides a defensible, cross-dimensional number without inventing a custom index.

Where interventions have significant tradeoffs (e.g., medication that improves mood but carries long-term organ risk), use **net** QALYs.

## Research approach
- **Ticket 1:** Profile Bob using real data for the 25th percentile income bracket across all five QoL dimensions.
- **Tickets 2–N:** One per intervention. For each: evidence-based QoL improvement for someone in Bob's profile, cost across all axes, confidence level. Exclude interventions too new for longitudinal data (e.g., GLP-1 agonists like Ozempic).
- **Synthesis ticket:** Normalize to a unified bang-for-buck ratio.

## Neutral framing for research agents
"For an American in the 25th percentile income bracket (with correlated health/lifestyle profile), rank common lifestyle and medical interventions by cost-effectiveness measured in QALYs gained per dollar and per unit of effort."

No mention of walking or any expected winner in research prompts.

## Graph concept
Scatter or bubble chart. X-axis = total composite cost. Y-axis = QALY gain. Bubble size = evidence confidence level. The punchline should be visually immediate: walking sits alone in the top-left corner.
