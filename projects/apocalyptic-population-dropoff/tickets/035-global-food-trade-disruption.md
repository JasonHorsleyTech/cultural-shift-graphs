# Ticket 035: Global Food Trade Disruption — Cascading Effects on US Food Security

## What to research

The US is both a major food exporter and importer. In a global breadbasket failure, international food trade collapses. What does the US lose when trade stops?

## Specific data needed

1. **US food trade balance:**
   - US food exports: what, how much, to whom? (The US is the world's largest food exporter)
   - US food imports: what, how much, from whom?
   - Net caloric position: does the US produce more calories than it consumes? (Yes, significantly — the US is a net food exporter)
   - Key imports: tropical fruits, coffee, cocoa, seafood — how much is calorically important vs. luxury?

2. **Self-sufficiency scenario:**
   - If all food trade stopped tomorrow (but US agriculture was fine), could the US feed itself?
   - Answer is likely yes for basic calories — the US produces far more than it consumes
   - But: current agriculture depends on imported fertilizer components, machinery parts, etc.

3. **Fertilizer supply chain:**
   - US imports ~50% of its nitrogen fertilizer
   - Potash: primarily from Canada and Belarus
   - Phosphate: US has domestic supply (Florida, Idaho)
   - Without fertilizer imports, how much does US crop yield drop?
   - Timeline: existing soil nutrients last how long before yields crash?

4. **Export ban cascading effects:**
   - When one country bans food exports, others follow (happened during 2007-2008 food crisis)
   - Russia/Ukraine grain export disruptions (2022) as recent analogue
   - Game theory of food trade: cooperation breaks down under scarcity

5. **US food vulnerability:**
   - The US's main vulnerability is NOT food quantity but FERTILIZER
   - Without imported fertilizer, yields decline 30-50% within 2-3 years
   - Also: fuel for farming, spare parts for machinery
   - Climate-disrupted US agriculture + no imports = how many can be fed?

6. **Political/social stability under food pressure:**
   - US has not experienced genuine food scarcity since the Dust Bowl (1930s)
   - At what point does food insecurity trigger social unrest?
   - Historical: food riots, French Revolution, Arab Spring — all linked to food prices

## Where to look

1. **USDA ERS** — food trade data, self-sufficiency metrics
2. **USDA FAS (Foreign Agricultural Service)** — global trade flows
3. **USGS** — mineral commodities (fertilizer data)
4. **IFA (International Fertilizer Association)** — global fertilizer trade
5. **Chatham House (2015)** — "Chokepoints and Vulnerabilities in Global Food Trade"
6. **Headey & Fan (2008)** — 2007-2008 food price crisis analysis

## Result format

Key Data Points:
- `us_caloric_self_sufficiency_pct` — current (without trade disruption)
- `us_caloric_production_without_fertilizer_imports_pct` — after 1 year, 3 years
- `critical_import_dependencies` — fertilizer components, fuel, machinery
- `food_insecurity_social_unrest_threshold`

## What good data looks like

- The key insight should be: the US CAN feed itself in the short term, but CANNOT maintain current yields without fertilizer imports
- Distinguishes between "trade stops for 1 year" (manageable) vs. "trade stops for 5 years" (yield collapse from fertilizer depletion)
- Notes that in a breadbasket failure scenario, the US is BOTH a victim and the world's best-positioned survivor
- This ticket provides the food supply context for the breadbasket failure mortality curve
