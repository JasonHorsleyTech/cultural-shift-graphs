# Long-Haul Truck Driver — Freight

Trucking moves 70% of US freight by value. The driver sits in a cab for 11 hours a day. The customer (shipper) pays a per-mile or per-load rate. Somewhere between the shipper and the driver, the money gets sliced up by brokers, carriers, fuel, insurance, and equipment costs.

**Fundamental worker:** Long-haul truck driver (Class 8, over-the-road)
**Customer cost proxy:** Average freight rate per mile or per load (DAT, ATRI, and ATA publish this data). Or total trucking industry revenue divided by number of drivers.

**Interesting angles:**
- Owner-operators vs. company drivers: completely different ratio stories. Owner-operators absorb equipment costs but keep more of the gross. Company drivers get a salary but the company captures the spread.
- Deregulation (Motor Carrier Act 1980) — this was trucking's version of airline deregulation. What happened to the driver's share?
- Freight brokers are a massive middleman layer that barely existed before deregulation. They take 15-25% of the shipping rate and never touch the cargo.
