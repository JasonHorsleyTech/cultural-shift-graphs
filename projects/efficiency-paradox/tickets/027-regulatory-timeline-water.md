# Regulatory Timeline: Water Efficiency and Conservation Policies

## What to Research

A detailed timeline of US water efficiency regulations and conservation programs, with focus on the residential/public supply sector. This domain has one of the strongest regulatory stories — water per capita fell significantly, largely due to mandates.

## Where to Look

1. **Primary:** EPA WaterSense program history and documentation.
2. **Secondary:** Congressional Research Service (CRS) reports on EPAct 1992 water provisions.
3. **Tertiary:** Alliance for Water Efficiency — history of water efficiency policy.
4. **For drought/conservation orders:** California State Water Resources Control Board (for CA-specific mandates as case study).
5. **Municipal programs:** Major utilities' conservation program histories (but focus on federal/state mandates for consistency).

## Scope

Cover:
1. **Energy Policy Act of 1992 (EPAct)** — the watershed (pun intended) federal regulation:
   - Toilet maximum: 1.6 GPF (effective Jan 1994)
   - Showerhead maximum: 2.5 GPM at 80 psi
   - Faucet maximum: 2.2 GPM (later 2.0 via DOE)
   - Urinal maximum: 1.0 GPF
2. **EPA WaterSense program (launched 2006):**
   - Voluntary labeling for fixtures exceeding federal minimums
   - Toilets: ≤1.28 GPF for WaterSense label
   - Showerheads: ≤2.0 GPM
   - Irrigation controllers, landscape programs
3. **DOE clothes washer water standards:** Water Factor requirements (2004, 2007, 2015 rulemakings)
4. **State and local mandates:**
   - California: SB X7-7 (2009, 20% per-capita reduction by 2020), drought emergency orders (2014-2017)
   - Texas, Colorado, Arizona mandatory restrictions during droughts
5. **Plumbing code changes:** Uniform Plumbing Code and International Plumbing Code adopted federal fixture standards; some jurisdictions require WaterSense-level.
6. **Water pricing:** Did any major utilities move to tiered/conservation pricing? (This isn't regulation exactly, but it's a deliberate policy tool.)

## Data Gaps

- National-level attribution of savings to specific policies is hard — water is locally managed, and each utility's experience differs.
- The relative contribution of regulation vs. pricing vs. drought awareness is debated in the literature. Provide the best available estimates.

## What "Good Data" Looks Like

- Clear timeline showing EPAct 1992 as the dominant federal intervention.
- Evidence that the post-1985 decline in per-capita water use correlates with the stock turnover of pre-EPAct fixtures.
- Some quantification of the California drought effect (2014-2017) — did it persist after the drought ended?
- The key narrative: water's high EDC is primarily explained by regulation (you couldn't buy a 5-GPF toilet after 1994 even if you wanted to) combined with slow stock turnover. This is not "people chose to use less water" — it's "the government made the infrastructure physically incapable of delivering as much water per use."
