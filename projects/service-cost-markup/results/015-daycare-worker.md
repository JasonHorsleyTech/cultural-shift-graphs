# Early Childhood / Childcare — Daycare Worker

## Summary

The childcare worker ratio is among the highest of any industry at 43–51%, yet the worker remains near poverty wages. This is the structural paradox: mandated staff-to-child ratios (typically 1:4 for infants, 1:8 for preschoolers) cap the revenue each worker can generate at roughly $45,000–$62,000 per year. Even taking half of that leaves workers earning $20,000–$32,000. The ratio dipped to its lowest (~43%) from 2010–2015 as childcare costs outpaced stagnant wages, then recovered post-2018 as tight labor markets and minimum wage increases pushed childcare wages up significantly. Unlike college or healthcare, the "bloat" in childcare isn't administrative overhead — labor already consumes 60–80% of revenue. The problem is that the denominator (children per worker) is structurally capped by regulation.

## Methodology

**Worker compensation:** Median annual wage for BLS occupation code 39-9011 (Childcare Workers). Data from 2012–2022 sourced from BLS OES via usawage.com historical tables. Data from 2004–2011 derived from recruiter.com's mean annual wage trend (sourced from BLS OES), adjusted downward by ~5% to approximate median (the mean-to-median ratio for this occupation is roughly 1.05 based on years where both are available). Pre-2004 estimates extrapolated from wage growth trends and corroborated against known data points (e.g., Seattle childcare workers earned ~$5.21/hr in 1989 per Cornell labor history research, above national average). The 2024 figure ($32,050) comes from BLS OOH reporting median hourly of $15.41 × 2,080 hours.

**Customer cost:** Average annual cost of center-based childcare per child, blended across age groups (infant, toddler, preschool). Anchored to a 2020 national blended average of ~$10,600/year, derived from known data points: infant center-based care averaged $12,300 nationally in 2020 (Child Care Aware), while toddler and preschool care costs are lower. Cross-validated against Child Care Aware's 2023 all-age national average of $11,582 (CPI-projected from 2020 anchor = $11,860, within 2.4% of reported figure). All other years derived using the BLS Consumer Price Index for Day Care and Preschool (base year 1990 = 100), which provides a consistent longitudinal measure of childcare price changes from 1990–2026.

**Children per worker:** Blended staff-to-child ratio across age groups in center-based care. The 2019 NSECE (National Survey of Early Care and Education) provides the anchor: 121,000 center-based providers employed 1.82 million staff who worked directly with 9.49 million children — yielding 5.2 children per staff member nationally. This blended ratio reflects the mix of mandated ratios by age: typically 1:3–1:4 for infants, 1:4–1:6 for toddlers, and 1:7–1:10 for preschoolers, weighted by enrollment patterns that skew toward older children. Earlier decades estimated at slightly higher ratios (5.5–6.0) reflecting less formalized regulatory enforcement and higher proportions of informal care arrangements.

**Why this proxy:** Average annual childcare cost per child is the most direct "what the customer pays" measure. It captures the full price parents pay for the service, regardless of whether it goes to labor, rent, or overhead. Using the daycare-specific CPI to derive consistent figures across decades ensures longitudinal comparability even where direct cost surveys aren't available for every year.

## Notes

- **The paradox explained:** Childcare is one of the few industries where the worker already captures roughly half of what the customer pays — a ratio that would be remarkable in healthcare, education, or legal services. Yet the worker is still poor. The structural constraint is the mandated staff-to-child ratio: a childcare worker can serve at most 4–8 children depending on age, capping total revenue per worker at $45,000–$65,000. After the worker takes ~50%, only $20,000–$30,000 remains for rent, insurance, supplies, food, administration, and profit. This is why most daycare centers are small businesses barely breaking even.

- **No villain in the cost structure:** Unlike college (where administrative bloat is a major driver) or healthcare (where billing complexity and administrative overhead consume enormous shares), childcare costs are overwhelmingly labor. The Center for American Progress finds labor is 60–80% of total program expenses. The remaining 20–40% covers rent, utilities, food, supplies, insurance, and administration — none of which is easily reducible. There are no shareholders extracting profits; most centers operate as small businesses or nonprofits.

- **The ratio dip (2010–2015):** Childcare costs rose steadily through the 2010s (CPI index: 240 in 2010 → 275 in 2015, +15%), but childcare worker wages barely moved ($19,300 → $20,320, +5%). This compressed the ratio to its lowest point (~43%). Workers were getting a smaller share of a growing pie, while remaining at poverty wages.

- **Post-2018 recovery:** Tight labor markets, state minimum wage increases, and pandemic-era attention to "essential workers" drove childcare wages up sharply: +38% from 2018 ($23,240) to 2024 ($32,050). Childcare costs also rose but more slowly (+25% over the same period), pushing the ratio back above 50%.

- **Pre-1990 data limitation:** Formal childcare as an industry barely existed before the 1970s. The BLS didn't track childcare-specific CPI until 1990. Pre-1990 estimates are based on fragmentary sources and should be treated as rough indicators only. The U.S. government didn't systematically collect childcare cost data until the early 1980s.

- **Children per worker is the key variable:** This ratio is the most uncertain input in the calculation, and it matters enormously. At 4 children per worker (infant care), the ratio would be higher (~60%+) but revenue per worker drops to ~$40,000. At 8 children per worker (older preschoolers), the ratio drops (~30%) but revenue per worker rises to ~$80,000. The blended 5.0–5.2 figure from NSECE 2019 is the best available national average, but any given center's economics depend heavily on its age-group mix.

- **Graph component note:** This industry will plot very differently from most others — it will appear near the top of the chart (high ratio), telling a story not of institutional bloat but of structural constraint. Consider annotating or color-coding it differently to signal that a high ratio isn't always "good for the worker."
