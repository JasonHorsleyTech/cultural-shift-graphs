# Television — Quality Rubric

## What to research

Define what "a television" meant in ~1955 and build a quality rubric. The explore phase expects this to be a "progress wins" case — the modern product is better in every measurable way. Verify this and define what the quality-equivalent would be.

## Research tasks

1. **1955 baseline:** A console or tabletop TV set. What were the specs? (~15-21" screen, black and white, 2-13 channels via antenna, vacuum tube technology, required periodic tube replacement, furniture-grade cabinet.) What brands were standard (RCA, Zenith, Admiral, Philco)?

2. **Quality attributes to score (minimum 5):**
   - Screen size
   - Picture quality (resolution, color, contrast)
   - Sound quality
   - Reliability / maintenance requirements (tube replacement vs. solid-state)
   - Content access (3-13 free channels vs. hundreds of channels/streaming)
   - Physical design / form factor
   - Energy consumption
   - Lifespan (years before replacement)

3. **This is almost certainly "progress wins."** A $250 2025 TV is objectively better than a 1955 TV on every technical dimension except possibly:
   - **Repairability:** 1955 TVs were repairable (replace tubes). Modern TVs are disposable.
   - **Content access model:** 1955 TV content was free (antenna). 2025 content requires subscriptions. The TV purchase price tells only part of the story.
   - **Lifespan:** 1955 TVs lasted 10-20 years (with maintenance). Modern TVs last 5-10 years.

4. **The total-cost-of-viewing question:** Angle-02 flagged that the relevant comparison might be "what does it cost to watch TV in your living room" rather than "what does a TV cost." In 1955: buy TV + free antenna content. In 2025: buy TV + $100-200/month in subscriptions and internet. This is an important annotation, not the primary metric. Collect data on it.

## Data source hierarchy

1. Sears/Montgomery Ward catalogs — TV models, specs, and prices from 1950s onward
2. Consumer electronics industry history (Consumer Electronics Association)
3. Academic studies on television technology evolution
4. BLS CPI "television" component

## Output format

Follow the **Type 2: Quality rubric** format from plan.md. Overall assessment should almost certainly be "Progress wins."

## What "good data" looks like

- Clear documentation that the modern product is better on most dimensions
- The 1-2 dimensions where 1955 wins (repairability, content access model) are noted
- Quality-equivalent defined as a specific modern product (probably any mid-range 55"+ TV at $300-500)
- Note that the quality-equivalent framing doesn't cleanly apply — progress genuinely won here
