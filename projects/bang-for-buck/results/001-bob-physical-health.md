# Result: Bob's Physical Health Baseline

## Source
- **Primary sources:** CDC BRFSS, NHANES, NCHS Data Briefs, MMWR reports, CDC PCD
- **Date accessed:** 2026-05-01

## Findings

Bob is a statistical composite representing the 25th percentile of US household income (~$30,000/year, roughly ≤130% FPL). His physical health profile is substantially worse than the general population across nearly every metric — higher obesity, more chronic disease, less physical activity, worse diet quality, higher smoking rates, and less preventive care. The one surprising finding: fast food consumption is actually *lower* in his income bracket than among higher-income Americans, though overall diet quality is still worse due to reliance on cheap, calorie-dense foods at home.

**Notes:** The diet picture is nuanced. Low-income adults actually eat *less* fast food than high-income adults (31.7% vs 42.0% on a given day) — a counterintuitive finding. Lower-income households purchase more calorie-dense, nutrient-poor foods (cereals, pasta, potatoes, fatty meats) and fewer fresh vegetables, seafood, and whole grains.

## Bob's Physical Health Summary Profile

For intervention tickets, use this composite:

- **BMI:** Likely overweight or obese (~39% obesity prevalence in bracket)
- **Chronic conditions:** Elevated diabetes (~14–16%), hypertension (~47%), and cardiovascular risk (~31% with ≥2 CVD risk factors)
- **Physical activity:** ~87% not meeting full guidelines; about half as likely as higher-income peers to be sufficiently active
- **Diet:** Cooks at home frequently but with lower-quality ingredients; very low vegetable intake (6.8% meeting guidelines); less fast food than higher-income peers but worse overall diet quality
- **Smoking:** 20–25% current smokers (vs ~7–12% in higher brackets); widening disparity
- **Mobility:** Elevated limitations driven by obesity, arthritis, and sedentary lifestyle
- **Healthcare access:** Likely underinsured; skips preventive care due to cost; uses ER more for preventable conditions; 38.6% of uninsured delay/skip care

## Gaps and Caveats

- **Income bracket alignment:** Most federal data uses poverty-income ratio (PIR) rather than dollar amounts. ≤130% FPL ≈ ~$19,500 for an individual, ~$26,500 for a household of 2 in 2024. Bob at $30k household income falls near the ≤130% FPL boundary for a 2-person household or the 130–200% range for an individual. Statistics for both brackets are relevant.
- **Temporal spread:** Data spans 2007–2024 across different surveys. The most recent obesity and physical activity data is 2021–2023; diet data is 2013–2019; home cooking data is 2007–2008. Trends generally show worsening outcomes for low-income groups relative to higher-income groups over this period.
- **Severe obesity:** No income-stratified data on BMI ≥40 was found. The national rate is 9.7%; Bob's bracket likely runs higher but this is estimated, not measured.
- **Hypertension by income:** The exact prevalence for the lowest income bracket varies by source (47% in one analysis vs general statements of "significantly higher" elsewhere). The ~47% figure is the best available but comes with wide uncertainty.
- **Mobility limitations:** Income-specific data is weak. Associations are clear but precise prevalence for ≤130% FPL is not well quantified in available sources.
- **Insurance status:** Bob's insurance situation is highly variable by state. In Medicaid expansion states, he likely qualifies for Medicaid. In non-expansion states, he may fall in the coverage gap. The ~20–25% uninsured rate for his bracket is an estimate that blends expansion and non-expansion state populations.
- **Interaction effects:** Obesity, diabetes, hypertension, physical inactivity, and poor diet cluster together. The individual prevalence rates overstate the number of distinct conditions because they co-occur in the same individuals.
