# The Persuasion Gap

## Core insight

Political arguments are workshopped for in-group applause, not cross-group persuasion. Both sides do it. The arguments that go viral within your tribe are almost never the arguments that would actually convince the other tribe.

## Examples

**Left → Right (coal):**
- Argument the left makes: "Coal is destroying the planet, it's dirty." Resonates with people who care about the environment. Bounces off the right.
- Argument the left *could* make: "Coal is an absurdly inefficient way to generate electricity. You're powering a car with 30 two-stroke engines under the hood when you could just get a V8. We have way better options." The right likes efficiency and power. That lands.

**Right → Left (trans safety):**
- Arguments the right makes: whatever they say amongst themselves about guns. Doesn't land with the left at all.
- Argument the right *could* make: "Every trans person should take a concealed carry class and be armed at all times. Hate crimes against trans people would plummet overnight. Self-defense works." The left cares about protecting vulnerable groups — that framing lands.

## The pattern

Each side optimizes arguments for internal virality (likes, retweets, "hell yeah" from your people) rather than cross-aisle persuasion. The most in-group-viral argument may be the *least* effective at convincing the opposition.

## Open questions

- Where does the data come from? Polling? Surveys? Could use existing research on moral foundations theory (Jonathan Haidt's work maps moral priorities by political orientation — could inform which argument framings would land).
- Might be more of a sketch than a research graph — the concept is the insight, and made-up-but-directionally-correct data could communicate it.
- Could tie into Haidt's moral foundations: Care/Harm, Fairness, Loyalty, Authority, Sanctity. Left over-indexes on Care/Fairness, right on Loyalty/Authority/Sanctity. Arguments fail when they appeal to foundations the audience doesn't prioritize.
