# Mental Health — Therapist / Psychologist

## Summary

Therapy is the closest thing to a pure labor transaction: one skilled person, a quiet room, and an hour of their time. The fundamental worker ratio has been remarkably stable at 57-69% across five decades — among the highest of any service industry. Managed care in the 1990s compressed per-session reimbursement but failed to collapse the ratio because therapists adapted by going private-pay, increasing volume, or both. The emerging structural threat is online therapy platforms (BetterHelp, Talkspace), which insert a technology middleman into a historically middleman-free profession, pushing platform therapist ratios down to an estimated 30-45%.

## Methodology

### Worker: Clinical psychologist / licensed therapist

The "fundamental worker" is the licensed clinician conducting individual outpatient psychotherapy — a clinical psychologist (PhD/PsyD), licensed clinical social worker (LCSW), or licensed professional counselor (LPC). For longitudinal consistency, compensation data is anchored to clinical psychologists via APA salary surveys (1995, 2001, 2009, 2015) and BLS Occupational Employment Statistics (2024), with earlier decades estimated from Census professional earnings data and professional growth trends.

Key anchor points:
- **2001**: APA salary survey — median $72,000 for licensed doctoral clinical psychologists in direct service
- **2009**: APA salary survey — median $87,015 for the same population
- **2015**: APA/NSCG — median $85,000 for all psychologists (private practice median: $120,000)
- **2024**: BLS — median $95,830 for clinical and counseling psychologists

Earlier decades (1970-1990) are back-estimated using ~3-4% annual nominal growth from the 2001 anchor, cross-referenced with Census data on professional/technical worker earnings and the known 1984 session fee of $60 (which implies mid-$30K salaries for practitioners whose income derived primarily from session revenue).

### Customer cost: Per-session total cost (all payers)

"Customer cost" is the total amount spent per therapy session — insurance payment plus patient copay for insured visits, or the full listed fee for private-pay visits. This is blended across payer types, weighted by approximate insurance acceptance rates (~60-65% of therapists accept insurance in recent decades).

Key anchor points:
- **1984**: Psychotherapy Finances annual survey — median session fee $60
- **2007**: PMC study of MEPS/claims data — in-network adult psychotherapy $98, out-of-network $123
- **2017**: Same study — in-network $84 (declined 14%), out-of-network $149 (increased 21%); SimplePractice survey median $120
- **2019**: SimplePractice — average session rate $123
- **2023-24**: National average $135-143 (SimplePractice, PMC, Psychology Today directory data)

The in-network session cost *declined* from 2007 to 2017, while out-of-network costs rose sharply, creating a widening gap. The blended average continued to rise because the share of private-pay sessions grew.

### Customers per worker: Sessions per year

A full-time therapist conducts approximately 20-25 client sessions per week over 46-48 working weeks, yielding ~1,000-1,100 sessions per year. I use 1,050 as the central estimate for most decades. For 2020, I reduced to 1,000 to reflect pandemic disruption, widespread burnout, and the transition to telehealth.

### Why this proxy works

The ratio = Annual compensation / (Session cost x Sessions per year). This captures the therapist's share of total session revenue. The gap between 100% and the ratio represents overhead (office rent, liability insurance, billing software, continuing education), administrative time, employer/platform margins, and insurance company intermediation. For therapy, this gap is unusually small compared to most service industries because the physical overhead is genuinely minimal — a therapist needs a room, a chair, and a license.

## Notes

### Why the ratio is high and stable

Therapy is structurally a high-ratio profession. The physical overhead is negligible — a therapist's "equipment" is their training and their attention. Solo practitioners in the 1960s-1980s operated with overhead rates of 20-30% (office rent, liability insurance, phone, appointment book). Even today, a solo telehealth therapist can operate with overhead under $2,000/month. This means 60-75% of every dollar the customer pays can flow directly to the therapist.

### The managed care era (1990s-2000s)

Managed care organizations (MCOs) entered mental health aggressively in the early 1990s. Per-session in-network fees that had been set by individual practitioners were replaced with pre-negotiated rates, declining more than 40% from the late 1980s peak in real terms. But the ratio didn't collapse because therapists had three escape valves:

1. **Going private pay**: By the 2020s, 35% of private practice therapists accept no insurance at all. This trend accelerated through the managed care era.
2. **Increasing volume**: Some therapists compensated for lower per-session rates by seeing more patients.
3. **Credential arbitrage**: Insurance reimburses PhD psychologists 10-20% more than master's-level therapists for identical services, creating incentive to up-credential.

The net effect: in-network session costs fell, out-of-network costs rose, and therapist salaries grew moderately. The ratio absorbed the disruption without fundamentally changing shape.

### The two-track system (2010s-present)

By 2017, the ratio of out-of-network to in-network prices reached 1.76x for adult psychotherapy (up from 1.28x in 2007). This represents a profession splitting into two parallel economies:

- **Private-pay track**: Therapists charging $150-250/session, no insurance billing, minimal overhead, ratio ~70-80%.
- **Insurance track**: Therapists accepting $84-110/session allowed amounts, significant administrative burden, ratio ~55-65%.

The blended ratio masks this growing bifurcation.

### Online therapy platforms: The new middleman

BetterHelp and Talkspace represent the first structural intermediary in a profession that historically had none. These platforms charge clients $260-436/month (typically covering 4 sessions + messaging), while paying therapists:

- **BetterHelp**: $30-70/session equivalent (average ~$30/hour); annual earnings ~$64,000 FTE
- **Talkspace**: $24-68/session depending on format; reported average ~$21/hour for messaging sessions

If a BetterHelp client pays ~$85-100/session equivalent and the therapist receives $40-50 for that session, the platform-mediated ratio drops to roughly **30-45%** — a dramatic compression from the traditional 60-70%.

This is the first time in the history of talk therapy that a significant share of sessions flow through an entity that adds no clinical value but captures 30-60% of the session fee. As of 2024, these platforms serve millions of clients but still represent a minority of total therapy sessions.

### Post-2020 demand explosion

The COVID-19 pandemic triggered a surge in mental health demand. Therapy sessions conducted via telehealth jumped from ~1% to over 40% of all sessions. This created an interesting dynamic: demand soared but therapist supply was constrained (licensure takes years). You'd expect this to increase therapist leverage — and it did for private-pay therapists, whose fees have risen ~13% since 2019. But insurance reimbursement rates remained stagnant (many insurers haven't increased psychotherapy rates in 10-20 years), so in-network therapists saw their ratio erode further against rising costs.

### Master's-level vs. doctoral-level divergence

Insurance reimburses LMFTs and LMHCs at 75% of the psychologist rate for identical CPT codes (e.g., ~$116 vs. $154 for a 60-minute session on Medicare). Master's-level therapists now outnumber doctoral psychologists in outpatient practice by a wide margin. Their median salary (~$50-60K in 2024) produces a lower individual ratio, but the session cost to the customer is also lower, keeping the ratio in a similar range. The divergence that matters is economic: master's-level therapists do the same clinical work for less pay, which benefits the system's efficiency but compresses the individual worker's share.
