# Taxi Drivers / Medallion Holders

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Taxi drivers and medallion owners (traditional for-hire vehicle operators)
- **Country/Region:** United States (concentrated in NYC, with national parallels in Chicago, SF, and other major cities)
- **Peak Year:** 2013 (medallion values); ~2012–2013 (traditional taxi ridership and employment)
- **Peak Workforce:** ~233,000 taxi drivers and chauffeurs nationally (BLS OOH, 2012–2014 estimates); ~51,400 licensed NYC medallion taxi drivers (NYC TLC, March 2014)
- **Primary Mechanism:** tipping_point
- **Cause Tags:** tech_platform, regulatory, consolidation
- **Killing Event:** Uber (launched 2010, UberX 2012) and Lyft (2012) bypassed the government-created medallion monopoly with app-based ride-hailing, collapsing medallion values from $1.3M to under $200K in five years and destroying the economic model of traditional taxi ownership.

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 2013 (medallion values began eroding; ridership peaked 2012–2013)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** ~2 years (NYC yellow taxi daily trips fell ~36% by early 2016)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** ~5 years (NYC fares down 44% by March 2019; NYC active medallion drivers roughly halved by ~2018–2019)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** ~10 years (NYC active drivers fell from ~51,400 in 2014 to ~11,662 in 2023, a ~77% decline; Chicago taxi trips fell 82% by 2023)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** N/A — the profession has not reached 90% decline nationally because many drivers transitioned to rideshare within the same occupational category
- **Decline Shape:** s_curve (slow erosion 2012–2014, accelerating collapse 2014–2019, partial stabilization post-2020)
- **Current Status:** contracted (10-50%) — traditional taxi driving is a fraction of its former self, but total for-hire driving actually expanded massively through Uber/Lyft. The profession was not eliminated but was fundamentally reconfigured.

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: measured

This event has two distinct income stories: (1) driver wages, which declined modestly, and (2) medallion asset values, which collapsed catastrophically.

**Driver wages (national, BLS OES medians for SOC 53-3041/53-3054):**

- **Income at Peak:** ~$24,300/year median (2013) (BLS OES, SOC 53-3041)
- **Income at Trough:** Uber drivers earned the equivalent of ~$9.21/hr (~$19,200/year FTE) after all expenses (Mishel/EPI, 2018)
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** Partially recovered — BLS median for taxi drivers (53-3054, which now includes rideshare) was $33,530 by 2023, but this category now mixes traditional taxi and rideshare drivers, and may reflect post-pandemic fare increases rather than real recovery for traditional drivers.

## Where They Went

This is an unusual displacement — the profession was reconfigured, not eliminated. Total for-hire drivers in the US actually *increased* dramatically.

| Destination | Est. % of Displaced | Basis | Notes |
|-------------|-------------------|-------|-------|
| Uber/Lyft/rideshare driver | ~50–60% | inferred | Many taxi drivers switched platforms; same work, worse economics, no asset value. NYC TLC saw rideshare driver counts surge 6x from 2015–2019 |
| Delivery services (DoorDash, UberEats, etc.) | ~10–15% | estimated | Gig economy expansion absorbed some; accelerated during COVID-19 |
| Exited driving entirely (retail, other service jobs) | ~15–20% | estimated | Especially older drivers and those without immigration flexibility |
| Bankruptcy / financial devastation | ~5–10% | tracked | ~1,000 NYC medallion owner-drivers filed for bankruptcy (Columbia HRL Review). At least 8 NYC taxi drivers died by suicide 2017–2018 (NYTWA) |
| Retired / left workforce | ~5–10% | estimated | Average taxi driver age was 47+; many older drivers exited permanently |

## Surplus Capture

- **Capital owners:** 60% — Uber and Lyft captured the lion's share through platform fees (Uber takes ~$8.33/hr in commissions and fees per Mishel/EPI), though Uber itself was unprofitable for years. Investors absorbed short-term losses betting on long-term market dominance. Tech investors replaced medallion-owning fleet operators as the capital class.
- **Consumers:** 30% — Dramatically improved service: app-based hailing, GPS tracking, transparent pricing, wider geographic coverage. Fares were initially subsidized by VC money (below cost). Consumer surplus was enormous but partially eroded as Uber/Lyft raised prices post-dominance.
- **Adjacent workers:** 5% — Some gains to new rideshare drivers who entered the market (particularly part-time/flexible workers), but at lower per-hour earnings than legacy taxi drivers. App developers, vehicle rental companies, and phone/data service providers benefited.
- **Government/public:** 5% — NYC gained a new per-ride tax ($2.75 surcharge on for-hire rides post-2019) and congestion pricing revenue. But governments lost medallion auction revenue and faced cleanup costs: $750M+ in credit union losses hit the NCUA Share Insurance Fund (federal).

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
- **Curb Mobility (formerly Taxi Magic/VeriFone):** Acquired and relaunched as Curb in 2016, built an Uber-like app for traditional taxis. Grew to 100,000+ drivers by partnering with taxi fleets nationwide. Active users grew 300% in one year. Eventually partnered with Uber itself to allow taxi pickups through the Uber app — the ultimate validation of the adaptation strategy.
- **Early medallion sellers (unnamed):** A small number of individual medallion owners sold at or near the $1M–$1.3M peak in 2012–2013. No prominent named example, but anyone who sold before mid-2014 avoided catastrophic losses. The window was narrow — about 18 months.

### False Movers
- **Individual medallion buyers (2012–2013):** Approximately 4,000 drivers purchased medallions near the peak, many taking out $800K–$1.3M loans. Banks and brokers targeted immigrant drivers with limited English, pushing predatory loan terms. These buyers lost everything — many owed $500K+ on an asset worth $100K–$200K. This was the single largest cohort of false movers in the dataset.
- **Gene Freidman ("The Taxi King"):** Amassed ~900 NYC medallions, making him the largest fleet operator. His companies filed Chapter 11 in 2016. He was indicted for tax fraud in 2017 (stealing $5M in MTA surcharges). Lost his empire, died in 2021 at age 50. His strategy of artificially bidding up medallion auction prices to inflate collateral values was the quintessential bad bet.

### Deniers
- **NYC Taxi and Limousine Commission:** The TLC continued to auction new medallions at inflated prices as late as 2013–2014, implicitly signaling that values would hold. The regulator was asleep at the wheel while the asset it controlled collapsed.
- **Taxi industry lobbying groups** fought to ban or regulate Uber/Lyft through legislation rather than innovating or adapting. The strategy of "restrict the new entrant" failed everywhere it was tried. The fundamental problem — consumers preferred app-based hailing — was not a regulatory problem.
- **Lenders (Melrose Credit Union, others):** Melrose Credit Union became the largest medallion lender, concentrating heavily in taxi medallion loans. They continued lending at inflated values into 2014–2015. Melrose was ultimately liquidated, costing the NCUA Share Insurance Fund over $740 million.

### Survival Pattern
Survivors were either not leveraged on medallion debt or willing to adopt the new platform — ideological commitment to "the taxi way" was the strongest predictor of failure.

## Unique Texture

1. **The suicide crisis was a leading indicator.** Between November 2017 and November 2018, at least 8 NYC professional drivers died by suicide, linked to medallion debt and income collapse. Douglas Schifter, a 61-year-old livery driver, shot himself in front of City Hall on February 5, 2018, after posting a 1,700-word note on Facebook describing how he worked 100+ hours per week and still faced financial ruin. The suicides triggered a political crisis that eventually led to debt relief. (NYTWA; NPR; Gothamist)

2. **The medallion crash was one of the fastest government-backed asset collapses in modern US history.** NYC individual medallions went from $1.3M (2013) to $137K at auction (2019) — a 90% decline in six years. For comparison, US housing prices fell ~33% peak-to-trough in the 2008 crisis over about five years. The medallion collapse was nearly 3x as deep and just as fast. (NCUA; NYC TLC)

3. **The government created the monopoly, then let a new entrant bypass it.** NYC capped medallions at 13,605 — a number set in 1937 — creating artificial scarcity that drove prices to $1.3M. Then the same government allowed Uber/Lyft to operate without medallions, effectively destroying the asset while continuing to collect taxes on it. The NCUA later described the crisis as a case study in "government malfeasance." (R Street Institute; NCUA)

4. **Predatory lending turned the medallion bubble into a humanitarian crisis.** Banks and credit unions deliberately targeted immigrant drivers with limited English, pushing $1M+ loans on assets that were already overvalued. When medallion values collapsed, ~1,000 owner-drivers filed for bankruptcy. The lending practices were later compared to subprime mortgage origination. Six credit union failures cost the federal Share Insurance Fund approximately $750 million. (Columbia Human Rights Law Review; Fideres; NCUA)

5. **The profession didn't die — it was precaritized.** Total for-hire drivers in NYC actually *increased* roughly 6x from 2015 to 2019 as Uber/Lyft flooded the market. But average driver earnings collapsed: the Mishel/EPI study found Uber drivers earned the equivalent of $9.21/hr after expenses and self-employment taxes (2018). The NYC pay floor ($17.22/hr, effective February 2019) was the first minimum wage for gig workers in the country — a direct response to the income crisis. (Mishel/EPI 2018; Parrott & Reich 2018; NYC TLC)

## Known Data Gaps

- **National taxi-only employment post-2018 is unmeasurable.** The BLS SOC code change in 2018 merged traditional taxi drivers with ride-hailing drivers (53-3054), making it impossible to track traditional taxi employment separately at the national level. This is the single biggest data gap in this event.
- **BLS OES excludes self-employed workers.** A substantial share of taxi drivers (especially medallion owner-operators) are self-employed and invisible to OES surveys. CPS data includes them but with smaller sample sizes and different methodology.
- **Pre-2012 BLS OES data for 53-3041** was not retrievable via web search. The BLS website blocked direct access. Historical employment figures for 2006–2011 are estimated from secondary sources.
- **Individual-level tracking of where displaced taxi drivers went** does not exist. The "where they went" estimates are inferred from industry-level data (NYC TLC rideshare driver counts, bankruptcy filings) rather than panel data tracking individual drivers.
- **Medallion price data outside NYC** is sparse. Chicago, Boston, and Philadelphia had medallion systems, but NYC dominates the data record because of its scale and the NCUA's involvement after credit union failures.
- **Uber/Lyft driver income data is contested.** Uber's self-reported figures ($55K/yr) differ dramatically from independent studies ($9.21/hr after expenses). The truth depends heavily on methodology (gross vs. net, per-trip vs. per-hour logged, expense assumptions). Both sides have incentives to distort.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium-high
- **Workforce numbers:** medium — National BLS data is robust but the SOC code change in 2018 breaks the time series. NYC TLC data is excellent for the local market but not nationally representative.
- **Income data:** medium — Driver wages are well-documented by BLS, but the more important story (medallion asset collapse) is tracked through auction/transfer records rather than standard income surveys. Rideshare driver income is hotly contested.
- **Where-they-went:** medium — Strong inference from NYC TLC data on rideshare driver growth, but no panel data tracking individual taxi drivers. Bankruptcy filings and suicide data are well-documented. The overall narrative (most switched to rideshare, some went bankrupt) is well-supported even without individual tracking.
