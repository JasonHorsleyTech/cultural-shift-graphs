# News Media — Journalist / Reporter

## Summary

Journalists have consistently captured only 3-5% of what customers (subscribers + advertisers) pay into the newspaper system. The advertising bubble of the 1990s-2000s inflated revenue per journalist past $1 million while salaries barely moved, compressing the ratio to under 3%. When the bubble burst, the ratio "improved" — not because journalists got paid more, but because revenue collapsed faster than headcount. The fundamental story: the advertising-funded newspaper model was never really about paying for journalism.

## Methodology

**Ratio formula:** Journalist median salary / (Annual revenue per reader × Readers per journalist)

This simplifies to: Journalist salary / (Total industry revenue / Number of newsroom journalists), which represents the journalist's share of total industry revenue per journalist employed.

**Worker compensation:** Newspaper reporter/correspondent median annual salary. Pre-2000 estimates are derived from the American Journalist survey series (Weaver/Wilhoit, Indiana University — studies in 1971, 1982, 1992, 2002, 2013), the late-1950s BLS figure of $122/week for staff reporters, and general wage growth trends. Post-2000 figures come from BLS Occupational Employment Statistics for SOC 27-3022 (Reporters and Correspondents), with 2020 adjusted downward from the combined 27-3023 category (News Analysts, Reporters, and Journalists — merged in 2018) to exclude higher-paid TV news analysts.

**Customer cost proxy:** Total U.S. newspaper industry revenue (advertising + circulation) divided by daily weekday circulation. This represents the effective revenue the system extracts per reader, including both what the reader pays (subscription) and what advertisers pay to reach that reader. This is the right proxy because journalism is funded by both streams — using subscription price alone would ignore that advertising historically funded 70-80% of newspaper operations.

**Revenue data:** Newspaper Association of America (NAA, now News Media Alliance) historical data via Pew Research Center. Advertising revenue: $3.7B (1960), $5.7B (1970), $14.8B (1980), $32.1B (1990), $48.7B (2000), $49.4B (2005 — ad revenue peak), $25.8B (2010), ~$18B (2015), $8.8B (2020). Circulation revenue: $1.6B (1960), $2.6B (1970), $5.5B (1980), $7.0B (1990), $10.7B (2000), ~$10.2B (2005), ~$10.0B (2010), ~$10.5B (2015), $11.5B (2020). Post-2012 advertising/circulation figures from Pew analysis of publicly traded newspaper company SEC filings.

**Daily weekday circulation:** Editor & Publisher data (through 2014), Pew/Alliance for Audited Media estimates (2015+). Rose from ~59M (1960) to peak ~63M (1984), then declined to ~24M by 2020 (print + digital combined). Key figures used: 58.9M (1960), 62.1M (1970), 62.2M (1980), 62.3M (1990), 55.8M (2000), 53.3M (2005), 43.4M (2010), 34.7M (2015), 24.3M (2020).

**Newsroom journalist count:** ASNE (American Society of News Editors) Newsroom Employment Census for daily newspapers. Confirmed data points: 43,000 (1978), 53,800 (1985), 56,900 (1990 — peak), 56,200 (2000), 54,100 (2005), 32,900 (2014). Estimated: ~32,000 (1960), ~38,000 (1970), ~45,000 (1980), ~41,000 (2010), ~33,000 (2015), ~24,000 (2020). The 2010 and 2020 estimates are based on Pew Research data showing 26% decline in total newsroom employment from 2008-2020, with newspaper sector hit hardest (57% decline in newspaper newsroom employees).

**Why readers per journalist stays stable (~1,000-1,800):** The number of readers per journalist narrowed from ~1,800 in 1960 to ~1,000 by 2000 as newsroom hiring expanded faster than circulation. After 2000, both circulation and employment declined at roughly similar rates, keeping the ratio around 1,000. The ratio's movement over time is driven almost entirely by revenue per reader, not by changes in journalists-per-reader.

## Notes

- **The advertising bubble distortion:** The most striking feature is how the advertising boom of the 1980s-2000s inflated "customer cost" from $90/reader to over $1,100/reader — a 12x increase — while journalist salaries only rose ~5x. The advertising revenue was paying for printing infrastructure, distribution networks, classified ad departments, and corporate profits. Very little of the advertising windfall trickled down to newsroom workers. When classified ads migrated to Craigslist (launched 1995, expanded nationally 2000-2004) and display advertising shifted to Google/Facebook, the revenue evaporated but the journalists' share barely changed.

- **Newsroom cuts vs. revenue collapse:** Newsroom employment was cut roughly in half from 2005 to 2020 (~54,000 to ~24,000). Total revenue fell by about two-thirds (~$60B to ~$20B). Because employment fell somewhat less than revenue, the ratio actually improved — each remaining journalist captures a slightly larger share. But this is cold comfort: fewer journalists means less coverage, especially of local news.

- **The hedge fund factor:** Starting around 2010, hedge funds (notably Alden Global Capital) began acquiring newspaper chains specifically to extract value. The playbook: buy a paper, cut staff to the bone, extract profit until the paper dies. This accelerated newsroom employment decline beyond what revenue declines alone would have caused. In hedge-fund-owned papers, the "fundamental worker ratio" would actually look better (fewer workers, same revenue) — which illustrates the limits of this metric as a measure of worker welfare.

- **Local vs. national divergence:** National outlets (NYT, Washington Post, WSJ) have successfully transitioned to digital subscriptions, with the NYT alone surpassing 10 million subscribers. These outlets can pay journalists significantly above the median. Local newspapers, which make up the vast majority by number, have been gutted. The aggregate data obscures this bifurcation.

- **BLS category change in 2018:** The SOC code for reporters changed from 27-3022 (Reporters and Correspondents) to 27-3023 (News Analysts, Reporters, and Journalists), merging in TV news analysts who earn significantly more. The 2020 salary estimate of $43,000 is adjusted downward from the 27-3023 median (~$49,000) to better represent print/digital reporters specifically. This introduces some uncertainty in the 2020 data point.

- **Pre-1978 employment data is estimated.** The ASNE census began in 1978 with 43,000 journalists. The 1960 and 1970 estimates (32,000 and 38,000) are extrapolated backward based on the number of daily newspapers (~1,750 in 1960, ~1,750 in 1970) and estimated staffing levels. These estimates could be off by ±20%.

- **"Revenue per reader" overstates what readers actually pay.** In 2000, the calculated "revenue per reader" was $1,065/year. But actual annual newspaper subscriptions cost $150-$250/year. The difference is advertising revenue per reader (~$800-$900). The graph should note that this "customer cost" includes massive invisible subsidies from advertisers. Today's digital subscription model ($10-$15/month = $120-$180/year) is actually closer to what customers are willing to pay for journalism itself.
