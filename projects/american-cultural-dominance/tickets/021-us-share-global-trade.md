# US Share of Global Trade and Exports

Track America's share of global trade over time — not just as an economic measure but as a vector for cultural influence. American products carry American branding, packaging, language, and lifestyle associations. When a country's imports shift toward US goods, American cultural markers follow.

**Metric:** US share of global merchandise exports and total trade (exports + imports), by decade (1800-present).

**Data proxy:** WTO historical trade statistics, UN Comtrade, USITC data, Maddison Project historical GDP/trade data, historical trade balance records.

**Interesting angles:**
- 19th century: US was a rising trade power but not dominant. British Empire controlled global trade. Track when the US overtook Britain.
- Post-WW2: US accounted for roughly half of global GDP and a massive share of trade. This was peak structural dominance.
- 1970s-80s: Japan and Germany's rise as export powers reduced US share. But US imports grew — American consumer demand shaped other countries' economies.
- China's WTO entry (2001): dramatically reshaped global trade. US share of exports declined but US remained the world's largest import market.
- Cultural dimension: American brands exported worldwide (Coca-Cola, Ford, IBM, Apple). Track US branded goods' share of global consumer spending if possible.
- Trade agreements as cultural vectors: NAFTA, various bilateral FTAs often included intellectual property and cultural services provisions.
