# Ticket 016: Thalidomide → Birth Defects

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific/clinical evidence that thalidomide causes birth defects (phocomelia) to its withdrawal from market and subsequent drug safety regulation. This is the FASTEST case in the dataset — the baseline for "how fast CAN society act?"

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Thalidomide was marketed in Germany from October 1957 (as Contergan). Reports of peripheral neuropathy appeared by late 1959. Widukind Lenz presented epidemiological evidence linking thalidomide to birth defects at a Düsseldorf pediatric conference in November 1961. William McBride published a letter in The Lancet in December 1961. When is the earliest credible clinical evidence of teratogenicity?

2. **Consensus date** — Was there a formal scientific body statement, or did the withdrawal effectively function as consensus? The speed was such that withdrawal preceded formal scientific review.

3. **First regulation** — Grünenthal (the manufacturer) withdrew thalidomide from the German market on November 27, 1961, shortly after Lenz's presentation. Other countries followed within days to weeks. The US had never approved it (Frances Kelsey at FDA held up the application). Confirm the withdrawal date and timeline across countries.

4. **US regulation** — Thalidomide was never marketed in the US. Frances Kelsey's refusal to approve the NDA is the US story. The Kefauver-Harris Amendment (1962) was a direct legislative response, requiring proof of drug efficacy and safety before marketing. This is the regulatory action.

5. **Why this was fast** — Visible, horrifying harm (deformed newborns). Single product with a single manufacturer. No industry coalition defending it. Sympathetic victims (babies). Direct, visible causal link.

6. **Later regulatory legacy** — Thalidomide is now approved (with extreme restrictions) for leprosy and multiple myeloma. The regulatory framework for teratogenic drugs (REMS, Thalomid REMS) is itself an outcome.

## Where to look

1. Lenz (1961) — original conference presentation and subsequent publications
2. McBride (1961) — Lancet letter
3. FDA history — Frances Kelsey and the thalidomide NDA
4. Kefauver-Harris Drug Amendments of 1962
5. Stephens & Brynner, "Dark Remedy" — comprehensive thalidomide history
6. PubMed — "thalidomide history teratogenicity"

## What "good data" looks like

- Precise dates (month-level, not just year) for the 1961 evidence and withdrawal — this case is measured in weeks, not years
- The US story (never approved, Kelsey's refusal, Kefauver-Harris Act) as the US regulatory response
- Context on why this was so fast — the factors that made rapid action possible are as important as the timeline itself

## Known pitfalls

- Thalidomide was never approved in the US, so the "US regulation date" is the Kefauver-Harris Amendment (1962), which changed the entire drug approval process. This is SYSTEMIC regulation, not thalidomide-specific withdrawal.
- The "early evidence" could be peripheral neuropathy reports (1959) or teratogenicity evidence (November 1961). Use the teratogenicity evidence since that's the specific harm in our metric.
- Category: product-withdrawal. The fastest category by nature.
- industry_opposition: none. This is the key contrast with long-lag hazards.
