# Result: Post-Collapse Agricultural Recovery Timeline

## Summary

After a sudden collapse of modern agriculture, food production recovery follows a predictable but painfully slow curve. Year 1 production is negligible (1-3% of current output) due to seed access delays, seasonal timing, and the learning curve of untrained farmers. The best modern analogue — Cuba's Special Period — saw caloric intake drop 27-40% despite retaining farming expertise, tropical growing conditions, and external aid. The Victory Gardens precedent shows organized civilian gardening can scale to 40% of vegetable production within 2-3 years, but that assumed intact infrastructure, seed supply chains, and supplemental commercial agriculture. Full recovery to pre-industrial levels (~20-25% of current US output) likely takes 10-20 years.

## Detailed Analysis

### Year 0-1: The Starvation Gap

**Stored food:** The US has minimal strategic grain reserves (~4M metric tons, enough for roughly 2-3 weeks of national consumption). However, grain elevators, warehouses, and retail distribution hold substantially more — perhaps 3-6 months of total supply if rationed. ALLFED estimates global food stocks at ~6 months.

**Seed availability:** Seeds exist in multiple forms: commercial seed company inventories (hundreds of millions of seed packets), grain elevators (grain is seed), unharvested fields (seasonal dependent), and home garden seed stocks. The bottleneck is not seed existence but distribution and knowledge. Most modern grain varieties require specific inputs, but potatoes, beans, and squash are relatively forgiving.

**What can be grown immediately (if collapse is in spring):**
- Fast-growing crops: radishes (25 days), lettuce (45 days), beans (60 days), potatoes (90 days)
- Per-acre caloric output with hand labor: ~1.5-3M calories/year for biointensive methods (Jeavons), but this assumes skilled practitioners. Novice yield is likely 30-50% of this.
- A single untrained person working 0.25-0.5 acres by hand might produce 375,000-750,000 calories in a good first year — roughly 1,000-2,000 cal/day for 1 person, at best.

**Seasonal timing matters enormously:** A collapse in April (planting season) allows immediate action. A collapse in October means 5-6 months before planting in most of the US, during which stored food is the only option. Winter collapse roughly doubles the starvation gap.

**Wild foraging:** Acorns provide ~110-140 cal/oz but require labor-intensive processing (leaching tannins). Wild plants, mushrooms, and nuts can supplement but not sustain populations. Realistic contribution: 50-200 cal/person/day for those with foraging knowledge, which is a tiny fraction of the population.

**Year 1 estimate: 1-3% of current production.** This accounts for scattered successful gardens, scavenged grain, wild foraging, and remaining livestock (which will be slaughtered rapidly). Most calories consumed in year 1 are stored food, not new production.

### Year 1-3: First Real Harvests

**Victory Gardens precedent (most optimistic analogue):**
- 1942 (year 1): 15M gardens planted, program just starting
- 1943 (year 2): 20M+ gardens, produced 10 billion pounds of food
- 1944 (year 3): ~40% of all US vegetables, 9-10M short tons
- Key caveat: Victory Gardens supplemented commercial agriculture. Gardeners had seed supply chains, fertilizer, tools, water, and agricultural extension advice. In a true collapse, none of these exist reliably.

**Cuban Special Period (most realistic analogue):**
- Cuba lost ~80% of petrochemical imports in 1991. Agricultural production fell 47-54% by 1994.
- Caloric intake dropped from 3,052 to as low as 1,863 cal/day (39% decline) by 1993.
- Recovery: By 1995-97, average reached 2,440 cal/day. Full recovery to ~3,280 cal/day took until 2003 — 12 years.
- Key caveat: Cuba retained farming expertise, tropical climate (year-round growing), and received external aid (food donations, Australian permaculture trainers). A post-collapse US has none of these advantages.

**Khmer Rouge (worst-case analogue):**
- Cambodia 1975-79: Forced agricultural collectivization by urban evacuees with no farming knowledge.
- Set impossible quotas (3 tons/hectare vs. historical 1 ton/hectare).
- Destroyed existing irrigation, killed draft animals, ignored expertise.
- Result: 25-33% of population died from starvation, disease, and execution in 4 years.
- Lesson: Untrained populations forced into agriculture with no knowledge infrastructure fail catastrophically.

**Learning curve:** The gap between "knows gardening exists" and "can subsistence farm" is 2-3 growing seasons minimum. First-year novice farmers routinely lose 50-80% of crops to timing errors, pest ignorance, soil mismanagement, and improper storage. By year 3, survivors who have been practicing have rudimentary competence.

**Year 2 estimate: 5-12% of current production.** First real harvests from scattered successful gardens and farms. Massive regional variation — agricultural regions with surviving farmers do much better than cities.

**Year 3 estimate: 10-20% of current production.** Learning curve starts paying off. Seed saving from year 1-2 crops provides better-adapted stock. Community-scale agriculture begins to organize.

### Year 3-10: Scaling Up

**Draft animal recovery:**
- Current US: ~7.2M horses (GAO estimates up to 9.2M equines total), but only ~424K are draft breeds.
- Horse gestation: 11 months, one foal per mare per year. Doubling draft stock from 424K takes ~4-5 years even with aggressive breeding.
- For comparison: In 1920, the US had 26M horses and mules supporting 106M people. Reaching even 10M working animals from the current base takes 8-12 years.
- Oxen can be trained from existing cattle (94M head in US), but training takes 1-2 years per animal. This is the faster path to animal-powered agriculture.

**Tool production:**
- Scrap metal is abundant (cars, buildings, appliances). Basic forge work to produce hoes, plows, and scythes from scrap is feasible with knowledge from surviving books and a small number of skilled metalworkers.
- Timeline: Year 2-3 for crude hand tools, year 5-7 for animal-drawn plows at meaningful scale.

**Irrigation without electric pumps:**
- Windmill-driven pumps, gravity-fed aqueducts, hand pumps, and hydraulic ram pumps are all buildable from salvaged materials.
- Historical precedent: These were the standard until rural electrification in the 1930s-40s.
- Timeline: Year 2-5 for individual improvised systems, year 5-10 for community-scale water management.

**Year 5 estimate: 18-35% of current production.** Organized farming communities with 3-4 years of experience, first generation of trained oxen, crude but functional tools, and expanding cultivated acreage. Still entirely dependent on rainfall or improvised irrigation in most areas.

**Year 10 estimate: 25-55% of current production.** Second generation of crops with locally adapted seeds. Draft animal population growing. Soil fertility improving through organic methods (cover cropping, composting). Regional trade networks beginning to form.

### Year 10-20: Approaching Pre-Industrial Levels

**Historical benchmark:** The US in 1900 had 76M people, 21.5M horses/mules, and pre-modern crop yields (30% of 2014 wheat yields, 18% of 2014 corn yields). This represents roughly 20-25% of current total US food output — but it fed the population that existed then.

**Soil recovery:** Modern monoculture has depleted soil organic matter across most US farmland. Organic methods (cover crops, rotation, composting) can rebuild basic fertility in 3-5 years, though full soil ecosystem recovery takes decades. The abandoned use of synthetic nitrogen is partially offset by nitrogen-fixing cover crops (clover, legumes).

**Knowledge preservation:** Agricultural knowledge survives in university libraries, county extension office publications, and surviving practitioners. The US has ~2M current farms, and farming communities in the Midwest, South, and Great Plains retain substantial practical knowledge. These regions become the anchors of food production.

**When does food production reach 1900 levels?** Likely 15-25 years post-collapse, assuming:
- Surviving population has declined significantly (making per-capita sufficiency easier to reach)
- Draft animal population has grown to 5-10M
- Agricultural knowledge has been transmitted through 3-5 growing seasons of practice
- Basic tool manufacturing from salvage is well-established
- Irrigation infrastructure rebuilt for critical areas

**Year 20 estimate: 35-70% of current production.** Wide range reflects uncertainty about surviving population, climate conditions, and whether organized governance facilitates agricultural coordination. The lower bound assumes ongoing conflict and resource competition; the upper bound assumes cooperative community structures emerge.

### Critical Variable: Surviving Population

The per-capita food picture improves dramatically as population drops. If a catastrophe kills 50-70% of the population in the first year (nuclear war, pandemic), the survivors face less competition for remaining resources:
- At 50% population (168M), year 5 food production of 25% current output = ~50% of needs covered by new production
- At 80% population loss (67M), year 5 food production of 25% current output = 100%+ of needs covered

This is the cruel math of post-collapse agriculture: recovery doesn't have to reach pre-collapse levels if the population has fallen far enough.

### ALLFED Alternative Foods (Industrial Contingency)

If any industrial capacity survives, ALLFED research identifies scaling alternatives:
- Methane single-cell protein: 7-11% of global protein needs by end of year 1 (with 24/7 construction)
- Seaweed farming: Scales faster than industrial protein in coastal areas
- Lignocellulosic sugar (from wood/paper): Convertible from existing paper mill infrastructure
- With trade and resilient foods combined: ~82% of global population could be fed even in nuclear winter

These are relevant only if some industrial infrastructure and energy supply survives — they do not apply to a total grid/infrastructure collapse.

## Data Gaps

- **Novice farmer failure rates:** No systematic study of first-year crop yields for completely untrained growers. Anecdotal evidence from homesteading literature suggests 50-80% crop loss in year 1, but this is not rigorous.
- **Seed viability at scale:** How much commercially stored seed is viable after 1+ years of uncontrolled storage? Seed companies store under controlled conditions; post-collapse warehouse seed degrades faster.
- **Regional variation:** Agricultural recovery in Iowa looks nothing like recovery in Arizona. No model accounts for the massive geographic variation in soil quality, rainfall, growing season, and surviving agricultural expertise.
- **Social organization:** All estimates assume people cooperate to farm. If social breakdown leads to resource conflict (the Khmer Rouge problem), agricultural recovery stalls or reverses. This is the largest single uncertainty.
- **Livestock slaughter rate:** The US has ~94M cattle, 74M pigs, 500M+ chickens. These represent enormous caloric reserves if slaughtered, but also the breeding stock for future animal agriculture. The rate at which livestock is consumed vs. preserved for breeding dramatically affects year 3-10 trajectories.
- **Soil condition post-industrial agriculture:** US farmland has been monoculture-farmed with synthetic inputs for decades. Actual fertility of this soil under sudden organic-only management is poorly documented.
