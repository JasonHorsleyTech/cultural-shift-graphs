# Ticket 005: Video Rental Workers (Blockbuster)

## Event
The collapse of the video rental industry as DVD-by-mail and then streaming displaced physical rental stores, ~2004–2014. A platform shift with a dramatic tipping point.

## Mechanism
**Tipping point** (tech_platform)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track video rental industry employment, not just Blockbuster:

- Blockbuster peak: ~84,300 employees, ~60,000 US (2004). 9,094 stores worldwide.
- Hollywood Video: ~28,000 employees at peak
- Total US video rental employment (NAICS 532282 — Video tape and disc rental)
- Key dates: Netflix DVD-by-mail (1997), Netflix streaming (2007), Redbox expansion (2004–2009), Blockbuster bankruptcy (Sept 2010), last Blockbuster closes (except Bend, OR — 2019)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages (QCEW), NAICS 532282
2. Blockbuster SEC filings (10-K annual reports) — employee counts, store counts, revenue
3. IBIS World industry reports for video rental
4. Netflix annual reports for competitive context

### Income Data
- Blockbuster median hourly wage (~$7–8/hour for store associates, ~2005)
- BLS wage data for NAICS 532282
- These were predominantly minimum-wage or near-minimum-wage jobs — the income story is different from Kodak or travel agents
- Track wages from 2000–2015

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS OES data for relevant SOC codes (rental/counter workers)
2. Blockbuster SEC filings
3. Glassdoor/PayScale historical data for Blockbuster (verify reliability)

### Where They Went
These were largely young, part-time retail workers — the displacement pattern differs from career professionals:
- Other retail (Best Buy, Walmart, Target)
- Food service
- General customer service
- The question: was this a "career death" or just a shift in entry-level retail jobs?

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS retail trade employment data
2. CPS data for young workers in service sector
3. Academic studies on retail sector churn

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Netflix (Reed Hastings pivoted from DVD to streaming). Redbox (automated kiosks — a partial pivot that also eventually died). Any store managers who transitioned to related businesses.
- **False movers:** Blockbuster's own online/by-mail service (Total Access, 2006) — too late, too expensive. Blockbuster's negotiations with Netflix in 2000 (turned down acquisition for $50M).
- **Deniers:** Blockbuster CEO Jim Keyes: "Neither Redbox nor Netflix are even on the radar screen in terms of competition" (2008). Other executive quotes.
- **Survival pattern:** Platform ownership (Netflix) vs. physical distribution (everyone else).

### Unique Texture
- Blockbuster turned down buying Netflix for $50 million in 2000. Netflix was worth $150B+ at peak.
- The Bend, Oregon Blockbuster — last one standing, became a tourist attraction
- Late fees: Blockbuster made ~$800M/year in late fees (~16% of revenue). Netflix's "no late fees" was the wedge.
- The speed: 9,000+ stores → 1 in ~10 years
- Redbox as a "false middle" — automated kiosks seemed like the future but streaming killed them too
