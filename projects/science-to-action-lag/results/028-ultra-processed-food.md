## Sources

1. Monteiro, C.A. (2009). "A new classification of foods based on the extent and purpose of their processing." Public Health Nutrition. — Introduced the NOVA classification system categorizing foods by degree of industrial processing.
2. Monteiro, C.A. et al. (2019). "Ultra-processed foods: what they are and how to identify them." Public Health Nutrition, 20(5), 936–941. — Formalized the NOVA four-group classification and defined ultra-processed foods.
3. Fiolet, T. et al. (2018). "Consumption of ultra-processed foods and cancer risk: results from NutriNet-Santé prospective cohort." BMJ, 360, k322. — First large prospective cohort study (n=104,980) showing 10% increase in UPF proportion associated with 12% higher overall cancer risk.
4. Srour, B. et al. (2019). "Ultra-processed food intake and risk of cardiovascular disease: prospective cohort study (NutriNet-Santé)." BMJ, 365, l1451. — Prospective cohort evidence linking UPF to cardiovascular disease.
5. Lane, M.M. et al. (2024). "Ultra-processed food exposure and adverse health outcomes: umbrella review of epidemiological meta-analyses." BMJ, 384, e077310. — Umbrella review of 45 meta-analyses (~10 million participants) finding convincing evidence (Class I) for CVD mortality, type 2 diabetes, anxiety, and common mental disorders.
6. The Lancet (2025). "Ultra-processed Foods and Human Health" — Three-paper series (November 2025) by 43 international experts reviewing evidence and calling for coordinated global policy action.
7. Ministry of Health of Brazil (2014). "Dietary Guidelines for the Brazilian Population." — First national dietary guidelines to incorporate NOVA classification, recommending avoidance of ultra-processed foods. Advisory, not binding.
8. Chile, Ley 20.606 (2012, implemented 2016). Food Labelling and Advertising Law. — Binding front-of-package warning labels, marketing restrictions to children, and school sale bans for foods exceeding nutrient thresholds. Targets nutrient profiles, not UPF category directly.
9. Colombia, Law 2277 of 2022 (tax reform). — Introduced tax on ultra-processed sugary drinks effective November 1, 2023; also mandated front-of-package octagonal warning labels (Resolution 810 of 2021, updated 2023).
10. WHO (2024). "Call for experts to develop a WHO guideline on consumption of ultra-processed foods." — WHO convened a Guideline Development Group; guideline still in development as of 2026.
11. CNN (2025). "Scientists criticize food manufacturers for massive profits from sales of unhealthy ultraprocessed food." — Reports UPF industry spent $1.15 billion on US lobbying between 1995–2020; industry-funded studies 5x more likely to show no UPF-obesity association.
12. Mexico front-of-package labeling law (2019/2020). — Octagonal warning labels on products high in sugar, fat, sodium, and calories. Uses nutrient thresholds, not UPF classification.

## Judgment calls

**Early evidence year (2018 vs. 2009):** Monteiro's 2009 NOVA classification proposed the conceptual framework and argued UPF was harmful, but it was a theoretical classification paper, not epidemiological evidence of a specific health link. Earlier ecological and cross-sectional studies (e.g., Canella et al. 2014 on household UPF availability and obesity in Brazil; Mendonça et al. 2016 prospective study in the SUN cohort linking UPF to overweight) provided suggestive evidence. The 2018 NutriNet-Santé cancer study (Fiolet et al., BMJ) is chosen as early_evidence_year because it was the first large-scale prospective cohort study in a major journal demonstrating a specific disease outcome (cancer) with dose-response data. This is consistent with the plan's definition requiring evidence that "established the link" rather than proposed a framework.

**Consensus year (null):** This is a judgment call. The 2024 BMJ umbrella review found "convincing evidence" for multiple outcomes, and the 2025 Lancet series represents a major scientific milestone. However, no formal body — WHO, a national academy of sciences, or equivalent — has issued a definitive consensus statement. The WHO Guideline Development Group for UPF was convened but has not published final guidelines. Setting consensus_year to null reflects the absence of a formal institutional statement, while acknowledging that scientific consensus is rapidly coalescing. If the WHO publishes its UPF guideline, that would become the consensus_year.

**First regulation (Colombia 2023 vs. Chile 2016):** Chile's Ley 20.606 (implemented 2016) is a binding law that effectively restricts many UPFs through nutrient-threshold-based labeling, marketing bans, and school sale prohibitions. However, it does not use the UPF category — it targets nutrient profiles (high sugar, sodium, saturated fat, calories). Colombia's 2023 tax specifically names "ultra-processed" drinks as the taxed category, making it the first OECD-country regulation to explicitly use the UPF classification in binding fiscal policy. Both countries are OECD members (Chile since 2010, Colombia since 2020). If the criterion is "regulation that meaningfully restricts UPF exposure regardless of labeling," Chile 2016 is the answer. If the criterion is "regulation that explicitly targets UPF as a category," Colombia 2023 is the answer. I chose Colombia 2023 because UPF-as-a-category is the distinctive feature of this hazard — nutrient-based regulation predates the UPF concept.

**Category (dietary-policy):** UPF is not a single substance that can be banned. Regulation takes the form of taxes, labeling mandates, and marketing restrictions — classic dietary policy tools. This is structurally different from substance bans or exposure limits and represents the weakest regulatory category.

**Industry opposition (strong):** The UPF industry spent $1.15 billion on US lobbying (1995–2020). Industry-funded research was 5x more likely to find no association with obesity. Industry groups have organized challenges to the NOVA classification's scientific validity, funded counter-research, and used tactics documented in the 2025 Lancet series as paralleling the tobacco industry playbook. This meets the "strong" threshold of well-funded, organized, sustained campaigns.

**Substitute available (partial):** Whole and minimally processed foods exist and are nutritionally superior, but UPFs are deeply embedded in global food systems due to cost, convenience, shelf life, and infrastructure. Replacing UPFs requires systemic food system changes, not a simple product swap. "Partial" reflects that alternatives exist in principle but the transition involves enormous economic and structural barriers.

**Harm visibility (low):** UPF harms are statistical, long-latency, and diffuse. No individual can point to their diet and say "that ultra-processed food caused my cardiovascular disease." Effects emerge over decades in population-level data. Confounding with other lifestyle factors further obscures the signal. This is the lowest visibility category.

**Lag interpretation:** With early_evidence_year=2018 and first_regulation_year=2023, the lag is only 5 years. This is misleadingly short because (a) the regulation is narrow (one tax in one country on one UPF subcategory), (b) no comprehensive UPF regulation exists anywhere, and (c) the science-to-action process is still in its early phases. The "5-year lag" should be interpreted as the lag to the first marginal regulatory response, not to meaningful population-level protection. The true lag is still accumulating.
