# Where Did the Money Go?

## The core question

Corporate consolidation + optimization has degraded quality across the board — food, goods, housing, furniture, clothing, electronics. The supposed tradeoff was lower prices. But prices haven't actually dropped relative to wages. So where did the surplus value go?

## The Sysco example

Sysco dominates restaurant food supply. Jalapeno poppers at a mom-and-pop restaurant are Sysco jalapeno poppers. Fries are Sysco fries. Eggs are Sysco eggs. The food is:
- Frozen (global supply chains, months in freezers)
- Full of preservatives (shelf life)
- Injected with sugar/salt/fat (to mask how terrible it tastes)
- Produced with the cheapest possible labor
- The absolute floor of quality

All of those compromises are supposedly in service of making it cheap. But eating at restaurants isn't cheaper. It's the same price or more expensive, adjusted for wages.

## The money trail I want to trace

Pick a specific product (jalapeno poppers, eggs, fries — something simple). Track:
- What did it cost to produce 30 years ago? (better ingredients, better labor conditions, local sourcing)
- What does it cost to produce now? (Sysco-optimized, global supply chain, degraded everything)
- What's the delta?
- Where did the delta go? Sysco's margins? Executive comp? Shareholders? Government? Middlemen?

## The broader version

This isn't just food. It's everything:
- Food quality (already did Grandpa's Eggs — showed A+ eggs stayed the same price, we just invented D- eggs)
- Furniture (real wood → particle board → plastic)
- Housing (quality of new construction has dropped)
- Clothing (lasted decades → fast fashion disposable)
- Electronics (repairable → planned obsolescence)
- Bicycles, appliances, tools — all worse

In every category: quality dropped, prices didn't. Someone captured the surplus.

## The billionaire math problem

Jeff Bezos has ~$200B. But he can't actually consume $200B worth of goods and services. He has maybe hundreds of "servants" (pilots, maids, cooks, crew). Not millions. There are maybe 50 people at his wealth level. Even if every billionaire is hoarding, is that enough to account for the total surplus extracted from 300M Americans across every product category?

Back-of-napkin: If Americans save $5/week on degraded eggs alone, that's $5 x 52 x 300M = $78B/year. And that's ONE product. Scale across every degraded product category and it's trillions. Where is it?

## Possible angles
- Sysco specifically: their margins over time, executive comp, shareholder returns
- Restaurant menu prices vs. Sysco wholesale costs over time
- The "quality-adjusted price" concept from Grandpa's Eggs, applied more broadly
- Total corporate profit share of GDP over time (this is a known dataset)
- Wage share of GDP vs. capital share of GDP over time
