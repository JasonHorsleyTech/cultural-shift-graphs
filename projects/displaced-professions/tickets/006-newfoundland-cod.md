# Ticket 006: Newfoundland Cod Fishers

## Event
The 1992 cod moratorium in Newfoundland — the largest mass layoff in Canadian history. A resource depletion event enforced as a regulatory shock, displacing 30,000–40,000 workers overnight from a province where cod fishing was 10% of employment.

## Mechanism
**Discrete shock** (resource, regulatory)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track Newfoundland fishing and fish processing employment from ~1985 to ~2010:

- Pre-moratorium: ~40,000 (20K fishers, 20K plant workers) — ~10% of provincial employment
- The moratorium: July 2, 1992. Federal Fisheries Minister John Crosbie's announcement.
- Post-moratorium employment trajectory
- Partial reopening attempts and their results
- Current Newfoundland fishing employment

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Statistics Canada — Labour Force Survey, Newfoundland and Labrador
2. Department of Fisheries and Oceans (DFO) — licensed fishers count, landing statistics
3. Kurlansky, "Cod: A Biography of the Fish That Changed the World" (1997)
4. Hamilton & Butler, "Outport Adaptations" (2001)
5. Schrank, "The Newfoundland Fishery: Ten Years After the Moratorium" (2005)

### Income Data
- Average fishing household income pre-moratorium vs. post
- NCARP payments ($225/week per fisher, 1992–1994)
- TAGS payments (1994–1998)
- Track community-level income from Census 1991, 1996, 2001, 2006

**Known caveats:** Government transfer payments mask true employment income. Need to separate market income from transfer income.

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Statistics Canada Census — income data for Newfoundland by subdivision
2. HRDC (Human Resources Development Canada) — NCARP/TAGS program data
3. Cashin Task Force Report (1993) — economic analysis of the moratorium impact
4. Academic studies on TAGS outcomes

### Where They Went
- Alberta oil sands — the "Newfie goes West" phenomenon. Quantify if possible.
- Crab/shrimp fisheries (temporary boom — the "shellfish bonanza")
- Retraining programs (completion rates were reportedly low — find numbers)
- Outmigration to Ontario, other provinces
- Early retirement / permanent income support
- Women plant workers — reportedly worse outcomes than male fishers

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Statistics Canada migration data (interprovincial)
2. Newfoundland population Census data 1991–2011
3. TAGS program evaluation reports
4. Gien, "Land and Sea Connection: The East Coast Fishery Closure" (2000)

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Fishers who pivoted to crab/shrimp early. Plant workers who retrained into healthcare or skilled trades. Communities that pivoted to tourism.
- **False movers:** Fishers who retrained but couldn't find work in new fields in rural Newfoundland. People who waited for the moratorium to be lifted.
- **Deniers:** "The cod will come back." Fishing industry leaders who opposed the moratorium. DFO's own pre-moratorium optimism about stock levels.
- **Survival pattern:** Geographic mobility (willingness to leave) and/or access to alternative fisheries.

### Unique Texture
- Population collapse: Newfoundland 580K (1992) → 508K (2007) — 12% decline. Some outport communities lost 30–50%.
- The cod still haven't fully recovered 30+ years later — this is a one-way door
- NCARP/TAGS cost: >$3 billion federal spending
- The cultural dimension — cod fishing was a 500-year-old way of life, not just a job
- John Crosbie's announcement — reportedly fishers threw things at him

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Provincial employment in fishing/processing annually from 1988–2005
- Population data for Newfoundland showing the outmigration wave
- NCARP/TAGS program costs and participant counts
- Alberta migration numbers from Newfoundland
- At least one community-level case study (population, employment before/after)
