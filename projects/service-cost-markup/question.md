# How much of what you pay actually reaches the person doing the work?

For services where a skilled (or unskilled) person does work for you — a doctor, a mechanic, a firefighter, a professor — how much of the total price reaches the person who actually provides the value you're paying for? And how has that ratio changed over time?

## The metric: Fundamental Worker Ratio

**For every dollar the customer pays, how many cents go to the fundamental worker's compensation?**

The "fundamental worker" is the person who provides the core value the customer is trying to purchase. Not their manager, not their billing department, not their marketing team. Just them.

Track this ratio across dozens of industries over decades. The absolute number doesn't matter — what matters is how the line moves. A shrinking ratio means money is being funneled away from the worker into institutional overhead, regulatory compliance, middlemen, advertising arms races, or profit extraction. A growing or stable ratio means the industry is efficiently connecting buyer to laborer.

## The hypothesis

Two paths of capitalism will emerge:

1. **Convergent industries** — competition drives costs down, the ratio stays tight, the worker gets a fair share. The market is doing what it's supposed to do.

2. **Divergent industries** — moats, middlemen, regulatory capture, and institutional bloat hollow out the worker's share even as prices rise. The gap between "what you pay" and "what the worker earns" widens every decade.

The graph shows which is which. The viewer decides what to do about it.

## Why it's interesting

Everyone has gut feelings about which industries are ripping them off. Nobody has graphed the actual ratio across enough industries to see the pattern. College "feels" overpriced. A haircut "feels" fair. But what does the data actually show? And when did the divergent industries start diverging — was it deregulation? Consolidation? A specific law?

## Concrete example (napkin math)

A college professor makes ~$80,000/year and teaches ~120 students/year (4 classes x 30 students). Each student pays ~$40,000/year in tuition. Total revenue from one professor's students: $4.8M. Professor's share: 1.7%. Where's the other 98.3%?

A barber makes ~$35,000/year, cuts ~2,500 heads/year, charges ~$25/cut. Total revenue: $62,500. Barber's share: 56%. That's a 33x difference in how much of your dollar reaches the worker.

## Scope

- **Services only.** Not products.
- **Still-existing professions only.** No dead jobs (travel agent, switchboard operator). Those are a spin-off idea.
- **Skilled or unskilled, doesn't matter.** Uber driver counts. Surgeon counts.
- **One-to-many worker roles per service.** Hospital gets doctor + nurse + surgeon as separate lines against the same bill. Airlines get pilot + flight attendant. This reveals who's winning and losing within the same industry.
