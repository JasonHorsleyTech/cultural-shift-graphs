# Ticket 012: Resilient Community Archetypes — Who Survives Best?

## What to research

Certain populations within the US are dramatically better positioned for post-collapse survival. Quantify these groups and their expected survival rates to calibrate the "who makes it" portion of the curve.

## Specific communities to profile

For each: population size, why they're resilient, estimated 1-year survival rate, and limitations.

1. **Amish / Old Order Mennonite:**
   - Population: ~380K Amish, ~100K+ Old Order Mennonite
   - Already practicing pre-industrial agriculture
   - Draft animals, hand tools, seed saving, community social structure
   - Limitations: concentrated in PA/OH/IN, small absolute numbers

2. **Active military + dependents on bases:**
   - Population on US military installations
   - Supply reserves, organized command structure, medical facilities, armed defense
   - Limitations: supplies are finite; mandate to maintain order may draw resources outward

3. **Mormon (LDS) community:**
   - Population: ~7M in US, heavily concentrated in UT/ID/AZ
   - Cultural tradition of 1-year food storage (Bishop's Storehouse system)
   - Strong community organization (ward structure)
   - Limitations: what % actually maintain full year's supply? (estimates vary: 30-60%)

4. **Small-scale farmers / homesteaders:**
   - How many Americans currently operate small farms / homesteads with some self-sufficiency?
   - USDA defines "small farm" — how many are actually subsistence-capable?
   - Limitations: most "small farms" are hobby operations, not subsistence

5. **Fishing communities:**
   - Commercial fishing fleet workers and families
   - Coastal communities where fishing is primary economy
   - Caloric self-sufficiency from fishing alone

6. **Survivalist / prepper community:**
   - Population estimates (very uncertain — maybe 10-20M with some preparation, 1-3M "serious")
   - Average supply duration
   - Limitations: supplies are finite; community cooperation varies; many preppers plan for individual/family survival, not community

7. **Native American reservations:**
   - Some reservations have agricultural traditions, land, and community structure
   - Others face extreme poverty and resource scarcity
   - Wide variation — can't generalize

## Where to look

1. **Census Bureau** — Amish, LDS, farming population data
2. **USDA** — farm size and type classifications
3. **DoD** — military installation population
4. **LDS Church statistics** — membership and storehouse program
5. **Prepper survey data** — FEMA/Red Cross household preparedness surveys
6. **NOAA** — fishing community data
7. **BIA (Bureau of Indian Affairs)** — reservation population and resources

## Result format

Key Data Points table. Each row is one community type with:
- `population` — how many Americans fall in this category
- `estimated_1year_survival_pct` — with uncertainty range
- `primary_resilience_factor` — what makes them survive
- `primary_limitation` — what could still kill them

Include a total: what fraction of the US population has significantly above-average survival probability?

## What good data looks like

- Population numbers are sourced, not guessed
- Survival estimates are honest about uncertainty
- Total "resilient" population is probably 10-20M (3-6% of US) — if much higher, question the definition of "resilient"
- Notes overlaps (a Mormon small farmer in rural Utah checks multiple boxes)
