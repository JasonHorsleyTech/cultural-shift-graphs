# Ticket 014: Typesetting / Linotype Operators

## Event
The displacement of Linotype operators and manual typesetters by phototypesetting (1960s–70s) and then desktop publishing (1980s–90s). A platform shift — DTP wasn't a better Linotype, it was an entirely different workflow that eliminated the specialist role.

## Mechanism
**Tipping point** (tech_platform)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track US typesetting/composition employment from ~1960 to ~2000:

- Peak: estimate total compositors, typesetters, Linotype/Monotype operators. The International Typographical Union (ITU) had ~120,000 members at peak (verify year).
- Key dates: Phototypesetting machines (1960s), Atex/Harris systems in newspapers (1970s), Apple Macintosh + LaserWriter + Aldus PageMaker (1985 = "DTP revolution"), QuarkXPress (1987), ITU merged into CWA (1987)
- The transition happened in waves: newspapers first (1970s), book publishing (1980s), all commercial printing (1990s)
- Current: "typesetter" as a job title is effectively extinct

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS OES data, SOC for compositors/typesetters (historical codes — may be discontinued)
2. International Typographical Union records (membership data)
3. US Census occupational data via IPUMS
4. Romano, "Machine Writing and Typesetting" (1986)
5. Bringhurst, "The Elements of Typographic Style" (background on the craft)

### Income Data
- Linotype operators were among the best-paid blue-collar workers — union wages were significantly above manufacturing average
- Track ITU scale wages from 1960–1990
- Compare to what desktop publishing paid (graphic designers doing layout)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. ITU wage scales (union records, trade publications like "The Typographical Journal")
2. BLS wage data for compositors/typesetters
3. BLS wage data for graphic designers (SOC 27-1024) as the successor occupation

### Where They Went
- Graphic design / desktop publishing (those who learned the new tools)
- Paste-up / production art (a transitional role that also died)
- Press operation (staying in printing but moving to a different stage)
- General manufacturing
- Retirement (many were older, highly skilled workers)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS employment data for graphic designers and desktop publishing specialists
2. Printing industry association reports
3. CWA/ITU merger documentation on workforce transitions

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Typesetters who learned PageMaker/QuarkXPress and became desktop publishers or graphic designers. Newspapers that retrained composing room staff.
- **False movers:** Investment in phototypesetting systems in the early 1980s — expensive machines that were obsoleted by cheap DTP within 5 years.
- **Deniers:** "A Macintosh can't do real typesetting." Union resistance to computerized composition in newspaper composing rooms (significant and well-documented).
- **Survival pattern:** Willingness to learn the replacement technology. The skill was typography knowledge, not machine operation — those who understood design survived.

### Unique Texture
- The ITU was one of the oldest and most powerful US trade unions (founded 1852). It couldn't save the profession.
- The 1978 New York newspaper strike — unions fighting against computerized typesetting
- Linotype operators had to read text *backwards and upside down* in metal type — an extraordinary skill made worthless overnight
- The "1985 desktop publishing revolution" — one of the fastest skill obsolescences in modern history
- Mark Twain was an early investor in the Paige typesetting machine (a failed Linotype competitor) — lost his fortune

## What Good Data Looks Like
- ITU membership or BLS employment data spanning 1960–2000 (at least 6 data points)
- Wage data showing the premium Linotype operators commanded
- Graphic designer employment growth as a counterweight to typesetter decline
- Timeline of technology transitions (hot metal → phototype → DTP) with rough adoption dates
- The union response documented with specific dates and actions
