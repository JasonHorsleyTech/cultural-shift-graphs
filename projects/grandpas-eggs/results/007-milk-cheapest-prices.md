# Milk (Whole, Conventional) — Cheapest Retail Prices

## Summary

Whole milk retail prices roughly quintupled in nominal terms from ~$0.82/gallon in 1950 to ~$4.07/gallon in 2025. The trajectory has three distinct regimes: (1) slow, steady increases through the 1960s as milk was heavily price-regulated and stable; (2) sharp inflation-driven spikes in 1973–74 and 1978–80; and (3) a volatile but relatively flat period from the mid-1990s onward, with cyclical swings driven by feed costs and dairy herd economics. A major structural break occurs in 2022 when post-pandemic inflation pushed milk past $4/gallon for the first time.

The data splices three BLS measurement eras: compiled retail gallon prices (1950–1979), BLS average half-gallon prices doubled (1980–1994), and BLS average gallon prices (1995–2025). The 1994→1995 splice introduces an apparent ~14% drop that is methodological, not real — gallon jugs are cheaper per ounce than two half-gallons.

## Methodology

### Three-era splice

This series stitches together three measurement approaches:

**Era 1 (1950–1979): Compiled retail gallon prices.** The BLS did not publish a clean per-gallon average price series before 1980. Prices come from:
- **1950–1959:** Annual retail gallon prices compiled by fiftiesweb.com from BLS data. Cross-validated against BLS half-gallon data where available ($0.41/half-gal in 1950 × 2 = $0.82/gal ✓).
- **1960, 1965, 1970, 1975:** Anchor points derived from BLS half-gallon prices (published in University of Missouri Library guide and other compilations) doubled to gallon-equivalent, cross-validated against Taste of Home compilation.
- **Intermediate years (1961–64, 1966–69, 1971–74, 1976–79):** Interpolated between anchor points using the BLS Consumer Price Index for Fresh Whole Milk (CUUR0000SEFJ01 equivalent from in2013dollars.com) as a shape guide. The interpolation uses CPI-weighted linear scaling between each pair of anchor points, ensuring exact agreement at both endpoints. These estimates carry low confidence; the general trajectory is reliable but individual year values may be off by 5–10%.

**Era 2 (1980–1994): BLS half-gallon × 2.** Series APU0000709111 — Average Price: Milk, Fresh, Whole, Fortified, per Half-Gallon, U.S. City Average. Annual averages of monthly data, doubled to estimate gallon-equivalent cost. This systematically overestimates actual gallon jug prices by approximately 3–10%, because gallon containers are cheaper per ounce than two half-gallons. The Taste of Home compilation ($2.20 for 1985 vs. our $2.27) suggests the overestimate is about 3% in the mid-1980s.

**Era 3 (1995–2025): BLS per-gallon.** Series APU0000709112 — Average Price: Milk, Fresh, Whole, Fortified, per Gallon, U.S. City Average. Monthly data from July 1995 onward. Annual averages computed from monthly observations. This series includes both organic and non-organic milk in its sample, though conventional milk dominates the average.

### The 1994→1995 splice discontinuity

The 1994 half-gallon × 2 value ($2.88) and the 1995 gallon value ($2.48) show an apparent 14% drop that is entirely methodological. The BLS Fresh Whole Milk CPI actually increased slightly from 1994 (131.2) to 1995 (131.1) — milk prices were essentially flat. The drop reflects the switch from measuring two half-gallons to measuring one gallon jug.

**For the graph phase:** The simplest correction is to scale the 1980–1994 half-gallon × 2 values down by ~13% (multiply by 0.87) to align with the gallon series. This factor is derived from the 1995 overlap: $2.48 (gallon) / $2.86 (half-gal Jan-Dec avg × 2) ≈ 0.87. The pre-1980 compiled data appears to already reflect gallon prices (fiftiesweb values match gallon pricing, not half-gallon × 2), so no adjustment is needed there.

### What "cheapest" means here

This is the **average retail price** for conventional whole milk, not the absolute lowest price available. BLS average prices reflect what urban consumers actually pay at supermarkets, including sale prices but averaged over the year and across regions. The "cheapest" distinction matters primarily for the quality-equivalent ticket (grass-fed organic), which represents a premium tier. For the period 1950–1997, there was effectively only one quality tier, so this price IS both the cheapest and the only option.

### Structural breaks and policy context

- **Federal Milk Marketing Orders (1937–present):** Establish minimum prices processors must pay farmers for fluid milk, creating a price floor. These orders stabilized prices but also prevented competitive discounting at the farm level.
- **Nixon-era price controls (1971–1974):** May have temporarily suppressed retail dairy prices during the inflationary 1973–74 food price spike. The CPI for fresh whole milk shows a sharper increase than retail gallon prices during this period.
- **Dairy Price Support Program:** USDA purchased surplus dairy products to maintain farm prices, funded through the Commodity Credit Corporation. This buffered retail prices from severe drops but also prevented efficiency gains from fully flowing to consumers.
- **Corn/ethanol mandate effects (2005–2008):** Ethanol demand raised corn prices, which raised feed costs, which raised milk prices. The 2007–2008 spike is partly attributable to this.
- **2022 inflation:** Post-pandemic supply chain disruptions, elevated input costs (feed, fuel, labor), and broad food inflation pushed milk past $4/gallon for the first time.

### Home delivery vs. store-bought transition

In the early 1950s, over half of consumer milk was home-delivered, which cost more than store-bought. By the mid-1970s, home delivery had fallen to ~7% of sales. The prices in this series aim to capture store-bought retail prices, not home delivery. For the 1950s data, there may be some blending of delivery and store prices in the compiled figures, but by 1960 the distinction is minimal for BLS average price data.

## Known gaps

- **1961–1964, 1966–1969, 1971–1974, 1976–1979:** No direct BLS retail price observations. Values are CPI-weighted interpolations between anchor points. Confidence is low for individual years but the trajectory is reliable.
- **1980–1994 absolute level:** Half-gallon × 2 overestimates gallon prices by ~3–13%. The magnitude of overestimation may have varied over time as gallon jugs became more common.
- **2025:** Partial year — October data missing from BLS release. Average of 11 available months.
- **Pre-1960 unit ambiguity:** Milk was sold in quarts, half-gallons, and (increasingly) gallons. "Per gallon" prices from the 1950s may reflect quart × 4 or half-gallon × 2 calculations rather than actual gallon jug purchases, introducing ~5–10% variation depending on container size.
