# Ticket 012: Vinyl Chloride → Liver Cancer

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that vinyl chloride causes liver angiosarcoma to binding workplace exposure standards. This is expected to be a relatively FAST response case — useful as a comparison point.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Animal studies showed vinyl chloride caused cancer in the late 1960s-early 1970s. The first human cases of liver angiosarcoma in vinyl chloride workers were reported by John Creech and Maurice Johnson at B.F. Goodrich in January 1974. Maltoni's animal studies (1970-1973) preceded the human cases. What's the earliest credible evidence?

2. **Consensus date** — The link was accepted rapidly. IARC classification? NIOSH/OSHA determination? The speed of consensus is part of the story.

3. **First OECD regulation** — OSHA issued an Emergency Temporary Standard for vinyl chloride in April 1974 — just months after the Creech & Johnson report. Was any other country faster?

4. **US regulation** — OSHA Emergency Temporary Standard (April 1974), permanent standard (October 1974) — reduced PEL from 500 ppm to 1 ppm. Remarkably fast regulatory action.

5. **Industry opposition** — The plastics industry initially claimed the 1 ppm standard would destroy the PVC industry. They achieved compliance without catastrophe. Some industry insiders (particularly in Europe) knew about the risk before the 1974 public disclosure.

6. **Why this was fast** — The harm was rare and specific (liver angiosarcoma is extremely uncommon outside vinyl chloride exposure), making the causal link undeniable. The mechanism of action was clear from animal studies. Industry couldn't plausibly deny causation.

## Where to look

1. OSHA vinyl chloride standard regulatory history (29 CFR 1910.1017)
2. IARC Monograph on vinyl chloride
3. Creech & Johnson (1974) — original case report
4. Maltoni (1970s) — animal carcinogenicity studies
5. Markowitz & Rosner, "Deceit and Denial" — chapter on vinyl chloride

## What "good data" looks like

- Precise dates for the 1974 regulatory response — this is one of the fastest science-to-regulation transitions in the dataset
- Context on WHY it was fast (rare, specific cancer type; clear causal mechanism; animal evidence preceded human cases)
- Documentation of the lag between Maltoni's animal studies (early 1970s) and the emergency standard — was the delay in acting on animal data a mini-lag within the larger story?

## Known pitfalls

- European manufacturers knew about vinyl chloride cancer risk before the 1974 US disclosure. If European evidence predates US, the "early evidence" date may be earlier than 1974.
- The 500 ppm to 1 ppm reduction was dramatic — industry claimed it was impossible, then achieved it within a few years. This is a case where regulation drove innovation.
