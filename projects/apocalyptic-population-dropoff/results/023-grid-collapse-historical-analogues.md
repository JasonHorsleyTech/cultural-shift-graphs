# Result: Grid Collapse — Historical Analogues and Partial Precedents

## Summary

Six partial precedents for prolonged grid collapse were analyzed: Puerto Rico (2017), Venezuela (2016–present), Iraq (2003+), Lebanon (2020–present), the 2003 Northeast US blackout, and Texas Winter Storm Uri (2021). The most critical finding is that **every analogue received external aid** — no precedent exists for a nationwide grid collapse without outside help. Puerto Rico is the single most informative analogue: a near-total grid destruction in a modern infrastructure context, where the majority of excess deaths came from disrupted medical care, not direct storm damage. Excess death rates during the acute crisis phase ranged from ~0.1 to ~0.4 per 1,000 population per month across analogues where data exists, but these rates would almost certainly accelerate nonlinearly without external aid.

**Primary cause of death:** Disrupted or delayed medical care (1/3 of excess deaths attributed directly to healthcare interruption per the Harvard survey). **Key lesson:** The grid took 11 months to fully restore even with massive federal aid (FEMA, Army Corps of Engineers, utility crews from mainland US). 45% of the island was still dark 3 months post-hurricane. Mortality was driven by chronic medical dependency, not acute trauma — this is the pattern most relevant to a US-wide grid collapse.

**Key lesson:** Venezuela demonstrates what **gradual grid degradation without external aid** looks like. The 25% population exodus is the most striking datum — when infrastructure collapses slowly enough, people leave rather than die in place. This is relevant to US grid collapse only if there's somewhere to go. Also shows: 60% physician loss is catastrophic for healthcare-dependent populations.

**Primary cause of death:** Violence (overwhelmingly — ~92% of Lancet 2006 excess deaths). Infrastructure-related excess mortality (degraded water/sanitation, electricity loss affecting hospitals, medication shortages) accounted for a minority of excess deaths but is poorly isolated in the data.

**Key lesson:** Iraq is a **confounded analogue** — you cannot separate grid-collapse mortality from war mortality. The grid operated at 30–50% of pre-war capacity for years even with $5.56 billion in US reconstruction spending. However, the infrastructure degradation → water/sanitation → disease pathway is visible in the data and relevant. The Lancet studies remain methodologically controversial (wide confidence intervals, disputed sampling), though peer-reviewed.

**Primary cause of death/harm:** Air pollution from diesel generators (300%+ increase since 2019), degraded hospital capacity, medication shortages. Direct excess mortality from electricity loss is not well quantified — Lebanon's crisis is overlaid with economic collapse, Beirut port explosion (2020), and political paralysis, making isolation of grid-specific mortality impossible.

**Key lesson:** Lebanon demonstrates **societal adaptation to chronic grid loss** — private generators became ubiquitous, creating a parallel (expensive, polluting) power system. Society doesn't simply collapse at 2–4 hours/day of state power; it degrades expensively. This adaptation pathway requires fuel availability and purchasing power, neither of which would exist in a US-wide grid collapse scenario. Also demonstrates that chronic low-level grid supply (vs. zero) produces a qualitatively different crisis.

### Northeast US Blackout (2003)

| parameter | value | range_low | range_high | unit | timeframe | source | confidence |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| population_affected | 55000000 | 50000000 | 55000000 | people | Aug 14-16 2003 | NERC / US-Canada Task Force | high |
| grid_loss_pct | 100 | 100 | 100 | pct | in affected areas | NERC 2004 | high |
| duration_days | 1.5 | 1 | 4 | days | Aug 14-16 2003 (most <2 days, some up to 4) | NERC 2004 | high |
| excess_deaths_nyc | 90 | 60 | 120 | people | Aug 14-31 2003 | Anderson & Bell, Epidemiology 2012 | high |
| excess_deaths_total | 11 | 11 | 100 | people | Aug 14-16 2003 | official (11) vs. epidemiological (~100 NYC alone) | medium |
| excess_death_rate_per_month | 0.17 | 0.11 | 0.23 | deaths_per_1000_per_month | annualized from NYC 2-day data | Anderson & Bell 2012 | medium |
| external_aid_available | 1 | 1 | 1 | boolean | immediate (surrounding areas unaffected) | — | high |

**Primary cause of death:** In NYC, mortality increased 122% for accidental deaths and 25% for non-accidental (disease-related) deaths. Heat-related mortality was a factor (August in NYC without air conditioning). Those aged 65–74 were particularly vulnerable. Researchers found no evidence of short-term mortality displacement (i.e., the deaths were not simply pulled forward from the following days — they were truly excess).

**Key lesson:** Even a **1–2 day blackout** in a modern US city produces measurable excess mortality, primarily among the elderly and medically fragile. The annualized death rate is misleadingly high because it can't be linearly extrapolated — a 2-day blackout's per-day death rate is driven by acute vulnerabilities (heat, elevator entrapment, traffic chaos) that don't scale linearly with duration. The real value of this analogue is the threshold insight: **the mortality curve changes character around 72 hours**, when food spoilage, water pressure loss, and medication gaps begin to dominate over acute hazards.

**Primary cause of death:** Hypothermia (65% of official deaths — 158 of 246 confirmed deaths were from cold exposure). Remaining deaths from carbon monoxide poisoning (people running generators/cars indoors), medical care disruption, and exacerbation of chronic conditions. The winter context is critical — the same duration blackout in summer would have a different mortality profile (heat-related rather than hypothermia).

**Key lesson:** Demonstrates that **season dramatically affects the mortality profile** of grid failure. 3–5 days without heat in Texas winter killed hundreds. Also shows the gap between official counts (246) and epidemiological estimates (700–978) — official attribution undercounts by 3–4x, a pattern consistent with Puerto Rico and other disasters.

## Synthesis: Scaling to US-Wide Permanent Grid Collapse

The critical analytical gap these analogues reveal:

1. **All analogues had external aid.** Puerto Rico had FEMA and the Army Corps. Texas had mutual aid from other states. Venezuela had neighboring countries absorbing refugees. Iraq had $5.56B in US reconstruction funding. Lebanon has generator fuel imports. A full US grid collapse has no external aid at US scale — no country can send enough transformers, generators, or food for 335 million people.

2. **Excess death rates from analogues with external aid cluster around 0.1–0.4 per 1,000 per month.** Without aid, rates would be dramatically higher, but no analogue tells us how much higher.

3. **The migration valve matters enormously.** Venezuela lost 25% of its population to emigration rather than death. Puerto Rico lost 5–8%. In a US-wide collapse, there is no comparable migration destination at scale.

4. **Medical dependency is the first killer.** Across every analogue, disrupted medical care — not starvation, not violence, not exposure — was the primary or co-primary driver of excess mortality in the first weeks to months. This aligns with the insulin/dialysis/ventilator dependency data from other tickets in this project.

5. **The 72-hour threshold is real.** Short blackouts (2003 NE, Uri) produce mortality mainly from acute hazards. After ~72 hours, the character of mortality shifts to chronic dependency failures. After weeks, it shifts again to supply chain collapse (food, water, sanitation). After months, it shifts to carrying-capacity constraints.

6. **Official death counts systematically undercount by 3–10x.** Puerto Rico: 64 official vs. 2,975–4,645 estimated. Texas: 246 official vs. 700–978 estimated. Any grid collapse mortality modeling should use excess mortality methods, not official attribution.

## Data Gaps

- **Venezuela overall excess mortality is unknown.** The government stopped publishing vital statistics in 2018. The maternal and infant mortality increases are documented, but total excess mortality during the grid/economic crisis is unquantifiable from available data.
- **Iraq infrastructure-specific mortality cannot be isolated from violence.** The Lancet studies estimate total excess mortality but don't separate grid-related deaths from combat-related deaths in a usable way.
- **Lebanon excess mortality from grid collapse specifically is not studied.** The AUB pollution research quantifies generator-pollution health effects but not total grid-collapse mortality.
- **No analogue exists for permanent grid loss without external aid in a modern industrialized nation.** All extrapolation to that scenario is fundamentally speculative.
- **Nonlinear scaling is the biggest unknown.** Excess death rates from a 3.4M-person island with federal aid cannot be linearly scaled to a 335M-person nation without aid. The rate would be higher — but whether it's 2x, 5x, or 20x higher is not answerable from historical data.
