# Result: Median Earnings, Full-Time Year-Round Women, 1955–2024

## Source
- **Primary source:** U.S. Census Bureau, Current Population Survey, 1956–2025 Annual Social and Economic Supplements (CPS ASEC). Historical Income Tables: People, Table P-36 — "Full-Time, Year-Round All Workers by Median Income and Sex: 1955 to 2024." Downloaded from https://www2.census.gov/programs-surveys/cps/tables/time-series/historical-income-people/p36ar.xlsx
- **Date accessed:** 2026-04-30

## Methodology
- **Definition:** Median total money earnings of female full-time, year-round workers. "Full-time, year-round" means 35+ hours per week, 50+ weeks per year. As with the men's series (ticket 014), Table P-36 reports median income for FTYR workers, which for this population is overwhelmingly wage/salary earnings.
- **Universe:** Female persons 15 years old and over (beginning with March 1980 CPS); 14 years old and over for earlier years. **All ages**, not restricted to 25–64. The 25–64 age restriction specified in the ticket is not available as a consistent historical time series from Census. All-ages FTYR is the accepted fallback per the ticket instructions — consistent with the approach used for men in ticket 014.
- **Age composition note:** Including workers under 25 and over 64 in the FTYR population slightly depresses the median relative to a 25–64 restriction. The effect is comparable to the men's series (roughly 2–5% lower than a 25–64 restriction in recent years). Critically, the age composition of female FTYR workers has changed more dramatically than men's over this period — in the 1950s–1960s, the female FTYR population was much smaller and more concentrated in specific age groups and occupations (clerical, teaching, nursing), which affects representativeness of early years.
- **Known methodology changes:** Same breaks as the men's series (ticket 014), as both come from Table P-36:
  - **1967 (footnote 12):** First year with full CPS redesign providing reliable annual estimates by sex.
  - **1980:** Age threshold changed from 14+ to 15+.
  - **2013 (footnote 38/39):** CPS ASEC redesigned questionnaire. Dual estimates published. New-methodology value used from 2013 forward.
  - **2017 (footnote 40):** Processing system updated (improved imputation, revised top-codes). Dual estimates published. New-processing value used from 2017 forward.
  - **2020 (footnote 41):** Pandemic-year anomaly — fewer FTYR workers, stimulus effects, composition changes.
- **Dual-estimate years:** For 2013 and 2017, Census published estimates under both old and new methodology/processing. This table uses the **new methodology** estimate for each break year to maintain forward consistency. The old-methodology values are noted in the Gaps and Caveats section.

## Gaps and Caveats

- **Age restriction not achieved:** Data is for all-ages (15+/14+) female FTYR workers, not the preferred 25–64 age band. The Census Bureau does not publish a consistent historical time series of median earnings restricted to ages 25–64. This matches the approach used for men (ticket 014), ensuring the two series are directly comparable.
- **Pre-1967 data:** Before 1967, worker count data ("Number with income") is not available (marked "N" in Census tables). Median earnings values are available from P-60 published reports. These early years may use slightly different survey instruments than the modern CPS ASEC, but the full-time year-round definition has been consistent. The female FTYR population was quite small in the 1950s–1960s (roughly 6–10 million compared to 40+ million today), so median estimates have wider confidence intervals for these early years.
- **Dual estimates at methodology breaks:**
  - **2013:** New methodology = $40,700; old methodology = $40,600 (difference: +$100 or +0.2%).
  - **2017:** New processing = $45,830; old processing = $44,380 (difference: +$1,450 or +3.3%). The new-processing increase was notably larger for women than for men (where it was −0.6%), suggesting the improved imputation captured previously underreported female earnings.
- **2020 pandemic anomaly:** The 2020 value ($52,610) reflects pandemic composition effects — many lower-wage workers left FTYR status, pushing the median of the remaining pool upward. The number of female FTYR workers dropped from 52,040 thousand (2019) to 46,000 thousand (2020), a loss of 6 million or 11.6%. This was a slightly larger proportional drop than for men (10.2%), consistent with women's disproportionate concentration in pandemic-affected service industries. The jump overstates underlying wage growth.
- **2009 anomaly:** Median earnings rose slightly from $36,690 (2008) to $37,230 (2009) despite the Great Recession, likely due to compositional effects similar to men's (lower-wage workers disproportionately losing FTYR status).
- **Female-to-male earnings ratio trend:** Women's FTYR median earnings as a share of men's:
  - 1955: 64.5% ($2,735 / $4,241)
  - 1960: 60.7% ($3,296 / $5,434)
  - 1973: 56.6% ($6,488 / $11,470) — the nadir
  - 1980: 60.5% ($11,590 / $19,170)
  - 1990: 71.1% ($20,590 / $28,980)
  - 2000: 74.9% ($29,120 / $38,890)
  - 2010: 76.6% ($38,440 / $50,150)
  - 2020: 81.0% ($52,610 / $64,940)
  - 2024: 80.9% ($61,020 / $75,440)
  The ratio declined through the 1960s (as men's real earnings surged while women's grew more slowly), bottomed around 1973, then rose steadily as women's earnings grew faster than men's from the late 1970s onward. The ratio has plateaued at ~81% since roughly 2020.
- **Composition changes over time:** The female FTYR workforce has changed dramatically over this period. In the 1950s–1960s, female FTYR workers were concentrated in clerical, teaching, and nursing occupations. By the 2000s–2020s, the population is far more occupationally diverse. This means the median is not tracking the "same kind of worker" over time — it tracks a shifting pool that now includes many more professional and managerial women.
- **Real earnings growth much stronger for women than men:** Unlike men (whose real median earnings stagnated from 1973 to ~2017), women's real median earnings roughly tripled from 1960 to 2024 in inflation-adjusted terms. This sustained growth reflects occupational upgrading, increased educational attainment, and narrowing discrimination — but also the expanding pool of women working full-time year-round.
- **Confidence assessment:**
  - 1967–2024: **High** — direct CPS ASEC survey estimates with large sample sizes.
  - 1955–1966: **Medium** — derived from P-60 published reports with consistent methodology but smaller samples (especially for women), no published worker counts, and a narrower occupational base.
