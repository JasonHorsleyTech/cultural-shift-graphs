# Ticket 012: Freed Slaves / Post-Emancipation Labor

## Event
The displacement of ~4 million enslaved people from the plantation labor system following the 13th Amendment (1865). The most massive discrete labor shock in US history — an entire workforce freed overnight without infrastructure, capital, or labor market access.

## Mechanism
**Discrete shock** (regulatory, regime)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track the labor status of formerly enslaved people from ~1860 to ~1900:

- 1860 Census: ~3.95 million enslaved people, ~90% in agriculture
- 1865: Emancipation. Freedmen's Bureau established.
- 1870 Census: first enumeration of Black Americans as free individuals with occupations
- Track Black employment by sector in the South: 1870, 1880, 1890, 1900
- The Great Migration (1910s–1970s) as a delayed labor market adjustment

**Source hierarchy:**
1. US Census, 1860–1900 (via IPUMS for occupational microdata)
2. Freedmen's Bureau records (National Archives)
3. Foner, "Reconstruction: America's Unfinished Revolution" (1988)
4. Du Bois, "Black Reconstruction in America" (1935)
5. Ransom & Sutch, "One Kind of Freedom: The Economic Consequences of Emancipation" (2001)
6. Historical Statistics of the United States

### Income Data
**Known caveats:** Enslaved people had no "income" in a conventional sense. Post-emancipation "income" under sharecropping was often near-zero after debts to landowners. This event requires different metrics:

- Land ownership rates for Black Americans (1870–1900)
- Literacy rates (1870–1900)
- Share of crop retained under sharecropping (typically 50% of harvest, minus supply advances)
- Black per capita income as % of white per capita income (1870–1900)
- Wage data for Black agricultural laborers vs. white laborers in the South

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Ransom & Sutch (2001) — detailed economic analysis of sharecropping
2. Census data on literacy, property ownership
3. Higgs, "Competition and Coercion: Blacks in the American Economy, 1865–1914" (1977)
4. Margo, "Race and Schooling in the South, 1880–1950" (1990)

### Where They Went
The majority stayed in agriculture — but the *terms* changed:
- Sharecropping (the dominant outcome — ~75% of freed people in the South by 1880)
- Tenant farming
- Wage labor on former plantations
- Skilled trades (blacksmithing, carpentry — some had learned these as enslaved people)
- Urban migration to Southern cities
- The Exodusters (1879) — migration to Kansas
- The Great Migration (decades later, 1910s+)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Census occupational data 1870–1900
2. Foner (1988)
3. Ransom & Sutch (2001)
4. Painter, "Exodusters: Black Migration to Kansas After Reconstruction" (1976)

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Freed people who acquired land (estimate: ~5% owned land by 1880, ~25% by 1910). Black mutual aid societies and churches as economic networks. Specific named examples if findable.
- **False movers:** "40 acres and a mule" — Sherman's Special Field Order No. 15 (Jan 1865) granted land, then revoked by Andrew Johnson. People who built on this promise lost everything.
- **Deniers:** Former slaveholders who expected the labor system to continue essentially unchanged. "Black Codes" as an attempt to recreate slavery in all but name.
- **Survival pattern:** Access to land, literacy, skilled trades, and community networks.

### Unique Texture
- This is simultaneously a liberation AND a displacement — the moral framing is unique in the dataset
- Sharecropping as a form of re-ensnarement — legally free, economically trapped
- The Freedmen's Bureau: one of the first large-scale government retraining/resettlement programs in US history
- Convict leasing as a "where they went" category — re-enslaved through the criminal justice system
- Black land ownership as a measure of recovery (peaked early 1900s, then declined under Jim Crow)

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Census data on Black occupations in the South: 1870, 1880, 1890, 1900
- Land ownership rates for Black Americans over time
- Literacy rates as a proxy for human capital development
- Sharecropping terms (% of crop, debt arrangements)
- At least 2 specific community-level stories
