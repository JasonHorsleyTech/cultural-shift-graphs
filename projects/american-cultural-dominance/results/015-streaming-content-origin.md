# US Content Share on Global Streaming Platforms

## Summary
US-produced content dominates global streaming viewership, averaging ~61% of Netflix's global top 10 slots since mid-2020, even as platforms invest heavily in local content. The trajectory shows a gradual decline from near-total US dominance in early streaming years (2015–2017, when Netflix's international libraries were overwhelmingly American) to a more contested ~57–60% share by 2024–2025, driven by breakout non-English hits (Squid Game, Money Heist, La Casa de Papel) and EU content quotas. Despite this, six US media companies still account for over 50% of all global content spending ($126B in 2024), and platforms like Disney+ export an almost entirely American catalog internationally.

## Penetration Score Definition
**Denominator:** US-produced content's share of the most-watched content (top 10 lists / engagement-weighted viewership) on major global streaming platforms in non-US markets.
**0% anchor:** Zero US-produced content appears in top-watched lists on streaming platforms outside the US — all popular streamed content is locally or regionally produced.
**100% anchor:** Every title in the top-watched lists on streaming platforms outside the US is American-produced — complete US dominance of global streaming viewership.

## Methodology

The penetration score measures US-produced content's share of the most-watched content on major global streaming platforms in non-US markets.

**Primary proxy (2020–present):** Netflix weekly global top 10 data, tracked by FlixPatrol, which assigns points based on rankings across all Netflix markets. US content's share of total top 10 points provides the core metric. This is supplemented by Netflix's official engagement reports (viewing hours by language category) and weighted with estimates for Disney+ (heavily US), Amazon Prime Video (more mixed), and Apple TV+ (mostly US).

**Secondary proxy (2015–2019):** Estimated from Netflix catalog composition data, Ampere Analysis reports on content commissioning by country of origin, and the launch timing of other platforms. These years carry lower confidence as top 10/viewership data wasn't published.

**Early years (2010–2014):** Rough estimates based on Netflix's international expansion timeline and library composition. Before 2016, Netflix operated in limited markets and offered predominantly US-licensed content internationally.

**Weighting across platforms:** Netflix is weighted most heavily as the largest global streamer by international subscribers (~280M+ total, ~60% international). Disney+ (~130M subscribers, 2/3 international) pulls the composite score upward due to its heavily American catalog. Amazon Prime Video (~200M+ subscribers) pulls slightly downward due to significant local content investment (especially India, Japan, UK).

**Consistency:** The same core metric — US content's share of the most popular titles in non-US markets — is applied throughout, though the precision of measurement improves dramatically after Netflix began publishing top 10 lists in mid-2020.

## Notes

- **The "catalog vs. viewership" gap:** Netflix's catalog has become increasingly international (52% of 2024 spending is non-US), but US content still captures a disproportionate share of viewership (~60%+ of top 10 slots). American content punches above its catalog weight in global popularity.
- **Disney+ as an American content delivery vehicle:** Disney+ is functionally a US cultural export platform. Its most-watched titles (Marvel, Star Wars, Pixar, Disney Animation) are overwhelmingly American. As Disney+ grew to 130M+ subscribers (2/3 international), it became a significant channel for US content penetration with no meaningful local content strategy.
- **The Squid Game phenomenon is real but bounded:** Korean content surged from ~4.5% to 11.8% of Netflix top 10 in Q4 2021, but US content still held ~59%. Non-English breakouts are becoming more frequent (Money Heist, Lupin, Dark, La Palma) but haven't fundamentally displaced US dominance — they've nibbled at the margin.
- **EU AVMS Directive:** The 30% European content quota (2018) represents the most significant regulatory response to US streaming dominance. Netflix met this by 2022 in almost all European markets, but compliance is measured by catalog share, not viewership — US content still dominates European viewership on the platform.
- **Structural advantage:** Six US media companies control >50% of global content spending ($126B in 2024). This spending dominance, combined with English as the global lingua franca and established IP franchises, creates a self-reinforcing cycle of US content dominance on streaming platforms.
- **Pre-2020 data is estimated.** The shift from ~85% (2015) to ~65% (2020) reflects Netflix's deliberate strategy to commission local content as it expanded internationally, but the exact trajectory is approximate.
