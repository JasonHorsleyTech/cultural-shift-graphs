# Men's Haircut — Cheapest and Quality-Equivalent Prices

## Summary

Men's haircut prices follow the textbook Baumol's cost disease trajectory: a purely labor-intensive service with no meaningful productivity gains, where prices rise roughly in line with economy-wide wages. Before ~1975, there was a single tier (the neighborhood barbershop). After Supercuts launched in 1975 and Great Clips followed in 1982, a budget chain tier emerged. The gap between budget chains and standard barbershops was negligible through the 1980s but widened significantly from ~2000 onward, driven by the "barbershop renaissance" pushing independent shop prices upward while budget chains competed aggressively on price. By 2025, budget chains average ~$20 vs. ~$33 for a standard independent barbershop — a 65% premium, though the premium is in service scope (time in chair, razor cleanup, finishing) rather than cut quality.

## Methodology

### Pre-1975 (single tier)

Anchored to the most commonly cited price points: ~$1.00 in 1950 and ~$1.25 in 1955 (corroborated by White Plains, NY library records and widely cited in historical sources). Intermediate years interpolated using the BLS CPI for Personal Care Services (FRED series CUUR0000SEGC), with upward adjustments where hard evidence shows haircut-specific prices rising faster than the broader index (e.g., 1966 St. Louis barber union data).

Haircut prices rose at roughly 5–6% annually from 1950–1975, outpacing overall CPI (~2–3%) — consistent with Baumol's cost disease even before the term was widely applied. The barber's output (one haircut per 20–25 minutes) hasn't changed, but barber wages must track rising economy-wide productivity.

### Post-1975 budget tier (Line A)

The "cheapest mainstream" product shifted from "neighborhood barbershop" (pre-1975) to "budget chain" (post-~1985) as Supercuts (founded 1975) and Great Clips (founded 1982) scaled nationally. Key anchor points:

- **1975:** Supercuts opened at $6, but this was premium-convenience positioning. The cheapest was still the neighborhood barber at ~$3.50.
- **1985:** Supercuts at $8 (documented via TV advertising). By this point, some budget barbers undercut at $6–$7.
- **2005:** Great Clips regular prices documented at $4.99–$13 depending on region (AnandTech forum, 2005). National average ~$9–$10.
- **2019:** Great Clips ~$13 (Dallin Crump pricing documentation, pre-pandemic).
- **2025:** Great Clips $19–$21 (PriceListo, SalonRates, multiple current sources). Supercuts $23–$28.

Budget chain prices were remarkably stable from ~1995–2010 (low-inflation era + aggressive chain competition), then accelerated sharply post-2015 and especially post-pandemic.

### Post-1975 quality-equivalent tier (Line B)

The quality-equivalent is a standard independent neighborhood barbershop at the $25–$40 range (per quality rubric), offering 20–30 minutes of service, razor neck cleanup, and finishing touches. This is NOT the premium tier ($50–$80+).

Key anchor points:
- **Early 2000s:** "Standard men's cut $12–$18" (BarbersTake.com industry survey).
- **Early 2010s:** "Low-to-mid $20s" (BarbersTake.com).
- **2025:** Standard independent barbershop national average $30–$35 (BarbersTake.com, quality rubric synthesis).

The barbershop renaissance (~2008–present) created a new premium tier above the old baseline, pushing the standard independent shop into a middle tier. This accelerated the gap between budget chains and standard barbershops, even though the core product (one haircut) didn't change.

### Divergence dynamics

Pre-1975: Single price for a single product. Line A = Line B.

1975–2000: Gradual divergence as budget chains scaled. The gap was modest — $1–$4 in nominal terms, 10–40% in percentage terms. Budget chains competed on speed and convenience, not dramatically lower prices.

2000–2025: Accelerating divergence. The barbershop renaissance pushed standard shop prices upward (more service scope, better ambiance, higher rents in revitalized neighborhoods), while budget chains held prices aggressively. By 2025, the gap is ~$13 (65% premium), almost entirely in service wrapper rather than cut quality.

## Known Gaps

- **1950–1965 prices:** Based on scattered anecdotal sources (library records, newspaper mentions, forum recollections) rather than systematic surveys. Confidence is medium at best.
- **1980–1995 prices:** The weakest part of the dataset. Few systematic price surveys exist for this era. Budget chain prices are interpolated from the 1975 Supercuts opening price and the 1985 Supercuts TV ad price. Standard barbershop prices are CPI-interpolated.
- **Regional variation:** All prices are rough national averages. In 2025, Great Clips ranges from ~$16 (Mississippi) to ~$28 (California). Standard barbershop ranges from $24 (Alabama) to $60+ (New York, Hawaii). A single national number hides substantial geographic variation.
- **Tipping:** All prices are pre-tip. 1955 tipping norms for barbers were typically 10–15%. Current norms are 15–25%. Tips effectively increase the "real" cost of a haircut more in 2025 than 1955, but this is not captured in the price data.
