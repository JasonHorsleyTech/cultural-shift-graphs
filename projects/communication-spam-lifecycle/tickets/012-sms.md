# 012: SMS / Text Messages

**System:** Short Message Service (SMS) and MMS
**Time range:** 2000s–present (US mainstream era)
**Expected data quality:** Good — CTIA reports, RoboKiller/Truecaller data, FCC complaints

## Research task

Trace SMS from clean personal communication to spam-laden. Text messaging had a remarkably long clean period in the US — partly because per-message fees acted as a natural spam barrier (like telegrams). Once unlimited texting plans became standard (~2008-2010) and mass-texting platforms dropped costs to near zero, spam texts exploded. By the 2020s, spam texts have overtaken spam calls as the primary phone-based nuisance.

## Key years to sample

- 2000 (SMS adoption growing, per-text fees ~$0.10-0.20)
- 2003 (texting becoming mainstream among younger users)
- 2005 (SMS surpassing voice calls for teens)
- 2008 (unlimited texting plans becoming standard)
- 2010 (peak SMS volume in US, iMessage/WhatsApp emerging)
- 2012 (text spam becoming noticeable — Pew surveys show growing complaints)
- 2015 (spam texts rising, OTT messaging apps growing)
- 2018 (political campaign texting explodes — peer-to-peer texting loophole)
- 2020 (COVID-era scam texts surge)
- 2022 (spam texts overtake robocalls as #1 nuisance — ~225 billion spam texts/year)
- 2025 (present state — RCS adoption, continued spam growth)

## Data sources to check

- **CTIA** — annual wireless survey (SMS volume, subscriber data)
- **RoboKiller** — spam text statistics and reports
- **Truecaller** — US Spam & Scam Report
- **FCC** — complaint data on unwanted texts
- **Pew Research** — mobile technology use surveys
- **Proofpoint** — mobile threat reports
- **FTC** — consumer complaint data
- **TCPA** and **TRACED Act** as they apply to texts

## Dimensions to collect

1. **Spam percentage** — % of texts that are spam/scam/unsolicited. RoboKiller and Truecaller publish estimates for recent years. Harder for pre-2010.
2. **Total message volume** — total SMS/MMS messages per year in US. CTIA publishes this.
3. **Primary users** — % of US population for whom texting is primary personal communication
4. **Total users** — % of US mobile subscribers who text regularly
5. **User cost per message** — per-text fees (early: $0.10-0.20) vs. unlimited plans ($0 marginal cost). Track the transition. Inflation-adjusted.
6. **User time per message** — seconds (short messages by design)
7. **Infrastructure cost per message** — carrier cost per SMS. SMS travels on signaling channels — nearly zero marginal cost. This is why carriers' per-text charges were one of the most profitable pricing schemes in telecom history.
8. **Platform ARPU** — carrier revenue per subscriber from messaging
9. **Barrier to reach** — phone-number
10. **Reach per message** — one-to-one (but mass texting platforms send to thousands)
11. **Gatekeeping** — open (anyone can text any mobile number)
12. **Years to spam saturation** — from unlimited texting (~2008) to majority spam (~2022?) = ~14 years. Or from first mainstream use (~2002) = ~20 years.
13. **Regulatory response** — TCPA (applies to texts), TRACED Act (2019), FCC text blocking rules
14. **Defenses** — carrier spam filters (relatively new), "Report Junk" feature (iOS), spam text blocking apps, short code verification

## Special notes

SMS perfectly illustrates the cost thesis. When texts cost $0.10 each, spam was economically nonviable. The moment unlimited plans made texts free to users AND mass-texting platforms made sending free to spammers, the system started dying. The transition happened in a very clear, dateable window. Also interesting: carriers charged $0.10-0.20 per text when the actual infrastructure cost was essentially zero — one of the most profitable products in telecom history. They were accidentally creating a spam barrier as a side effect of price gouging.
