# Newfoundland Cod Fishers

## Event Summary
- **Profession:** Cod fishers and fish plant processing workers
- **Country/Region:** Canada, Newfoundland and Labrador
- **Peak Year:** 1990
- **Peak Workforce:** ~40,000 (24,000 harvesters + 25,160 plant workers, with overlap in seasonal counting) (Heritage Newfoundland; Statistics Canada Labour Force Survey)
- **Primary Mechanism:** discrete_shock
- **Cause Tags:** resource, regulatory, environmental
- **Killing Event:** On July 2, 1992, Federal Fisheries Minister John Crosbie declared an indefinite moratorium on the Northern cod fishery after stocks collapsed to 1% of historic levels — the largest mass layoff in Canadian history.

## Decline Metrics
- **Decline Start Year:** 1992 (instantaneous — moratorium imposed July 2)
- **Years from Peak to 25% Decline:** 0 (immediate — 75% of cod workforce displaced on day one)
- **Years from Peak to 50% Decline:** 0 (immediate)
- **Years from Peak to 75% Decline:** 0 (immediate for cod-specific employment; total fishing employment including shellfish pivot: ~17 years to reach ~25,000 from ~40,000)
- **Years from Peak to 90% Decline:** N/A for total fishing; effectively instant for cod-specific employment (cod workforce went from ~30,000 to 0)
- **Decline Shape:** cliff (cod employment collapsed overnight; total fishing industry partially recovered via shellfish but at lower employment levels)
- **Current Status:** remnant (<10% peak) — The moratorium was lifted in 2024 after 32 years with a small 18,000-tonne quota. Cod-specific employment remains a tiny fraction of its former scale. Total fishing employment (~21,000 in 2010) is roughly half the 1990 peak, sustained by shellfish, not cod.

## Income Trajectory

### Data Quality: estimated

Income data for Newfoundland fishers is complicated by the seasonal nature of the work and heavy reliance on Employment Insurance (EI) as a structural part of fishing income. Pre-moratorium individual income data is sparse; post-moratorium data comes from Heritage Newfoundland and Statistics Canada Census.

- **Income at Peak:** ~$30,000–$35,000 CAD/year (estimated, 1990) — based on NCARP payment calculations derived from 1989–1991 average EI earnings of $225–$406/week (Heritage Newfoundland)
- **Income at Trough:** $0 market income, replaced by NCARP payments of $225–$406/week ($11,700–$21,100/year) in 1992–1994 (Heritage Newfoundland)
- **Income Recovery at 10 Years Post-Peak:** ~73% — Average fisher income in 2001 was $25,400 (including ~$10,000 EI), vs. estimated ~$35,000 peak. Plant workers earned $19,701 in 2001, significantly below the $32,000 provincial average (Heritage Newfoundland)
- **Inflation Adjustment Method:** Figures are nominal CAD; no consistent inflation adjustment applied due to sparse baseline data. Canadian CPI would be appropriate for adjustment.

## Surplus Capture

This is an unusual case — the "surplus" is ecological recovery rather than economic efficiency. The moratorium destroyed economic value to (attempt to) restore natural capital.

- **Capital owners:** 0% — Fishing companies like FPI (Fishery Products International) saw their cod assets evaporate. Some pivoted to shellfish processing.
- **Consumers:** 10% — Cod was replaced by imported fish at slightly higher prices. Consumers of crab and shrimp benefited from increased NL shellfish supply.
- **Adjacent workers:** 25% — Shellfish fishers who were already in the industry or pivoted early captured significant income from the crab/shrimp boom. Crab landing values went from $13M (1990) to $229M (2002).
- **Government/public:** 65% — The primary "beneficiary" is the ecological commons. The government spent $3 billion on adjustment programs. The cod stock, while still not fully recovered, was preserved from total extinction. The moratorium served as a global cautionary tale that influenced fisheries management worldwide.

## Foresight Stories

### Adapters
1. **Fogo Island Co-operative Society** — Established in 1967, the co-op pivoted rapidly to snow crab, shrimp, and turbot processing after the moratorium, building dedicated plants for each species. Fogo Island later became a globally recognized tourism destination through Zita Cobb's Shorefast Foundation (founded 2003), which built the luxury Fogo Island Inn and created 400+ year-round jobs by 2020, stabilizing the population at ~2,300.
2. **Early shellfish fishers** — Fishers who retooled their boats for crab and shrimp in 1992–1994 caught the leading edge of the shellfish boom. By 1995, snow crab alone was worth $181M — more than cod had ever been worth. The total landed value of NL fisheries doubled from $277M (1990) to $517M (2002).

### False Movers
1. **TAGS retraining participants who stayed in rural NL** — Thousands enrolled in government-funded training programs for new occupations but couldn't find jobs in their remote communities. Training without geographic mobility proved largely futile; the programs "did not adequately prepare them for work in other fields, nor did they significantly reduce the number of people dependent on the fishery" (Heritage Newfoundland).
2. **Fishers who waited for the moratorium to lift** — The moratorium was announced as a two-year measure. Many fishers declined retraining or relocation, expecting to return to cod fishing. The moratorium lasted 32 years.

### Deniers
1. **DFO scientists and management (pre-moratorium):** A 1989 internal review revealed that DFO had "overestimated cod populations by as much as 100 per cent" during the 1980s. An internal 1992 report charged that "scientific information surrounding the northern cod moratorium, specifically the role of the environment, was gruesomely mangled and corrupted to meet political ends" and that management was fostering "an attitude of scientific deception, misinformation and obfuscation." Inshore fishers reported declining catch sizes for years, but DFO ignored this ground-level intelligence in favor of offshore trawler data.
2. **Fishing industry leaders** who lobbied against the moratorium and pushed for continued high quotas even as stocks collapsed. In 1986, scientists recommended cutting the total allowable catch in half — the recommendation was ignored for socioeconomic reasons.

### Survival Pattern
Survivors were those who either moved geographically (to Alberta, Ontario) or moved within the fishery to shellfish species — mobility, whether occupational or physical, was the common thread.

## Unique Texture

1. The cod moratorium was announced as a two-year measure. It lasted 32 years, finally being lifted in 2024 — and even then with a cautious 18,000-tonne quota, a fraction of the 810,000-tonne peak catch in 1968. (Britannica; CBC News)
2. Newfoundland's provincial population dropped from 568,475 (1991) to 512,930 (2001) — a 10% decline, with rural areas losing 18% (from 264,023 to 216,734). Some outport communities lost 30–50% of their residents. (Statistics Canada Census; Heritage Newfoundland)
3. The total value of NL fish landings actually doubled within a decade of the moratorium — from $277M in 1990 to $517M in 2002 — but it was crab and shrimp, not cod. The industry recovered economically while the workers and communities did not. (Heritage Newfoundland)
4. Federal Fisheries Minister John Crosbie, when confronted by angry fishers at Bay Bulls on July 1, 1992, shouted back: "I didn't take the fish from the goddamn water!" — a line that became iconic shorthand for political helplessness in the face of ecological collapse. (Canadian Geographic; CBC News)
5. The government spent over $3 billion on NCARP and TAGS adjustment programs between 1992 and 1998, yet these programs were widely judged a failure — they "did not adequately prepare [fishing people] for work in other fields, nor did they significantly reduce the number of people dependent on the fishery." (Heritage Newfoundland)

## Known Data Gaps

- **Pre-moratorium individual fisher income:** No consistent annual income data for individual fishers before 1992. The $30,000–$35,000 estimate is back-calculated from EI base earnings used for NCARP payments — this is a proxy, not a direct measurement. Fishing income was highly seasonal and variable.
- **Alberta migration specifics:** While the "Newfie goes West" pattern is well-documented culturally, precise counts of how many displaced cod workers specifically went to Alberta oil sands vs. other Alberta industries are not available. Interprovincial migration data exists at the provincial level but doesn't break down by former occupation.
- **Women plant workers outcomes:** An estimated 15,000 women were directly employed in the fishery pre-moratorium, and ~10,000 were eligible for compensation. Their outcomes are widely reported as worse than male fishers', but systematic tracking data is thin.
- **TAGS retraining completion rates:** The programs are described as having "limited success" and low completion rates in multiple sources, but specific completion percentages were not found in available sources.
- **Annual fishing employment 1993–1994:** The transition years between moratorium and shellfish boom have patchy employment data; most sources jump from 1992 to 1995 or later.

## Confidence Assessment
- **Overall:** medium-high
- **Workforce numbers:** medium-high — Multiple consistent sources (Heritage NF, Statistics Canada, Britannica) agree on the ~30,000–40,000 displaced figure, though exact counts vary by definition (direct vs. indirect, seasonal vs. annual)
- **Income data:** medium — Pre-moratorium income is estimated from EI calculations rather than directly measured; post-moratorium data is available but conflates market income with transfer payments
- **Where-they-went:** medium — Outmigration is well-documented at the provincial level (~60,000 left in a decade); specific occupational destinations are inferred from concurrent industry growth rather than individual-level tracking
