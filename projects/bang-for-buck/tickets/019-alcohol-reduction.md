# Ticket 019: Alcohol Reduction (Brief Interventions) — QALY Evidence and Cost

## Objective

Research the cost-effectiveness of alcohol reduction through brief interventions for Bob's profile. Heavy drinking rates are elevated in lower-income brackets, and brief alcohol interventions are among the most cost-effective preventive measures known.

## What to research

### QALY Evidence
1. **Brief alcohol intervention CEA** — a 5-15 minute screening + counseling session in primary care. This is the studied intervention, not "willpower."
2. **QALY gain** — typically modest per person but extraordinarily cheap
3. **For heavy/hazardous drinkers specifically** — not all Bobs drink heavily, but the intervention targets those who do
4. **Outcomes:** liver disease prevention, accident reduction, violence reduction, cardiovascular effects (moderate drinking is debated as protective — what does current evidence say?)
5. **AUDIT/CAGE screening tools** — the intervention is triggered by screening, which is cheap

### Cost Analysis
1. **Brief intervention cost** — typically part of a routine doctor visit. Incremental cost: $5-20 per encounter.
2. **Screening cost** — AUDIT questionnaire takes 2 minutes. Near-zero marginal cost.
3. **For Bob:** the intervention is essentially free if it happens during any doctor visit
4. **Intensive programs (if needed)** — AA (free), outpatient counseling ($50-200/session), residential treatment ($5,000-30,000)
5. **Focus on the brief intervention** as the primary data point — it's the one with clean CEA data and the lowest cost

### Access and Barriers
1. **Requires a doctor visit** — which Bob may not have regularly
2. **Stigma** — alcohol screening can feel judgmental
3. **Motivation** — brief interventions work through motivational interviewing, not coercion
4. **Cultural factors** — drinking norms in Bob's social circle

## Where to look

1. **Tufts CEA Registry** — "alcohol brief intervention" or "alcohol screening"
2. **USPSTF** — alcohol screening recommendation and CEA
3. **NICE Guidelines** — alcohol use disorders, brief interventions
4. **Cochrane Reviews** — "Brief interventions for heavy alcohol users"
5. **WHO** — AUDIT tool, brief intervention evidence base
6. **Key studies:**
   - Solberg et al. — cost-effectiveness of alcohol screening and brief intervention
   - SAMHSA SBIRT (Screening, Brief Intervention, Referral to Treatment) data

## Output format

Use the full intervention result format from plan.md. Note that this applies primarily to the subset of Bobs with hazardous drinking patterns.

## What "good data" looks like

- QALY estimates for brief intervention specifically (not residential treatment or AA)
- Cost that reflects the near-zero marginal cost of adding screening to an existing visit
- This should be one of the cheapest interventions with surprisingly decent QALY returns
