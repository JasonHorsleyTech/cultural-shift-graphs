# State College — Quality Rubric

## The 1960 baseline product

A year at a state flagship public university circa 1960. Tuition was nominal ($200–$400/year at most state schools). The typical student attended a large, rapidly expanding campus — enrollment at state universities surged 45% between 1945–1960 and would double again by 1970. The experience was utilitarian: students attended large lectures (often standing-room-only during the enrollment boom), lived in spartan dormitories (communal bathrooms, bunk-bed-packed rooms), and had access to a physical card-catalog library. Career services were minimal or nonexistent — you graduated and found a job through personal networks or newspaper classifieds. Mental health services existed at ~2,000 schools by 1960 but were rudimentary. The student body was overwhelmingly white (~94%) and predominantly male (~64%). Most courses were taught by full-time, tenure-track faculty. Graduation rates aren't directly comparable (the 6-year tracking metric didn't exist until the 1990s), but degree attainment was low — only 7.7% of Americans held a bachelor's in 1960.

Brand examples: University of Michigan, UC Berkeley, University of Wisconsin-Madison, Ohio State — the flagships that were already large (10,000–25,000 students) by 1960.

## Quality-equivalent product definition

This is where the college rubric breaks from the pattern of other commodities. **There is no clean "quality-equivalent" product** because 2025 state college is better on 9 of 11 scored attributes. The quality degradation is concentrated in two dimensions:

1. **Faculty composition** — the shift from tenure-track to contingent faculty is real and significant. A student today is much more likely to be taught by an adjunct with no office hours, no research program, and no long-term investment in the department. This degrades the mentorship and intellectual-community dimensions of education.

2. **Administrative overhead** — a larger share of each tuition dollar goes to administration, compliance, student services infrastructure, and amenities rather than instruction. This is a cost-efficiency degradation, not a student-experience degradation (students benefit from many of those services).

**If forced to define a quality-equivalent:** The closest 2025 match to the 1960 faculty-attention experience would be an honors college at a state flagship (smaller classes, more tenured faculty) or a mid-tier private liberal arts college. These run $15,000–$25,000/year (in-state honors program with room/board) to $40,000–$55,000/year (private liberal arts). But this comparison is misleading — it captures the ONE dimension where 1960 was better while ignoring the nine dimensions where 2025 is superior.

**Recommended approach for the graph:** Rather than pricing a "quality-equivalent" product (which would be dishonest), use a single price line (in-state tuition at public 4-year universities) and annotate the two degraded dimensions. The story for college is **not** quality degradation — it's cost explosion despite quality improvement. This makes college the most interesting case in the project: the gap between Line A and Line B is near zero (or arguably negative — 2025 is better), yet the cost has exploded anyway. The quality tax framing doesn't apply; this is pure Baumol's cost disease plus state funding withdrawal plus administrative bloat plus amenities competition.

## Divergence timeline

**There is no meaningful quality-tier divergence for public college.** Unlike eggs or suits, you can't buy a "cheap" and "premium" version of state college tuition at the same institution. The product is the product.

What DID happen:
- **1965–1980:** The "golden age" of affordable state college. Massive state subsidies kept tuition low. GI Bill and baby boom drove enrollment. Faculty were mostly tenure-track.
- **1980–2000:** State funding began declining as a share of university budgets. Tuition started rising faster than inflation. Adjunct hiring accelerated. The modern "amenities arms race" began.
- **2000–2020:** State funding cuts accelerated (especially post-2008 recession). Tuition growth outpaced wages dramatically. Administrative positions proliferated. Contingent faculty became the majority.

The structural break isn't a quality-tier split — it's a funding-model shift from state subsidy to student tuition revenue, compounded by administrative growth and amenity spending.

## Overall assessment

**Mixed — but primarily a cost story, not a quality story.**

On a straight attribute count: 2025 state college is **better** on 9 of 11 dimensions (course diversity, lab facilities, library resources, career services, campus facilities, student diversity, graduation rates, mental health services, and student-faculty ratio is roughly stable). It is **worse** on 2 dimensions (tenure-track faculty share, administrative overhead).

The honest conclusion: **The modern product is genuinely superior on most measurable dimensions, yet costs dramatically more.** This makes college a poor fit for the "hidden quality tax" framing and an excellent fit for "cost explosion despite quality gains." The graph should present it as the counterpoint case — proof that the quality-tax narrative doesn't explain all cost increases. Sometimes things just get more expensive even as they get better.

The 2 degraded dimensions (adjunct-ification and admin bloat) are real problems that deserve annotation, but they don't dominate the overall quality picture. A 2025 student gets more diverse course offerings, better research facilities, vastly superior information access, real career services, better campus amenities, a more diverse peer group, and better support services — taught by a less stable, less invested faculty, inside a more bureaucratic institution.

## Confidence level

**High** for the direction of each attribute (improved/degraded/same). The directional assessments are well-supported by NCES, AAUP, and other federal data sources.

**Medium** for specific numbers (e.g., exact faculty composition percentages in 1960, pre-1980 graduation rates). NCES data on faculty composition starts reliably in 1969–1975; earlier figures are estimates. Graduation rates weren't formally tracked until the 1990s.

**High** for the overall assessment that college is primarily a cost story, not a quality-degradation story. This conclusion is robust across data sources and holds even if individual attribute scores shift.
