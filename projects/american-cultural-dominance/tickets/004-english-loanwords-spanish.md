# English Loanwords in Spanish

Spanish is spoken across 20+ countries with wildly different levels of American cultural exposure. Latin American Spanish has absorbed far more English than Peninsular Spanish, and proximity to the US matters — Mexican Spanish has more anglicisms than Argentine Spanish. This geographic gradient is itself a measure of cultural pressure.

**Metric:** Number of new English-origin loanwords entering standard Spanish dictionaries per decade (Real Academia Española / Diccionario de la lengua española).

**Data proxy:** RAE dictionary editions, which explicitly mark anglicisms. Academic studies on anglicisms in Latin American vs. Peninsular Spanish.

**Interesting angles:**
- 19th century: early anglicisms entered through commerce and technology (club, bar, fútbol).
- Post-WW2: American influence entered Latin America through film, music, and consumer brands.
- US-Mexico border creates a unique contact zone — "Spanglish" is a living measure of integration.
- RAE periodically accepts anglicisms it previously rejected — the lag between street usage and official acceptance is data.
- Compare Mexico (direct US border) vs. Spain (ocean away) vs. Argentina (even further) — the gradient should map to cultural proximity.
- Internet era: Latin American tech vocabulary is heavily English-derived even when Spanish alternatives exist.
