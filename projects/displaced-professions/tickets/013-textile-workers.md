# Ticket 013: US Textile Mill Workers

## Event
The slow decline of US textile manufacturing employment as production moved offshore, ~1970s–2000s. A classic gradual erosion driven by globalization and reinforced by automation of remaining domestic operations.

## Mechanism
**Gradual erosion** (globalization, tech_tool)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track US textile mill employment from ~1960 to present:

- Peak: ~1.3 million in textile + apparel manufacturing combined (BLS, early 1970s). Distinguish textiles (mills) from apparel (cut-and-sew) if possible.
- Key dates: Multi-Fibre Arrangement begins (1974), NAFTA (1994), China joins WTO (2001), MFA expires (2005)
- Current: ~100,000–120,000 in textile mills (NAICS 313/314), ~80,000 in apparel (NAICS 315) — verify
- Geographic concentration: Carolinas, Georgia, New England

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS Current Employment Statistics (CES), NAICS 313 (Textile Mills), 314 (Textile Product Mills), 315 (Apparel Manufacturing)
2. Census Bureau Annual Survey of Manufactures
3. US International Trade Commission reports on textile/apparel imports
4. Cline, "The Future of World Trade in Textiles and Apparel" (1990)
5. Collins, "Threads: Gender, Labor, and Power in the Global Apparel Industry" (2003)

### Income Data
- BLS median wages for textile workers over time
- Compare to manufacturing average — were textile wages below average? (yes — one of the lowest-wage manufacturing sectors)
- Track real wages from 1970–2020

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS OES and CES wage data for textile/apparel manufacturing
2. CPI-U for inflation adjustment
3. Census Bureau Annual Survey of Manufactures (production worker wages)

### Where They Went
- Other manufacturing (if available locally)
- Retail / service sector (often at lower wages)
- Unemployment / disability / retirement
- Geographic: many textile towns had no alternative employers. "Mill town" to "ghost town" dynamic.
- Key geographies: Fall River MA, Kannapolis NC, Greenville SC, Dalton GA (carpet capital)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS Local Area Unemployment Statistics for textile regions
2. Census Bureau County Business Patterns for textile-heavy counties
3. Academic studies on deindustrialization in the South and New England
4. Minchin, "Empty Mills: The Fight Against Imports and the Decline of the U.S. Textile Industry" (2013)

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Companies that moved upmarket (technical textiles, performance fabrics). Workers who retrained for healthcare or skilled trades.
- **False movers:** Companies that invested heavily in automation expecting to compete with Asian labor costs. Workers who relocated to follow mills that eventually closed anyway.
- **Deniers:** The decades-long fight for tariff protection. Industry lobbying against NAFTA and WTO accession for China.
- **Survival pattern:** Geographic mobility and/or retraining into non-manufacturing sectors.

### Unique Texture
- The Carolina textile corridor — hundreds of mill towns where the mill was the only employer
- The racial dynamics: Southern textile mills were historically white; integration happened as the industry was already declining
- Kannapolis, NC: Cannon Mills/Pillowtex employed ~7,500 → closed 2003 → largest single layoff in NC history at the time
- The irony that US cotton production remained strong but manufacturing moved offshore
- Trade Adjustment Assistance (TAA) as a policy response — did it work?

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Annual employment in textiles + apparel from 1970–2020
- Import penetration rates over time (% of domestic consumption from imports)
- Wage data relative to manufacturing average
- At least 2 mill town case studies (population, employment, economic indicators)
- Trade policy timeline (MFA, NAFTA, WTO) mapped against employment trajectory
