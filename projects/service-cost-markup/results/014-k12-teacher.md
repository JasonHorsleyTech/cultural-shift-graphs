# K-12 Public Education — Public School Teacher

## Summary

The K-12 teacher's share of per-pupil spending has nearly halved over six decades — from 44% in 1960 to 26% in 2020. The ratio was rock-stable through the 1960s, then began a steep, sustained decline starting in the 1970s that has never reversed. Per-pupil spending has grown relentlessly (driven by special education mandates, administrative expansion, benefits/pension costs, and facility spending), but almost none of that growth has reached teacher paychecks. The 1980s saw a brief partial recovery after the "Nation at Risk" alarm, but the long-term trend resumed immediately.

## Methodology

**Ratio formula:** Teacher Salary / (Per-Pupil Total Expenditure × Pupil-Teacher Ratio)

This captures the classroom teacher's salary as a share of total education spending attributable to one teacher's worth of students. The denominator represents the total cost of educating one classroom — everything from the teacher's salary and benefits to administration, facilities, buses, lunch programs, special education aides, counselors, and technology.

**Worker compensation:** Estimated average annual salary of teachers in public elementary and secondary schools (all teachers combined — not separated by elementary vs. secondary). This is salary only, excluding benefits. NCES Table 211.50, reported in current (nominal) dollars.

**Customer cost proxy:** Total expenditure per pupil in fall enrollment in public elementary and secondary schools. This is the broadest measure — it includes current expenditures (instruction, support services, non-instruction) plus capital outlay and interest on school debt. NCES Table 236.55, reported in unadjusted dollars.

**Customers per worker:** Public school pupil-teacher ratio. NCES Table 208.20. The PTR counts all teachers (including special education, ESL, etc.), not just classroom teachers, which makes it a "number of teachers hired per student" rather than "class size." This is appropriate because the salary figure is also an average across all teacher types.

**Year alignment:** Salary and per-pupil data are reported by school year (e.g., "1979-80"). PTR is reported by fall year (e.g., "fall 1979" = start of the 1979-80 school year). For the 1959-60 and 1969-70 school years, the closest available PTR data is from fall 1960 and fall 1970 respectively (one year offset); from 1975 onward, annual PTR data allows exact alignment.

**Why total expenditure rather than current expenditure?** Total expenditure includes capital outlay (buildings, equipment), which is part of what taxpayers fund. Using current expenditure alone would exclude ~15% of per-pupil costs and slightly overstate the teacher's share. Total expenditure gives the most honest picture of "what the system costs per student."

**Why salary rather than total compensation?** Benefits data (health insurance, pension contributions) is not consistently available in the NCES salary tables across the full 1960–2021 range. Using salary alone understates the teacher's true share, since benefits have grown faster than salary (from ~21% to ~24% of current expenditures between 2011 and 2021). This means the ratio decline is real but slightly less dramatic when total compensation is considered.

## Notes

- **The 1960s stability is striking.** The ratio held at exactly 44% across the entire decade. Teacher salaries and per-pupil spending grew in near-lockstep, and class sizes were still large (25.8 → 22.3), meaning most spending went directly to instruction.

- **The 1970s were the inflection.** The ratio dropped 7.5 percentage points in a single decade (44.0% → 36.5%). Three forces converged: (1) the Education for All Handicapped Children Act of 1975 (later IDEA) created unfunded mandates for special education services, (2) class sizes dropped sharply (22.3 → 19.1), meaning schools hired more teachers per student without proportionally increasing individual salaries, and (3) inflation outpaced teacher salary growth.

- **The 1980s "Nation at Risk" blip.** The ratio actually ticked UP from 36.5% (1980) to 37.7% (1985) — the only reversal in the entire dataset. The 1983 "Nation at Risk" report created a national panic about education quality and prompted salary increases. But the effect was temporary; by 1990 the ratio had fallen back to 35.3%.

- **Special education spending is a massive confound.** In 1999-2000, the average expenditure per special education student was $12,474 vs. $6,556 for general education students — nearly double. Special education enrollment has grown from ~8% to ~15% of students. This spending shows up in per-pupil totals but employs specialized staff (aides, therapists, counselors) rather than general classroom teachers.

- **Administrative and non-teaching staff growth.** Between 2002 and 2020, total public school staff grew 13.2% while enrollment grew only 6.6%. Non-teaching staff grew ~20%. This means more of each education dollar goes to counselors, data specialists, compliance officers, technology coordinators, and administrators — none of whom are captured in the "teacher salary" numerator.

- **Benefits are eating salary budgets.** The share of current expenditures going to staff salaries dropped from 59% to 55% between 2011 and 2021, while benefits (especially pension contributions) rose from 21% to 24%. Between 2002 and 2020, benefit spending per student grew by $1,745 compared to only $573 for salary spending per student. Teachers aren't getting poorer — their employers are spending more per teacher, just not on salary.

- **The PTR paradox.** Class sizes dropped 40% over 60 years (25.8 → 15.4), which means society hired far more teachers per student. In theory, this should have RAISED the ratio (more teacher-salary spending per student). Instead, the ratio collapsed because non-teacher spending grew even faster than the combined effect of more teachers and higher individual salaries.

- **Comparison to college professor ticket.** This ticket is a natural companion to the college professor research. Both show declining worker ratios, but the mechanisms differ: college costs are driven by tuition increases and administrative bloat in a market system, while K-12 costs are driven by mandates, pension obligations, and staffing growth in a tax-funded system. The K-12 decline (44% → 26%) is comparable in magnitude to what the college data likely shows.

- **State variation is enormous** but not modeled here. In 2021-22, average teacher salary ranged from ~$36,000 (Mississippi) to ~$92,000 (New York), while per-pupil spending ranged from ~$8,000 (Utah) to ~$28,000 (New York). The ratio likely varies from ~20% to ~40% depending on state. A future deep-dive could chart this.
