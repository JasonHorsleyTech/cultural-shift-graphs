# US Dollar as Global Reserve Currency

## Summary
The US dollar's share of global foreign exchange reserves traces one of the clearest arcs of American structural dominance. From essentially zero in 1900 (when sterling and gold ruled), the dollar rose to overtake sterling in the 1920s, was formalized as the anchor currency at Bretton Woods in 1944, peaked at ~85% of forex reserves in the early 1970s, crashed to 47% by 1990 as the yen and Deutsche mark gained ground, rebounded to 71% by 1999, and has since gradually eroded to ~57% as central banks diversify into nontraditional currencies. Even at its modern "low," the dollar remains overwhelmingly dominant — the euro, its nearest competitor, holds only ~20%.

## Penetration Score Definition
**Denominator:** US dollar's share of total allocated global foreign exchange reserves (excluding gold reserves). 100% means every central bank's foreign currency holdings are denominated in US dollars.
**0% anchor:** No central bank holds any dollar-denominated reserves. The dollar is not used in international finance.
**100% anchor:** Every central bank's entire foreign exchange portfolio is in US dollars. Complete monetary hegemony.

## Methodology
The penetration score equals the dollar's share of global allocated foreign exchange reserves, which is a natural 0-100% metric requiring no normalization.

**1965-2025:** Data from the IMF's Currency Composition of Official Foreign Exchange Reserves (COFER) database, as compiled in the Wikipedia "Reserve currency" article which sources IMF annual reports and COFER quarterly releases. This is the gold standard (no pun intended) for reserve composition data. From 1999 onward, quarterly data is available; from 1965-1998, annual or periodic snapshots.

**1945-1960:** Estimates based on IMF annual reports, Triffin's estimates, and contextual data about sterling's measured decline from ~55% in the early 1950s. Lower confidence — systematic forex reserve composition data was not tracked in the modern sense.

**1900-1940:** Based primarily on Eichengreen and Flandreau (2009) "The Rise and Fall of the Dollar, or When Did the Dollar Replace Sterling as the Leading Reserve Currency?" which reconstructed reserve composition from central bank balance sheets in the 1920s-1930s. Pre-1920 estimates are extrapolations from the dollar's negligible international role before the Federal Reserve's creation (1913). Confidence is low for this period.

**Important methodological note:** The denominator is *foreign exchange* reserves (currency holdings), not *total* reserves (which include gold). Before WWII, gold dominated reserves and forex was a small share of total holdings. This means the 1970s-era 85% figure represents 85% of the forex component, not 85% of all reserves. This is consistent with how IMF COFER data is reported and is the standard metric used in all modern reserve currency analysis.

**Valuation effects:** Some of the apparent decline in dollar share (especially post-2020) reflects the dollar weakening against other currencies, which mechanically reduces its share when measured at market exchange rates. The IMF notes that adjusted for exchange rate changes, the decline is less dramatic than raw figures suggest.

## Notes
- **The 1970s peak is misleading without context.** The 85% share in 1970-1975 occurred during Bretton Woods and its immediate aftermath, when most currencies were pegged to the dollar by treaty. This was structural lock-in, not free-market preference. The subsequent drop to 47% by 1990 partially reflects the *normalization* after other currencies became freely tradeable alternatives.
- **The petrodollar system (1974)** — Saudi Arabia's agreement to price oil exclusively in dollars — is often cited as propping up dollar demand. While important, the data shows dollar share actually *fell* from 85% to 58% between 1975 and 1980, suggesting the petrodollar system slowed rather than prevented the post-Bretton Woods decline.
- **The euro's limited impact.** Launched in 1999, the euro was widely predicted to challenge dollar dominance. It peaked at ~28% of reserves around 2009 and has since settled at ~20%. It absorbed the reserve shares previously held by the Deutsche mark, French franc, and Dutch guilder rather than taking share from the dollar.
- **"Stealth erosion" (Eichengreen's term):** The dollar's post-2000 decline isn't flowing to any single rival but to a basket of nontraditional currencies — AUD, CAD, CNY, SEK, KRW. This fragmentation means no single currency threatens the dollar's dominance, but the dollar's share keeps slowly falling.
- **China's renminbi:** Despite China's push for internationalization and the RMB's inclusion in the IMF's SDR basket (2016), it holds only ~2% of global reserves as of 2025 — a rounding error compared to the dollar. Capital controls remain the binding constraint.
- **This metric directly maps to American structural power.** When your currency is the world's reserve, you can run persistent trade deficits, borrow cheaply, and impose financial sanctions with global reach. The dollar's reserve status is both a measure of and a mechanism for American dominance — a self-reinforcing loop.
