# Film Screen Quotas and Import Restrictions

Countries passing laws to restrict Hollywood imports is itself evidence of cultural pressure — you don't legislate against something that isn't a threat. Track every country that has implemented screen quotas, import quotas, or subsidy systems specifically to protect domestic cinema from American dominance.

**Metric:** Number of countries with active film screen quotas or import restrictions on foreign (primarily US) films, by decade. Secondary: timeline of when each major country enacted and/or repealed quotas.

**Data proxy:** UNESCO cultural policy databases, WTO trade dispute records (US has filed multiple complaints against screen quotas), national film institute policy archives, GATT/WTO "cultural exception" negotiations.

**Interesting angles:**
- UK Cinematograph Films Act (1927): one of the first screen quotas, specifically targeting Hollywood dominance.
- France: screen quotas since 1946, continuously maintained. The "exception culturelle" is France's core position in trade negotiations.
- South Korea: 146-day screen quota reduced to 73 days (2006) under US-Korea FTA pressure. Track domestic market share before and after.
- China: 20 foreign films/year quota (1994), raised to 34 (2012). The US has pushed for expansion in every trade negotiation.
- "Cultural exception" at GATT Uruguay Round (1993): EU refused to include audiovisual services in free trade. The negotiation itself is a data point.
- Countries that HAD quotas and dropped them under trade pressure — what happened to their domestic film industries?
- Count of countries with active quotas over time: is it growing or shrinking?
