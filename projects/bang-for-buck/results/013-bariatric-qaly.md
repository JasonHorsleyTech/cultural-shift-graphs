# Result: Bariatric Surgery — QALY Evidence and Long-Term Outcomes

## Summary

Bariatric surgery (primarily Roux-en-Y gastric bypass and sleeve gastrectomy) produces substantial, durable QALY gains — approximately 1.9–2.1 discounted QALYs over a lifetime horizon, or roughly 0.08 QALYs per year annualized over 20 years. Benefits are front-loaded (years 1–5 show the largest gains) and diminish somewhat with weight regain but remain significant at 10–20 year follow-up. The SOS study showed 3.0 additional years of life expectancy. Surgical mortality is low (0.08% for primary procedures) but post-operative mental health risks (elevated suicide rate, substance use disorders) and nutritional complications reduce the net QALY gain. Evidence quality is high — this is one of the most studied surgical interventions in cost-effectiveness literature.

## QALY Evidence

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.08
- **Range:** 0.05 – 0.12 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** Borisenko et al. 2017 (UK EHR-based CEA, lifetime horizon); Patel et al. 2022 (US, T2D population, 5-year horizon)
- **Study type:** Multiple cost-effectiveness analyses and long-term cohort studies
- **Population studied:** Adults with BMI ≥35–40, mean age 46–55, mix of comorbidities
- **Population match to Bob:** Good — studies include BMI 35–45 populations with comorbidities common in lower-income brackets
- **Key caveats:**
  - Lifetime QALY of ~2.1 discounted at 3.5% over ~35 remaining years = ~0.06/year average
  - 5-year data shows 0.088/year (RYGB) to 0.062/year (sleeve) in T2D patients — higher due to diabetes remission benefits
  - Benefits are front-loaded: maximum weight loss years 1–3, some regain by year 5–10
  - Mental health risks (suicide, substance use) partially offset gains but are not always captured in CEA models

### Procedure-specific QALY data

| Procedure | 5-year incremental QALYs (vs no surgery) | Lifetime incremental QALYs | Source |
|-----------|------------------------------------------|---------------------------|--------|
| Roux-en-Y Gastric Bypass (RYGB) | 0.44 (0.21–0.86) | 1.9 (17.07 vs 15.17) | Patel 2022; Wang 2013 |
| Sleeve Gastrectomy (SG) | 0.31 (0.13–0.66) | ~1.4 (16.56 vs 15.17) | Patel 2022; Wang 2013 |
| Mixed (bypass/sleeve/banding) | — | 2.14 | Borisenko 2017 |

### Long-term outcomes (SOS Study, 20+ year follow-up)

- Mean weight change: -23% at 2 years, -17% at 10 years, -18% at 20 years (surgery) vs ~0% (control)
- Adjusted median life expectancy: +3.0 years (95% CI 1.8–4.2) vs controls
- Reduced incidence of: diabetes, MI, stroke, cancer
- But: 5.5 years shorter life expectancy than general (non-obese) population

### Annualized QALY calculation rationale

Using RYGB as the primary procedure (most effective, most data):
- **Conservative (lifetime):** 1.9 QALYs ÷ 30 years remaining = 0.063/year
- **Moderate (20-year horizon):** Benefits front-loaded, so 20-year average ≈ 0.08/year
- **Aggressive (5-year horizon):** 0.44 ÷ 5 = 0.088/year (T2D population, peak benefit window)
- **Best estimate for Bob:** 0.08/year — reflects a 20-year horizon with some weight regain factored in

## Access and Barriers

- **Activation energy:** High
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - Most insurers require 6–12 months of documented supervised weight loss before approval
  - Without insurance: $20,000–$35,000 out of pocket is prohibitive at $30k income
  - Many programs require psychological evaluation, nutritional counseling, and documented comorbidities
  - Geographic access: bariatric surgery centers concentrated in metro areas
  - Post-operative care requires ongoing access to medical follow-up, supplements, and dietary compliance
  - Time off work for surgery (2–4 weeks recovery)

## Side Effects and Risks

- **30-day mortality:** 0.08% primary procedures (RYGB: 0.07–0.2%; SG: 0.03–0.08%)
- **Major complications at 5 years:** RYGB 29%, SG 22% (cumulative, includes minor)
- **Nutritional deficiencies:** Iron, B12, calcium, vitamin D — lifelong supplementation required
- **Dumping syndrome:** 20–50% of RYGB patients (nausea, cramping after sugary/fatty foods)
- **Surgical revision rate:** SG 2.91% at 5 years; RYGB 1.46% at 5 years
- **Mental health risks:**
  - Suicide rate: 19.4 per 100,000 patient-years (elevated vs general population)
  - Increased alcohol use disorder: 8–23% develop problematic alcohol use post-surgery
  - Psychiatric illness presentations 2–3x higher post-surgery vs pre-surgery
- **GERD:** Increased with sleeve gastrectomy; reduced with RYGB

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate:** 0.62 (follow-up appointment attendance); dietary guideline adherence ~48%
- **Source:** PMC11679841 (2024 systematic review); SOARD systematic review 2018
- **Notes:** The anatomical change is permanent (100% "adherence" to the surgery itself), but post-operative behavioral compliance — dietary guidelines, supplements, follow-up visits, exercise — is where patients fail. 52% report poor dietary adherence. Weight regain affects ~50% of patients to some degree (though complete regain is rare at 3–4%). Employment status correlates with follow-up adherence (65.6% employed vs 50% unemployed — relevant for Bob).

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 1
- **Tier 1** = Multiple RCTs or high-quality meta-analyses, Bob-adjacent populations
- **Key studies:**
  - Swedish Obese Subjects (SOS) Study — 20+ year prospective controlled trial, 2,010 surgical vs 2,037 control (Sjöström et al., NEJM 2007; Carlsson et al. 2020)
  - Borisenko et al. 2017 — UK lifetime CEA using electronic health records (n=3,882 surgical, 3,882 matched controls)
  - Patel et al. 2022 — US cost-effectiveness of SG vs RYGB vs medical therapy in T2D (JAMA Network Open)
  - Wang et al. 2013 — Lifetime CEA comparing RYGB, LAGB (Obesity Surgery)
  - LABS Consortium — Multi-center US longitudinal bariatric outcomes study
- **Limitations:**
  - Most CEA models extrapolate 3–5 years of clinical data to lifetime — true 20+ year QALY measurement is only from SOS (which used older procedures)
  - Mental health QALY decrements often not fully captured in models
  - Population mismatch: most studies include patients with insurance (required for surgery); Bob's uninsured access scenario is poorly studied
  - Interaction between low income and post-operative adherence likely reduces real-world effectiveness for Bob's bracket

## Confidence Level

**High.** Bariatric surgery is one of the most thoroughly studied interventions in CEA literature. The QALY evidence is robust with multiple meta-analyses, long-term cohort data (SOS at 20+ years), and well-validated economic models. The main uncertainty is in annualizing lifetime gains (benefits are non-linear over time) and in applicability to Bob's specific demographic (uninsured, lower-income, potentially lower post-operative adherence). The 0.05–0.12 range captures this uncertainty adequately.
