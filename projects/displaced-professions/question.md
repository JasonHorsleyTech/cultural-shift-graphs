# What happens when a profession dies?

**Core question:** Across all of history, when an industry or profession collapses — whether from technology, cultural shift, political upheaval, resource depletion, or regulation — how fast does it happen, who survives, and where does everyone else go?

## Why this matters
Personal AI anxiety as an engineer. The question behind the question: "what's about to happen to me, and what does history say about how it goes?"

## The angle
This isn't just "technology kills jobs." There are at least 9 distinct types of profession collapse, and the *type* may predict the *shape* of the displacement:

1. **Technological displacement** — a better tool (power loom, automobile, internet)
2. **Cultural shift** — values change, customer base evaporates (church organists → secularization)
3. **Regime/political collapse** — system falls, takes workforce with it (USSR, samurai, colonial administrators)
4. **Resource depletion** — the thing you harvest runs out (cod, fur, guano, gold)
5. **Regulatory abolition** — government makes it illegal (slavery, child labor, asbestos)
6. **Globalization/trade** — undercut by cheaper production elsewhere (textiles, auto, steel)
7. **Environmental collapse** — nature pulls the rug (potato blight, Dust Bowl, Aral Sea)
8. **War reshaping** — conflict restructures the workforce (servant class post-WWI)
9. **Consolidation/monopoly** — job still exists but concentrates (family farms, bookstores, newspapers)

**Key hypothesis:** Tech displacement is a slow bottom-up erosion. Regime collapse is instant and total. Cultural shift is glacial. Resource depletion has a tipping point. If this pattern holds across examples, that's the insight.

## What to research per event

### Structured core (every event)
- Profession, collapse type, peak workforce (absolute + % of workforce)
- The killing event, year of event, year peaked, year effectively dead, duration of decline
- Tier breakdown: did the bottom die first or did everyone go?
- Where each tier went (successor industries)
- Income trajectory: 25th/50th/75th percentile, tracked 10-20 years post-displacement
- Surplus capture: efficiency gains to capital, adjacent workers, or consumers?
- Economic aftermath

### Foresight stories (every event)
- **Adapters:** Who saw it coming and pivoted successfully? What did they do, how early?
- **False movers:** Who took reasonable steps that turned out wrong? What was the bad bet?
- **Deniers:** Who refused to believe? What was the wrong conventional wisdom?
- **The pattern:** Common thread in survival — retraining, mobility, management layer of replacement tech, luck?

### Open-ended texture (per event, agent's discretion)
Each collapse has unique well-documented aftermath data. Agents should chase whatever's most interesting for each specific event — whaling town divorce rates, post-USSR alcoholism, freedmen census data, Dust Bowl migration patterns, cod moratorium EI dependency. The unique texture IS the story. Cross-cutting patterns may emerge when compiled.

## Target events (~15-20, scope during planning)
Aim for 2-3 well-documented examples per collapse type. Prioritize events with the richest available data. Full candidate list:

- **Tech:** hand weavers/power looms, whalers/kerosene, switchboard operators, human computers, typists/word processors, travel agents/internet
- **Cultural:** church organists, hatmakers/JFK, domestic servants post-WWI
- **Regime:** USSR collapse, samurai/Meiji, colonial administrators/decolonization
- **Resource:** Newfoundland cod, fur trappers, gold rush towns
- **Regulatory:** slavery/abolition, child labor laws
- **Globalization:** US textiles, Detroit auto, Sheffield steel
- **Environmental:** Irish potato blight, Dust Bowl
- **War:** European servant class post-WWI
- **Consolidation:** family farms, local newspapers

## Visualization approach

### Per-event template (5 standardized panels + 1 unique)
1. **The Squeeze** — stacked area: workforce by tier over time
2. **The Scatter** — Sankey: where each tier went
3. **The Income Shock** — small multiples: income trajectories by percentile
4. **The Ripple** — bar chart: who captured the efficiency gains
5. **The Foresight** — timeline of adapter vs. denier actions relative to collapse
6. **Unique texture** — per-event bonus using best available aftermath data

### Meta-graph
Summary scatter across all events. X = years from event to death. Y = income recovery at 10 years. Bubble size = workforce displaced. Color = collapse type. Speculative "AI + engineering" dot optional.

## Known complications
- Pre-industrial data is estimates. Income data thin before ~1900.
- "Dead" is subjective (there are still blacksmiths). Need a consistent threshold.
- Some events span multiple types (whaling = tech + resource depletion).
- Foresight stories are survivorship-biased.
- National vs. global workforce % — decide during planning.
