# Result: Diet Overhaul (Home Cooking vs. Fast Food)

## Summary

Shifting from a typical lower-income American diet (heavy on fast food, processed food, and sugar-sweetened beverages) to a healthier home-cooked diet anchored on Mediterranean/DASH principles is one of the most impactful lifestyle interventions — but also one of the hardest to sustain. The QALY evidence is strong in aggregate but indirect: no single RCT measures "diet overhaul → QALYs." Instead, the estimate is synthesized from Mediterranean diet mortality meta-analyses (HR 0.74 for high vs. low adherence, representing a 26% all-cause mortality reduction), a US microsimulation modeling a 29% improvement in diet quality scores (~229 million QALYs over 30 years for the adult population), and SNAP healthy food incentive modeling (0.52 QALYs per person over a lifetime for a modest dietary improvement). The financial picture is surprisingly nuanced: while the healthiest diets cost ~$1.50/day more per person than the least healthy (Rao et al. 2013 meta-analysis), switching from fast food to home cooking partially or fully offsets this premium. For Bob, the net annual cost is roughly $0–$300 depending on his current eating-out frequency. The killer problem is adherence — dietary change dropout rates exceed 40% at 12 months in structured programs, and are worse for self-directed change. Time commitment is the highest of any intervention in this study at ~5 hours/week for cooking, shopping, and planning.

## QALY Evidence

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.05
- **Range:** 0.03 – 0.08 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** Synthesis of Mediterranean diet mortality meta-analyses, US diet quality microsimulation (Jardim et al. 2021), and SNAP incentive CEA (Mozaffarian et al. 2018)
- **Study type:** Meta-analyses of observational cohorts + microsimulation modeling
- **Population studied:** General adult populations in high-income countries; Mediterranean diet cohorts predominantly Southern European; microsimulation uses US adult population aged 25+
- **Population match to Bob:** Fair — Most studies use general populations, not specifically low-income. Bob's worse baseline diet quality (lower AHEI-2010 score at 25th percentile) means larger room for improvement, but also more barriers to achieving it. The mortality reduction estimates may overstate achievable gains because observational studies can't fully control for the "healthy user" confound (people who eat well also exercise, don't smoke, etc.).
- **Key caveats:**
  - "Diet overhaul" is not a standardized intervention — it encompasses dozens of possible dietary changes, making it harder to pin down than a specific drug or procedure
  - No RCT has measured "switch from fast food to home cooking" as a unified intervention with QALY endpoints
  - The PREDIMED trial (the strongest RCT evidence for Mediterranean diet) studied high-CVD-risk adults in Spain aged 55-80, not low-income Americans in their 30s-40s
  - Observational studies cannot fully exclude residual confounding — healthier eaters differ from unhealthy eaters in many unmeasured ways
  - The estimate already accounts for the fact that a practical "overhaul" achieves ~60-70% of the maximum theoretical dietary shift, not perfect Mediterranean adherence

### Evidence Stream 1: Mediterranean Diet Mortality Meta-Analyses

**Sofi et al. (2010, updated)** — Meta-analysis of prospective cohort studies on Mediterranean diet adherence and mortality.
- High vs. low adherence: **26% reduction in all-cause mortality** (pooled HR 0.74)
- Each 2-point increment in Mediterranean Diet Score associated with ~10% reduction in all-cause mortality
- Consistent across European and American cohorts

**Sotos-Prieto et al. (2017, NEJM)** — Association of changes in diet quality with total and cause-specific mortality.
- 73,710 participants from NHS, NHSII, and HPFS cohorts, 12 years follow-up
- 20-percentile increase in AHEI-2010 score: **8-17% reduction in total mortality** depending on the index used
- HR 0.91 (95% CI 0.85-0.97) for AHEI-2010 improvement over 12 years

**Conversion to annual QALYs:**
- For a 40-year-old with ~38 years remaining life expectancy and Bob's elevated baseline mortality risk (~0.3-0.5% annually due to obesity, sedentary lifestyle)
- 15-26% mortality reduction (practical range for a real-world diet shift, not the full theoretical maximum) → ~1.5-3.0 life years gained over remaining lifespan
- Annualized mortality-based QALY component: ~0.03-0.06 QALYs/year
- Plus morbidity/quality-of-life improvement (reduced fatigue, better digestive health, improved mood from nutritional adequacy): +0.01-0.02 QALYs/year

### Evidence Stream 2: US Microsimulation of Diet Quality Improvement

**Jardim et al. (2021)** — Diet quality improvement and 30-year population health and economic outcomes: a microsimulation study.
- Modeled a 2-quintile shift in AHEI-2010 score (29-30% improvement) for US adults aged 25+
- 30-year results: **229.4 million QALYs gained** across the US adult population, 0.8% improvement over baseline
- Annualized per-person estimate: ~0.035-0.040 QALYs/year (derived from ~200M+ adults)
- Also projected $144 billion/year in healthcare cost reduction by year 30
- This represents a substantial but achievable dietary improvement — from the bottom quintile to the middle quintile of diet quality

### Evidence Stream 3: SNAP Healthy Food Incentive CEA

**Mozaffarian et al. (2018, PLOS Medicine)** — Cost-effectiveness of financial incentives for improving diet through Medicare and Medicaid.
- 82 million adults aged 35-80 on Medicare/Medicaid
- Healthy food incentive (30% subsidy on fruits, vegetables, whole grains, nuts, seafood, plant oils): **8.40 million QALYs gained** over lifetime
- Per-person lifetime QALYs: ~0.10 (over ~20 years mean remaining life = ~0.005/year)
- ICER: $13,194/QALY (highly cost-effective)
- Note: This models a MODEST dietary change (response to a 30% price subsidy), not a full overhaul. The full overhaul would produce larger effects.

### Evidence Stream 4: Sugar-Sweetened Beverage Reduction

**Soda tax modeling studies (2020-2023)** — SSB reduction alone contributes meaningfully:
- National SSB tax modeling: 2.44-5.02 million QALYs over lifetime for US population
- Oakland SSB tax evaluation: 94 QALYs per 10,000 residents over 10 years
- SSB reduction is one component of the broader diet overhaul

### QALY Synthesis

| Source | Annual QALY estimate | Notes |
|--------|---------------------|-------|
| Mediterranean diet mortality meta-analyses | 0.04-0.07 | Mortality + morbidity combined; adjusted down for practical adherence |
| US microsimulation (2-quintile AHEI shift) | 0.035-0.040 | Most rigorous population-level estimate |
| SNAP incentive CEA | ~0.005 | Much more modest intervention than full overhaul |
| **Synthesized midpoint** | **0.05** | Weighted toward microsimulation; adjusted up slightly for Bob's worse baseline |

## Cost Analysis (for Bob at ~$30k household income)

- **Total annual cost (best estimate):** $250
- **Cost range:** $0 – $700 (low – high)
- **With insurance (if applicable):** N/A (food costs, not medical)
- **Without insurance:** N/A
- **Cost notes:**

### Food Cost Differential

**Rao et al. (2013, BMJ Open)** — Meta-analysis of 27 studies across 10 high-income countries:
- Healthiest vs. least healthy diets: **$1.50/day more per person** ($1.01-$1.95/day)
- Annualized: **~$548/year** ($369-$712) for one person
- Largest differential in meats/protein ($0.29/serving more for healthier options)

### Cooking at Home Offset

**Wolfson & Bleich (2015, Public Health Nutrition)** — National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey analysis:
- Cooking at home associated with better diet quality at lower total food expenditure

**Seattle Obesity Study (Monsivais et al. 2014):**
- High cooking frequency (6+ times/week): $273/month total food per capita
- Low cooking frequency (0-3 times/week): $330/month total food per capita
- **Monthly savings from frequent home cooking: ~$57** ($684/year)
- Away-from-home spending dropped from $133 to $65/month

### Net Cost for Bob

Bob's current estimated food spending (BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey, lowest-to-second quintile):
- Total food: ~$5,000-6,500/year
- Food away from home: ~$1,500-2,500/year (fast food heavy)

Post-overhaul:
- Reduced food away from home: save ~$800-1,500/year
- Increased grocery spending for healthier ingredients: add ~$400-800/year (Rao et al. premium, partially mitigated by bulk cooking)
- Net ingredient quality premium: add ~$548/year (Rao et al.)
- Net cooking savings offset: subtract ~$400-1,000/year

**Net annual cost: roughly $0-$500, midpoint ~$250**

The wide range ($0-$700) reflects that Bobs who currently eat a lot of fast food may break even or save money, while Bobs who already eat cheap groceries (ramen, processed food) and add healthier ingredients see a meaningful cost increase.

### Startup Costs

- Basic cookware set (pot, pan, baking sheet, knife, cutting board): $50-100 at Walmart/Target
- Initial pantry staples (oil, spices, rice, beans, flour): $30-50
- Total startup: $80-150, midpoint $100
- Amortized over 3 years: $33/year

### SNAP Eligibility Note

At $30k gross household income, Bob is likely NOT eligible for SNAP:
- Single person: gross income limit $1,632/month ($19,584/year) — Bob exceeds this
- Household of 2: limit $2,215/month ($26,580/year) — Bob exceeds this
- Household of 3+: limit $2,798/month ($33,576/year) — Bob MAY qualify depending on household size
- If SNAP-eligible, benefits average ~$200/month for a single person, substantially reducing the net cost of the diet overhaul

## Time Commitment

- **Hours per week:** 5.0
- **Category:** High (3+ hr/wk)
- **Breakdown:**
  - Cooking 5 nights/week × 40-60 min: 3.3-5.0 hrs/wk
  - Grocery shopping 1-2 trips/week: 1.0-2.0 hrs/wk
  - Meal planning/prep: 0.5 hrs/wk
  - Total range: 4.8-7.5 hrs/wk, midpoint ~5.0
- **Notes:** This is the highest time commitment of any intervention in this study. Time is arguably the biggest barrier for Bob — shift work, long hours, fatigue, and the cognitive load of meal planning when you're not used to cooking. Batch cooking on weekends can reduce weeknight time but requires upfront planning capacity.

## Access and Barriers

- **Activation energy:** High
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - **Food deserts:** ~13% of the US population (39 million people) lives in low-income, low-access areas more than 1 mile (urban) or 10 miles (rural) from the nearest full-service grocery store (USDA Economic Research Service, 2022). Bob may face a 30+ minute round trip to buy fresh produce.
  - **Time poverty:** Cooking adds 4-7 hours/week. Bob's irregular work hours, potential second job, and commute leave limited time and energy. Fast food's appeal is speed and convenience, not just taste preference.
  - **Cooking skills gap:** Low-income households report lower cooking confidence and fewer cooking skills. "Just learn to cook" underestimates the skill acquisition curve.
  - **Kitchen limitations:** Smaller kitchens, fewer appliances, limited storage for bulk purchases in some lower-income housing.
  - **Social/cultural friction:** Bob's peer group may eat fast food together. Bringing a packed lunch can be socially isolating. Family members may resist dietary changes.
  - **Cognitive bandwidth:** Poverty taxes decision-making capacity (Mullainathan & Shafir, "Scarcity"). Meal planning requires the kind of executive function that financial stress depletes.
  - **Food insecurity:** 13.7% of US households experienced food insecurity in 2024 (USDA). When food is scarce, caloric density and shelf stability trump nutritional quality.

## Side Effects and Risks

- **Nutritional deficiency risk (low):** If Bob eliminates food groups without understanding substitutions, possible short-term deficiencies. Mitigated by any basic nutrition education.
- **Time stress (moderate):** Adding 5+ hours/week of cooking obligations to an already time-poor life can increase stress, partially offsetting the health benefits.
- **Social friction (low-moderate):** Different eating from peers/family. Can be socially isolating in work environments or social settings where fast food is the norm.
- **Orthorexia risk (very low):** Obsessive focus on "clean eating" is rare in this population but worth noting as a general risk of dietary focus.
- **Initial digestive adjustment (low):** Suddenly increasing fiber intake from more vegetables, whole grains, and beans can cause temporary GI discomfort. Gradual transition mitigates this.

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate:** 30%
- **Source:** Meta-analysis of weight loss/dietary intervention studies (Lemstra et al. 2016, Patient Preference and Adherence); dietary intervention adherence literature review (Desroches et al. 2013, Cochrane)
- **Notes:**
  - Structured dietary intervention programs report ~60% adherence at 12 months, but this drops to 30-40% at 24+ months
  - Self-directed dietary changes (Bob buying a cookbook and trying to change on his own) have even lower sustained adherence, estimated at 15-25%
  - The 30% estimate reflects a middle ground: Bob receives some guidance (e.g., from a primary care provider or community program) but doesn't have ongoing structured support
  - Factors predicting dropout: high perceived difficulty, lack of social support, limited cooking skills, food environment barriers — Bob scores poorly on all of these
  - Dietary adherence is among the lowest of all lifestyle interventions. For comparison, medication adherence is typically 50-70% and exercise adherence 40-50%
  - The biggest predictor of sustained dietary change is whether the new diet is enjoyable and convenient. A Mediterranean-style diet built around foods Bob actually likes has better odds than a prescriptive plan.

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 2
- **Tier justification:** Strong observational evidence from large cohorts (Mediterranean diet mortality data from studies with 100,000+ participants), multiple converging evidence streams (meta-analyses, microsimulations, CEAs), but no direct QALY measurement for "diet overhaul" as a unified intervention. The QALY estimate is synthesized from mortality reduction data and modeling studies rather than directly measured. Population match is Fair (general populations, not specifically low-income Americans). The intervention itself is poorly standardized — "diet overhaul" could mean many different things — which limits comparability across studies.
- **Key studies:**
  1. **Sotos-Prieto et al. (2017)** — "Association of Changes in Diet Quality with Total and Cause-Specific Mortality." NEJM. 73,710 participants, 12-year follow-up. 8-17% mortality reduction per 20-percentile AHEI improvement.
  2. **Jardim et al. (2021)** — "Diet quality improvement and 30-year population health and economic outcomes: a microsimulation study." Public Health Nutrition. 229.4 million QALYs over 30 years for a 2-quintile AHEI shift.
  3. **Rao et al. (2013)** — "Do healthier foods and diet patterns cost more than less healthy options?" BMJ Open. Meta-analysis of 27 studies: $1.50/day premium for healthiest diet.
  4. **Mozaffarian et al. (2018)** — "Cost-effectiveness of financial incentives for improving diet through Medicare and Medicaid." PLOS Medicine. 8.40 million QALYs gained from healthy food incentive; ICER $13,194/QALY.
- **Limitations:**
  - No unified "diet overhaul" RCT with QALY endpoints exists
  - Mediterranean diet evidence is predominantly from Southern European populations
  - Observational studies cannot fully exclude healthy-user confounding
  - Cost estimates rely on limited data for Bob's specific income bracket and food environment
  - The QALY estimate is model-derived, not directly observed
  - Adherence data for low-income populations specifically is sparse
