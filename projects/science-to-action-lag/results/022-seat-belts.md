## Sources

1. Hugh DeHaven, crash survivability research at Cornell University, Crash Injury Research program established 1942. Published foundational work on human tolerance to deceleration forces.
2. Cornell Aeronautical Laboratory, "Automotive Crash Injury Research" report, 1953 — confirmed effectiveness of lap-type seat belts in preventing ejection and reducing injuries.
3. American Medical Association, 1954 — voted to support installation of lap belts in all automobiles.
4. Ralph Nader, "Unsafe at Any Speed," 1965 — catalyzed political momentum for auto safety regulation.
5. National Traffic and Motor Vehicle Safety Act of 1966 (US) — established Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standards, required manufacturers to install seat belts.
6. Federal Motor Vehicle Safety Standard 208 (FMVSS 208), effective January 1, 1968 — required lap and shoulder belts in all new US passenger vehicles.
7. Victoria, Australia, Road Safety Act 1970 — first jurisdiction in the world to mandate compulsory wearing of seat belts (effective December 1970). Road toll dropped 13% in the following year.
8. New York State mandatory seat belt law, signed July 1984, effective December 1, 1984 — first US state to require seat belt use by front-seat occupants.
9. NHTSA National Occupant Protection Use Survey (NOPUS) — seat belt usage rates: ~14% in 1983, ~50% by late 1980s, 82% in 2007, 91.2% in 2024.
10. PubMed: "Reduction in road fatalities and injuries after legislation for compulsory wearing of seat belts: experience in Victoria and the rest of Australia" — documented immediate 50% reduction in serious injury risk.

## Judgment calls

**Early evidence year (1953 vs 1942):** DeHaven's crash survivability research began at Cornell in 1942, but his early work focused on general crash survivability principles (falls, aircraft crashes) rather than automotive seat belts specifically. The 1953 Cornell Aeronautical Laboratory report is the first controlled study specifically confirming seat belt effectiveness in automobiles. Used 1953 rather than 1942 because the earlier work, while foundational, didn't directly study seat belts in cars.

**Consensus year (1954):** The AMA's 1954 vote to support seat belt installation in all cars is the first formal endorsement by a major body. An alternative would be 1966 (Congressional action via NTMVSA), but that's a political/legislative milestone rather than a scientific consensus statement. The AMA vote directly responded to the Cornell research and represents medical/scientific consensus.

**First regulation — wearing vs. equipment mandate:** The ticket instructions specify using the WEARING mandate (behavioral requirement) rather than the equipment mandate (installation requirement). Installation mandates (e.g., US 1968) don't directly change behavior or save lives unless people actually buckle up. Victoria 1970 is the correct "action" date because it's the first law that actually required people to wear seat belts, producing an immediate 13% reduction in fatalities.

**US regulation year (1984 vs 1968):** Consistent with the wearing-mandate logic above, used 1984 (New York state law) rather than 1968 (federal equipment mandate). While state-level, this became the de facto national standard — all 50 states except New Hampshire (for adults) eventually passed mandatory seat belt laws, driven by federal incentive programs. There is no federal usage mandate.

**Industry opposition (moderate vs. none):** The plan.md field definition lists seat belts as an example of "none" opposition, but research shows the Big Three automakers (GM, Ford, Chrysler) actively argued against both equipment and usage mandates throughout the 1950s-60s. They claimed seat belts would hurt sales, ruin styling, and that the public didn't want them. However, opposition was SHORT-LIVED (~15 years) compared to tobacco (50+ years) or lead (decades). The industry eventually embraced seat belts as cheaper than redesigning crash structures. Rated "moderate" — organized but brief resistance, not a sustained multi-decade campaign.

**50% exposure reduction (1990):** NHTSA data shows usage was ~14% in 1983. After New York's law (1984) and subsequent state laws, usage climbed rapidly. By the late 1980s, usage was approximately 46-50%. Used 1990 as the approximate year national usage crossed the 50% threshold, consistent with NHTSA survey data trajectory.

**Substitute available (yes):** Seat belts themselves were cheap, proven technology with no cost barrier. The question of "substitute" is slightly awkward for behavioral mandates — the "substitute" here means there was no structural barrier to compliance (belts cost ~$10/car, already installed by 1970). No expensive systemic change was required; people just had to buckle an existing device.
