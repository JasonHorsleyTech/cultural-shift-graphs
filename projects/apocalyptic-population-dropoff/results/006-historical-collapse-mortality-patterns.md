# Result: Historical Societal Collapse — Mortality Patterns and Timelines

## Summary

Ten historical collapses were analyzed for population decline patterns, mortality rates, and timelines. The best-documented cases (Leningrad, Cambodia, Ireland, Great Leap Forward) provide usable mortality curves. Key finding: rapid collapses (Leningrad, Rwanda) kill 25-40% within 6-12 months, while prolonged collapses (Ireland, Rome, Maya) show sustained multi-year or multi-decade declines with lower peak mortality rates but comparable or higher total losses. The critical variable is whether the population has escape routes (emigration, evacuation, external aid).

Population base: 2,500,000 trapped civilians. Deaths as % of that base.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 720 | 1.0 | 0.5 | 1.5 | starvation_onset_shelling | EHNE / Wikipedia |

**Notes:** Evacuations (~1M people via Road of Life and rail) mean the effective population shrank dramatically during the siege. Death rates are calculated against the initial trapped population of ~2.5M. If calculated only against those who remained in the city throughout, the mortality rate would be higher. The peak killing period was the first winter (months 4-6), with ~60% of all siege deaths occurring in winter 1941-42.

## Mortality Timeline — Irish Great Famine

Population base: 8,200,000. Deaths as % of that base. Emigration tracked separately.

| time_hours | cumulative_deaths_pct | deaths_pct_low | deaths_pct_high | primary_cause | source |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 8760 | 0.5 | 0.2 | 1.0 | starvation_onset | 1845: partial crop failure, few excess deaths |
| 17520 | 3.0 | 2.0 | 4.0 | starvation_typhus_dysentery | 1846: total crop failure, first starvation deaths |
| 26280 | 8.0 | 6.0 | 10.0 | typhus_typhoid_dysentery_starvation | 1847 ("Black '47"): peak year, ~400K deaths |
| 35040 | 10.0 | 8.0 | 13.0 | disease_starvation | 1848: continued high mortality |
| 43800 | 11.5 | 9.0 | 14.5 | disease_starvation | 1849: still elevated |
| 52560 | 12.0 | 10.0 | 15.0 | disease | 1850: beginning to abate |
| 61320 | 12.0 | 10.0 | 18.0 | disease | 1851-52: famine officially ends |

**Notes:** The Irish Famine is distinctive because emigration (2.1M people, or ~26% of population) exceeded deaths. Combined population loss was ~38% (deaths + emigration) over 7 years, with the long tail continuing for decades — Ireland's population didn't bottom out until ~4.4M around 1901 (a 46% total decline from 1844). The famine's primary killer was infectious disease (typhus, typhoid, relapsing fever, dysentery), not literal starvation — the weakened population was decimated by epidemics. This pattern is highly relevant to modern collapse scenarios where medical infrastructure fails.

## Collapse Speed Classification

For the graph's purposes, these collapses sort into three speed categories:

**Flash collapses (days to months):**
- Rwanda: 11% dead in 100 days. Violence-driven. No analogue for grid-down but demonstrates maximum human killing rate.

**Rapid collapses (months to a few years):**
- Leningrad: 40% dead in 2.4 years, with ~60% of deaths in first 6 months. Best analogue for urban starvation / grid-down. Peak mortality at months 4-6 when food stores depleted.
- Cambodia: 22% dead in 3.75 years. Steady-state killing. Demonstrates mortality from forced agricultural transition + violence.
- Great Leap Forward: 5.5% of national population dead in 3 years (but 15-30% in worst-affected provinces). Demonstrates state-caused agricultural collapse at scale.

**Slow collapses (decades to centuries):**
- Ireland: 12% dead + 26% emigrated over 7 years, continued declining for 50+ years. Demonstrates famine + disease + emigration feedback loop.
- Post-Soviet Russia: 5% excess deaths over 12 years. Demonstrates controlled decline with functioning international aid.
- Rome, Maya, Bronze Age: Multi-century declines of 90%+. Too slow to map onto the graph's 10-year x-axis, but show that total population loss can be extreme given enough time.

## Data Gaps

- **Leningrad monthly data beyond winter 1941-42:** Specific monthly death counts for spring/summer 1942 and all of 1943 are not well-reported in English-language sources. The timeline for months 7-20 of the siege relies on interpolation between the well-documented peak and the end-state.
- **Cambodia year-by-year breakdown:** No reliable year-by-year death toll exists. Total estimates and qualitative phasing are available, but the mortality timeline is interpolated.
- **Great Leap Forward provincial variation:** The national mortality rate (5.5%) masks enormous provincial variation (some provinces saw 15-30% death rates). Province-level data would be more useful for modeling but is politically sensitive and hard to access.
- **Bronze Age Collapse population:** Essentially unknown. Archaeological evidence shows abandonment and destruction but population figures are archaeological guesswork.
- **Maya population:** Recent LIDAR surveys have dramatically revised population upward (from ~5M to 13-16M), but pre- and post-collapse estimates remain uncertain.
- **Post-collapse stabilization times** are poorly documented for most events. When did the dying stop? For Leningrad and Rwanda, the end is clear (siege lifted / RPF victory). For Ireland, excess mortality tapered but emigration continued for decades. For ancient collapses, "stabilization" is an archaeological judgment call.
- **Social dynamics during decline:** None of these collapses have good data on violence between civilians, cooperation rates, or organized survival strategies. The social modeling gap identified in plan.md applies to all historical cases too.
