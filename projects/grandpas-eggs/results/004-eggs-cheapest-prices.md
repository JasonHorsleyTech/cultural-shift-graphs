# Eggs (Dozen) — Cheapest Prices

## Summary

Egg prices were remarkably stable in nominal terms from 1950 through the early 1970s, hovering around $0.50-0.65/dozen. The 1973 food crisis pushed prices permanently higher. From 1980 to ~2005, prices fluctuated in a $0.78-$1.34 band. After 2006, a structural upward shift occurred, partly from rising feed costs and partly from the BLS sample broadening to include specialty eggs. Two avian influenza outbreaks (2015, 2022-2025) caused dramatic spikes. Pre-~1990, there was only one tier of eggs — the price reported here is also the quality-equivalent price for that period.

## Methodology

### Primary series: BLS Average Price (APU0000708111)

The core data source is the Bureau of Labor Statistics Average Price series for "Eggs, grade A, large, per dozen" in U.S. City Average (series APU0000708111). BLS representatives collect monthly retail prices in 75 urban areas for the Consumer Price Index program. Annual figures are the arithmetic mean of 12 monthly observations.

### Pre-1980 data

The BLS APU series in its current form covers approximately 1980 onward. For 1950-1979, data was sourced from USDA retail food price surveys and early BLS average price compilations as reported in the USDA Food Cost Review and secondary compilations (The Daily Meal, citing USDA/BLS historical data). These pre-1980 prices measure the same product (Grade A large eggs, per dozen, retail) but through earlier survey methodologies. The splice is smooth — 1979 ($0.86) to 1980 ($0.84) shows no discontinuity.

### "Cheapest" vs. BLS average

The BLS APU series tracks the average price across all Grade A large eggs sampled, which since approximately 2012 explicitly includes "organic, non-organic, cage free, free range, and traditional varieties." This means:

- **Pre-1990:** The BLS price IS the cheapest price. Only one tier of eggs existed.
- **1990-2005:** Very small premium tier emerging (organic/free-range <5% of market). BLS average ≈ cheapest price.
- **2006-2015:** Growing specialty segment. BLS average slightly above cheapest conventional. Estimated gap: 5-10%.
- **2016-2025:** Specialty eggs ~25-30% of market. BLS average increasingly overstates cheapest conventional eggs. Estimated gap: 10-20%. For example, in 2024, BLS average was $3.17 while Walmart conventional Grade A large eggs were roughly $2.50-2.80 in non-spike months.

For this project, the BLS series is used throughout for consistency. The graph phase should note that the "cheapest" line for post-2010 years is conservatively high; the actual cheapest conventional eggs were somewhat cheaper.

### Pre-quality-divergence note

Before ~1990, Line A (cheapest) and Line B (quality-equivalent) are the same. The prices in this file apply to both lines for that period. The quality-equivalent ticket (005) will focus on post-divergence pricing.

### Structural breaks

1. **1973 food price crisis:** Eggs jumped ~50% in one year ($0.52 → $0.78) and never returned to 1960s levels. Driven by global grain shortages, export policies, and general inflation.
2. **2007-2008 feed cost spike:** Corn prices doubled due to ethanol mandates and commodity speculation. Eggs rose from $1.31 to $1.99. Financial crisis crashed demand; prices fell to $1.66 by 2009.
3. **2015 HPAI (avian influenza):** Worst poultry health disaster in US history — 50M+ birds culled Dec 2014-Jun 2015 (~12% of laying flock). Prices spiked 61% above prior year for May-December 2015. Annual average: $2.47.
4. **2022-2025 HPAI (ongoing):** Second, third, and fourth HPAI waves. 138.7M+ birds affected since 2022. Prices spiked from $1.67 (2021) to $2.86 (2022), partially recovered, then spiked again to a monthly peak of $6.23 in March 2025 before declining to $2.35 by March 2026.

## Known gaps

- **October 2025:** BLS did not collect data due to a federal appropriations lapse. The 2025 annual average is calculated from 11 months instead of 12.
- **Pre-1950 data:** Available back to 1937 from USDA sources but not included per ticket scope.
- **Grade specification pre-1960:** Older data may not have strictly specified "Grade A Large." The USDA grading system for eggs was formalized in 1970; prior to that, the eggs surveyed were functionally the same product but may have been described differently in some surveys.
- **Cheapest vs. average post-2012:** As noted in methodology, the BLS series overstates the cheapest conventional egg price by an estimated 10-20% for recent years. No nationally consistent series tracks only conventional eggs.
