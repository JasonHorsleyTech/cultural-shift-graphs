# Ticket 010: Typists / Word Processor Operators

## Event
The gradual elimination of dedicated typists and word processing operators as personal computers became standard office equipment, ~1980s–2010s. Workers who once formed entire departments became redundant when everyone could type their own documents.

## Mechanism
**Gradual erosion** (tech_tool)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track US employment for typists, stenographers, and word processor operators from ~1970 to present:

- Peak: likely 1970s–1980s. Typing pools and word processing centers were standard in large offices.
- BLS SOC codes: 43-9022 (Word Processors and Typists) — track this from earliest available data
- Also track: 43-9021 (Stenographers) — related decline
- Key transitions: IBM Selectric (1961), dedicated word processors (Wang, 1976), IBM PC (1981), Microsoft Word (1983), Windows 95 (mass adoption of PCs)
- Current: ~41,000 word processors/typists (BLS, 2023) — verify

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS OES data, SOC 43-9022 (Word Processors and Typists)
2. US Census occupational data via IPUMS (1970–2000)
3. Historical Statistics of the United States for pre-BLS era
4. Strom, "Beyond the Typewriter: Gender, Class, and the Origins of Modern American Office Work" (1992)

### Income Data
- BLS median wages for word processors/typists over time
- Compare to general administrative assistant wages — was the typing specialist premium eliminated?
- Track from 1990–2023

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS OES median annual wage for SOC 43-9022
2. CPI-U for inflation adjustment
3. BLS data for administrative assistants (SOC 43-6014) as comparison

### Where They Went
- Administrative assistant / executive assistant (the role absorbed typing duties)
- Data entry (SOC 43-9021)
- Customer service
- General office support
- The key insight: typing didn't disappear — it was distributed to everyone. The specialist role became a general skill.

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS employment data for administrative assistants and secretaries
2. CPS occupation-switching data
3. Academic studies on office work transformation

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Typists who learned word processing software early and became the "computer person" in the office. Wang Laboratories' retraining programs for their own customers' staff.
- **False movers:** Dedicated word processing centers that tried to persist as centralized services within companies.
- **Deniers:** The assumption that executives and professionals would "never type their own letters."
- **Survival pattern:** Becoming a power user of the replacement technology. Moving up to administrative assistant rather than down to data entry.

### Unique Texture
- The typing pool as a gendered institution — almost entirely female
- The transition from "typing is a specialized skill" to "everyone types" — a universal skill redistribution
- Speed of adoption: the PC went from novelty to standard office equipment in ~15 years
- The paradox: more typing happens today than ever, but typists as a profession are nearly extinct
- The Wang word processor as the "missing link" between typewriters and PCs

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Employment numbers spanning 1970–2020 (at least 8 data points)
- Concurrent growth in administrative assistant roles
- Wage comparison between typists and admin assistants over time
- Timeline of technology transitions (typewriter → dedicated WP → PC → everyone types)
- Gender demographics
