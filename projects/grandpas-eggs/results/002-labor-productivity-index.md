## Methodology

**Series used:** OPHNFB from the Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED) database, which mirrors the BLS Major Sector Productivity and Costs program series PRS85006092. This is the standard nonfarm business sector output-per-hour index for all workers.

**How it's computed by BLS:** Labor productivity is calculated by dividing an index of real output by an index of hours worked for all persons (employees, proprietors, and unpaid family workers) in the nonfarm business sector. The quarterly series is seasonally adjusted. Annual values shown here are averages of the four quarterly index values.

**Base year:** 2017=100. The ticket anticipated a 2012=100 base, but BLS rebased the series to 2017=100 in a recent revision. This does not affect the graph — the graph phase will compute cumulative growth ratios from 1955, so the base year cancels out.

**No splicing needed.** This is a single continuous series maintained by BLS since 1947. No gaps, no source changes.

**Notable anomalies:**
- **1974 and 1979-1982:** Productivity stagnated during the oil crisis and stagflation era. The index barely moved from 45.2 (1974) to 50.2 (1981) — seven years of near-zero productivity growth.
- **2020:** A sharp compositional spike to 109.1 (from 103.6 in 2019). This is NOT real productivity improvement — COVID layoffs disproportionately hit lower-productivity service-sector jobs, biasing the index upward. Hours dropped faster than output. The effect partially reversed in 2022 (dip to 109.8) as those workers returned.
- **2009-2010:** Similar but smaller compositional effect during the Great Recession. Productivity appeared to surge as firms shed workers faster than output fell.

**Growth eras (annualized rates):**
- 1947-1973 (postwar boom): ~2.8%/yr — the "golden age" of productivity growth
- 1973-1995 (productivity slowdown): ~1.4%/yr — the great deceleration
- 1995-2005 (IT boom): ~2.9%/yr — brief return to postwar-era growth rates
- 2005-2019 (new normal): ~1.3%/yr — secular stagnation in productivity
- 2019-2025 (post-COVID): ~2.2%/yr — uptick possibly driven by remote work, AI adoption, and compositional effects still unwinding
