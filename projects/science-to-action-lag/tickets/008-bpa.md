# Ticket 008: BPA → Endocrine Disruption

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that bisphenol A (BPA) is an endocrine disruptor with health effects to binding regulation restricting BPA in consumer products.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — BPA's estrogenic properties were discovered in the 1930s (Dodds & Lawson, 1936). Modern concerns emerged from vom Saal's studies in the 1990s showing low-dose endocrine effects in mice. When did credible evidence of harm at consumer-exposure levels emerge?

2. **Consensus date** — When did a major body formally state BPA poses a health risk? Look for: EFSA opinions, FDA assessments, WHO/FAO joint assessments, Endocrine Society statements. Note: regulatory agencies have repeatedly revised their BPA risk assessments — EFSA drastically lowered the tolerable daily intake in 2023.

3. **First OECD regulation** — Canada declared BPA toxic and banned it in baby bottles (2010). France banned BPA in all food packaging (2015). EU banned BPA in baby bottles (2011). Which was first?

4. **US regulation** — FDA banned BPA in baby bottles and sippy cups (2012), but this followed industry's voluntary phase-out. No comprehensive US federal ban on BPA in food packaging as of 2026.

5. **Industry opposition** — The American Chemistry Council and plastics industry funded studies disputing low-dose effects. Moderate to strong opposition using the "regulatory science" playbook.

6. **Substitute availability** — BPA-free alternatives exist (BPS, BPF, Tritan), but some substitutes may also be endocrine disruptors ("regrettable substitution"). Note this irony.

## Where to look

1. EFSA BPA risk assessment history (2006, 2015, 2023 opinions)
2. FDA BPA regulatory actions and timeline
3. Health Canada BPA assessment and baby bottle ban
4. Endocrine Society scientific statements on BPA
5. PubMed — "bisphenol A regulation history" or "BPA endocrine disruption"
6. Laura Vandenberg et al. (2012) — review of low-dose BPA effects

## What "good data" looks like

- Clear distinction between BPA's estrogenic properties (known since 1936) and evidence of harm at ACTUAL EXPOSURE LEVELS from consumer products (1990s-2000s). Use the latter for early_evidence_year.
- Regulation dates that distinguish between baby-bottle-only bans (narrow) and comprehensive food-packaging bans (broad). Use the first binding restriction that reduced population exposure.
- FDA's 2012 "ban" was arguably formalizing an industry-led phase-out — note this context.

## Known pitfalls

- BPA is a case where the science is STILL contested at the regulatory level — EFSA and FDA have reached different conclusions on safe exposure levels. This doesn't mean there's no consensus, but note the ongoing regulatory divergence.
- "BPA-free" substitutes may not be safer — this is relevant context but not the primary data point.
- The 1936 evidence is about BPA's PROPERTIES (it's estrogenic), not about harm from consumer products. Don't conflate "we knew it was estrogenic" with "we knew it was harmful at exposure levels."
