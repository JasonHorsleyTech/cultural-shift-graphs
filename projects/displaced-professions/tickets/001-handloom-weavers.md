# Ticket 001: Handloom Weavers vs. Power Looms

## Event
The displacement of English handloom weavers by power looms, ~1810–1860. The canonical example of slow, bottom-up technological erosion.

## Mechanism
**Gradual erosion** (tech_tool)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Build a timeline of handloom weaver employment in England/Britain from ~1790 to ~1870. Key data points to find:
- Peak employment (~1810–1813, estimated 250K–300K cotton weavers)
- 1820, 1830, 1840, 1850, 1860 estimates
- Power loom counts at matching dates (for context)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Bythell, *The Handloom Weavers* (1969) — the definitive monograph
2. UK Census returns 1801, 1811, 1821, 1831, 1841, 1851 (occupational data begins properly in 1831)
3. Select Committee on Hand-Loom Weavers (1834–35) and Royal Commission (1838–41)
4. Parliamentary Papers and Blue Books
5. Thompson, *The Making of the English Working Class* (1963) — secondary but widely cited
6. Allen, "The British Industrial Revolution in Global Perspective" (2009)

### Income Data
Wage data for handloom weavers over time. The 25s/week (1800) → 5s/week (1838) narrative from the Royal Commission.

**Known caveats:** The Royal Commission data is advocacy — witnesses were disproportionately worst-off. The figures are directionally correct but the precision is false. Report the data AND the caveat.

**Source hierarchy:**
1. 1838 Royal Commission testimony (wages by region and cloth type)
2. Bythell wage tables
3. Boot, "Real Incomes of the British Middle Class" (1999) or comparable reconstructions
4. Bank of England historical CPI for inflation adjustment

### Where They Went
Track concurrent employment growth in Lancashire and Yorkshire for:
- Factory work (power loom tending)
- General laboring
- Mining
- Construction
- Poor Law / workhouse admissions

**Source hierarchy:**
1. UK Census occupational tables for Lancashire, 1831–1861
2. Factory inspectors' reports (annual from 1833)
3. Poor Law Commission records
4. Bythell ch. 8–9 for qualitative evidence

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Weavers who transitioned to power loom tending early (~1815–1825). Any named examples in Bythell or Thompson.
- **False movers:** Luddite strategy — machine-breaking as economic strategy. Named leaders if findable.
- **Deniers:** The widespread belief that power looms couldn't handle fine fabrics. When did this prove wrong?
- **Survival pattern:** Fine-fabric and specialty weavers survived longest. Quantify if possible.

### Unique Texture
Look for the most striking human details:
- Workhouse admission rates for weavers vs. general population in Lancashire
- The "golden age" wages of 1800 vs. the starvation wages of 1840 — the 80% pay cut while still employed
- Weaver communities that persisted and what happened to them
- The irony that factory wages were HIGHER than late-stage handloom wages

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Workforce timeline with at least 6 data points from 1790–1870
- Wage data showing the full decline curve (at least 4 data points)
- At least 3 successor occupations with rough percentage estimates
- Specific named examples for at least 2 of the 3 foresight categories
- Clear sourcing for every number
