# Vinyl Chloride → Liver Angiosarcoma

## Data

- slug: vinyl-chloride
- hazard: Vinyl chloride
- harm: Liver angiosarcoma
- category: exposure-limit
- early_evidence_year: 1971
- early_evidence_desc: Pier Luigi Viola published the first animal carcinogenicity study showing vinyl chloride caused tumors (skin carcinomas, lung tumors, osteochondromas) in rats exposed by inhalation (Cancer Research, May 1971). Cesare Maltoni's more comprehensive studies (1971-1973), funded by Montedison, ICI, Solvay, and Rhone-Progil, subsequently demonstrated liver angiosarcoma in rats at exposures as low as 250 ppm.
- consensus_year: 1974
- consensus_desc: IARC first evaluated vinyl chloride in 1974 and classified it as carcinogenic. The link between vinyl chloride and liver angiosarcoma was accepted with extraordinary speed after the Creech & Johnson human cases were reported in early 1974, corroborating Maltoni's animal data. Formal IARC Group 1 classification came in Volume 19 (1979).
- first_regulation_year: 1974
- first_regulation_country: United States
- first_regulation_desc: OSHA Emergency Temporary Standard issued April 5, 1974, reducing permissible exposure from 500 ppm to 50 ppm. Permanent standard followed October 4, 1974, setting PEL at 1 ppm (8-hour TWA) — a 500-fold reduction from the prior limit. Effective January 1, 1975.
- us_regulation_year: 1974
- us_regulation_desc: OSHA Emergency Temporary Standard (April 5, 1974) at 50 ppm; permanent standard (October 4, 1974) at 1 ppm TWA. EPA also banned vinyl chloride as aerosol propellant in 1974. The 1 ppm PEL (29 CFR 1910.1017) remains in effect today.

## Judgment calls

**Early evidence year (1971 vs. 1969 vs. 1959):** Viola presented unpublished data as early as 1969, and Dow Chemical had confidential internal data showing liver damage at 200 ppm in 1959. However, the plan specifies "credible scientific paper" as the threshold, not unpublished or proprietary data. Viola's 1971 published paper in Cancer Research is the first peer-reviewed evidence establishing vinyl chloride as a carcinogen. Used 1971.

**Why not 1974 for early evidence:** The Creech & Johnson human cases (January-February 1974) are often cited as the watershed moment, but Viola's 1971 animal study and Maltoni's 1972 findings (shared with industry) predate the human cases. The animal evidence was credible and specific — it just wasn't acted upon because industry suppressed it.

**Consensus year (1974):** The scientific community accepted the vinyl chloride-angiosarcoma link with unusual speed once the human cases were public. IARC evaluated it in 1974. The formal Group 1 classification came in 1979 (Volume 19), but consensus was functionally achieved in 1974. Used 1974 because the regulatory response was driven by immediate scientific agreement, not a delayed formal consensus process.

**First regulation (US, not Europe):** The US was the first mover via OSHA's April 1974 emergency standard. European countries set exposure limits in the 5-10 ppm range during the mid-1970s but generally followed the US action. The irony is that European manufacturers (Solvay, Montedison, ICI) had Maltoni's animal data by late 1972 — over a year before OSHA acted — but European regulators were not informed.

**Industry opposition (moderate):** The vinyl chloride industry suppressed Maltoni's findings (1972-1974) and initially claimed the 1 ppm standard would destroy the $14 billion PVC industry. However, unlike tobacco or lead, the opposition was relatively short-lived. Once the human cases were undeniable and the cancer type was pathognomonic, industry pivoted to compliance. They achieved the 1 ppm limit within a few years through closed-loop polymerization technology. Rated "moderate" rather than "strong" because the organized opposition collapsed quickly compared to multi-decade campaigns in other cases, even though the data suppression was serious.

**Substitute available (partial):** There is no substitute for PVC itself — the regulation was about reducing worker exposure, not banning the product. Engineering controls (closed-loop systems, improved ventilation) constituted a "partial" substitute for the prior open-air manufacturing process. Industry achieved compliance without catastrophic cost, contradicting their own predictions.

**Harm visibility (medium):** Liver angiosarcoma is an extremely rare cancer (normally ~25 cases/year in the US), making the cluster at B.F. Goodrich epidemiologically conspicuous. The rarity actually made the causal link clearer — it was almost a fingerprint for vinyl chloride exposure. However, the harm was still delayed (cancer, not acute poisoning), and cases only became visible after years of exposure. Rated "medium" because while the attribution was exceptionally clear once identified, the harm was not immediate or visible to workers during exposure.

**Why this was fast:** This is one of the fastest science-to-regulation transitions in the dataset (3 years from published evidence to emergency regulation). Key factors: (1) the cancer type was extremely rare and practically pathognomonic for vinyl chloride exposure, making denial implausible; (2) animal data preceded and corroborated the human cases; (3) the cases emerged during a period of activist regulatory posture at OSHA under the Nixon/Ford administration; (4) the target was an occupational exposure limit (narrow scope), not an industry-wide product ban.

**The mini-lag within the story:** There is a 2-3 year gap between Maltoni's animal findings (late 1972 industry briefing) and the emergency standard (April 1974). During this period, industry actively concealed the data from regulators and workers. The regulatory clock only started when the human cases forced public disclosure. This sub-story illustrates how industry information suppression can create additional lag even when the science-to-regulation pipeline is otherwise fast.
