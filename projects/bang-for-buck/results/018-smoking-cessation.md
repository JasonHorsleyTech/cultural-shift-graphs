# Result: Smoking Cessation (NRT, Varenicline, Counseling)

## Summary

Smoking cessation is arguably the single most cost-effective health intervention in modern medicine — for the ~30% of Bobs who smoke. At Bob's income level, smoking prevalence is 25-30% (vs ~10% in the top quartile), making this relevant to a substantial fraction of the target population. Quitting at age 35-44 gains approximately 9 life-years and avoids ~90% of excess smoking-related mortality. The annualized QALY gain of ~0.20 per year (for a successful quitter) makes this the highest-impact intervention on the chart — but the brutal reality is a ~20% success rate per quit attempt, and even lower in low-income populations. The cost can be literally zero via state quitlines that provide free NRT and counseling (1-800-QUIT-NOW), or $100-200/attempt with OTC nicotine patches. The cost-effectiveness ratio in published literature ranges from $128 to $5,100 per QALY — at every price point, dramatically below the $50,000-100,000/QALY willingness-to-pay threshold.

## QALY Evidence

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.20
- **Range:** 0.10 – 0.30 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** Jha et al. (NEJM 2013), AHRQ Cromwell et al. (1997), Igarashi et al. (Preventive Medicine 2021), CDC Smoking Cessation Report (2020)
- **Study type:** Large cohort analyses + multiple meta-analyses + cost-effectiveness analyses
- **Population studied:** General adult smokers, primarily ages 25-65 across US, UK, and pooled international cohorts
- **Population match to Bob:** Good — low-income smokers tend to smoke more heavily and have worse baseline health, meaning absolute QALY gains from cessation may be even larger. Multiple studies include socioeconomically diverse populations.
- **Key caveats:**
  - The 0.20/year figure is for a person who successfully quits permanently. The adherence rate captures the difficulty of achieving this.
  - QALY gains are not uniform across years — early years include withdrawal discomfort and modest weight gain (temporary QoL loss), while later years show large mortality and morbidity reductions.
  - Benefits are front-loaded in terms of cardiovascular risk (50% reduction within 1 year) but back-loaded for cancer risk (approaches non-smoker levels only after 15-20 years).
  - The estimate applies only to the ~30% of Bobs who currently smoke. For non-smokers, this intervention is irrelevant.

### QALY Derivation

**Mortality component:**
- Jha et al. (NEJM 2013): Smokers who quit at ages 35-44 gained ~9 life-years compared to those who continued smoking. This landmark study covered 217,000 adults in the US National Health Interview Survey with follow-up through the National Death Index.
- Quitting before age 40 reduces smoking-attributable mortality risk by ~90%.
- Igarashi et al. (2021): Exclusive cigarette smokers aged 25-29 who continue smoking lose ~8.1 QALYs over their lifetime compared to never-smokers.
- Quitting at 40 recovers an estimated 6-7 lifetime QALYs (undiscounted) from mortality reduction alone.
- Annualized over ~35 remaining years: 6.5 / 35 ≈ 0.19 QALYs/year from mortality.

**Quality-of-life component:**
- EQ-5D studies consistently show former smokers have 0.03-0.05 higher utility scores than current smokers after the initial withdrawal period.
- This represents immediate, ongoing QoL improvements from better respiratory function, cardiovascular fitness, taste/smell restoration, and reduced chronic disease burden.
- Adds ~0.03-0.05 QALY/year on top of mortality gains, but with overlap (some of the EQ-5D difference reflects anticipated mortality risk), so net additional: ~0.01-0.03/year.

**Synthesis:**
- Mortality-based estimate: ~0.19/year
- QoL adjustment: +0.01-0.03/year
- Early-years withdrawal/weight gain penalty: -0.01-0.02/year (temporary, first 1-2 years)
- Averaged over remaining lifetime: ~0.20/year (range: 0.10-0.30)
- Lower bound (0.10) accounts for lighter smokers, partial relapse, and conservative discounting
- Upper bound (0.30) accounts for heavy smokers (1+ pack/day) and Bob's worse baseline health

### Comparison to cost-effectiveness literature

Published cost-per-QALY ratios provide a consistency check:
- AHRQ (Cromwell et al.): Smoking cessation interventions range from $1,108 to $4,542/QALY
- Varenicline vs. unaided cessation: ~€320/QALY (Bolin et al.)
- Brief physician advice: $1,240-$3,620/QALY
- NRT + counseling: $1,133-$1,774/QALY
- All ratios indicate extremely high cost-effectiveness, consistent with large QALY gains at modest cost.

## Cost Analysis (for Bob at ~$30k household income)

- **Annual direct cost:** $150 (amortized cost of quit attempts over 3 years)
- **One-time startup cost:** $0 (no equipment needed)
- **Startup cost amortized over 3 years:** $0/year
- **Total annual cost (best estimate):** $150
- **Cost range:** $0 – $500 (low – high)
- **With insurance (if applicable):** $0 (ACA mandates coverage of smoking cessation medications and counseling at no cost-sharing)
- **Without insurance:** $0-$500 (depending on pathway and number of attempts)
- **Cost notes:**
  - **Free pathway (quitline):** State quitlines (1-800-QUIT-NOW) are available in all 50 states plus DC, Guam, and Puerto Rico. Many states provide 8 weeks of free NRT (patches, gum, or lozenges) to eligible callers. Counseling is always free. Total cost: $0.
  - **OTC NRT pathway:** Store-brand nicotine patches at Walmart/CVS run ~$25-35 per 14-patch box. An 8-week step-down course requires ~4 boxes: $100-140. Nicotine gum (store brand): ~$25-40 per 100-piece box, 8-12 week supply ~$75-120. GoodRx prices as low as $11 for some NRT products.
  - **Generic varenicline pathway:** Retail price without insurance: ~$630 for starter pack + ~$780 for remaining 2 months = ~$1,400 for a 12-week course. With GoodRx/SingleCare coupons: as low as $30 for 60 tablets, making a full course ~$60-180. Plus FQHC visit for prescription: $35-50. Total with discount: $95-230.
  - **Expected cost per successful quit:** Most smokers require 3-7 quit attempts before succeeding with pharmacotherapy support. Blending free (quitline) and paid (OTC NRT) attempts, expected total cost to successfully quit: $200-750. Amortized over 3 years: $67-250/year.
  - **Money saved by quitting:** A pack-a-day smoker at ~$8/pack spends ~$2,920/year on cigarettes. Even a half-pack/day smoker saves ~$1,460/year. The intervention pays for itself many times over, but these savings are not reflected in the cost figure per plan methodology.

- **Category:** Low (<1 hr/wk)

## Access and Barriers

- **Activation energy:** High
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - **Addiction severity:** Nicotine dependence is one of the strongest substance addictions. Low-income smokers tend to smoke more heavily and have higher nicotine dependence scores.
  - **Stress as relapse trigger:** Bob's life involves more financial stress, job insecurity, and environmental stressors — all major relapse triggers. Financial strain mediates the relationship between socioeconomic status and continued smoking.
  - **Social environment:** Smoking rates are higher in Bob's social network. Peer smoking is a powerful cue and barrier to cessation.
  - **Low motivation and self-efficacy:** Lower socioeconomic groups report lower confidence in ability to quit and lower motivation to attempt quitting.
  - **Incomplete medication courses:** Low-income smokers are less likely to complete full pharmacotherapy courses. One-fifth of smokers who receive medication prescriptions fail to fill them.
  - **Targeted marketing:** Tobacco companies disproportionately market to low-income communities.
  - **Neighborhood environment:** Greater proximity to tobacco outlets, lower social cohesion in neighborhoods.
  - **Positive access factors:** Quitlines are phone-based (no transportation needed), NRT is OTC (no prescription for patches/gum/lozenges), and the free pathway has genuinely zero financial barriers.

## Side Effects and Risks

- **Nicotine withdrawal symptoms** (irritability, anxiety, difficulty concentrating, increased appetite): Peaks at 2-4 days, largely resolves within 2-4 weeks. Managed but not eliminated by NRT.
- **Weight gain:** Average 4-5 kg (8-11 lbs) in the first year after quitting. Partially offset by improved metabolic health markers. More pronounced in lower-income populations with limited dietary options.
- **NRT side effects:** Skin irritation (patches, 30-50% of users), jaw soreness/hiccups (gum), throat irritation (lozenges). All mild and self-limiting.
- **Varenicline side effects:** Nausea (25-30%, usually mild), insomnia (10-15%), vivid dreams (10-15%). The FDA removed the black box warning for neuropsychiatric events in 2016 after the EAGLES trial (8,144 participants) found no significant increase in neuropsychiatric adverse events vs. placebo. Brief varenicline recall (2021) due to nitrosamine impurity; generics re-entered the market.
- **Psychological distress from failed attempts:** Repeated unsuccessful quit attempts can reduce self-efficacy and increase hopelessness. This is a real but unmeasured harm.

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate:** 0.20 (20% achieve 12-month sustained abstinence with pharmacotherapy + behavioral support)
- **Source:** CDC (2020), Cochrane Reviews of NRT (2018), Cahill et al. Cochrane Review of Varenicline (2016)
- **Notes:**
  - Cold turkey quit rate: 3-5% at 12 months
  - NRT alone: 10-15% at 12 months
  - NRT + quitline counseling: 15-20% at 12 months
  - Varenicline: 25-33% at 12 months (clinical trials); real-world primary care: ~28.8% at 2 years
  - Low-income populations have lower quit rates: 2-4% per attempt in some socioeconomically disadvantaged cohorts, though this improves significantly with medication and structured support
  - Median number of attempts before permanent cessation: 6-30 (estimates vary widely)
  - The 20% figure represents a weighted average across methods and populations, acknowledging that Bob's socioeconomic barriers reduce success below clinical trial rates but that pharmacotherapy substantially improves on cold turkey rates
  - Adherence to medication course itself: ~30-50% complete full recommended course of NRT or varenicline

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 1
- **Tier 1** = Multiple RCTs or high-quality meta-analyses, Bob-adjacent populations
- **Key studies:**
  - Jha P, et al. "21st-Century Hazards of Smoking and Benefits of Cessation in the United States." NEJM 2013; 368:341-350. (217,000 US adults, mortality follow-up)
  - Cromwell J, et al. "Cost-effectiveness of the clinical practice recommendations in the AHCPR guideline for smoking cessation." JAMA 1997; 278(21):1759-66. (Foundational CEA)
  - Cahill K, et al. "Nicotine receptor partial agonists for smoking cessation." Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2016. (Varenicline meta-analysis)
  - Hartmann-Boyce J, et al. "Nicotine replacement therapy versus control for smoking cessation." Cochrane Database Syst Rev 2018. (NRT meta-analysis)
  - Anthenelli RM, et al. "Neuropsychiatric safety and efficacy of varenicline, bupropion, and nicotine patch (EAGLES trial)." Lancet 2016; 387:2507-20. (8,144 participants, safety)
- **Limitations:**
  - Long-term QALY gains are modeled from mortality cohort data, not directly measured via EQ-5D/SF-6D in cessation trials
  - Low-income-specific cessation trial data is limited; most RCTs draw from general clinical populations
  - Multiple quit attempts make per-attempt success rates hard to translate into lifetime probability estimates
  - Interaction effects with Bob's other potential interventions (exercise, SSRIs, diet) are unstudied
