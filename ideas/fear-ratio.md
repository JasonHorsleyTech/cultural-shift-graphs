# Fear Ratio

A graph of how many times Americans collectively think about a fear vs. how many people actually die from it. The ratio itself is the data point.

## The metric

**Fear-thoughts-per-actual-death.**

Take 300 million Americans. Estimate how frequently the average person thinks about a given fear (sharks, terrorism, school shootings, heart disease, car accidents). Multiply frequency by population to get total annual "fear thoughts." Divide by actual annual deaths from that cause.

Sharks: maybe everyone thinks about it once a year = 300M fear-thoughts. ~5 deaths/year in the US. Ratio: 60,000,000 to 1.

Heart disease: maybe only people over 50 think about it regularly, and even then not often. But 700,000 deaths/year. Ratio would be shockingly low — possibly inverted (more deaths than fear-thoughts).

## Data sources

- **Fear frequency:** Gallup annual worry polls, Chapman University Survey of American Fears (asks what people worry about and how often), other survey data on perceived risk
- **Actual deaths:** CDC WONDER database, actuarial tables. Easy and authoritative.

Won't get exact thought-counts but can estimate "% of population that worries about X frequently" and multiply out to annual fear-thought estimates.

## Examples to research

High ratio (lots of fear, few deaths):
- Sharks
- Terrorism
- School shootings / mass shootings
- Plane crashes
- Kidnapping (stranger danger)
- Nuclear war
- Snake bites
- Lightning

Low ratio (few fears, lots of deaths):
- Heart disease
- Car accidents
- Falls (especially elderly)
- Diabetes
- Stroke
- Drowning (non-ocean, like bathtubs and pools)
- Medical errors
- Opioid overdose (before it became news)
- Antibiotic-resistant infections

## Graph concept

Log-scale bar chart. Each bar is the fear-to-death ratio for one cause. Sharks and terrorism towering on one end, heart disease and car accidents barely visible on the other. The visual punchline: we spend our fear budget on the wrong things.

## Tone

Not preachy. Not "you're an idiot for being afraid of sharks." More like: here's what your brain is doing, and it's funny.

## Open questions

- Exact proxy for "fear thoughts" — survey data on worry frequency is probably the best available
- Whether to include things people SHOULD fear more (medical errors, falls)
- Time axis or just snapshot? Could be interesting to show how terrorism fear spiked post-9/11 and then decayed while actual risk was near-zero the whole time
