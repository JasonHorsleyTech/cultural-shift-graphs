# Result: Bob's Mental Health and Social Baseline

## Source
- **Primary sources:** NHANES (Aug 2021–Aug 2023), CDC BRFSS (2022), SAMHSA NSDUH (2022–2023), NHIS (2020–2022), CDC MMWR (2024)
- **Date accessed:** 2026-05-01

## Findings

Bob — our 25th-percentile-income composite American (~$30,000 household income, roughly 100–200% of the federal poverty level) — faces substantially worse mental health outcomes than higher-income Americans across nearly every dimension measured. Depression prevalence is 2–3x higher than the top income quartile, anxiety symptoms follow a similar gradient, and social isolation is dramatically elevated. The one counterintuitive finding: binge drinking *prevalence* is actually lower in Bob's bracket than among higher earners, though when Bob does drink heavily, he drinks more per occasion and suffers worse health consequences.

### Depression

Depression is the most robustly documented mental health disparity by income. NHANES data using the PHQ-9 screener (score ≥10) for Aug 2021–Aug 2023 shows a steep income gradient:

| Family Income (% FPL) | Depression Prevalence | Female | Male |
|---|---|---|---|
| <100% FPL | 22.1% | 26.0% | 17.4% |
| 100–199% FPL | 19.2% | 24.2% | 13.1% |
| 200–399% FPL | 13.1% | 14.4% | 11.7% |
| ≥400% FPL | 7.4% | 8.8% | 6.1% |

Bob's bracket (roughly 100–200% FPL) has a depression prevalence of ~19%, approximately 2.6x the rate of the highest income group. Overall depression prevalence has risen from 8.2% to 13.1% between 2013–2014 and 2021–2023.

Among adults with household income <$25,000, 32.0% report a history of depression (CDC MMWR 2024, using BRFSS data).

### Anxiety

Anxiety follows a similar income gradient, though the data is less granular by income bracket:

- Overall adult anxiety symptom prevalence (any symptoms, GAD-7): 18.2% in 2022, up from 15.6% in 2019 (NHIS 2022).
- Adults with family income <100% FPL have the highest anxiety symptom prevalence, with a significant linear trend across income categories.
- Adults with household income ≤$19,999 have 2.06x the odds (95% CI: 1.17–3.63) of screening positive for generalized anxiety disorder compared to those earning ≥$75,000.
- Among adults with household income <$25,000: 19.4% report feelings of worry, nervousness, or anxiety frequently, compared to 12.7% nationally (CDC 2022).

### Frequent Mental Distress

BRFSS defines "frequent mental distress" as ≥14 poor mental health days in the past 30 days:

- Households <$20,000/year: **31.0%** experience frequent mental distress
- Households ≥$100,000/year: **9.5%**
- National average (2022): 15.9%
- Among adults with household income <$25,000: **27.2%** report frequent mental distress (CDC MMWR 2024)

This is perhaps the most striking disparity: low-income adults are 3x more likely to experience persistent mental distress than high-income adults.

### Serious Mental Illness

SAMHSA/NSDUH data on serious mental illness (SMI) — defined as a mental, behavioral, or emotional disorder resulting in serious functional impairment — shows a clear poverty gradient among adults aged 26+:

| Poverty Status | SMI Prevalence |
|---|---|
| Below poverty line | 7.5% |
| At poverty line | 4.1% |
| Above poverty line | 3.1% |

Adults below the poverty line are 2.4x more likely to have SMI. Among the estimated 9.8 million adults with SMI nationally, 2.5 million (25.5%) live below the poverty line. Note: the most detailed income-stratified SMI data available is from the 2015 NSDUH spotlight; the overall 2023 NSDUH reports 5.0% overall SMI prevalence among adults but does not publish income-stratified estimates in its summary reports.

### Social Isolation and Loneliness

Loneliness and social disconnection show some of the largest income-based disparities of any mental health indicator:

**Loneliness (CDC MMWR 2024, BRFSS 2022):**

| Household Income | Loneliness Prevalence | Lack of Social/Emotional Support |
|---|---|---|
| <$25,000 | 47.9% | 39.8% |
| $100,000–$149,999 | 23.6% | 14.6% |
| ≥$150,000 | 21.2% | 13.8% |

Nearly half of adults in Bob's income bracket report loneliness — more than double the rate of high earners.

**Time spent socially isolated (ATUS analysis, 2010–2019):**

| Annual Household Income | Social Isolation (min/day) | Family Engagement (min/day) |
|---|---|---|
| <$25,000 | 245 | 130 |
| $25,000–$50,000 | 194 | 166 |
| $50,000–$100,000 | 169 | 179 |
| ≥$100,000 | 147 | 198 |

The lowest income group spends an additional 596 hours/year in social isolation compared to the highest. The lowest income group also experienced the largest decline in companionship over the study period (377-hour decline). Notably, low-income Americans spend slightly *more* time with neighbors/acquaintances/coworkers (~82 min/day vs ~72 min/day), suggesting their social networks are structured differently — more peripheral contacts, fewer deep family/friend relationships.

### Substance Use

**Alcohol — the counterintuitive pattern:**
- Binge drinking *prevalence* is actually **higher** among higher-income adults ($75,000+). Lower-income adults report lower rates of binge drinking.
- However, among those who do binge drink, lower-income adults drink **more per occasion** (higher intensity/frequency) and suffer disproportionate harm.
- Lower SES is associated with 66% increased alcohol-related mortality in men and 78% in women.
- This "alcohol harm paradox" — less drinking overall but worse outcomes — is a well-documented phenomenon driven by fewer protective factors, worse baseline health, and less access to treatment.

**Smoking:**
- Below poverty threshold: ~19.5% current smokers
- <$35,000 household income: 21.4%
- $35,000–$74,999: 15.7%
- $75,000–$99,999: 11.4%
- ≥$100,000: 7.1%
- Smoking rate disparities by income are *widening* in recent birth cohorts despite overall declines.

**Illicit drug use:**
- NSDUH collects substance use data by income level in its detailed tables, but the summary reports do not publish income-stratified prevalence. General population rates: 19.4% of adults used illicit drugs in the past year (2023 NSDUH). Lower-income populations have higher rates of substance use disorder (SUD) even when overall use rates are similar or lower — paralleling the alcohol harm paradox.

### Mental Health Treatment Access

- Among "near poor" adults (100–200% FPL), approximately 23% received mental health treatment in 2023.
- 25% of adults with any mental illness (AMI) reported unmet need for treatment (2022–2023).
- Cost is the dominant barrier: 42% of those with unmet needs cited cost (2022); 59.8% said treatment would "cost too much" (2023).
- Among those with moderate-to-severe depression symptoms: 54.8% of *uninsured* adults avoided care due to affordability (vs 30.2% with commercial insurance).
- 9.2% of adults with AMI are uninsured (5+ million people).

For Bob specifically: if uninsured, he faces a >50% chance of avoiding needed mental health care due to cost. Even if insured (likely Medicaid at his income level), 17% report insurance doesn't cover enough.

### Sleep

Sleep difficulties show a clear income gradient:

**Trouble falling asleep (most days/every day, past 30 days — NHIS 2020):**
- <100% FPL: 21.9%
- ≥200% FPL: 12.6%

**Trouble staying asleep:**
- <100% FPL: 22.1%
- ≥200% FPL: 16.9%

**Insufficient sleep (<7 hours, NHANES 2017–2020):**
- Low-income (PIR <1) vs high-income: 22% higher prevalence on workdays, **108% higher on non-workdays**
- Food insecurity amplifies the effect: very low food security associated with 50% higher insufficient sleep (workdays) and 129% higher (non-workdays) vs food-secure adults.

The non-workday disparity is particularly striking — it suggests low-income sleep problems aren't just about work schedules but reflect chronic stress, poor sleep environments, and other structural factors.

### Suicide Risk

- Poverty independently increases odds of first-time suicide ideation or attempt by 35% (AOR=1.35, 95% CI: 1.05–1.73) after adjusting for demographics and psychiatric factors.
- 5.0% of adults had serious thoughts of suicide in 2023 (general population, NSDUH).
- County-level data: counties in the highest income tertile had suicide rates of 13.5/100,000 vs 15.2/100,000 in the lowest tertile (13% lower).
- The income-suicide relationship is strongest among American Indian/Alaska Native populations (rate ratio 0.49) and weakest among women (rate ratio 0.98).
- Among adults with household income <$25,000: stress prevalence is 24.1% (CDC MMWR 2024).

### Cognitive Function

Limited income-stratified data is available for cognitive difficulty. The Census ACS collects self-reported "serious difficulty concentrating, remembering, or making decisions" but published cross-tabulations by income are sparse. General evidence suggests lower SES is associated with worse executive function outcomes, likely mediated by chronic stress, sleep deprivation, and untreated mental health conditions — but quantifying this specifically for Bob's bracket would require direct analysis of ACS microdata.

## Gaps and Caveats

- **Anxiety data by FPL bracket:** The NHIS/NHSR 213 report documents a clear income gradient for GAD-7 anxiety symptoms but the specific percentages by FPL bracket are locked in supplementary tables not available through the web summary. The OR of 2.06 for the lowest vs highest income group is the best available summary statistic.
- **Substance use by income:** NSDUH detailed tables contain income-stratified substance use data, but the summary reports don't publish these breakdowns. The "alcohol harm paradox" (lower prevalence but worse outcomes) is well-documented but complicates simple prevalence comparisons.
- **SMI data currency:** The most detailed income-stratified SMI data (SAMHSA spotlight) uses 2015 NSDUH data. More recent NSDUH cycles collect this data but publish it only in detailed tables, not summary reports. The gradient is likely similar or steeper given rising inequality.
- **Cognitive function:** No nationally representative income-stratified cognitive function data was found in published summaries. This remains a gap.
- **Treatment data limitations:** Mental health treatment rates by income are complicated by the fact that lower-income adults are simultaneously more likely to need treatment AND less likely to receive it. The 23% treatment rate for "near poor" adults should be interpreted alongside the 19%+ depression prevalence — many who need treatment aren't getting it.
- **COVID-era shifts:** Much of this data spans 2020–2023 and reflects pandemic-era mental health impacts, which fell disproportionately on lower-income Americans. Pre-pandemic disparities were already significant but somewhat smaller.
- **Bob's bracket ambiguity:** At $30K household income, Bob falls roughly at 100–200% FPL (depending on household size). Some data sources use income brackets ($20K, $25K, $35K thresholds) while others use FPL ratios. We've presented both where available, but there is inherent imprecision in mapping between systems.

## Bob's Composite Mental Health Profile

Synthesizing across all sources, Bob's baseline mental health profile:

- **~19% probability of current depression** (PHQ-9 ≥10), ~32% lifetime history
- **~2x the odds of clinical anxiety** vs the highest income bracket
- **~27–31% chance of frequent mental distress** (14+ bad days/month)
- **~48% likely experiencing loneliness**; ~40% lacking adequate social support
- **~4 hours/day spent in social isolation** (vs ~2.5 hours for high earners)
- **~20% chance of being a current smoker** (vs ~7% for ≥$100K)
- **Lower binge drinking prevalence** than high earners, but worse outcomes when he does drink
- **~22% prevalence of sleep difficulties** (trouble falling/staying asleep)
- **~23% chance of receiving mental health treatment** if he has a diagnosable condition
- **>50% chance of avoiding needed MH care due to cost** if uninsured
- **35% elevated risk of suicidal ideation** relative to above-poverty adults
- **7.5% SMI prevalence** if below poverty (vs 3.1% above)
