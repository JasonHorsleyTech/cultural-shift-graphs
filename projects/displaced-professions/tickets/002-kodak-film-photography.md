# Ticket 002: Kodak / Film Photography Workers

## Event
The collapse of film photography employment as digital cameras crossed a quality/price threshold, ~2003–2012. A platform shift, not a tool improvement — digital isn't a better film camera, it's an entirely different paradigm.

## Mechanism
**Tipping point** (tech_platform)

## What to Research

### Workforce Timeline
Track Kodak employment AND broader film photography industry employment (film manufacturing, processing, retail photo labs).

- Kodak peak: ~145,000 employees (1988). Track from 1985 to 2015.
- Fujifilm employment for comparison (they pivoted — interesting contrast)
- US photo finishing industry employment (SIC/NAICS codes for photofinishing labs)
- Key dates: digital camera sales surpass film (2003), Kodak stops making film cameras (2004), Kodak bankruptcy (2012)

**Source hierarchy:**
1. Kodak annual reports and SEC filings (10-K) — employee counts are reported
2. BLS Occupational Employment Statistics, NAICS 812921 (Photofinishing Laboratories) and 333316 (Photographic equipment manufacturing)
3. Photo Marketing Association International (PMAI) industry reports
4. Academic case studies (e.g., Lucas & Goh, "Disruptive technology: How Kodak missed the digital photography revolution," 2009)

### Income Data
BLS wage data for relevant SOC codes. This is a post-1950 US event — clean data should exist.

- Median wages for photographic process workers (SOC 51-9151)
- Kodak-specific compensation data from annual reports if available
- Track whether survivors' wages went up (as with travel agents) or stayed flat

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS OES data, SOC 51-9151 and related codes
2. Kodak SEC filings for average compensation
3. Rochester, NY area wage data (Kodak was the dominant employer)

### Where They Went
Rochester, NY is the key geography — Kodak was the city's largest employer.

- Track Rochester employment by industry 2000–2015
- What industries absorbed ex-Kodak workers? (Healthcare, education, tech?)
- Fujifilm's pivot to medical imaging, cosmetics — did they retain workers?
- Rochester unemployment rate vs. national average

**Source hierarchy:**
1. BLS Local Area Unemployment Statistics for Rochester MSA
2. Census Bureau County Business Patterns for Monroe County, NY
3. Rochester Democrat and Chronicle reporting on Kodak layoffs
4. Academic studies on Rochester's economic transition

### Foresight Stories
- **Adapters:** Fujifilm (pivoted to medical imaging, cosmetics, materials). Kodak employees who left for digital companies pre-collapse.
- **False movers:** Kodak's own digital camera division (they invented the digital camera in 1975! but couldn't cannibalize their film business).
- **Deniers:** Kodak leadership quotes about film's staying power. The famous internal memo situations.
- **Survival pattern:** What distinguished Kodak employees who landed well from those who didn't?

### Unique Texture
- Kodak invented the digital camera (Steve Sasson, 1975) but suppressed it — the ultimate denier story
- Rochester's "company town" dynamics — housing prices, population change
- The speed: 145K employees → bankruptcy in ~24 years, but the real collapse was ~9 years (2003–2012)
- Fujifilm vs. Kodak as a natural experiment in adaptation vs. denial

## What Good Data Looks Like
- Annual Kodak headcount from 1985–2015
- BLS industry employment data for photo processing/manufacturing
- Rochester MSA employment data showing sector shifts
- Fujifilm employment/revenue data for contrast
- Clear sourcing, especially for the "invented the digital camera" narrative
