# Ticket 028: Ultra-Processed Food → Health Outcomes

## Objective

Research the timeline from scientific evidence that ultra-processed food (UPF) consumption is associated with adverse health outcomes to any binding regulation restricting UPF. This is an EARLY-STAGE in-progress case — consensus is forming but regulation is minimal.

## What to research

1. **Early evidence date** — Carlos Monteiro introduced the NOVA food classification system in 2009, categorizing foods by degree of processing. Large cohort studies linking UPF to health outcomes emerged from ~2018 onward (NutriNet-Santé cohort, UK Biobank analyses). When did credible evidence linking UPF to disease emerge?

2. **Consensus date** — Is there formal consensus yet? WHO has addressed UPF in some documents. The FAO acknowledged NOVA. Some national dietary guidelines mention processing levels (Brazil 2014, France). Look for the strongest formal scientific body statement. This may still be "null" if no formal consensus statement exists.

3. **First OECD regulation** — Brazil's dietary guidelines (2014) recommend avoiding UPF — but are these "binding regulation" or dietary advice? Chile's food labeling and marketing laws (2016) restrict marketing of foods high in sugar/fat/sodium (many UPFs) but don't use the UPF category. Is there ANY binding regulation specifically targeting UPF as a category?

4. **US regulation** — No federal UPF-specific regulation. Dietary Guidelines Advisory Committee has discussed processing levels but the official guidelines haven't adopted the concept.

5. **Current status** — Document where the science-to-action process currently stands. Is this in the "evidence accumulating" phase, the "consensus forming" phase, or the "regulation being debated" phase?

## Where to look

1. Monteiro et al. (2009) — NOVA classification system
2. Srour et al. (2019) — NutriNet-Santé cohort study on UPF and cardiovascular disease
3. Monteiro et al. (2019) — "Ultra-processed foods: what they are and how to identify them" (Public Health Nutrition)
4. Brazil dietary guidelines (2014) — Ministry of Health
5. Chile food labeling law (Ley 20.606, 2012/2016)
6. WHO/FAO discussions of food processing classification

## What "good data" looks like

- Honest characterization of whether consensus EXISTS yet (it may not)
- Clear distinction between dietary guidelines (advisory) and binding regulation
- The current position on the science-to-action timeline — "still in evidence phase" or "consensus forming" or "early regulation"
- regulation_status: in-progress or none.

## Known pitfalls

- "Ultra-processed food" is a CATEGORY, not a substance. You can't "ban" UPF the way you ban a chemical. This makes regulation structurally different — it requires classification systems, not substance identification.
- The evidence is largely observational (cohort studies), not experimental. Confounding is a serious concern — UPF consumers differ from non-UPF consumers in many ways. This may be why consensus is slow to form.
- Category: dietary-policy (if any regulation exists) or "none" if there's truly no binding regulation.
- This is the dataset's most speculative case. It's included to show WHERE a hazard sits EARLY in the science-to-action pipeline.
