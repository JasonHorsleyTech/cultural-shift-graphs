# Coca-Cola Global Expansion

## Summary
Coca-Cola's geographic spread is one of the cleanest single-brand measures of American commercial-cultural penetration. From a single Atlanta pharmacy in 1886 to presence in every sovereign nation except Cuba and North Korea by 2012, Coca-Cola's expansion curve closely tracks American global influence. Key inflection points include the Woodruff-era international push (1920s), the WWII military bottling program (64 overseas plants built to supply troops, then kept open), the post-colonial catch-up period (1960s–70s), and the post–Cold War surge into former communist states (1990s). The brand's near-universal presence today (99% of sovereign states) makes it the most geographically distributed American consumer product in history.

## Penetration Score Definition
**Denominator:** Percentage of the world's recognized independent sovereign states where Coca-Cola has regular commercial operations (bottling or authorized distribution — not merely grey-market imports).
**0% anchor:** Coca-Cola exists only within the United States; no other nation has ever seen the product.
**100% anchor:** Every sovereign state on Earth has active Coca-Cola commercial operations.

## Methodology
The penetration score is computed as: (number of sovereign states with regular Coca-Cola commercial operations) / (total recognized sovereign states in that year) × 100.

**Numerator:** Countries where Coca-Cola has authorized bottling, distribution, or regular commercial sales. Excludes grey-market imports and one-off exports. For pre-1940 data, the distinction between "export market" and "bottling operation" blurs — the numerator includes countries with either regular imports or local bottling.

**Denominator:** Widely recognized independent sovereign states at each time point. This count changes significantly over time: ~50 in 1900 (many regions were colonies), ~70 by 1930, ~82 by 1950, ~115 by 1960 (African decolonization), ~140 by 1970, ~160 by 1980, ~192 by 2000, ~195 by 2010. Sources: UN membership records supplemented with non-member recognized states.

**Key methodological notes:**
- The 1931 figure of "76 countries" (Dalzell) and the 1939 figure of "44 countries" (multiple sources) appear contradictory. The 76 likely includes colonies and territories counted as separate markets (e.g., "British India," "French West Africa"), while 44 counts sovereign states with bottling operations. The Depression also likely closed marginal operations. I use 30 sovereign states for 1930 as a conservative middle estimate.
- The apparent penetration plateau/dip from 1950–1960 is real: decolonization created new sovereign states faster than Coca-Cola could enter them. The denominator grew by ~40% while the numerator grew by ~38%.
- Company claims of "200+ countries" include territories, dependencies, and sub-national entities (Puerto Rico, Hong Kong, etc.). The sovereign-state count is lower.
- The 99% ceiling (not 100%) reflects the Cuba/North Korea holdouts, both due to US trade embargoes rather than Coca-Cola's unwillingness to enter.

## Notes
- **WWII as commercial-military symbiosis:** The 64 overseas bottling plants built during WWII are perhaps the most striking example of American military expansion doubling as commercial expansion. Eisenhower's 1943 order turned the US military logistics chain into a Coca-Cola distribution network. The plants were "temporary" but most converted to permanent civilian operations — by 1950 they were supplying local markets, not soldiers.
- **"Cocacolonization" as evidence of impact:** The fact that the French Communist Party coined a term for Coca-Cola's expansion (1949) and threatened to "barricade Paris" to keep it out is itself a data point. Countries don't coin defensive neologisms for culturally inert products.
- **India's 16-year ban (1977–1993):** India expelled Coca-Cola over trade secret disclosure requirements. During the ban, local brands (Thums Up, Gold Spot, Limca) thrived. Upon re-entry in 1993, Coca-Cola immediately acquired these brands — buying the competition that had grown in its absence.
- **China re-entry (1979):** Coca-Cola was the first American consumer brand to re-enter China after the 1978 opening. Originally present in Shanghai and Tianjin from 1927–1949, the brand returned via a deal with China's national food import/export corporation. The first 3,000 cases were shipped by train from Hong Kong to Beijing.
- **The 99% ceiling:** The final 1% (Cuba, North Korea) is unreachable not because Coca-Cola lacks the will but because US trade embargoes prohibit it. This makes the ceiling a measure of American geopolitical limits, not brand reach. Grey-market Coca-Cola reportedly exists in both countries.
- **Brand recognition vs. operations:** Coca-Cola claims 94% global logo recognition. The geographic-presence metric (99% of nations) may actually understate cultural penetration — the brand is recognized even in places it cannot legally sell.
- **Depth vs. breadth:** This metric measures only geographic breadth (% of countries). It does not capture depth of penetration. Mexico has higher per-capita Coca-Cola consumption than the United States. A depth metric would tell a different story, but the ticket scope is geographic spread.
