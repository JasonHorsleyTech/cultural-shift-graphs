# Result: AMOC Collapse — Effects on US Climate and Agriculture

## Summary

AMOC collapse remains one of the most debated climate tipping points. Current measurements show AMOC has weakened ~15% from pre-industrial levels, with deep transport at 26°N declining from ~20 Sv to ~15 Sv over 2004–2023. Collapse probability estimates range from 25–70% by 2100 depending on emissions scenario (Drijfhout et al. 2025), though a 2025 Nature paper argues Southern Ocean dynamics make full collapse unlikely. For the US specifically, AMOC collapse would cause moderate cooling in the Northeast (3–5°C without greenhouse offset), rapid East Coast sea level rise (~50 cm additional), and significant but not civilizational agricultural disruption — the US is less dependent on Gulf Stream warmth than Europe, making this a compounding stressor rather than a standalone extinction-level event.

## Data Gaps

- **US-specific temperature modeling under AMOC collapse is a major gap.** Most studies quantify European impacts in detail but treat North America as secondary. The 3–5°C Northeast cooling figure is inferred from North Atlantic SST anomaly proximity, not direct US land surface modeling. The net effect (AMOC cooling vs. greenhouse warming) is even more uncertain.
- **US agricultural yield impact has not been directly modeled.** The Global Tipping Points Report explicitly flags US and China studies as "essential" but not yet conducted. The -10% US estimate above is my extrapolation from global figures, noting the US is less exposed than Europe (further from North Atlantic SST anomalies, more diverse agricultural geography).
- **US growing season changes are not in the literature.** UK data (Ritchie et al. 2020) shows growing season rainfall dropping ~123 mm and arable land suitability dropping from 32% to 7%, but translating this to US regions is speculative.
- **Collapse vs. weakening distinction is critical.** Portmann et al. (2026) project 51% weakening by 2100 as the observationally constrained central estimate. A 50% weakening has very different consequences than a full collapse (shutdown to <5 Sv). Most impact studies model full collapse, not intermediate weakening states.
- **The scientific debate is genuinely unresolved.** Baker et al. (2025, Nature) present a credible physical mechanism (Southern Ocean upwelling) for why full collapse may not occur. An April 2026 arXiv preprint shows Ditlevsen's statistical method produces spurious mid-century predictions even from non-tipping data. Meanwhile, four mooring arrays show "meridionally consistent" weakening, and Portmann (2026) finds weakening 60% faster than unconstrained models predict.
- **AMOC collapse timeline is not a clean probability.** It depends enormously on emissions pathway and Greenland melt rates, neither of which can be predicted with confidence.

## Scientific Debate Summary

Three camps exist in the post-2023 literature:

**Camp A — Collapse is more likely than IPCC says:**
- Van Westen et al. (2024, Science Advances): Physics-based early warning shows AMOC "on tipping course," threshold around 2.5°C global warming
- Drijfhout et al. (2025, Env. Research Letters): 70% collapse probability under high emissions, 37% mid-range
- Portmann et al. (2026, Science Advances): Observationally constrained projections show 51±8% weakening by 2100, 60% stronger than multi-model mean
- Multiple mooring arrays show consistent weakening since early 2000s

**Camp B — Collapse is unlikely this century:**
- Baker et al. (2025, Nature): Southern Ocean upwelling sustains AMOC even under 4x CO2 and massive freshwater forcing
- IPCC AR6 (2021): "Medium confidence" no abrupt collapse before 2100

**Camp C — Timeline predictions are unreliable:**
- April 2026 arXiv preprint: Ditlevsen's method produces spurious predictions; with full uncertainty propagation, collapse estimates extend millennia
- SST fingerprints as AMOC proxies introduce large structural uncertainty

## Relevance to Scenario 8 (Climate Cascades)

AMOC collapse is a major component of the climate cascade scenario but is **not civilizational on its own for the US.** Key points for the graph:

1. **The US is less exposed than Europe.** European agriculture depends on Gulf Stream warmth far more than US agriculture. European crop losses (~30%) would dwarf US losses.
2. **The US impact is primarily through second-order effects:** global food price spikes (30–100%), trade disruption, East Coast sea level rise, and cascading ecological shifts (Amazon die-back, Sahel desertification reducing global food supply).
3. **The sea level effect is the most concrete US-specific threat:** ~50 cm additional rise on the East Coast, happening over years not centuries, with flood exposure more than doubling.
4. **AMOC collapse triggers irreversible feedbacks:** 47–83 ppm CO2 released from oceans (PIK 2026), additional ~0.2°C warming, and the collapse itself becomes irreversible above 350 ppm CO2 (we're at ~430 ppm).
5. **For the mortality curve,** AMOC collapse alone probably adds single-digit percentage mortality over decades (mainly through agricultural disruption, extreme weather, and displacement). Its danger is as a cascade multiplier — it makes every other climate stressor worse.
