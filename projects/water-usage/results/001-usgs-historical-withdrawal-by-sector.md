# USGS Historical Water Withdrawal by Sector (1950–2020)

## Summary

Complete US water withdrawal time series from USGS 5-year compilations (Circulars 115 through 1441), covering 14 reporting periods from 1950 to 2015 plus partial 2020 data from PP 1894D. Total withdrawals rose steadily from 180 Bgal/d in 1950 to a peak of 440 Bgal/d in 1980, then declined to 322 Bgal/d by 2015 — the lowest level since before 1970 — even as population more than doubled. The decline is driven by thermoelectric efficiency gains (closed-loop cooling replacing once-through), reduced industrial activity, and conservation.

### Supplementary: Livestock Category Changes

The "Livestock" column has discontinuities due to category redefinitions:

- **1950–1980**: Livestock only (drinking water, feedlot operations). Small values (1.5–2.2 Bgal/d).
- **1985**: Livestock includes fish farming. Jump to 4.47 Bgal/d reflects addition of fish farms, not a real livestock increase.
- **1990–1995**: Livestock includes "Animal Specialties" subcategory (horses, fish farming). Values 4.50–5.49 Bgal/d.
- **2000+**: Livestock and Aquaculture split into separate categories. Livestock drops to 1.76 Bgal/d (true livestock only); Aquaculture appears at 3.70 Bgal/d.

## Methodology

**Primary backbone: USGS Circular 1268, Table 14.** This table presents nationally aggregated withdrawal estimates at 5-year intervals from 1950–2000 in a single consistent format. Values for 1950–1980 are rounded to two significant figures per USGS convention; 1985–2000 are rounded to three significant figures.

**Disaggregated 1985–2000 data** comes from the individual USGS water-use circulars for each period (Circular 1004 for 1985, Circular 1081 for 1990, Circular 1200 for 1995, Circular 1268 for 2000). These report industrial, mining, and commercial as separate categories.

**2005–2015 data** comes directly from USGS Circulars 1344 (2005), 1405 (2010), and 1441 (2015), which report all eight standard categories individually.

**2020 data** from USGS Professional Paper 1894D uses model-based annual estimates for three sectors only (CONUS), and is not directly comparable to the state-compilation methodology used for 1950–2015.

### Derived values

- **1995 Industrial (22.4 Bgal/d):** Circular 1268 states self-supplied industrial withdrawals in 2000 (19.7 Bgal/d) were "12 percent less than in 1995." Back-calculated: 19.7 / 0.88 = 22.4.
- **2000 Mining (3.95 Bgal/d):** Calculated as residual: Total (408) minus all other directly-reported categories (195 + 137 + 43.3 + 3.59 + 19.7 + 1.76 + 3.70) = 3.95. Cross-checked: combined domestic + livestock + aquaculture + mining reported as "less than 13 Bgal/d" → 3.59 + 1.76 + 3.70 + 3.95 = 13.0. Consistent.
- **2005 Population (301 million):** Circular 1344 states population grew "more than 5 percent" from 2000 (285.3M). Estimated at 301M. Consistent with Census Bureau mid-decade estimates.
- **1995 Mining/Commercial:** Not derivable separately. Combined commercial+mining in 1995 was approximately 6.7 Bgal/d (from Table 14 "Other Industrial" of 29.1 minus derived industrial of 22.4).

### Population source

Population figures for 1950–2000 are from USGS Circular 1268 Table 14 (sourced from US Census Bureau estimates). For 2010 and 2015, population figures are from their respective USGS circulars. The USGS uses Census Bureau July 1 estimates, which differ slightly from the decennial census counts.

## Confidence Level

**High confidence** for the main time series (1950–2015). The USGS 5-year compilations are the authoritative source for US water use data. All primary values come directly from published USGS circulars.

**Medium confidence** for derived values (1995 industrial, 2000 mining, 2005 population). These are calculated from USGS-stated relationships and residual totals. The methods are documented and cross-checked.

**Medium-low confidence** for the 2020 partial data. PP 1894D covers only three sectors using a different methodology (model-based vs. state compilation) and excludes Alaska/Hawaii. Useful for trend direction but not directly comparable to the 1950–2015 series.
