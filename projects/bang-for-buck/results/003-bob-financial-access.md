# Result: Bob's Financial, Access, and Time-Use Baseline

## Source
- **Primary sources:** Census Bureau CPS ASEC 2024, BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey 2024, BLS American Time Use Survey 2024, Kaiser Family Foundation, MEPS, Bureau of Transportation Statistics, HRSA, Commonwealth Fund
- **Date accessed:** 2026-05-02

## Findings

Bob's financial reality is defined by a paradox: the lowest income quintile spends more than it earns. The BLS Consumer Expenditure Survey shows average annual expenditures of $35,046 for the lowest quintile in 2024, against a quintile income ceiling of ~$29,932 — a structural deficit filled by credit, transfer payments, and dissaving. After essentials (housing, food, transportation, healthcare), Bob has effectively zero discretionary budget. Any intervention that costs real money competes directly with food, rent, or transportation.

Healthcare access is a patchwork. About 16.5% of those under 65 near or below 200% FPL are uninsured, and even those with Medicaid or employer coverage face significant barriers — provider shortages, high deductibles, limited time, and transportation gaps. The mental health system is particularly threadbare: 65% of non-metropolitan counties lack a single psychiatrist, and Medicaid reimbursement rates drive providers away from low-income patients.

Time is tighter than it looks. While lower-income individuals report slightly more leisure time on average (due to lower workforce participation), those who are employed face irregular schedules, shift work, and longer commutes — making consistent health routines difficult. Bob's 5+ hours of daily "leisure" is heavily dominated by TV/screens (~2.6 hrs/day), not available for structured interventions.

## Composite Picture: Bob's Financial/Access Profile

Bob earns ~$30k/year gross, takes home ~$2,000–$2,500/month after taxes (potentially more with EITC). His budget is underwater: housing (~$900–$1,100), food (~$460), transportation (~$500–$700 if he has a car), and healthcare absorb essentially everything. He has no savings buffer — a $400 unexpected expense would be a crisis.

He probably has some form of health coverage (Medicaid or employer-sponsored), but it comes with high cost-sharing that makes him functionally underinsured. He can see a primary care doctor at an FQHC regardless of insurance, but mental health care is a desert. If he needs a therapist, he's looking at months-long waits or $150+/session out of pocket.

He probably has a car, but it costs him 30–38% of his take-home pay. If he doesn't have a car, his world shrinks dramatically. Public transit may or may not exist in his area.

His time is theoretically available (5 hours of daily leisure), but practically constrained by irregular work schedules, fatigue, and the cognitive load of financial stress. The "free" interventions (walking, sleep hygiene) are genuinely accessible if he can overcome activation energy. Anything costing $50+/month is competing with groceries.

**Bottom line for intervention costing:** Bob's realistic discretionary budget for health spending is $0–$50/month. Interventions above $600/year need to be framed as replacing an existing cost (e.g., cooking replaces fast food) or accessed through insurance/assistance programs. Time-intensive interventions need to work with irregular schedules, not against them.

## Gaps and Caveats

- **ATUS income breakdown:** The published ATUS tables don't cleanly report leisure/exercise by income quintile. The time figures above are national averages, not Bob-specific. Microdata analysis would improve this but is out of scope.
- **Geographic heterogeneity:** Bob's experience varies enormously between a Medicaid-expansion state (OR, CA) and a non-expansion state (TX, FL). Insurance status, provider density, and walkability all shift. The profile above is a national composite.
- **Transfer income:** The spending-exceeds-income pattern for the lowest quintile reflects unreported or non-cash income (SNAP, housing assistance, informal support). Bob's actual consumption capacity may be ~$5,000–$10,000 higher than his reported income suggests.
- **EITC and tax credits:** Bob's effective income may be meaningfully higher than $30k if he has dependents — EITC alone can add $3,000–$7,000. This is important for cost analysis but hard to pin down without knowing household composition.
- **Post-pandemic shifts:** Medicaid unwinding (2023–2024) pushed millions off coverage. The 16.5% uninsured rate at <200% FPL may have risen since the latest data. The landscape is actively shifting.
- **Race/ethnicity interaction:** Income and race are correlated in ways that compound access barriers (provider bias, neighborhood segregation, transportation infrastructure). Bob's profile would look different if further stratified by race, but that's out of scope for this ticket.
