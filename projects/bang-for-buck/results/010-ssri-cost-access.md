# Result: Generic SSRIs — Cost and Access for Uninsured/Underinsured

## Summary

Generic SSRIs are among the cheapest pharmaceutical interventions available, with medication costs as low as $4/month through discount programs. However, the total cost of treatment extends well beyond the pill price — prescriber visits, not medication, dominate Bob's annual expense. For an uninsured person at the 25th income percentile, the realistic annual cost ranges from ~$150 (FQHC + Walmart generics) to ~$1,200 (no discounts, private practice). The most likely scenario for Bob — community health center or telehealth plus GoodRx-discounted generics — runs approximately $400/year. No routine lab monitoring is required for SSRIs, which is a significant cost advantage over many other medications. Real-world adherence is poor: only ~25-30% of patients remain on generic SSRIs at 12 months, with cost, side effects, and stigma all contributing to drop-off.

## QALY Evidence

Note: Ticket 009 (SSRI QALY evidence) was not yet complete at time of writing. QALY estimates below are derived from independent research during this ticket.

- **Net QALY gain per year (best estimate):** 0.05
- **Range:** 0.02 – 0.10 (low – high)
- **Primary source:** Katon et al. (2006), "Cost-effectiveness of a Primary Care Depression Intervention," Archives of General Psychiatry; Hollingworth et al. (2020), PANDA trial (sertraline vs placebo, NIHR)
- **Study type:** RCTs and cost-effectiveness analyses
- **Population studied:** Primary care patients with major depressive disorder
- **Population match to Bob:** Fair — most studies use general primary care populations; some include lower-income patients but few specifically target 25th-percentile demographics
- **Key caveats:**
  - The Katon et al. study found 0.041 QALYs gained over 12 months for enhanced depression care (including but not limited to medication)
  - EQ-5D utility scores for depression states: remission = 0.70, moderate depression = 0.52, severe = 0.39 (Schakel et al. 2021)
  - Moving from moderate depression to remission = +0.18 utility; but only ~30% of SSRI patients achieve full remission (vs ~20% untreated), so the average population-level gain is much smaller
  - SSRI NNT for response = 7 (Arroll et al. 2009 Cochrane review)
  - Side effects (sexual dysfunction ~30-40%, weight gain ~25%, emotional blunting ~20%) reduce net QALY gain
  - Real-world adherence of ~25-30% at 12 months further erodes the population-level benefit vs trial estimates

### Lab Work

- **SSRIs do not require routine laboratory monitoring** — unlike lithium, TCAs, or antipsychotics
- **Optional baseline labs:** Thyroid panel (TSH) to rule out thyroid-related depression — $50–$100 without insurance, but not required for SSRI initiation
- **Monitoring labs:** Only indicated if clinical concerns arise (e.g., hyponatremia in elderly patients)
- **Cost impact:** Minimal to zero — this is a meaningful cost advantage over other psychiatric medications

## Time Commitment

- **Hours per week:** 0.3
- **Category:** Low (<1 hr/wk)
- **Breakdown:**
  - Taking medication: ~1 minute/day (~0.1 hr/wk)
  - Doctor visits: 4/year × 1.5 hours (including travel + wait time) = 6 hr/year (~0.12 hr/wk)
  - Pharmacy trips: 12/year × 30 minutes = 6 hr/year (~0.12 hr/wk); can be reduced with 90-day fills or mail-order
- **Note:** The daily pill-taking is trivial; the time burden is almost entirely the periodic doctor visits and pharmacy logistics

## Access and Barriers

- **Activation energy:** Medium
- **Barriers for Bob:**
  - **Stigma:** Mental health stigma is significantly elevated in lower-income populations; ~545,000 young adults in SAMHSA data reported fear of discrimination from employers, friends, or family as a barrier to mental health treatment
  - **Prescriber access:** Must see a licensed prescriber (MD, NP, PA) for initial prescription and ongoing refills; ~2.2 million individuals fall in Medicaid coverage gap in non-expansion states
  - **Wait times:** FQHC wait times for mental health appointments can be 2–6 weeks; private practice may not accept uninsured patients
  - **Transportation:** Regular pharmacy trips (monthly without 90-day fills); in-person visits require reliable transportation
  - **Prescription refill logistics:** Monthly pharmacy visit, requires remembering refills, potential issues with prior authorizations even for generics
  - **Shift work:** Follow-up appointments during business hours conflict with shift work schedules common at this income level
  - **Cultural factors:** Some populations distrust pharmaceutical interventions or prefer non-medication approaches to mental health
  - **Recognition:** Depression is underdiagnosed in lower-income populations; many potential beneficiaries don't recognize symptoms as treatable

## Side Effects and Risks

- **Sexual dysfunction** (decreased libido, anorgasmia): 30–40% incidence; most common reason for voluntary discontinuation in younger patients
- **Weight gain:** 15–25% experience clinically significant weight gain (>7% body weight) over 12 months; problematic given Bob's likely elevated baseline BMI
- **Emotional blunting / apathy:** ~20% report feeling emotionally "flattened"; distinct from depression symptoms
- **Gastrointestinal effects** (nausea, diarrhea): 15–25%; usually transient (first 2–4 weeks)
- **Insomnia or sedation:** Varies by specific SSRI; sertraline and fluoxetine more activating, paroxetine more sedating
- **Discontinuation syndrome:** Occurs in 20–50% when stopping abruptly; dizziness, "brain zaps," irritability; not dangerous but distressing; shorter half-life SSRIs (paroxetine) worse than longer (fluoxetine)
- **Hyponatremia:** Rare (~1.5% in elderly); monitor in patients >65
- **Increased suicidal ideation:** FDA black box warning for ages <25; rare but requires monitoring in early weeks
- **Serotonin syndrome:** Very rare with SSRI monotherapy; risk increases with drug interactions

## Adherence

- **Real-world adherence rate:** 0.28 (28% at 12 months)
- **Source:** Adherence and Persistence Across Antidepressant Therapeutic Classes, CNS Drugs 2017 (US claims analysis, >520,000 patients); Italian population-based study (PMC 2023) found 68.7% discontinuation within 12 months
- **Notes:** Adherence drops steeply — ~41% at 3 months, ~21-25% at 12 months. Generic SSRIs have worse adherence than branded or SNRI alternatives. Key reasons for discontinuation: feeling better and stopping prematurely (~30%), side effects especially sexual dysfunction (~25%), cost of visits/medication (~15%), stigma (~10%), lack of perceived benefit (~20%). Lower-income patients face additional barriers (pharmacy access, appointment scheduling) that likely push adherence below general-population rates, though specific data for the 25th percentile is sparse.

## Evidence Quality

- **Tier:** 1
- **Tier justification:** SSRIs have the deepest cost-effectiveness evidence base of almost any medical intervention. Multiple large RCTs (STAR*D, PANDA), high-quality meta-analyses (Cipriani et al. 2018 Lancet, Arroll et al. Cochrane), and extensive NICE/Tufts CEA Registry entries. The QALY methodology for depression is mature with validated EQ-5D utility mappings.
- **Key studies:**
  - Cipriani et al. (2018), "Comparative efficacy and acceptability of 21 antidepressant drugs," Lancet — network meta-analysis of 522 trials, 116,477 participants
  - Hollingworth et al. (2020), PANDA trial — sertraline vs placebo in primary care, NIHR-funded
  - Katon et al. (2006), "Cost-effectiveness of a Primary Care Depression Intervention," Archives of General Psychiatry — 0.041 QALY gain over 12 months
  - Schakel et al. (2021), "Utility scores for different health states related to depression," Quality of Life Research — EQ-5D reference values
  - Arroll et al. (2009), "Antidepressants versus placebo for depression in primary care," Cochrane — NNT = 7
- **Limitations:**
  - Cost data is fragmented across sources (GoodRx, MEPS, FQHC fee schedules) — no single authoritative source for uninsured treatment costs
  - Most QALY studies use insured populations with better access and higher adherence; Bob's real-world benefit may be lower
  - Side effect impact on net QALYs is estimated, not directly measured in most CEA studies
  - Cost-effectiveness varies enormously with insurance status — Medicaid makes SSRIs nearly free; uninsured makes them a moderate expense
